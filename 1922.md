# MACRO/MICRO GLOBAL ECONOMIC ASSESSMENT - YEAR: 1922

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

1922 was a hinge year between the immediate post-World War I dislocation and the more expansionary middle 1920s. The world economy was recovering from the 1920-1921 slump, but recovery was highly uneven. The United States, the United Kingdom, France, Germany, Canada, Italy, Belgium, and the Soviet republics grew strongly in real terms from depressed bases. Japan grew only slowly, Czechoslovakia contracted, and large agrarian economies such as China and India remained difficult to measure with modern national-account precision.

The central macroeconomic divide was deflation versus currency collapse. Many advanced economies experienced falling consumer prices as wartime inflation unwound and central banks moved toward monetary stabilization. Germany was the most important exception: its real economy was recovering, but the papiermark was collapsing into hyperinflation. Austria was also close to financial collapse before League of Nations stabilization. This split shaped capital flows, savings behavior, trade competitiveness, and political stability.

1922 was also a year of monetary reconstruction. The Genoa Economic and Financial Conference tried to build a postwar monetary order and gave official legitimacy to the gold-exchange standard idea. It did not solve reparations, German fiscal weakness, or Soviet reintegration. The Treaty of Rapallo between Germany and Soviet Russia showed how excluded or isolated economies could build bilateral alternatives when multilateral reconstruction failed.

At the micro level, the mechanisms of recovery were sectoral and uneven. Automobiles, electricity, radio, chemicals, oil, construction, and reconstruction-heavy industries advanced, while coal, shipbuilding, old textiles, and agriculture remained weak in several economies. Ford's acquisition of Lincoln, the formation of the British Broadcasting Company, and the first clinical use of insulin illustrate how corporate organization, communications, and healthcare innovation were beginning to reshape future productivity, consumption, and human capital.

Data quality is a defining issue for 1922. The GDP ranking below uses Maddison Project Database 2023 GDP per capita and population, expressed in constant 2011 international dollars. Direct 1922 observations are used where available; China and Poland are included by bracketed Maddison interpolation because excluding them would create a misleading ranking. Soviet-bloc and non-market economy figures should be treated as rough historical reconstructions, not official contemporary accounts.

## 2. KEY THEMES OF 1922

**1. Postwar recovery after the 1920-1921 recession.**  
The most visible theme was rebound. The constructed MPD-based world GDP estimate rises from about $4,232.9bn to $4,488.8bn in 2011 international dollars, or about 6.0 percent. France, the United Kingdom, Germany, Canada, Italy, Belgium, and the Soviet republics grew rapidly from low bases. This mattered historically because it made the 1920s look like a recoverable decade rather than a permanently broken postwar depression. The forward linkage was the mid-1920s boom in several economies, but also the buildup of financial fragility.

**2. Deflation in many advanced economies, hyperinflation in Central Europe.**  
The United States, the United Kingdom, France, Belgium, the Netherlands, Spain, Canada, Japan, and Sweden recorded deflation or near-deflation in the JST Macrohistory series, while Germany recorded hyperinflation and Austria required international stabilization. The theme is significant because it shows that "postwar inflation" did not move uniformly. Some economies prioritized currency credibility and debt stabilization; others were overwhelmed by fiscal and political constraints. The forward linkage was Germany's 1923 hyperinflation climax and the broader interwar struggle over gold-standard restoration.

**3. The failed attempt to rebuild a cooperative monetary order.**  
The Genoa conference sought European reconstruction, Soviet reintegration, and principles for a gold-exchange standard. It offered a conceptual bridge between the pre-1914 classical gold standard and later managed international monetary systems, but it failed to settle reparations or restore trust. The affected countries were mainly European, but the implications were global because sterling, the dollar, and gold formed the core of international payments. The forward linkage was the fragile gold-exchange order of the mid-1920s and its collapse in the Great Depression.

**4. Reparations, war debts, and public balance sheets.**  
France and Italy carried extremely high public debt ratios in the JST data; Britain also carried a very high debt burden, while the United States had a lower but still elevated wartime ratio. German fiscal weakness and reparations uncertainty destabilized the mark. The historical significance is that postwar debt politics became a macroeconomic regime problem, not just a fiscal accounting problem. The forward linkage was the Ruhr crisis of 1923, Dawes Plan of 1924, and the international debt chain connecting Germany, France, Britain, and the United States.

**5. The Soviet New Economic Policy and partial market restoration.**  
The Soviet republics recovered in 1922 after civil war, famine, and war communism. The New Economic Policy reintroduced private trade and small-scale markets while the state kept control of heavy industry and strategic sectors. This was significant because it proved that the early Soviet economy could recover through a mixed regime, not only through centralized command. The forward linkage was the NEP recovery of the 1920s and the later turn toward forced collectivization and Five-Year Plans.

**6. Commodity normalization after wartime boom.**  
Oil prices had fallen from the 1920 spike to roughly $1.61 per barrel by 1922 in commonly cited BP-derived historical series. Gold remained fixed at $20.67 per troy ounce under the U.S. gold standard framework. Agricultural exporters such as Canada, Argentina, Australia, India, Brazil, and the Dutch East Indies were recovering but remained exposed to price swings. The forward linkage was persistent farm distress in several economies despite industrial recovery.

**7. Industrial modernization beneath macro instability.**  
Even where macro data were weak, microeconomic modernization continued. Automobiles, electrification, petroleum distribution, radio broadcasting, chemicals, and medical innovation were expanding. These sectors mattered because they changed household consumption, urban infrastructure, advertising, transport costs, and human capital. The forward linkage was the consumer-durable and communications economy of the later 1920s.

### 3C. Trade & Geopolitics

The Genoa conference was the year's most important economic-diplomatic meeting. It attempted to solve problems of reconstruction, reparations, Soviet trade relations, currency stabilization, capital flight, and central-bank cooperation. Its failure to settle reparations or integrate Germany and Soviet Russia into a stable framework was one of the clearest limits of postwar multilateralism.

The Treaty of Rapallo, signed by Germany and Soviet Russia in April 1922, renounced mutual claims and restored diplomatic and economic relations. Economically, it showed that excluded states could bypass the Allied-centered order. Politically, it alarmed France and Britain and deepened the sense that reparations and reconstruction were unresolved.

Tariff and trade barriers remained important but fragmented. The European economy had new borders, new currencies, and new state institutions after the collapse of empires. Colonial trade channels remained crucial for Britain, France, the Netherlands, and Japan. Regional integration bodies such as the later EEC did not yet exist, and ASEAN did not exist.

### 3D. Commodities & Energy

Oil was becoming more central to transport, military logistics, shipping, and industry. The 1922 price of roughly $1.61 per barrel reflected retreat from the 1920 spike as production expanded. The United States, Mexico, the Dutch East Indies, and the Baku region were important in the wider oil story, though production data differ by source and jurisdiction.

Gold was not a freely floating modern asset. The U.S. official gold price remained $20.67 per troy ounce, making gold a monetary anchor rather than an ordinary alternative asset. Copper, lead, zinc, and other base metals were recovering from the postwar collapse, but evidence points to weak early-1922 conditions rather than a synchronized global boom.

Agricultural conditions were central to welfare. Wheat, cotton, coffee, sugar, rubber, tea, rice, jute, wool, and beef linked farmers and colonial exporters to global demand. Many farm economies had not recovered from the price collapses after World War I, even where national GDP was rising.

### 3E. Labor & Demographics

Labor markets were not comparable across countries. The United Kingdom's unemployment rate was high, the United States still had elevated unemployment, and several small European economies had severe labor slack. Germany's measured unemployment looked low in some series, but hyperinflation makes the statistic misleading because workers could be employed while real wages and savings collapsed.

Demographically, the world remained young and agrarian outside the advanced industrial economies. China and India dominated population scale. Urbanization continued in the United States, Western Europe, Japan, and Latin American cities such as Buenos Aires and São Paulo. Migration was economically important, but U.S. immigration restriction after the 1921 Emergency Quota Act changed transatlantic labor flows.

**Economic narrative:**

The United States was the largest economy by a wide margin and was moving out of the sharp 1920-1921 downturn. Output growth resumed, CPI prices were still falling, and the Federal Reserve eased money-market conditions. The New York Federal Reserve discount rate moved down during the year, while fiscal balances improved as wartime spending receded. The United States also remained the central creditor economy for postwar finance, even though it did not join the Genoa monetary program.

The main domestic tension was the split between industrial recovery and rural weakness. Manufacturing, automobiles, electric machinery, construction, and consumer durables were improving, while farm prices and farm debt remained difficult after the collapse of the wartime agricultural boom. The economy was becoming more consumption-led and credit-intensive, a pattern that would define much of the later 1920s.

**Micro/sectoral highlights:**

Automobiles, electrical goods, petroleum, chemicals, and residential construction were the clearest growth drivers. Ford acquired Lincoln in 1922, and mass-production carmaking continued to reshape steel, rubber, glass, petroleum distribution, roads, retail credit, and household mobility. Railroads were recovering but faced competition from motor transport. Banking conditions were healthier than in 1920-1921, but agricultural banks remained exposed to weak land and crop prices. Data on consumer confidence and national real-estate prices are not available in a modern comparable form.

### 6C. Currencies

The U.S. dollar was the key gold-linked creditor currency. Sterling remained a reserve and trade currency but was not yet restored to prewar gold parity. Policymakers in London treated credibility and eventual restoration as a central objective, even while easing Bank Rate in 1922.

Germany's papiermark was the most important currency crisis. It depreciated rapidly and set the stage for the 1923 hyperinflation climax. Austria also faced severe currency instability before the League stabilization program. France and Italy had weaker fiscal and currency positions than the United States or United Kingdom, although they did not experience German-style hyperinflation in 1922.

### 6D. Alternative assets

Gold was a monetary anchor, not a free-floating hedge in the modern sense. The official U.S. price remained $20.67 per troy ounce. In countries with unstable currencies, foreign exchange, gold, real estate, inventories, and equities could function as stores of value, but legal access and market prices varied.

Real estate data are limited. JST housing total returns for 1922 show positive returns in the United States, United Kingdom, France, Belgium, Switzerland, and Finland, and negative or weak returns in the Netherlands, Denmark, and Sweden. These estimates are useful but not fully comparable across national housing markets.

## 7. RISKS, CRISES & BLACK SWANS

The largest active crisis was German hyperinflation. In 1922 it was already a systemic currency failure, not merely high inflation. The real economy still expanded, but savings, contracts, tax collection, wage bargaining, and political legitimacy were being destroyed. With hindsight, 1922 was the penultimate year before the full 1923 collapse and Rentenmark stabilization.

Austria was another active crisis. The Austrian state was fiscally and monetarily fragile after the Habsburg collapse. The League of Nations stabilization framework in October 1922 was one of the first major international financial rescue programs of the interwar era. It helped prevent a deeper immediate collapse but came with fiscal discipline and external oversight.

The Soviet economy was recovering but fragile. NEP created a practical recovery mechanism, but famine aftereffects, inflation, weak industry, and urban-rural price imbalances remained serious. With hindsight, the later "scissors crisis" between industrial and agricultural prices showed that recovery itself could create new distributional tensions.

Agricultural debt was a hidden vulnerability in several economies. The United States, Canada, Australia, Argentina, Brazil, and India all had regions where farmers were exposed to postwar price declines and debt burdens. This risk did not produce a single 1922 global crash, but it weakened rural banks and political coalitions.

Japan's financial system remained fragile after the 1920 panic. With hindsight, the 1923 Great Kanto earthquake would soon become a major shock to banking, insurance, construction, and fiscal policy. That was not knowable in 1922, but the fragility was already visible.

A broader tail risk was failure of monetary reconstruction. Genoa did not solve reparations, debt, or Soviet reintegration. With hindsight, the interwar gold-exchange system would remain structurally fragile, dependent on confidence, fiscal discipline, and cooperation that were often absent.

## 8. YEAR-OVER-YEAR DELTA (1922 vs. 1921)

1922 was materially better than 1921 in aggregate output. The constructed MPD-based world estimate rose about {world_growth:.1f} percent. The largest top-20 rebounds were France at about +17.9 percent, the Former USSR at about +15.9 percent, Canada at about +14.6 percent, the United Kingdom at about +9.6 percent, Belgium at about +9.8 percent, Germany at about +8.8 percent, and Italy at about +8.4 percent. The main negative case in the top 20 was Czechoslovakia, down about -2.6 percent.

The monetary delta was more complex. Deflation continued in many advanced economies, but interest rates were lower than in 1921 in key money centers. This helped recovery but also reinforced the focus on restoring currency credibility. In Germany, the change from 1921 to 1922 was a worsening currency crisis despite real output growth. Austria moved from near-collapse toward League-supervised stabilization.

The geopolitical delta was significant. In 1921, the postwar order still looked unsettled but potentially negotiable. In 1922, Genoa exposed the limits of cooperation, while Rapallo showed that Germany and Soviet Russia could create a separate channel. These shifts altered expectations for 1923, when the Ruhr crisis would move from risk to reality.

The sectoral delta favored industry and construction over agriculture. Reconstruction, autos, electricity, and urban activity improved. Farm sectors remained strained by lower prices, debt, and uneven demand. Consumer durables and radio began to look more important as future growth engines.

The power-balance delta favored the United States. Its economy was the largest, its currency was credible, its financial markets were recovering, and its creditor position was strong. Britain remained central to trade and finance, but sterling's role was more contested. Germany remained industrially powerful but financially unstable. China and India were huge by population and total GDP, but low income per capita and weak centralized statistical systems limited their policy influence.

## 9. DATA CONFIDENCE & GAPS

The most reliable statistics are for the United States, the United Kingdom, France, Canada, Australia, Sweden, the Netherlands, Belgium, Italy, Japan, Spain, and other advanced economies covered by the JST Macrohistory Database. Even there, unemployment, housing, and financial indicators are not always directly comparable.

GDP rankings are estimates, not contemporary official national accounts. The main ranking uses MPD 2023 GDP per capita and population. GDP is expressed in constant 2011 international dollars, not nominal 1922 U.S. dollars. China and Poland are included by bracketed interpolation between Maddison benchmarks; this is more historically plausible than excluding them, but it lowers confidence. MPD direct observations are stronger for the United States, India, Germany, United Kingdom, France, Japan, Italy, Former USSR, Indonesia, Spain, Canada, Argentina, Belgium, Netherlands, Australia, Czechoslovakia, Mexico, and Brazil.

The Former USSR figure is especially uncertain. It is a reconstructed aggregate for a non-market, post-civil-war economy. A plausible uncertainty band of 20-30 percent should be assumed. The same caution applies, though in different ways, to colonial economies such as India and the Dutch East Indies and to fragmented China.

World GDP is a constructed estimate from available country data and interpolation. It is not an official world national account. World trade volume, global capital-flow totals, global unemployment, global wage growth, global R&D spending, global corporate rankings, and most global sector output values are Data not available in a single reliable 1922 series from the sources consulted.

Current-account, budget, public-debt, interest-rate, and exchange-rate data are strongest for JST countries. They are weak or unavailable for China, India, Indonesia, Poland, Argentina, Czechoslovakia, Mexico, Brazil, and the Former USSR in this report. Exchange-rate data can be particularly misleading because some series use different local-currency unit conventions and because hyperinflation destroys annual-average interpretability.

The weakest evidence in this document concerns corporate rankings, country-level micro indicators outside advanced economies, global trade volume, and real-estate conditions. The strongest evidence concerns MPD-derived GDP/population estimates, JST macro-financial indicators for advanced economies, official or quasi-official interest-rate histories, and well-documented historical events such as Genoa, Rapallo, Austrian stabilization, and the first clinical use of insulin.
