# MACRO/MICRO GLOBAL ECONOMIC ASSESSMENT - YEAR: 1930

### 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

1930 was the year the 1929 financial shock became a worldwide depression in output, trade, prices, credit, and employment. The Maddison Project Database 2023 available-country total used here is approximately 5,458.1 billion 2011 international dollars, down -3.47 percent from 1929. This is a reconstructed PPP total, not an official world national-account release.

The central macro force was deflation under the interwar gold standard. Countries defending gold parity often tried to adjust through wages, prices, budgets, and credit contraction rather than currency depreciation. This made debt heavier in real terms and pushed distress through banks, exporters, farmers, construction, and durable-goods producers.

Policy choices intensified the downturn. The United States passed Smoot-Hawley in June, Japan returned to gold in January, Germany moved toward deflationary austerity under reparations pressure, and most governments still treated balanced budgets and currency defense as priorities. The Bank for International Settlements opened in 1930, but international monetary cooperation was weakening as the depression deepened.

### 2. KEY THEMES OF 1930

**1. Gold-standard deflation.** The gold-exchange standard transmitted monetary tightness across countries. The United States, Britain, Germany, France, Japan, the Netherlands, Belgium, Sweden, and many linked economies were affected. The forward link was clear: countries leaving gold in 1931 or soon after gained more monetary flexibility.

**2. Trade protection and retaliation.** Smoot-Hawley did not cause the whole depression, but it worsened confidence and encouraged retaliation. Export-dependent economies such as Canada, Argentina, Australia, Germany, Japan, Brazil, Belgium, the Netherlands, and Czechoslovakia were especially exposed.

**3. Commodity deflation.** Wheat, wool, coffee, rubber, sugar, silk, cotton, metals, and oil all mattered as transmission channels. Primary producers faced lower cash income, weaker collateral, lower fiscal revenue, and reduced import capacity.

**4. Financial fragility.** Asset-price declines turned into credit stress. U.S. bank failures intensified late in 1930, while Germany and other debtor economies carried refinancing risks that became more dangerous in 1931.

**5. Orthodoxy before Keynesian policy.** Most governments had not yet accepted large countercyclical fiscal stimulus. Austerity, wage adjustment, and currency defense dominated policy thinking in Germany, Britain, Japan, Australia, Italy, Poland, and many gold-linked economies.

**6. Political stress and regime change.** Brazil's revolution, Spain's regime transition, Germany's political radicalization, Japan's policy backlash, and Soviet forced planning show that 1930 was also a political-economy crisis.

### 3. MACROECONOMIC OVERVIEW

#### 3A. Global Aggregates

**World GDP and growth rate.** MPD 2023 rows with both 1929 and 1930 observations sum to about 5,458.1 billion 2011 international dollars in 1930, down -3.47 percent. This document calculation is useful for scale, but it is not an official world total.

**Global trade volume.** Data not available as a single harmonized 1930 trade-volume figure in the validated sources used here. Directionally, trade contracted sharply as incomes, prices, credit, and policy cooperation weakened.

**Reserve currency dynamics.** Bretton Woods did not exist. Gold was the anchor, while sterling and the U.S. dollar were major reserve and settlement currencies. China remained a major silver-standard exception.

**Inflation and commodities.** The dominant trend was deflation. U.S. crude oil first-purchase price was about $1.19 per barrel, and official U.S. gold remained $20.67 per fine troy ounce. #### 3C. Trade & Geopolitics

Smoot-Hawley, signed on June 17, 1930, was the central trade-policy event. It amplified an income-driven trade collapse and encouraged retaliation. There was no Cold War, EEC, or ASEAN. The relevant geopolitical frame was interwar reparations, imperial trade, colonial dependency, and unresolved post-World War I financial commitments.

#### 3D. Commodities & Energy

OPEC did not exist. Oil, coal, metals, grains, wool, silk, coffee, sugar, and rubber were depressed by weaker industrial demand, weaker transport demand, and lower purchasing power. Gold was a monetary anchor, not a freely floating alternative asset inside gold-standard systems.

#### 3E. Labor & Demographics

Unemployment rose sharply in industrial economies, but definitions are not comparable across countries. Rural distress in China, India, Indonesia, Brazil, and Eastern Europe often appeared as underemployment, debt stress, and lower farm income rather than measured unemployment. Migration and labor mobility were increasingly restricted as jobs disappeared.

### 7. RISKS, CRISES & BLACK SWANS

The active crisis was the Great Depression: output contraction, deflation, rising unemployment, trade collapse, bank stress, and commodity-price weakness. The main near-miss was the gold-standard and banking crisis that would erupt more fully in 1931. With hindsight, the most visible policy mistakes were defending gold too long, tightening fiscal policy into contraction, escalating tariffs, and underestimating banking fragility.

Tail risks that materialized included trade retaliation, U.S. banking stress, Brazil's political rupture, worsening German political risk, and commodity-exporter distress. Tail risks that had not yet fully materialized included Britain leaving gold, Japan's second gold suspension, the deepest U.S. bank failures, New Deal intervention, and Italy's later IRI rescue system.

### 8. YEAR-OVER-YEAR DELTA (1930 vs. 1929)

The shift from 1929 to 1930 was the shift from crash to depression. MPD available-country total GDP fell -3.47 percent. Among the top 20, the largest MPD contractions were Australia -9.53 percent, United States -9.49 percent, Japan -7.65 percent, Brazil -5.96 percent, Spain -5.05 percent, Poland -4.63 percent, Italy -4.52 percent, Argentina -4.13 percent, Canada -3.33 percent, and Czechoslovakia -3.26 percent.

Policy also changed. Smoot-Hawley made trade conflict explicit. Japan's gold return made deflation worse. Germany moved deeper into austerity. The BIS opened as a new institution, but the system it was meant to stabilize was already weakening. Rankings changed only slightly: Sweden entered the top 20 and Mexico exited.

### 9. DATA CONFIDENCE & GAPS

Higher-confidence statistics exist for the United States, United Kingdom, Canada, Australia, Sweden, the Netherlands, Belgium, France, Germany, and Japan, although modern-style national accounts were often reconstructed later. Medium-confidence cases include Italy, Spain, Poland, Czechoslovakia, Argentina, and Brazil. Low-confidence cases include China, India, Indonesia, and the Former USSR.

GDP, GDP per capita, population, and growth figures in the top-20 table are MPD estimates. They are not official 1930 national accounts. The available-country world total is a calculation from MPD rows, not a published official world total. Soviet and other non-market GDP levels should be treated as rough approximations with likely 20 to 30 percent uncertainty.

The weakest evidence concerns inflation, unemployment, policy rates, debt/GDP, current-account balances, budget balances, exchange-rate annual averages, non-U.S. stock index returns, and corporate rankings.
