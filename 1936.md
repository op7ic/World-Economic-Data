# MACRO/MICRO GLOBAL ECONOMIC ASSESSMENT - YEAR: 1936

### 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

1936 was a late-Depression recovery year, but it was not a return to the pre-1929 world economy. Output expanded strongly in many major economies, asset markets recovered, commodity prices strengthened, and unemployment fell from crisis highs. Yet the recovery was politically and institutionally unstable. The old gold-standard order broke further, the remaining gold bloc retreated, exchange controls and capital controls remained widespread, and state direction of industry became more important in Germany, Japan, the Soviet Union, Italy, France, China, Brazil, and Mexico.

Using the Maddison Project Database 2023 method, with GDP estimated from real GDP per capita times population in constant 2011 international dollars, the largest 1936 economies were the United States, Former USSR, China, Germany, United Kingdom, India, France, Japan, Italy, and Indonesia. The MPD country-coverage sum for economies with available 1936 observations is about 5.98 trillion 2011 international dollars, implying roughly 6.3 percent growth from the comparable 1935 country-coverage total. This is not a literal fully measured world GDP total, because country coverage and historical reconstruction are incomplete. It is best read as a consistent historical PPP estimate across available country series.

The year's biggest monetary event was the September 1936 Tripartite Agreement among the United States, United Kingdom, and France after France left the gold standard. This reduced the immediate threat of competitive devaluation among the main democratic financial powers, but it did not rebuild a fully open international monetary system. The dollar and sterling had already left old gold-standard parities, and the franc's devaluation confirmed that internal recovery had become more important than defending pre-Depression exchange rates.

The year's biggest real-economy shifts were rearmament and state-directed investment. Germany's Four Year Plan, Japan's move after the February 26 Incident, Soviet defense-industrial planning, Italy's Ethiopian-war aftershocks, and French and British rearmament all redirected resources toward heavy industry, transport, chemicals, aviation, and military supply. Spain's civil war was the clearest shock: MPD estimates suggest a very large GDP collapse in 1936, and the country's financial and industrial system split into rival wartime economies.

The microeconomic pattern was uneven. Housing, automobiles, consumer durables, and construction helped the United States and United Kingdom; heavy industry and defense supply drove Germany, Japan, the USSR, and Czechoslovakia; commodity rebound helped Canada, Australia, Brazil, Indonesia, and parts of Latin America; labor reforms transformed France; and monetary reform reshaped China. 1936 therefore sits between two eras: the Depression recovery of the mid-1930s and the militarized, controlled, and conflict-driven economies of the late 1930s and 1940s.

### 2. KEY THEMES OF 1936

**1. Recovery without normalization.** The world economy was growing again, and several major economies posted strong real GDP growth. The United States, Germany, Japan, Brazil, Czechoslovakia, Mexico, and the Netherlands grew rapidly by MPD estimates. However, employment, trade, and finance did not normalize. Unemployment remained high in the United States, United Kingdom, Canada, Netherlands, and Australia by the standards of their own later recoveries. World trade was recovering but remained fragmented by tariffs, quotas, exchange controls, clearing agreements, and imperial preferences. The forward linkage was the 1937-1938 setback in several economies and the increasing importance of state-led demand.

**2. Final collapse of the interwar gold-bloc constraint.** France's September devaluation and the Tripartite Agreement shifted the international monetary regime. The United Kingdom had left gold in 1931, the United States had devalued in 1933-1934, and France's move ended the main democratic defense of old gold parity. Countries most affected included France, the Netherlands, Switzerland, and gold-linked financial centers. The significance was not merely exchange-rate technicality: it was a policy-regime shift from external parity first to domestic recovery first.

**3. Rearmament as macroeconomic stimulus and microeconomic allocator.** Germany, Japan, the USSR, Italy, the United Kingdom, France, and Czechoslovakia all saw defense demand become more important. In Germany, hidden finance and exchange controls directed resources toward rearmament. In Japan, Takahashi's assassination weakened fiscal restraint. In the USSR, the Second Five-Year Plan increased the economic significance of defense industry. The forward linkage was a late-1930s investment boom in heavy industry, aircraft, chemicals, metals, and transport equipment, but also a squeeze on consumer goods and external balances.

**4. Managed trade, protection, and bilateralism.** International commerce did not recover through a simple liberalization wave. The United States pursued reciprocal trade agreements, the British Empire maintained imperial preference, Germany relied on clearing agreements and foreign-exchange allocation, Italy faced sanctions and autarkic policy after Ethiopia, and colonial economies depended on metropolitan trade channels. This mattered because micro-level firms did not face a single global market. They operated in segmented currency and quota zones.

**5. Commodity rebound and primary-producer relief.** Grain prices, crude oil prices, and many raw materials improved from early-1930s lows. This helped Canada, Australia, Brazil, Argentina, Indonesia, Mexico, and parts of the United States. The effect was not uniform: producers with debt burdens or drought exposure remained vulnerable, and price recovery did not restore pre-1929 agricultural prosperity everywhere. The forward linkage was continued sensitivity to global demand and to state price-support policies.

**6. Labor-market regime shifts.** 1936 was a landmark year for labor institutions. France's Matignon Agreements, paid holidays, collective bargaining, and the 40-hour week changed employer-worker relations. In the United States, New Deal labor institutions and industrial unionism gained practical importance. In Germany, Italy, Japan, and the USSR, labor organization was increasingly subordinated to state, party, or military goals. The same global recovery therefore produced very different labor-market regimes: democratic bargaining in some economies, coercive mobilization in others.

**7. Asia's monetary and industrial transition.** China's 1935 fabi reform carried into 1936 as a major attempt at monetary centralization after the silver crisis. Japan's industrial structure shifted further toward heavy and chemical industry. India remained a large colonial agrarian economy with important textile, jute, steel, and railway sectors. Indonesia remained a colonial export platform. The forward linkage was decisive: the 1937 Sino-Japanese War would transform East Asian fiscal, monetary, and industrial structures.

### 3. MACROECONOMIC OVERVIEW

#### 3A. Global Aggregates

**World GDP and growth.** The MPD country-coverage total for available 1936 observations is approximately 5.98 trillion constant 2011 international dollars, compared with approximately 5.63 trillion for 1935 using the same available-country coverage. This implies growth of about 6.3 percent. This is a reconstructed PPP estimate, not a contemporary current-dollar national-account total. It excludes countries without 1936 observations and uses later historical estimates for several economies.

**Global trade volume.** Data not available as a single fully recomputed 1936 global trade-volume number in this document. The Federico-Tena-Junguito historical trade dataset provides import and export series at current and constant 1913 prices for many polities through 1938, and the League of Nations World Economic Survey is the key contemporary cross-country source. The qualitative pattern is clear: trade recovered from the trough but remained below a fully integrated pre-Depression system because of tariffs, quotas, exchange controls, clearing agreements, and currency blocs.

**Reserve currency dynamics.** The dollar and sterling were the leading reserve and settlement currencies. The dollar was tied to the US Treasury gold price of 35 dollars per ounce, while sterling operated in a managed post-gold-standard regime. The franc ceased to be a hard gold-bloc anchor after September 1936. Germany, Italy, the USSR, China, and several emerging economies used exchange controls or administratively managed systems, which limited free reserve-currency convertibility.

**Global inflation trends.** Inflation was generally mild in the United States and United Kingdom, stronger in France and Italy, and distorted in controlled economies. France combined devaluation, wage gains, and fiscal stress. Germany's measured CPI was low, but price controls hid rearmament pressure. The Netherlands still experienced deflation before its late gold exit.

**Key commodities.** Gold was officially priced at 35 dollars per ounce in the United States. The EIA historical series reports the US crude-oil first purchase price at about 1.09 dollars per barrel for 1936. US grain prices rose strongly by contemporary Survey of Current Business reporting, with the BLS grain index about 7 percent higher than in 1935. Copper and some other metals improved with industrial recovery, but a single cross-country annual price table is not reported here; data not available in the compiled format used for this document.

**International capital flows.** Capital moved toward perceived monetary safety, especially the United States and sterling assets, until the Tripartite stabilization reduced some immediate currency-war risk. France and other gold-bloc economies faced capital flight before devaluation. Germany and Italy relied heavily on controls, clearing agreements, and administrative allocation rather than free international capital markets.

#### 3B. Monetary & Financial Conditions

The monetary environment was unusually easy in the Anglo-American economies. US short-term rates were very low, and gold inflows expanded monetary conditions. The United Kingdom held Bank Rate at 2 percent, a historic low policy stance, while cheap money supported housing, public debt service, and investment. Germany's financial conditions were controlled rather than conventionally easy: official interest rates and CPI understated the true pressure created by rearmament and foreign-exchange scarcity. Japan's conditions moved toward fiscal dominance after the February 1936 assassination of Takahashi Korekiyo.

There was no Bretton Woods system in 1936. The world was in an interwar mixture of managed currencies, gold remnants, sterling-area arrangements, exchange controls, and bilateral clearing systems. The Tripartite Agreement reduced open competitive depreciation among the United States, United Kingdom, and France, but did not create a rules-based postwar-style system.

Equity markets were strongest where recovery expectations were highest. The Dow Jones Industrial Average closed 1936 with a 24.82 percent annual gain, while a total-return S&P series reports a 33.92 percent gain. Several European markets also recovered in local terms, but standardized open data for all major exchanges are not available here. Government bond yields remained low in the United States and United Kingdom, while Italy and some continental economies had higher yields reflecting fiscal, currency, or political risk.

#### 3C. Trade & Geopolitics

1936 had no EEC, ASEAN, WTO, or Bretton Woods institutions. Trade policy was fragmented. The United States continued reciprocal trade-agreement liberalization within a still-protectionist world. The British Empire operated with imperial preference. Germany used exchange controls and bilateral clearing to prioritize raw materials and rearmament. Italy's Ethiopian war and sanctions strengthened autarkic policy. Spain's civil war disrupted Iberian trade and foreign exchange.

Geopolitically, the world economy was becoming militarized. Germany remilitarized the Rhineland in 1936 and launched the Four Year Plan. Japan's military influence increased after the February 26 Incident. The USSR was planning under growing security concerns. China faced Japanese pressure and was trying to centralize currency and finance before full-scale war. These were economic as well as political events because they redirected investment, imports, labor, and public finance.

#### 3D. Commodities & Energy

There was no OPEC in 1936. Oil pricing was shaped by US production, state-level proration, private company behavior, and international concession systems. The US crude-oil first purchase price was around 1.09 dollars per barrel. Gold was fixed officially at 35 dollars per ounce in the United States, making gold inflows a monetary issue as much as a commodity issue.

Agricultural staples were recovering from Depression lows. Grain prices rose in 1936, aided by demand recovery and supply conditions. Export economies such as Canada, Australia, Argentina, Brazil, and Indonesia benefited to varying degrees. Metals and industrial raw materials gained from manufacturing and rearmament demand, especially in economies linked to steel, machinery, and armaments.

#### 3E. Labor & Demographics

Employment improved but remained historically weak in several advanced economies. The United States unemployment rate in JST was 16.9 percent, the United Kingdom 9.4 percent, Canada 13.45 percent, the Netherlands 18.85 percent, and Australia 11.0 percent. These rates are not directly comparable because definitions, relief work, labor-force measurement, and social-insurance registration differed by country. Germany's low measured unemployment reflected rearmament, labor service, conscription, repression, and controls rather than a normal market recovery.

Demographically, the largest economies by total GDP were often not the richest per person. China and India ranked high by output because of population scale, while the United States, United Kingdom, Netherlands, Australia, Canada, and Belgium had much higher GDP per capita. Urbanization continued in industrial centers, but rural population shares remained very high in China, India, Indonesia, Brazil, Mexico, and the USSR. Migration was constrained by Depression-era restrictions and political barriers.

**Economic narrative and micro/sectoral highlights:**

Measured CPI inflation was subdued because prices and wages were controlled, not because demand was balanced. Spain's civil war produced emergency finance, expropriation, collectivization in some areas, and military control in others. Mexico and Brazil increased the policy importance of strategic sectors and labor regulation.

#### 5C. Innovation & technology

The economically important technologies of 1936 were aviation, radio, chemicals, electrification, automotive engineering, rail modernization, synthetic materials, and industrial process improvements. They were not neutral technologies: many were pulled into defense and state-planning systems. Germany and Japan emphasized aircraft, metallurgy, chemicals, and military engineering. The United States emphasized automobiles, electrical equipment, radio, consumer durables, and industrial production.

R&D spending by country for 1936 is not available in a comparable global dataset used here. Patent activity data exist in national archives but are not consolidated in this document. Major infrastructure themes included public works in the United States, housing in the United Kingdom, transport and industrial planning in the USSR and Germany, and strategic industrial geography in Poland and Czechoslovakia.

#### 5D. Labor markets & human capital

Labor-market recovery was real but incomplete. The United States still had Depression-scale unemployment, and Canada, the Netherlands, Australia, and the United Kingdom had substantial joblessness. France's labor movement achieved a historic institutional breakthrough in 1936. Germany, Italy, Japan, and the USSR increasingly subordinated labor to state objectives.

Sectoral employment shifted toward construction, manufacturing, defense supply, transport, and state works in the recovering economies. In agrarian economies, hidden rural underemployment remained more important than measured unemployment. Education and human capital data are uneven, but industrializing economies increasingly needed engineers, technicians, mechanics, clerks, and skilled factory labor. Migration remained constrained by Depression-era policies and political borders.

#### 6B. Fixed income

JST long-term interest-rate series give a useful, though not perfectly comparable, view of bond conditions. Approximate 1936 long rates were: United States 2.68 percent, United Kingdom 2.94 percent, Germany 4.64 percent, France 4.01 percent, Japan 3.97 percent, Italy 5.90 percent, Canada 2.97 percent, Netherlands 3.08 percent, Belgium 4.07 percent, Australia 3.58 percent, and Spain 4.31 percent. These are not identical instruments across countries. Yield-curve data by maturity are not available in a standardized global format here.

The main sovereign-risk stories were not simple default events. They were currency regime shifts, fiscal stress, hidden rearmament finance, and debt overhang. The United Kingdom carried very high public debt but benefited from low rates. France had high debt and devaluation pressure. Italy had war-related fiscal stress. Germany used hidden bills and controls. Spain's civil-war finance broke ordinary sovereign analysis.

#### 6C. Currencies

The US dollar's gold value was anchored by the 35 dollars per ounce official price. Sterling remained managed after its 1931 gold exit and was central to the sterling area. The French franc devalued in September 1936, and the Tripartite Agreement among the United States, United Kingdom, and France attempted to stabilize exchange expectations. The Netherlands and Switzerland followed the gold-bloc shift.

Germany's reichsmark was held under exchange controls, so the official rate cannot be interpreted as a free market price. Japan's yen was managed after earlier departure from gold. China's fabi legal-tender reform was still being consolidated after the 1935 silver-standard break. Many Latin American and colonial currencies operated under exchange controls, sterling links, or administrative allocation.

#### 6D. Alternative assets

Gold was not a free domestic investment asset in the United States in the modern sense, but it was central to international reserves and monetary expansion. Real estate recovered in the United States and United Kingdom where cheap money, housing demand, and public works supported construction. Farmland and commodity-linked assets improved from Depression lows but remained vulnerable to weather, debt, and export prices. Art, private equity, and other modern alternative-asset categories do not have comparable 1936 global data here.

### 7. RISKS, CRISES & BLACK SWANS

Spain's civil war was the most acute realized economic crisis of 1936. It destroyed ordinary national accounting, split financial institutions, and redirected resources toward war. The gold-bloc crisis was the main financial-system near-crisis, resolved partially through devaluation and the Tripartite Agreement. Italy's sanctions and Ethiopian-war costs produced fiscal and trade stress. China's currency reform stabilized one problem but did not remove the geopolitical risk that materialized in 1937.

Germany and Japan were the clearest systemic vulnerabilities building in plain sight. Rearmament generated growth and employment, but it also created import needs, fiscal pressures, labor controls, and strategic behavior that could not be reconciled with peaceful open trade. The Soviet Union's rapid industrialization created output gains and defense capacity, but also administrative distortions, consumer shortages, and political risks from purges.

With hindsight, the United States also carried a policy-mistake risk that materialized in 1937. Fiscal retrenchment, monetary sterilization, and reserve-requirement changes helped produce a recession after the 1936 boom. This was not fully obvious from 1936 equity-market optimism, but the economy's high unemployment and dependence on policy support made it vulnerable.

### 8. YEAR-OVER-YEAR DELTA (1936 vs. 1935)

The main structural break from 1935 to 1936 was monetary. In 1935, the gold bloc still mattered as a policy constraint. By late 1936, France had devalued and the Tripartite Agreement had created a looser stabilization arrangement among the United States, United Kingdom, and France. This reduced the immediate pressure for competitive devaluation but confirmed the end of the old gold-standard regime.

Growth accelerated or stayed strong in much of the MPD top 20. The United States grew nearly 10 percent by the MPD real estimate, Germany nearly 9 percent, Brazil roughly 9.6 percent, Czechoslovakia roughly 8.2 percent, Mexico roughly 7.8 percent, and the USSR roughly 7.9 percent. Spain was the stark exception, with an estimated contraction of almost 24 percent. Italy also contracted, reflecting war, sanctions, fiscal stress, and autarkic pressures.

The top-20 composition changed at the margin. Mexico entered the top 20, while Sweden exited. Spain fell from 11th to 14th. Brazil rose relative to Australia and Czechoslovakia. The top five remained the United States, Former USSR, China, Germany, and United Kingdom, but the meaning of their GDP differed sharply because the United States and United Kingdom were market economies, Germany was a controlled rearmament economy, the USSR was a non-market planned economy, and China was fragmented and only partly industrialized.

The sectoral delta was toward heavy industry, defense, construction, and commodities. Housing and consumer durables remained important in Anglo-American recovery. Rearmament gained weight in Germany, Japan, USSR, United Kingdom, France, Italy, and Czechoslovakia. Commodity rebound improved conditions in Canada, Australia, Brazil, Indonesia, and Mexico. Labor institutions shifted most dramatically in France.

### 9. DATA CONFIDENCE & GAPS

**Highest-confidence categories.** The highest-confidence data in this document are the MPD 2023 GDP per capita and population series for the broad historical ranking, the JST R6 macro-financial series for advanced economies, the US oil-price series from EIA, the US gold-price context from Federal Reserve history, the US equity returns from historical index compilers, and the text and dating of the Tripartite Agreement. Even here, the GDP ranking is a real PPP historical reconstruction, not a 1936 contemporary accounting table.

**Moderate-confidence categories.** Advanced-economy inflation, unemployment, long rates, public debt, budget balances, current accounts, and exchange rates are reasonably usable where JST provides data. However, unemployment definitions vary widely, fiscal data can omit off-budget or hidden liabilities, and exchange-rate series can be hard to compare during devaluation years. Germany's Mefo finance and Spain's civil-war finance are especially difficult to reconcile with ordinary public-finance tables.

**Low-confidence categories.** Soviet GDP, China GDP, colonial GDP for India and Indonesia, civil-war Spain, and developing-country fiscal or unemployment indicators are lower confidence. For the USSR and other non-market systems, output was not measured by market prices and official reporting had political incentives. For China, fragmented territorial control, silver-to-fabi monetary reform, and incomplete statistical administration make national indicators fragile. For colonies, statistics often measure trade and public finance better than household welfare or labor conditions.

**Major gaps.** Data not available for an independent official 1936 world GDP ranking from World Bank WDI or Penn World Table; both standard sources begin later for GDP comparisons. Data not available for a reliable global 1936 corporate ranking by revenue or market capitalization; standardized annual equity returns for all major world exchanges; comparable unemployment across all top 20 economies; R&D spending by country; global current-account aggregation; many developing-country fiscal ratios; and a single fully recomputed 1936 world trade-volume total in this document.

**Weakest claims.** The weakest quantitative claims are the non-market and crisis-economy GDP estimates, especially Former USSR, China, and Spain. The strongest qualitative claims are the monetary-regime break, the rearmament shift, the Spanish Civil War shock, the French Popular Front labor shift, the Japanese fiscal-military policy break after February 1936, and the broad late-Depression recovery pattern.
