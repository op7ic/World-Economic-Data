# MACRO/MICRO GLOBAL ECONOMIC ASSESSMENT - YEAR: 1962

It is not a current-dollar market-exchange ranking. Soviet-bloc and non-market estimates are explicitly uncertain, with a rough plus or minus 20-30 percent uncertainty band for output levels.

### 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

1962 was a strong expansion year inside a still-functioning Bretton Woods order. Maddison world GDP was about 9.14 trillion 1990 international Geary-Khamis dollars, up about 4.7% from 1961. The United States remained the system's largest economy and monetary anchor, but the fastest visible industrial momentum came from Japan, Western Europe, Spain, Canada, and several middle-income economies.

The key macro pattern was growth under fixed exchange rates. The dollar was officially convertible into gold at $35 per ounce, most major currencies were pegged, and capital controls limited destabilizing flows. Yet stress signs were visible: the Federal Reserve opened swap lines, the London Gold Pool defended the gold price, Canada returned to a fixed exchange rate, and U.S. equities suffered the Kennedy Slide.

At the micro level, growth was transmitted through autos, steel, chemicals, housing, electricity, transport, oil, consumer appliances, and early electronics. In market economies, firms, households, and banks converted income growth into durable-goods demand and investment. In the USSR, China, and Poland, plan allocation, official prices, and rationing make standard market indicators weak or non-comparable.

Several 1962 decisions had long forward linkages. The European Common Agricultural Policy began, the U.S. Trade Expansion Act prepared the Kennedy Round, OPEC's early producer-bargaining logic continued, and the Cuban Missile Crisis highlighted the resource burden and tail risk of Cold War rivalry.

### 2. KEY THEMES OF 1962

**1. Bretton Woods stability required active defense.** The fixed-rate system still worked, but the Gold Pool, Federal Reserve swap lines, and Canadian parity change showed that confidence had to be managed. This theme links 1962 directly to the later 1960s dollar-gold strains.

**2. Catch-up growth was still powerful.** The United States was largest, but Japan, Spain, Italy, France, the Netherlands, Canada, and West Germany grew faster. Capital deepening, technology transfer, exports, and urbanization narrowed parts of the productivity gap with the United States.

**3. Trade liberalization and regional integration accelerated.** The EEC's Common Agricultural Policy and the U.S. Trade Expansion Act made trade policy a strategic Cold War and Atlantic-alliance instrument, not just a tariff issue.

**4. Non-market economies were large but hard to measure.** The Soviet Union, China, and Poland ranked high in Maddison, yet official prices, plan targets, and administrative allocation make GDP, unemployment, exchange-rate, and productivity comparisons uncertain.

**5. Oil politics were becoming institutionalized.** Crude prices were low by later standards, but OPEC's creation and early producer demands over posted prices made petroleum a strategic bargaining issue. Venezuela's top-20 position reflected oil's scale.

**6. Household modernization was a growth mechanism.** Automobiles, appliances, housing, roads, televisions, and electricity converted macro growth into daily living-standard changes in rich and high-growth economies, while developing and socialist economies lagged in different ways.

**7. Financial markets were controlled, but not irrelevant.** Capital controls limited global contagion, yet the Kennedy Slide, Canadian exchange pressure, bond-yield movements, and gold-market defense showed that expectations mattered.

### 3. MACROECONOMIC OVERVIEW

#### 3A. Global Aggregates

Maddison world GDP was about 9.14 trillion 1990 international Geary-Khamis dollars, with estimated real growth of 4.7%. This is a historical purchasing-power-style estimate in 1990 international Geary-Khamis dollars, not a nominal-dollar aggregate. IMF reporting indicates world trade expanded in value from roughly $95 billion in 1959 to about $124 billion by 1962, with about 5 percent growth from 1961 to 1962. Reserve-currency dynamics were dollar-centered, with sterling still important but secondary. Inflation was moderate in many advanced economies and higher or less reliably measured in several Latin American and developing economies. Oil was cheap and stable, gold was fixed officially at $35 per ounce, and capital flows were dominated by official finance, trade credit, aid, controlled bank lending, and direct investment.

#### 3B. Monetary & Financial Conditions

The major economies operated under Bretton Woods fixed exchange rates. The United States held the discount rate around 3 percent while balancing domestic expansion against dollar-gold credibility. The United Kingdom defended sterling at $2.80. Japan maintained ¥360 per dollar. West Germany, France, Italy, the Netherlands, Switzerland, India, Mexico, and Spain had fixed or tightly managed official parities. Canada returned to a fixed par value of US$0.925 per Canadian dollar in May 1962. U.S. equities corrected sharply, with the Dow down about 10.8 percent for the year, while U.S. 10-year Treasury yields were around 4 percent late in the year.

#### 3C. Trade & Geopolitics

The EEC deepened through the launch of the Common Agricultural Policy. The United States passed the Trade Expansion Act, giving the president negotiating authority for broader tariff reductions and preparing the later Kennedy Round. The Cuban Missile Crisis did not create a sustained economic crash, but it showed how Cold War risk could dominate expectations. The Sino-Indian War shifted India's fiscal and infrastructure priorities. Decolonization continued to affect trade, aid, and investment flows.

#### 3D. Commodities & Energy

Oil supply was ample and prices were low, but producer politics were changing. OPEC's founding members were challenging posted-price reductions and seeking stronger control over revenues. Metals demand followed autos, construction, electrification, machinery, and defense-linked industry. Agriculture was uneven: advanced economies improved productivity, while China and India faced food-security constraints. In Europe, the CAP made agriculture a central regional policy issue.

#### 3E. Labor & Demographics

The Maddison population series puts world population near 3.14 billion. Labor markets were shaped by urbanization, rural-to-industrial migration, and postwar demographic trends. West Germany, France, Italy, Switzerland, and the Netherlands faced labor shortages and migration pressures. Japan absorbed rural workers into factories and construction. India, China, Indonesia, Mexico, and Brazil still had very large agricultural labor shares.

#### 5B. Corporate landscape

A verified global ranking of 1962 companies by revenue or market capitalization is not available. Data not available for a reliable top-20 corporate list. The most important firms were in oil, autos, steel, chemicals, electrical machinery, finance, retail, and telecommunications. Examples include General Motors, Ford, IBM, General Electric, AT&T, Royal Dutch Shell, Unilever, Volkswagen, Siemens, BASF, Bayer, Fiat, Renault, Toyota, Nissan, Sony, Matsushita, Toshiba, Hitachi, and state-linked enterprises in France, Italy, India, China, and the Soviet bloc. The main corporate story was scale, export capability, technology absorption, and state involvement rather than a wave of globally comparable IPOs or M&A.

#### 5C. Innovation & technology

The main technologies were mainframe computing, semiconductors, aerospace, telecommunications, nuclear power, chemicals, pharmaceuticals, and industrial automation. U.S. R&D was tied to corporations, universities, defense, and space. Japan's advantage was rapid commercialization and manufacturing learning. Europe specialized in autos, chemicals, rail, aircraft, power systems, and industrial engineering. Globally comparable 1962 patent and R&D tables are not available here.

#### 5D. Labor markets & human capital

Labor shifted from agriculture toward industry and services. The United States was already service-heavy; Europe and Japan were industrializing and urbanizing; India, China, Indonesia, Mexico, and Brazil retained large rural labor forces. Wage growth was strongest where productivity catch-up and industrial labor demand were strongest. Unemployment comparisons are weak because definitions differ and socialist economies reported employment administratively.

### 6. FINANCIAL MARKETS

#### 6A. Equity markets

The best-documented event was the U.S. Kennedy Slide. The Dow Jones Industrial Average ended 1962 near 652.10, down about 10.8 percent. The S&P 500 suffered a sharp midyear decline before recovering part of the loss. Data not available for a verified global table of every major stock exchange's annual return. Outside the United States and United Kingdom, bank finance was generally more important than public equity finance.

#### 6B. Fixed income

U.S. 10-year Treasury yields were around 4 percent in the second half of 1962. U.K. 10-year yields were higher, around the 5-6 percent range during parts of the year. Comparable 10-year sovereign-yield data for all top-20 economies are not available in a harmonized 1962 table. Sovereign debt stress existed in some developing economies, but official finance, IMF support, trade credit, and bank lending mattered more than liquid emerging-market bond trading.

#### 6C. Currencies

Most major currencies were fixed or tightly managed. Sterling was $2.80, the yen ¥360 per dollar, the DM about 4 per dollar, the French new franc about 4.94 per dollar, the lira about 625 per dollar, the rupee about 4.76 per dollar, the Mexican peso about 12.50 per dollar, and the Spanish peseta about 60 per dollar. Non-market and multiple-rate systems are not comparable: Soviet ruble, Chinese yuan, Polish zloty, Brazilian cruzeiro, Argentine exchange arrangements, and Indonesian rupiah figures require caution.

#### 6D. Alternative assets

Gold's official monetary price was $35 per ounce. The London Gold Pool defended that price in the private market. Real estate was important but not globally comparable; housing demand was strong in the United States, Canada, Australia, Japan, Spain, Italy, France, Germany, and the Netherlands. Commodity exposure mattered through producers such as Venezuela, the USSR, Canada, Australia, Argentina, and Brazil.

### 7. RISKS, CRISES & BLACK SWANS

The Cuban Missile Crisis was the year's largest tail-risk event. It did not create a sustained economic collapse, but it highlighted the economic weight of Cold War defense, science, communications, and industrial capacity. Canada's exchange-rate crisis was a concrete Bretton Woods stress event. The Kennedy Slide was a market-risk event rather than a recession signal. China was still recovering from the Great Leap Forward crisis; India absorbed the Sino-Indian War; Argentina's political crisis damaged confidence; Brazil and Indonesia showed rising macro instability.

With hindsight, the deepest vulnerability was Bretton Woods itself. Swap lines and the Gold Pool bought time but did not remove the contradiction between global dollar liquidity and gold convertibility. Another hindsight risk was oil: OPEC looked institutionally limited in 1962, but producer coordination was already forming.

### 8. YEAR-OVER-YEAR DELTA (1962 vs. 1961)

World GDP growth accelerated to about 4.7% in the Maddison series. The United States moved from recovery toward stronger expansion, though not full employment. Western Europe remained strong but moderated in some areas. Japan's high-growth model gained further scale. Spain surged after stabilization, while Argentina weakened and Poland contracted in the Maddison reconstruction.

Ranking changes were modest but meaningful. Venezuela entered the top 20 and Czechoslovakia exited. Japan moved ahead of France; France moved ahead of China; Italy moved ahead of India; the Netherlands moved ahead of Poland; Switzerland moved ahead of Czechoslovakia. Policy deltas were larger: the CAP began, the U.S. Trade Expansion Act passed, Canada fixed its currency, and central-bank swap lines became part of dollar defense.

New risks included the Cuban Missile Crisis, equity-market fragility, worsening Brazilian and Argentine instability, and continuing Chinese recovery uncertainty. Resolved or eased risks included the immediate U.S. recession risk from 1960-61 and some postwar European payments constraints.

### 9. DATA CONFIDENCE & GAPS

**High confidence.** The United States, Canada, the United Kingdom, Western Europe, Japan, Australia, and Switzerland have relatively reliable national accounts, central-bank records, exchange-rate parities, and market data.

**Medium confidence.** India, Mexico, Brazil, Argentina, Venezuela, and Spain have usable GDP series but weaker comparability for inflation, labor markets, fiscal balances, and informal activity.

**Low confidence.** The Soviet Union, China, Poland, and Indonesia require special caution. Plan prices, rationing, exchange controls, administrative allocation, informal activity, and crisis-period disruptions weaken direct comparison.

**Conflicts.** China is the clearest example: Maddison real output and WDI-style series tell different 1962 stories. Germany also has boundary complications because Maddison historical Germany, West Germany, and East Germany are not the same statistical object.

**Missing data.** A complete comparable 1962 table for CPI, unemployment, policy rates, public debt/GDP, current accounts, fiscal balances, stock returns, corporate market capitalization, IPOs, bankruptcies, household debt, and property prices does not exist in the source set used here. Where figures are not verified, the report says Data not available.
