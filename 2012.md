# MACRO/MICRO GLOBAL ECONOMIC ASSESSMENT - YEAR: 2012

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

2012 was defined by a fragile post-crisis world economy, a peak phase of euro-area breakup risk, and a marked slowdown in global trade. The World Bank's revised WDI data put world GDP at $75.96 trillion in current dollars and real world GDP growth at 2.73%. IMF April 2013 WEO data, compiled nearer to the time, put world current GDP at $71.71 trillion, market-exchange-rate growth at 2.48%, and PPP-weighted growth at 3.15%. The difference between World Bank and IMF totals is not an error in this report: it reflects revisions, source differences, and timing.

The central macro force was policy stabilization under strain. The Federal Reserve moved to open-ended mortgage-backed security purchases, the European Central Bank cut rates and signaled powerful backstop capacity after the summer crisis, the Bank of England expanded asset purchases, and the Bank of Japan moved toward clearer anti-deflation commitments. Monetary policy was therefore the shock absorber for weak private demand, fiscal consolidation, and banking-sector repair.

The most important real-economy surprise was the collapse in trade momentum. WTO data report merchandise trade growth of only 2.0% in 2012, down from 5.2% in 2011, while IMF WEO goods-and-services trade volume grew 2.47%. This weak trade performance mattered because it hit export-led economies, commodity producers, logistics hubs, and global manufacturers simultaneously. It also foreshadowed the lower trade elasticity that became a defining feature of the 2010s.

Within the top 20 economies, the United States and China remained the two dominant poles, Japan retained third place, and the euro-area core stayed large but weak. Brazil's slowdown, India's inflation and external-pressure mix, Spain's banking crisis, Italy's recession, and Turkey's soft landing showed that emerging and advanced economies were no longer moving in a simple post-2009 rebound. At the micro level, 2012 also marked a transition toward platform technology, mobile computing, shale energy, electric vehicles, and biotechnology, while legacy sectors such as traditional photography and parts of European banking were still being restructured.

## 2. KEY THEMES OF 2012

### 2.1 Euro-area crisis management became global macro policy

The sovereign-debt crisis moved from a regional fiscal issue to a global risk factor. Spain's recession, banking-sector rescue needs, and unemployment above 24% created systemic concern. Italy's debt ratio and recession kept bond markets focused on debt sustainability. The ECB's rate cuts, liquidity operations, and eventual commitment to defend monetary transmission changed the distribution of tail risk. The main countries affected were Spain, Italy, Greece, Portugal, Ireland, Germany, France, the United Kingdom through finance, and Switzerland through safe-haven flows. The forward linkage was clear: the euro area avoided a disorderly breakup in 2012, but at the cost of years of weak domestic demand and political stress.

### 2.2 Monetary policy reached deeper into unconventional territory

The United States, euro area, United Kingdom, Japan, and Switzerland all operated at or near the effective lower bound. The Fed's September 2012 decision to buy agency mortgage-backed securities at $40 billion per month and its December threshold guidance were turning points in communication. The ECB cut the deposit facility rate to zero in July. The SNB defended its minimum exchange rate against the euro. The forward link was the normalization problem: large central-bank balance sheets and forward guidance became routine tools rather than emergency exceptions.

### 2.3 Global trade decelerated sharply

The WTO's 2.0% merchandise trade-growth figure for 2012, versus 5.2% in 2011, shows that the post-2009 trade rebound had faded. Europe was the center of weakness, but the impact spread through China, Germany, South Korea, Mexico, the Netherlands, and commodity exporters. The slowdown linked macro demand to micro supply chains: weaker European consumption hurt Asian manufacturing orders, port volumes, logistics, and producer economies. The forward linkage was the lower-trade-growth regime of the 2010s.

### 2.4 Emerging markets slowed, but did not fail uniformly

China slowed from 9.46% to 7.86% growth, Brazil slowed to 1.92%, India faced high inflation and a current account deficit, and Turkey slowed from 10.98% to 4.81%. Indonesia and Saudi Arabia still grew strongly, while Russia benefited from oil. The common pattern was that easy post-crisis growth had ended. Domestic credit, infrastructure, commodity exposure, and external financing mattered more than the broad label "emerging markets."

### 2.5 Commodity prices stayed historically high but became more uneven

The IMF average petroleum spot price was about $105.01 per barrel, EIA reported Brent at roughly $111.67 per barrel and WTI at roughly $94.05 per barrel, and gold averaged about $1,669 per troy ounce. Copper, using IMF data distributed through FRED, averaged about $7,958.92 per metric ton, down from the 2011 average but still high by pre-2000 standards. FAO reported the annual food price index down 7% from 2011, although grain and weather shocks still mattered. The result was mixed: oil exporters and some miners gained, but the easy commodity-supercycle narrative became less reliable.

### 2.6 Digital platforms and mobile computing became macro-relevant

The Facebook IPO was not only a corporate finance event; it signaled that social networks, mobile advertising, and platform data had become large-scale economic assets. Smartphone ecosystems reshaped semiconductors, telecom investment, retail behavior, and media. Tesla's Model S deliveries in June 2012 were small in volume but economically significant as a signal about venture-backed industrial technology. CRISPR research published in 2012 had little immediate GDP effect, but hindsight shows it was a major innovation inflection.

## 3. MACROECONOMIC OVERVIEW

### 3A. Global Aggregates

World Bank WDI gives 2012 world GDP at $75.96 trillion, real world growth at 2.73%, population at 7.177 billion, and modeled global unemployment at 6.15%. IMF WEO April 2013 gives $71.71 trillion current GDP, 2.48% market-exchange-rate real growth, 3.15% PPP-weighted real growth, 3.92% world CPI inflation, and 2.47% world trade-volume growth. These sources agree on the qualitative picture: 2012 was slower than the early rebound years, but not a global recession.

Global trade was the weak point. WTO data show merchandise trade growth at 2.0%, with European import weakness the main drag. IMF WEO goods-and-services import volume grew 2.39%, while export volume grew 2.55%. Reserve-currency dynamics still centered on the U.S. dollar and euro, but the euro-area crisis increased demand for safe assets in U.S. dollars, Swiss francs, and core-euro sovereign bonds. The IMF COFER framework reports the official composition of reserves, but country-level reserve-currency holdings are not uniformly disclosed.

Commodity conditions were bifurcated. Oil remained historically expensive: IMF's average petroleum spot price was $105.01 per barrel. EIA's Brent and WTI averages show the Brent-WTI spread remained economically important. Gold averaged $1,669 per troy ounce, reflecting low real rates and safe-haven demand. Copper averaged $7,958.92 per metric ton, below 2011 but still elevated. FAO reported food prices down 7% over 2012 compared with 2011, but weather and grain volatility continued to affect low-income households and food importers.

### 3B. Monetary & Financial Conditions

The interest-rate environment was exceptionally loose. The Federal Reserve held the federal funds target at 0.00-0.25% and launched additional agency MBS purchases. The ECB cut its main refinancing rate to 0.75% and the deposit facility to 0.00% in July. The Bank of England held Bank Rate at 0.50% and expanded its Asset Purchase Facility to £375 billion. The Bank of Japan held a near-zero policy rate and expanded asset purchases. Switzerland maintained a 0.00-0.25% Libor target range and defended the CHF 1.20 per euro floor.

Credit conditions diverged. U.S. mortgage and corporate credit improved, while euro-area peripheral banks and sovereigns remained under pressure until ECB actions reduced tail risk. Emerging-market credit still grew, but Brazil, Turkey, India, and Indonesia showed the first signs that capital-flow dependence could become a constraint. Equity markets recovered strongly: the WFE reported global exchange market capitalization up 15% to about $54 trillion, and U.S. equity indices generated double-digit or high single-digit returns. Government bond yields in safe-haven markets remained extremely low, while Spanish and Italian spreads were the key stress indicators.

### 3C. Trade & Geopolitics

The euro crisis dominated trade channels, but geopolitics also mattered. Sanctions and oil-market risks around Iran supported oil-price risk premiums. Russia became a WTO member on 22 August 2012, formally integrating a major commodity exporter more deeply into the global trade system. Regional integration continued through the EU and ASEAN frameworks, but the main story was not new trade liberalization. It was weak demand, deleveraging, and policy uncertainty hitting trade-intensive economies.

### 3D. Commodities & Energy

Energy was both a macro stabilizer for producers and a tax on consumers. Saudi Arabia, Russia, Canada, Australia, and parts of the United States benefited from energy or resource investment. The U.S. shale boom increased crude production and changed medium-term expectations for global energy balances. Australia remained in a mining-investment boom, especially around iron ore and LNG. Commodity importers, including India and Turkey, faced external-balance pressure.

### 3E. Labor & Demographics

Labor markets were uneven and not directly comparable across countries. WDI modeled ILO estimates show U.S. unemployment at 8.07%, Spain at 24.79%, Italy at 10.66%, France at 9.84%, and Germany at 5.37%. China and India figures are not directly comparable with OECD unemployment definitions. The global population was about 7.177 billion. Urbanization and working-age population growth continued to support emerging-market consumption, but youth unemployment and labor-market dualism became key political economy risks in southern Europe and parts of the Middle East.

The United States moved from sluggish post-crisis recovery to a visibly firmer, but still incomplete, expansion. Real GDP growth improved to 2.29%, CPI inflation eased to 2.07%, and unemployment fell to 8.07%, while the general government deficit narrowed but remained large. Monetary policy was the dominant macro stabilizer: the Federal Reserve held the policy-rate floor and launched open-ended mortgage-backed securities purchases in September, while December guidance tied low rates to labor-market thresholds.

Micro dynamics were led by housing repair, autos, shale energy, and technology platforms. Household deleveraging continued, banks rebuilt capital, and consumer durables benefited from easier credit. Facebook's May IPO and Tesla's first Model S deliveries signaled the scale of platform and clean-technology finance, while Kodak's bankruptcy illustrated the cost of failed digital transition.

**Micro/sectoral highlights:**
- Growth drivers: housing recovery, autos, shale energy, cloud and mobile technology.
- Stress points: fiscal-cliff uncertainty, long-term unemployment, household deleveraging.
- Corporate events: Facebook IPO; Kodak Chapter 11; Tesla Model S deliveries began.

### 5D. Labor markets & human capital

The world economy had a labor-market split. Sectorally, manufacturing employment depended on export demand and automation, services absorbed urban labor, and construction cycles were decisive in Spain, China, Saudi Arabia, Australia, and Turkey.

## 6. FINANCIAL MARKETS

### 6A. Equity markets

Global equity markets recovered strongly as extreme euro breakup risk faded and central banks expanded support. The World Federation of Exchanges reported global market capitalization at WFE member exchanges up 15% to about $54 trillion. U.S. market data show the S&P 500 rising roughly 13.4%, the Nasdaq Composite rising roughly 15.9%, and the Dow Jones Industrial Average rising roughly 7.3%. Germany's equity market outperformed many peers as investors separated export champions from euro-area sovereign stress. Japan's equity market strengthened late in the year as expectations for more aggressive policy rose after the political transition.

Data are not consistently available in this report for every major stock exchange's year-end index level and total return. For exchanges where harmonized annual-return data were not extracted from official sources, the value is recorded as "Data not available" rather than guessed.

### 6B. Fixed income

Fixed-income markets were the center of systemic risk. U.S., German, U.K., Japanese, Canadian, Swiss, and Australian sovereign yields were low by historical standards because of weak growth, low policy rates, and safe-haven demand. Spain and Italy faced much higher spreads during the year, reflecting redenomination and debt-sustainability risk. The ECB's crisis response reduced the probability of disorderly outcomes, but the underlying debt, banking, and growth problems remained.

### 6C. Currencies

Currency markets reflected crisis and policy asymmetry. The euro was under pressure during the crisis phase but stabilized after stronger ECB commitments. The yen remained strong for much of the year but weakened late in 2012 as expectations of reflationary policy rose. The Swiss franc was constrained by the SNB's minimum exchange-rate policy against the euro. Commodity and emerging-market currencies were more mixed: the Brazilian real, Indian rupee, Turkish lira, and Indonesian rupiah all reflected some combination of external deficits, capital flows, and policy management.

### 6D. Alternative assets

Gold averaged about $1,669 per troy ounce, supported by low real rates, safe-haven flows, and central-bank balance-sheet expansion. Real estate was sharply bifurcated: U.S. housing was healing, Spanish property remained in crisis, London and parts of Canada and Australia were strong, and Chinese property was policy-managed. Commodities remained important as an asset class, but the 2012 signal was no longer uniformly bullish.

## 7. RISKS, CRISES & BLACK SWANS

The active crisis of 2012 was the euro-area sovereign and banking crisis. Spain's bank rescue, Italy's debt dynamics, Greece's restructuring aftermath, and doubts about monetary-union reversibility were the main systemic risks. The near-miss was a self-reinforcing sovereign-bank run in the euro periphery. ECB backstops changed expectations, but did not solve the growth problem.

The United States faced the fiscal cliff at year end, which was a policy risk rather than a private-sector balance-sheet shock. China faced hard-landing fears tied to property, local-government finance, and exports. India and Turkey faced external-financing and inflation constraints. Brazil faced a productivity and investment disappointment that was not yet fully understood. Commodity exporters faced the risk that high prices would mask weak diversification.

With hindsight, 2012 also showed vulnerabilities that became more visible later: euro-area political fragmentation, China's credit build-up, Brazil's stalled investment model, Turkey's dependence on external financing, and global trade's lower growth elasticity. These are hindsight judgments. They were visible as risks in 2012 but not yet certain outcomes.

## 8. YEAR-OVER-YEAR DELTA (2012 vs. 2011)

The main delta from 2011 was the loss of momentum. World Bank real world growth slowed, IMF market-exchange-rate growth was only 2.48%, and WTO merchandise trade growth fell to 2.0% from 5.2%. The top 20 country set did not change, but the relative ordering shifted, especially between the United Kingdom and France, Russia and Italy, Australia and Spain, South Korea and Spain, and Saudi Arabia and Switzerland.

Policy changed from temporary emergency support toward durable unconventional regimes. The Fed moved to open-ended asset purchases, the ECB signaled stronger backstop capacity, the BoE expanded purchases, and Japan moved closer to explicit reflation. Compared with 2011, fiscal policy was more restrictive in much of Europe, monetary policy more activist, and banking policy more central to macro outcomes.

Commodity dynamics also changed. Oil stayed high, but metals and food were less uniformly strong than the simple supercycle story implied. Emerging-market growth was less synchronized: Indonesia and Saudi Arabia were strong, China was slower but still fast, India was inflation-constrained, Brazil disappointed, and Turkey cooled deliberately. The micro shift was toward platform technology, mobile ecosystems, shale energy, and advanced biotech, even while old crisis sectors such as European banks and legacy photography were still restructuring.

## 9. DATA CONFIDENCE & GAPS

**High-confidence data:** GDP, GDP growth, population, GDP per capita, CPI inflation, unemployment, current account, debt, budget balances, and official exchange rates are based on World Bank WDI and IMF WEO series. OECD-country data are generally reliable, although revisions remain possible. Exchange-rate values are period averages and should not be confused with year-end rates.

**Moderate-confidence data:** Fiscal balances, government debt, and current-account values for countries with complex public sectors or major state-owned enterprises can differ across IMF vintages, national sources, and later revisions. China, Saudi Arabia, India, Indonesia, Russia, Turkey, and Brazil require extra caution on state-linked credit, public-sector boundary definitions, and off-balance-sheet obligations.

**Known source conflicts:** World Bank WDI revised GDP data and IMF WEO April 2013 data differ in the precise nominal GDP rankings. The major conflicts are United Kingdom versus France, Canada versus India, and South Korea versus Spain. This report uses World Bank WDI for the main ranking and provides the IMF ranking as a cross-check.

**Weakest evidence in this document:** detailed corporate and sectoral claims are less harmonized internationally than macro indicators. Corporate events are verifiable, but their macro importance is interpretive. Stock-exchange annual-return data are not uniformly sourced for all major exchanges in this report. Policy rates are comparable only with caution because policy frameworks differ: a single policy rate does not capture ECB liquidity operations, the Fed balance sheet, Turkey's corridor, or China's quantity controls.

**Data not available:** fully harmonized 2012 data for every major stock exchange's index level and total return were not extracted. Comparable unemployment definitions across all top-20 countries do not exist. Reserve-currency composition by holder country is not public. For some micro indicators, such as household debt, real-estate prices, and sectoral employment shares, harmonized 2012 global data are incomplete and not reported where not verified.
