# MACRO/MICRO GLOBAL ECONOMIC ASSESSMENT - YEAR: 2009

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

2009 was the year in which the global financial crisis became a synchronized real-economy recession, and then began to bottom out under extraordinary public intervention. World GDP in current U.S. dollars fell from about $64.3 trillion in 2008 to about $61.0 trillion in 2009 on World Bank data. IMF real-output estimates show the first postwar contraction of global activity, while the WTO recorded an unprecedented collapse in merchandise trade value and a double-digit fall in trade volume. The most important economic fact of the year was not only the recession itself, but the degree to which private balance-sheet distress, banking losses, inventory liquidation, and cross-border trade finance stress transmitted the shock across nearly all large economies.

The recession was not symmetrical. The United States, Japan, Germany, the United Kingdom, Italy, Spain, Canada, Mexico, Russia, Turkey, the Netherlands, Switzerland, and Belgium contracted sharply. China, India, Indonesia, and Australia kept growing, helped by domestic demand, bank credit, fiscal stimulus, commodity links, or a smaller direct exposure to structured-credit losses. Brazil and South Korea suffered a severe trade and industrial shock but rebounded quickly as China-led commodity demand, easier monetary policy, and inventory rebuilding returned. Mexico’s contraction was among the deepest in the top 20 because it combined U.S. demand dependence, manufacturing exposure, oil-revenue weakness, and the H1N1 shock.

Policy regime change defined the year. Major central banks moved to or near the zero lower bound, and the Federal Reserve and Bank of England made large-scale asset purchases a central policy instrument. The European Central Bank cut the main refinancing rate to 1.00 percent and relied on fixed-rate full-allotment liquidity operations and covered-bond purchases. Fiscal policy turned aggressively countercyclical in the United States, China, Japan, Europe, and many emerging markets. This stabilized output and financial markets, but it also shifted risk from private balance sheets to public balance sheets, setting the stage for the euro-area sovereign stress that intensified in 2010.

Microeconomically, 2009 was a year of demand destruction followed by selective reflation. Autos, construction, capital goods, luxury goods, shipping, and export manufacturing endured deep contractions. Banking moved from panic toward repair, but credit conditions remained tight for households and small firms. Technology and telecommunications continued to gain share because digital demand was more resilient and because the smartphone, broadband, cloud-computing, and semiconductor cycles were becoming structural rather than purely cyclical. Commodity markets rebounded from late-2008 lows, especially oil, copper, and gold, but the rebound reflected policy liquidity and Chinese restocking as much as final demand.

## 2. KEY THEMES OF 2009

**1. The first synchronized postwar global recession.** The economic-history significance of 2009 is that it converted a financial-market crisis into a global output and trade contraction. Advanced economies had the deepest output losses, but the trade shock transmitted distress to exporters everywhere. Germany and Japan contracted because their industrial and export models were directly exposed to collapsed external demand. Mexico, Korea, Russia, and Turkey were hit by the combined fall in trade, capital flows, and commodity prices. The forward linkage was the 2010-2011 policy debate about recovery, exit strategies, and public-debt sustainability.

**2. Emergency macro policy became a durable regime shift.** The Fed’s 0 to 0.25 percent target range, the Bank of England’s 0.5 percent Bank Rate and gilt purchases, the ECB’s 1.0 percent main refinancing rate and liquidity allotments, and the Bank of Japan’s 0.1 percent policy rate made unconventional policy normal for the next decade. At the time, these measures were framed as emergency stabilization. In hindsight, they began the long period of ultra-low rates, expanded central bank balance sheets, and asset-price sensitivity to monetary guidance.

**3. China’s stimulus changed the global recovery map.** China’s credit and infrastructure stimulus limited the fall in Chinese growth and revived global demand for steel, copper, coal, oil, and construction inputs. This disproportionately helped Australia, Brazil, Indonesia, Korea, and commodity-linked industrial suppliers. The forward linkage was powerful: by 2010, emerging Asia and commodity exporters were recovering faster than many advanced economies, but China also accumulated local-government debt and property-sector imbalances that became more visible later.

**4. Trade and inventories amplified the cycle.** World merchandise trade value fell about 23 percent in 2009, and trade volume contracted sharply. The shock was larger than the decline in GDP because internationally traded goods are concentrated in durable goods, intermediate inputs, autos, electronics, and capital equipment. Inventory liquidation in late 2008 and early 2009 caused a self-reinforcing production halt. The forward linkage was the rapid 2010 rebound once inventories normalized, but also a lasting reassessment of global supply-chain fragility.

**5. Public balance sheets replaced private balance sheets as the visible risk.** Bank rescues, automatic stabilizers, discretionary stimulus, and recession-driven revenue losses pushed deficits and debt higher. The United States and United Kingdom had double-digit or near-double-digit general government deficits. Japan’s already high debt rose further. Euro-area public debt concerns became concrete late in 2009 when Greece’s fiscal data problems triggered a loss of confidence. The forward linkage was the euro-area sovereign crisis and a global debate over austerity versus continued stimulus.

**6. Labor markets lagged the financial recovery.** Equity markets rebounded strongly from March 2009 lows, but unemployment kept rising in the United States, Spain, the United Kingdom, Canada, Turkey, Russia, and several euro-area economies. The ILO estimated global unemployment reached a record level. Youth unemployment and informal-sector stress were especially severe. The forward linkage was a slow household-deleveraging recovery in advanced economies and political pressure for job-focused stimulus.

**7. Emerging-market differentiation became clearer.** Countries with stronger bank balance sheets, less household leverage, policy room, and direct links to China recovered earlier. China, India, Indonesia, Korea, Brazil, and Australia performed better than many advanced economies. Russia and Mexico showed how commodity dependence or U.S. demand dependence could still create deep shocks. The forward linkage was the post-2009 rise of emerging-market capital inflows and the later concern about overheating and exchange-rate appreciation.

## 3. MACROECONOMIC OVERVIEW

### 3A. Global Aggregates

World GDP in current U.S. dollars was about **$60.98 trillion** in 2009, down from about **$64.31 trillion** in 2008 on World Bank WDI data. Real global output contracted on IMF measures, with advanced economies falling much more sharply than emerging and developing economies. The contraction was concentrated in late 2008 and early 2009, followed by stabilization in the second half of 2009 as stimulus, inventory restocking, and financial-market normalization took hold.

World merchandise trade experienced one of the sharpest declines in modern records. WTO data show the dollar value of world merchandise trade fell by about **23 percent** in 2009 to roughly **$12.1 trillion**, down from about **$16.1 trillion** in 2008. Trade volume fell less than trade value because commodity prices and manufactured-goods prices also declined, but the volume shock was still severe. Trade finance shortages, the durable-goods composition of trade, and inventory liquidation all amplified the collapse.

Reserve currency dynamics remained dollar-centered. The U.S. dollar was the dominant reserve currency, and the crisis initially strengthened demand for dollar liquidity. The Federal Reserve’s swap lines with foreign central banks and the IMF’s expanded lending capacity helped relieve dollar funding stress. The euro remained the second major reserve currency, but late-2009 concerns over Greece began to challenge the perception that euro-area sovereign risk was uniform.

Global inflation fell sharply as energy prices, capacity utilization, and wage pressure weakened. Several advanced economies recorded very low inflation or temporary deflation. Emerging-market inflation slowed but remained higher in Russia, India, Turkey, Brazil, Mexico, and Indonesia. Oil rebounded from crisis lows but averaged far below 2008. Brent monthly prices began 2009 near the low $40s per barrel and ended the year in the mid-$70s. Gold averaged about **$972 per troy ounce**, supported by safe-haven demand and concerns about monetary expansion. LME copper averaged about **$5,164 per metric ton**, recovering strongly from late-2008 lows as China restocked metals.

International capital flows moved from panic outflows to renewed emerging-market inflows during the year. UNCTAD estimated global FDI inflows fell by about **37 percent** in 2009 to roughly **$1.1 trillion**, reflecting weak corporate profits, scarce credit, and cancelled cross-border projects. Portfolio flows recovered earlier than FDI, especially into emerging-market equities and bonds after global risk appetite returned.

### 3B. Monetary & Financial Conditions

The interest-rate environment was extraordinarily loose. The Federal Reserve maintained a 0 to 0.25 percent federal funds target range and expanded asset purchases, including agency mortgage-backed securities, agency debt, and longer-term Treasuries. The Bank of England cut Bank Rate to 0.5 percent and launched a £75 billion asset purchase program in March, later expanded. The ECB cut the main refinancing rate to 1.00 percent by May and supplied liquidity through fixed-rate full-allotment operations. The Bank of Japan kept the policy rate around 0.1 percent and used credit-easing measures.

Exchange rates reflected both safe-haven flows and relative policy expectations. The yen was strong during the crisis, hurting Japanese exporters. The dollar was strong during acute funding stress but weakened against many risk currencies as risk appetite recovered. China kept the renminbi effectively stable against the dollar, which helped stabilize Chinese exporters but also intensified debate over external rebalancing. Many emerging-market currencies recovered in the second half of the year after deep 2008 depreciations.

Credit conditions improved but were not normal. Interbank spreads narrowed from crisis peaks, bank equity issuance resumed, and bond markets reopened for high-grade borrowers. Small firms, commercial real estate borrowers, households with impaired collateral, and leveraged companies still faced tight credit. Bank balance sheets were supported by public capital, guarantees, liquidity facilities, and accounting changes. The policy success of 2009 was to prevent a complete collapse of the banking system; the policy limitation was that credit repair took years.

Equity markets bottomed in March 2009 and then rallied strongly. The S&P 500, Euro Stoxx, FTSE 100, DAX, Nikkei, Hang Seng, Shanghai Composite, Bovespa, Sensex, and RTS all rose from crisis lows. The rally reflected lower tail risk, liquidity, China’s stimulus, inventory restocking, and depressed valuations. Bond markets showed a steepening yield curve in the United States and other economies as short rates were pinned near zero while longer yields rose with recovery expectations and debt supply.

### 3C. Trade & Geopolitics

2009 was not a year of a major global trade agreement; it was a year of crisis-management and protectionism avoidance. G20 leaders repeatedly pledged not to repeat the protectionist spiral of the 1930s. Some tariffs, local-content rules, bank-rescue conditions, and industrial subsidies nevertheless appeared. The U.S. safeguard tariff on Chinese tires and China’s responses signaled that trade tensions would continue even during recession.

The G20 replaced the G7 as the central forum for crisis macro coordination. London and Pittsburgh summits expanded IMF resources, endorsed fiscal stimulus while economies remained weak, and supported financial regulatory reform. The global crisis also strengthened the political case for Basel III, although the final bank-capital framework came later.

Geopolitically, economic flows were affected less by war than by credit contraction and policy coordination. Sanctions regimes existed, but the dominant cross-border economic constraint was private financial deleveraging. Regional integration continued: the EU faced internal stress but remained intact; ASEAN and Asian supply-chain integration benefited from China’s rebound; Mercosur and Latin American trade were affected by commodity swings and regional demand.

### 3D. Commodities & Energy

Oil was volatile but upward-trending after early-2009 lows. Brent averaged much lower than in 2008, when oil had reached record highs, but prices rose through 2009 as OPEC supply restraint, China demand, and global risk appetite improved. Energy investment slowed because the late-2008 price collapse weakened cash flow and project economics. Natural gas prices were pressured by recession and rising unconventional gas supply, especially in North America.

Industrial metals rebounded faster than final demand. Copper’s recovery was especially linked to Chinese infrastructure, stockpiling, and expectations of a global manufacturing rebound. Iron ore, steel, and coal markets were weaker than during the 2007-2008 boom but stabilized as Asia recovered. Agricultural staples were less dramatic than energy and metals: prices generally eased from 2008 food-crisis highs, but food affordability remained a major stress in lower-income economies because incomes and employment were hit.

### 3E. Labor & Demographics

Labor markets deteriorated after output began stabilizing. The United States unemployment rate averaged above 9 percent and peaked at 10 percent in October. Spain’s unemployment rate rose toward 18 percent, reflecting the collapse of construction and temporary employment. Turkey, Russia, Mexico, Canada, the United Kingdom, and many euro-area economies saw rising unemployment. Germany was a partial exception because short-time work schemes limited layoffs.

Migration flows were affected by recession. Construction-linked migrant workers in the Gulf, Spain, Ireland, the United Kingdom, and the United States faced weaker demand. Remittances slowed, affecting Mexico, South Asia, Eastern Europe, and parts of Southeast Asia. Demographic trends did not change suddenly, but 2009 reinforced structural divergence: aging in Japan and Europe increased fiscal pressure, while India, Indonesia, Mexico, and Turkey retained labor-force growth but faced job-quality problems.

The U.S. economy was the crisis epicenter and the largest policy laboratory. Housing remained weak, household net worth had fallen sharply, and banks were still repairing balance sheets. The American Recovery and Reinvestment Act provided fiscal stimulus, while the Fed used zero-rate guidance, QE, and credit facilities. The policy aim was to stop a debt-deflation spiral, restore mortgage credit, and stabilize employment.

Microeconomically, autos, construction, banking, and commercial real estate were the weakest sectors. General Motors and Chrysler went through government-supported restructurings. Large banks raised capital after stress tests, but community banks and real estate lenders remained vulnerable. Technology was more resilient: smartphones, cloud infrastructure, and internet advertising kept attracting investment. Households deleveraged, raised savings, and reduced discretionary spending, which slowed the consumer-led recovery.

Japan’s recession was driven by external demand. The collapse in global autos, electronics, and capital goods hit Japanese production more severely than domestic financial losses alone would suggest. Deflation returned, reinforcing long-run concerns about nominal GDP stagnation. Japan’s debt problem was visible but domestically financed, so it did not trigger the same market panic as later euro-area sovereign stress.

### 5B. Corporate landscape

The largest companies by revenue in or around 2009 were dominated by energy, retail, autos, and finance: Walmart, Royal Dutch Shell, ExxonMobil, BP, Toyota, Sinopec, China National Petroleum, State Grid, Chevron, and Total were among the top global revenue groups. Revenue rankings were distorted by oil-price volatility, so energy companies saw large dollar-revenue swings even when production changed less.

Major corporate events reflected crisis repair. General Motors and Chrysler were restructured with government support in the United States. Financial institutions raised capital after stress tests. Lloyds and RBS remained under UK state influence. Fortis and Dexia remained central to Belgian financial repair. In technology, smartphones and mobile ecosystems accelerated, with Apple’s iPhone momentum and Android’s expansion reshaping consumer electronics. In China, state banks and infrastructure-linked firms expanded rapidly because stimulus was credit-intensive.

M&A was depressed relative to the pre-crisis boom, but strategic deals and distressed restructurings continued. Mining and energy companies reassessed projects after commodity prices fell, then restarted plans as prices recovered. The corporate winners were firms with liquidity, state backing, domestic-demand exposure, or secular technology demand. The losers were highly leveraged firms, construction developers, banks with weak asset quality, and exporters tied to capital-goods cycles.

### 5C. Innovation & technology

2009 did not look like a technology revolution year at first glance because the macro environment was weak. In hindsight, it was important for digital platforms. Smartphone adoption accelerated, app ecosystems grew, and mobile data became a mass-market business. Cloud computing shifted from experimental to mainstream corporate planning. Semiconductor demand rebounded later in the year as electronics inventories normalized.

R&D spending was pressured in cyclical industries but more resilient in pharmaceuticals, software, defense-related research, and large technology firms. Patent activity continued to internationalize, with Korea, China, Japan, the United States, and Europe all central to global innovation flows. Infrastructure megaprojects were most visible in China, where stimulus financed rail, roads, power, and urban construction.

### 5D. Labor markets & human capital

The global labor shock was large and uneven. Manufacturing workers, construction workers, young workers, migrants, and temporary workers were hit hardest. Germany’s short-time work scheme reduced layoffs, while Spain’s temporary-contract structure amplified unemployment. In the United States, long-term unemployment became a major social and macroeconomic concern.

Wages generally slowed, but nominal wage rigidity meant employment carried much of the adjustment. Informal labor markets in India, Indonesia, Mexico, Brazil, and Turkey absorbed stress that official unemployment measures often undercounted. Education and skills policy became more important because the crisis accelerated the divide between tradable routine work and higher-skill digital or professional services.

### 6B. Fixed income

Short rates were pinned near zero in the United States, Japan, the United Kingdom, and Switzerland, and at 1 percent in the euro area. Yield curves steepened because investors expected recovery, public borrowing rose, and central banks anchored the front end. Approximate 10-year government yields around year-end were: United States near 3.8 percent, Germany near 3.4 percent, United Kingdom near 4.0 percent, Japan near 1.3 percent, Canada near 3.6 percent, Italy and Spain around 4 percent. Credit spreads narrowed from crisis levels but remained above pre-crisis norms. Sovereign risk became more visible late in the year with Greece.

### 6C. Currencies

The dollar weakened after the panic phase as risk appetite returned, but it remained the central funding and reserve currency. The yen stayed strong, hurting Japanese exporters. The euro recovered during much of 2009, although Greek fiscal concerns began undermining confidence late in the year. Commodity currencies such as the Australian dollar, Canadian dollar, and Brazilian real rebounded as China demand and risk appetite improved. The Chinese renminbi was effectively stable against the U.S. dollar.

Several emerging-market currencies were still weaker on annual averages than before the crisis. Russia managed a ruble depreciation. Mexico’s peso and Korea’s won recovered from stressed levels but remained volatile. Capital controls were not the dominant 2009 policy story, but several countries considered or used measures to manage inflows as risk appetite returned.

### 6D. Alternative assets

Gold was one of the defining alternative assets of 2009. The average gold price was about $972 per troy ounce, and the price moved above $1,000 as investors hedged financial-system risk, dollar weakness, and future inflation concerns. Oil recovered from late-2008 lows, with Brent moving from the low $40s early in the year to the mid-$70s by December. Copper and other industrial metals rallied sharply as China restocked.

Real estate diverged. U.S. housing stabilized but remained weak. Spain and parts of the UK and Ireland were still in correction. China’s property market revived under credit stimulus. Commercial real estate was a major concern in the United States, United Kingdom, and parts of Europe because vacancies rose and refinancing was difficult.

## 7. RISKS, CRISES & BLACK SWANS

The active crisis was the global financial crisis and recession. The main near-miss was a deeper banking collapse, avoided through central-bank liquidity, public guarantees, capital injections, deposit insurance expansion, and fiscal support. Another near-miss was a full protectionist trade spiral; governments used some defensive measures, but they did not recreate the 1930s tariff collapse.

Systemic vulnerabilities building in 2009 included public-debt risk, central-bank exit risk, moral hazard in too-big-to-fail banking, China’s credit and property imbalances, and the possibility that labor-market scarring would reduce potential output. The Greek fiscal-data crisis late in 2009 was a clear warning that the next phase could shift from private financial risk to sovereign risk.

Hindsight clearly matters. At the time, policymakers were primarily trying to prevent depression. In hindsight, some support measures delayed bank restructuring, encouraged asset-price dependence on monetary support, or shifted risk into public debt. However, the consensus among major institutions is that aggressive intervention prevented a more catastrophic collapse.

## 8. YEAR-OVER-YEAR DELTA (2009 vs. 2008)

The structural break from 2008 to 2009 was the move from financial panic to real-economy collapse and policy backstop. In 2008 the crisis escalated with Lehman Brothers, AIG, bank rescues, and liquidity panic. In 2009 the crisis showed up in unemployment, trade, industrial output, fiscal deficits, and sovereign balance sheets.

World GDP in current dollars fell, and world trade value fell by about 23 percent. Inflation moved from commodity-driven pressure in 2008 to disinflation or deflation in 2009. Oil moved from a record high above $140 per barrel in mid-2008 to crisis lows and then partial recovery. Equity markets moved from collapse in 2008 to a strong rebound in 2009 after March.

The ranking shifts were meaningful but not revolutionary. China nearly caught Japan in nominal GDP and would pass it in 2010. Russia fell from ninth to twelfth because oil prices, the ruble, and output collapsed. India rose as its domestic-demand economy kept growing. Indonesia and Belgium entered the World Bank top 20, while Poland and Saudi Arabia dropped out under the same country coverage.

Policy also pivoted. The Fed, BOE, ECB, BOJ, Bank of Canada, RBA, Banco Central do Brasil, RBI, Bank of Korea, Bank Indonesia, and others were in easing or emergency mode for most of the year. By late 2009, Australia and some emerging economies were already thinking about normalization, while the United States, Japan, the United Kingdom, and euro area remained focused on avoiding relapse.

## 9. DATA CONFIDENCE & GAPS

Data confidence is highest for GDP, inflation, exchange rates, central-bank policy rates, and fiscal indicators for the United States, Japan, Canada, Australia, and European OECD economies. It is also high for headline GDP in China, India, Brazil, Mexico, Korea, Turkey, Indonesia, and Russia, but comparability is lower for unemployment, fiscal balance, public debt, and some price measures. India’s unemployment data for 2009 are not comparable to OECD-style annual unemployment. China’s registered urban unemployment is not equivalent to a national labor-force survey. Indonesia, Mexico, Turkey, Brazil, and Russia also have labor-market informality that makes headline unemployment incomplete.

GDP rankings conflict slightly across World Bank, IMF, and UN series because of revisions, exchange-rate conversion, fiscal-year treatment, and coverage of territories or non-sovereign economies. Taiwan is a special case: the IMF includes Taiwan Province of China, while the World Bank country GDP series used here has no Taiwan GDP data. Because the user requested country coverage and World Bank/Maddison-type historical ranking discipline, the main top-20 list uses World Bank country series and flags Taiwan separately.

Government debt and deficit estimates are among the weakest cross-country comparisons. General government coverage, public corporation treatment, local-government liabilities, bank-rescue accounting, and off-budget entities differ materially. China’s official public debt figure understates the broader public-sector obligations created through local-government financing vehicles. Brazil’s debt concepts differ across net, gross, and public-sector definitions. Japan’s debt is high but domestically financed, making market-risk interpretation different from externally financed sovereign stress.

Sectoral and corporate data are less standardized than macro data. Revenue rankings depend on fiscal-year reporting and exchange rates. Market-cap rankings changed quickly during the equity rally. M&A, bankruptcies, and nationalizations are well documented for major advanced economies but less uniformly reported in emerging markets. Real-estate data for 2009 are uneven across countries and cities.

Important data that simply do not exist in fully comparable form for 2009 include global informal unemployment, household balance-sheet stress by income group across all top 20 economies, comparable small-business credit conditions, and harmonized real-time fiscal exposures from guarantees and bank rescues. Where these gaps matter, this document labels the data as approximate, not comparable, or unavailable.
