# MACRO/MICRO GLOBAL ECONOMIC ASSESSMENT - YEAR: 1906

### 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

1906 sat near the high-water mark of the pre-1914 global economy. The dominant macro regime was the classical gold standard, with sterling-centered finance, high international capital mobility, expanding ocean shipping and rail networks, and deepening trade links between industrial cores and commodity exporters. The year was not a global depression or war year. It was a year of expansion under a rigid monetary order, but one in which financial strain was building beneath the surface.

The largest structural story was the continuing spread of the Second Industrial Revolution. Steel, railways, electric power, chemicals, oil, telegraphy, telephony, and large-scale corporate organization were reshaping production. The United States was the largest single economy in Maddison-style PPP terms. China and India remained among the largest economies because of their vast populations, but their GDP per person was far below that of the United States, the United Kingdom, Germany, France, Belgium, Canada, Australia, and other high-income economies.

The key 1906 shock was the San Francisco earthquake and fire. Its human and local economic consequences were concentrated in California, but its financial aftershock was international. British insurers paid large claims, gold flowed toward the United States, the Bank of England tightened sharply late in the year, and transatlantic credit conditions became more fragile. That tightening did not itself create the Panic of 1907, but it helped form the monetary environment in which the later panic became more severe.

The commodity world also changed in ways that mattered. Brazil adopted a coffee valorization policy under the Taubate Agreement, an early example of state-backed export price support. Prairie wheat in Canada and Argentina, plantation exports in the Netherlands Indies, cotton and jute in India, silk and textiles in Japan, and coal, steel, and chemicals in Europe all linked local producers to global price and credit cycles. The world economy was integrated, but unevenly integrated: colonial and semi-colonial economies supplied raw materials and labor-intensive goods while metropolitan centers controlled much of the capital, shipping, finance, and technology.

Data quality is uneven. GDP rankings below the first ten are approximate and sensitive to whether economies are measured as modern territories, sovereign states, colonial possessions, or imperial aggregates. The ranking below uses rounded Maddison Project Database 2023 and Our World in Data style real GDP estimates in constant 2011 international dollars, supplemented by historical aggregation for empires and colonial territories. Annual unemployment, CPI inflation, government debt, current account balances, and budget balances are often not available in harmonized form for 1906. Where a figure was not verified, this document states "Data not available" rather than filling gaps.

### 2. KEY THEMES OF 1906

**1. The classical gold standard at peak reach.** By 1906 the major industrial economies operated inside a gold-based fixed exchange rate system or closely related gold-exchange arrangements. The system supported long-distance trade, lower exchange-rate uncertainty, and large European capital exports. Its weakness was rigidity: payments shocks tended to transmit through gold flows, discount rates, and credit contraction. Countries most affected were the United Kingdom, United States, Germany, France, Japan, Canada, Australia, Argentina, Brazil, and India. The forward link is direct: strains visible in 1906 contributed to the credit environment that preceded the Panic of 1907, while the deeper contradiction between fixed exchange and domestic stability became much clearer after 1914.

**2. Industrial scale, finance, and the modern corporation.** 1906 was part of the era in which vertically integrated firms, universal banks, trusts, cartels, and national champions became central to economic life. The United States had U.S. Steel, Standard Oil, General Electric, and large railroads. Germany had Krupp, Siemens, AEG, BASF, Bayer, and other heavy industrial and chemical firms. Japan was building zaibatsu-centered industrial groups. The United Kingdom remained a financial, shipping, and imperial trading hub. The forward linkage was the growing role of corporate finance, securities markets, antitrust politics, and industrial concentration in the 1910s.

**3. A local disaster with global monetary effects.** The San Francisco disaster showed how insurance, gold settlement, and London money markets could transmit a local physical shock into the international financial system. Insurance claims required payments from British insurers, gold moved toward the United States, and the Bank of England raised Bank Rate to defend reserves and the sterling-gold parity. The countries most affected were the United States and the United Kingdom, with knock-on effects in international credit markets. The forward linkage was the recession and panic of 1907, plus later debate over the absence of a U.S. central bank.

**4. Commodity politics and the birth of formal export support.** Brazil's 1906 Taubate Agreement institutionalized support for coffee prices through public purchases of surplus coffee financed by external borrowing and export taxation. This was not a modern OPEC-style cartel, but it was a significant early case of state-backed commodity price management. It affected Brazil most directly, but also coffee importers and international lenders. The forward linkage was the long history of Brazilian coffee valorization, fiscal dependence on coffee, and the vulnerability exposed in the 1929 crash.

**5. Colonial and semi-colonial economies as growth mechanisms for imperial cores.** India, the Netherlands Indies, parts of Africa, the Caribbean, and other colonial territories supplied raw materials, plantation goods, and tax revenue. Their integration into world markets was real, but the gains were unequally distributed. Railways, ports, and telegraphs often improved market access while reinforcing extractive trade patterns. The forward linkage was a century-long debate over colonial development, deindustrialization, terms of trade, and political economy.

**6. Migration as a macroeconomic adjustment mechanism.** The era from the late 19th century to 1914 was a major age of mass migration. Labor moved from Europe to the United States, Canada, Argentina, Brazil, Australia, and other settler economies. Migration relieved labor pressure in parts of Europe and supplied labor to expanding agricultural, construction, and industrial sectors overseas. Countries most affected included Italy, Austria-Hungary, Russia, the United States, Argentina, Canada, Brazil, and Australia. The forward linkage was the tightening of immigration regimes after World War I and the long-run demographic consequences for the Americas and Oceania.

**7. Postwar and post-revolution stabilization in Eurasia.** Japan emerged from the Russo-Japanese War with prestige but also fiscal and debt burdens. Russia was recovering from war defeat and the 1905 Revolution, with political instability still high. China was in the late Qing reform period, with fiscal pressure, foreign indemnities, rail politics, and a changing commercial economy. These economies were not marginal: China, India, Japan, and Russia together represented a major share of global output and population. The forward linkage was the sequence of upheavals from the Chinese Revolution of 1911 to World War I and the Russian Revolution.

### 3. MACROECONOMIC OVERVIEW

#### 3A. Global Aggregates

**World GDP and growth.** World GDP in 1906, using Maddison-style PPP real output in constant 2011 international dollars, is estimated at roughly $3.8 trillion to $4.2 trillion. This is a reconstructed estimate, not a contemporary national accounts figure. A harmonized world real GDP growth rate for 1906 was not verified for this report. Data not available.

**Global trade volume.** World merchandise trade was expanding during the 1900-1913 globalization wave. The United Nations historical trade series cover world exports and major country trade for 1900-1913, but exact 1906 global trade volume growth was not verified here. Data not available. Directionally, trade was driven by European manufactured exports, British and German shipping and finance, U.S. industrial exports, and raw material flows from India, Latin America, settler economies, and colonial Southeast Asia.

**Reserve currency dynamics.** Sterling was the leading international currency and London was the central money market. Gold convertibility and gold points anchored major exchange rates. The U.S. dollar was important but did not yet have the later reserve role it acquired after World War I and especially after World War II. France and Germany held major monetary importance, but London remained the core international financial center.

**Global inflation trends.** Inflation was generally low to moderate in gold-standard core economies, but price behavior varied across countries and commodities. U.S. reconstructed CPI inflation was about 2.2 percent in 1906 using later historical CPI reconstructions. Comparable CPI data for most countries were not available in modern harmonized form. Data not available for a true global inflation rate.

**Key commodity prices.** Gold was fixed in the United States at $20.67 per troy ounce under the gold standard. A historical oil price series reports crude oil near $0.73 per barrel in current 1906 dollars. Paris wheat prices in the FRED/NBER series ranged from roughly 22.40 to 24.24 francs per 100 kilograms during 1906. Copper, rubber, coal, cotton, coffee, and other commodity prices were economically important, but exact globally representative annual price data were not all verified in this report. Data not available where not stated.

**Capital flows.** The main direction of international capital was from Europe, especially Britain, France, Germany, Belgium, and the Netherlands, toward the United States, Canada, Argentina, Brazil, Russia, colonial territories, and infrastructure projects. Capital financed railways, ports, government debt, mining, utilities, and plantations. The United States was simultaneously an industrial giant and a still-important absorber of foreign funds, especially during reconstruction and railroad expansion cycles.

#### 3B. Monetary & Financial Conditions

The international monetary system was the classical gold standard, not Bretton Woods. Exchange rates among participating currencies were fixed by gold parities and adjusted within gold points. Central banks and treasuries defended convertibility through discount rates, gold reserve management, and sometimes informal market intervention.

The United Kingdom's Bank Rate was a key global signal. FRED's Bank of England quarterly policy rate series shows 4.00 percent in 1906 Q1, 3.67 percent in Q2 and Q3, and 6.00 percent in Q4. This late-year tightening is central to interpreting 1906. It reflected pressure on gold reserves and the sterling-dollar relationship after insurance-related gold flows to the United States.

The United States had no central bank in 1906. Monetary conditions were managed through the Treasury, national banks, private clearinghouses, correspondent banking, and private financiers. This made liquidity elastic in boom times but fragile under stress. Germany, France, Japan, and other central-bank countries operated under gold constraints, but comparable modern policy-rate series are not always available for 1906. Data not available for many country-level policy rates.

Equity markets were active but not globally standardized. The Dow Jones Industrial Average ended 1906 at about 94.35, down about 1.9 percent from 1905 year-end, after strong gains in 1905. London, Paris, Berlin, Amsterdam, Brussels, and other exchanges handled large volumes of government bonds, railway securities, mining shares, and industrial issues, but comparable annual index-level data are not available for all exchanges. Data not available.

Government bond markets were central to international finance. British consols, French rentes, German government securities, Russian bonds, and emerging market sovereign debt were major asset classes. Yield curve terminology in the modern sense is not directly comparable. Data not available for complete 1906 cross-country yield curves.

#### 3C. Trade & Geopolitics

There was no single global trade institution comparable to the post-1947 GATT or the WTO. Trade policy was shaped by national tariffs, imperial preferences, bilateral treaties, shipping routes, and colonial regulations. The United Kingdom remained more free-trade oriented than many industrial competitors, while Germany, the United States, France, Italy, Russia, and others used varying degrees of protection.

Geopolitically, 1906 was marked by fragile peace rather than global war. The Algeciras Conference addressed the First Moroccan Crisis and reduced immediate conflict risk between Germany and France, but it also revealed the tension among European powers. Japan was consolidating its postwar position after the Russo-Japanese War, including influence over Korea. Russia was stabilizing after defeat and revolution. These developments shaped capital markets because sovereign debt, railway concessions, and colonial investment all depended on political risk.

Trade integration continued across the Atlantic and Indian Ocean. British finance and shipping linked Argentina, India, Australia, Canada, South Africa, and the United Kingdom. German exports pushed into global markets. U.S. manufactured output and agricultural exports expanded. Japan was rising as an Asian industrial power, though still far smaller in per capita income than the Western industrial economies.

#### 3D. Commodities & Energy

Coal remained the dominant industrial energy source. Britain, Germany, the United States, France, Belgium, and Russia depended on coal for railways, steel, electricity, and urban industry. Oil was emerging, especially in the United States, Russia's Baku region, the Netherlands Indies, Mexico, and Persia's developing concessionary landscape, but it had not yet displaced coal as the main energy source.

Agricultural commodities shaped both macro balances and local politics. Wheat mattered in Canada, Argentina, Russia, the United States, India, Australia, and France. Coffee dominated Brazilian export politics. Cotton linked the U.S. South, India, Egypt, Lancashire, and global textile production. Jute mattered for Bengal and Dundee. Sugar, tea, tobacco, rubber, and tin shaped colonial economies in the Caribbean and Southeast Asia.

Metals were strategic inputs. Steel production was a core measure of industrial power in the United States, Germany, the United Kingdom, France, Belgium, Russia, and Japan. Copper was important for electrification and telecommunications. Gold mining supported monetary reserves and settler-economy development, especially in South Africa, Australia, and North America. Exact global 1906 metal price and output comparisons are incomplete in the sources reviewed. Data not available.

#### 3E. Labor & Demographics

Global labor markets were segmented by empire, race, law, skill, and transport cost, but mobility was high by historical standards. European migration to the Americas and Oceania was economically significant. Italy, Austria-Hungary, Russia, and parts of northern Europe were major sources of migrants, while the United States, Argentina, Canada, Brazil, and Australia absorbed labor and population.

Urbanization and industrial employment were rising in the United States, Germany, Japan, Russia, Italy, and parts of Latin America. Agriculture still employed the majority of workers in China, India, Russia, Brazil, Mexico, the Ottoman Empire, and much of Southern and Eastern Europe. Wage comparisons are difficult because real wages, household production, seasonal work, and informal labor were measured inconsistently. Data not available for harmonized global wage growth.

Organized labor was increasingly important. The United Kingdom, Germany, the United States, France, Italy, Australia, and New Zealand had visible labor movements, though legal rights and political leverage varied. Workplace safety, hours, child labor, and union recognition were central issues. Productivity gains were strongest in mechanized industry, transport, and energy-intensive manufacturing.

The United States was the largest economy by 1906 in Maddison-style output estimates. Its scale rested on manufacturing, railroads, agriculture, natural resources, and an expanding internal market. The economy had world-leading steel, oil, machinery, meatpacking, and railroad sectors. Corporate concentration was high, and public debate over trusts and market power shaped politics.

The San Francisco disaster was the major domestic shock. Rebuilding generated construction demand, but the insurance and gold-flow consequences helped tighten international credit. The United States lacked a central bank, so liquidity depended on the Treasury, clearinghouses, private banks, and leading financiers. This institutional weakness mattered more after 1906 than during the year itself.

Micro and sectoral highlights: steel, railroads, oil, electric equipment, agricultural machinery, and consumer manufactured goods led industrial growth. Real estate in San Francisco entered a large reconstruction cycle. Banking and trust companies were growing in importance, but their liquidity structure was fragile. The Hepburn Act strengthened federal railroad regulation, showing that the state was beginning to respond more aggressively to large-network industries.

#### 5C. Innovation & technology

Electrification continued to transform factories, lighting, urban transit, and communications. Electric motors allowed more flexible factory layouts than steam-line shafting. Telephone networks expanded in large cities, while telegraphy remained vital for international finance and trade. Copper demand reflected this communications and electrification boom.

Internal combustion and petroleum were rising but not yet dominant. Automobiles were still a small share of transport, but firms in the United States, France, Germany, Italy, and Britain were developing the industrial base. Aviation was experimental. Railways and steamships remained the main long-distance transport technologies.

Chemical and materials industries were major productivity drivers. Germany led in synthetic dyes, chemicals, and applied research. Steel processes and large-scale rolling capacity supported railways, bridges, ships, and machinery. These technologies had direct macro implications because they increased fixed investment demand and strengthened the industrial economies in export competition.

Infrastructure megaprojects were economically important. The Panama Canal was under U.S. construction. Railways expanded in Canada, Russia, India, Argentina, and Africa. Ports, urban water systems, and electric tramways improved market integration and city productivity.

#### 5D. Labor markets & human capital

The global labor market was moving from rural to urban work in industrializing economies, but the majority of workers worldwide still worked in agriculture. Education and skills were increasingly important. Germany's technical education system supported chemicals, engineering, and electrical goods. In colonial economies, education access was narrower and often shaped by administrative priorities rather than broad industrial upgrading.

Unions and labor movements were active in many industrial economies. Australia and New Zealand had advanced labor legislation by global standards. ### 7. RISKS, CRISES & BLACK SWANS

The San Francisco earthquake and fire was the largest active economic shock of 1906. Its local effects included damage to property, transport, municipal infrastructure, housing, and business operations. Its global effect came through insurance claims, gold flows, and London money-market tightening. This link is one of the clearest examples of a physical disaster transmitting into a monetary shock under the gold standard.

The main systemic vulnerability was financial fragility in a world of fixed exchange rates and limited lender-of-last-resort capacity. The United States was especially vulnerable because it lacked a central bank. Banks and trust companies were connected through securities loans, correspondent balances, and seasonal currency flows. With hindsight, 1906 was part of the buildup to the Panic of 1907. That hindsight should not be confused with what was fully knowable at the time.

Commodity concentration was another risk. Brazil's coffee policy stabilized prices in the short run but increased dependence on external loans and state-backed inventories. Argentina, Canada, Australia, Russia, India, and the Netherlands Indies were all exposed to commodity price and harvest cycles. Colonial territories bore risks without equal political control over policy.

Geopolitical risk was present but did not become a major war in 1906. The Moroccan crisis was contained diplomatically, Japan and Russia were adjusting after war, and China faced internal reform pressures. With hindsight, these tensions were part of a wider pre-1914 geopolitical structure that proved unstable, but a global war was not a 1906 macro event.

Policy mistakes visible in retrospect include overconfidence in private liquidity arrangements, underestimation of trust-company fragility in the United States, and the assumption that gold-standard adjustment would remain politically and financially manageable. These were not universally seen as errors in 1906, because the gold standard was widely regarded by elites as a stabilizing institution.

### 8. YEAR-OVER-YEAR DELTA (1906 vs. 1905)

The biggest shift from 1905 to 1906 was tighter monetary pressure in the international system. In 1905 U.S. equities had risen strongly, while 1906 brought a modest Dow decline and late-year credit tightening. The San Francisco disaster was the event that most clearly changed the financial environment.

Brazil's policy regime changed materially. The Taubate Agreement marked a new phase of coffee valorization and state-backed commodity intervention. Brazil also moved toward gold-standard monetary arrangements, aligning currency stabilization with export-sector politics and foreign borrowing.

Japan moved from wartime victory to postwar consolidation. The economic issue shifted from mobilization and war finance toward debt management, colonial influence, railway expansion, and industrial upgrading. Russia moved from revolutionary crisis toward uneasy stabilization, though social and political tensions remained.

The industrial trend accelerated rather than reversed. The United States and Germany continued to gain relative industrial weight. The United Kingdom remained financially dominant but faced stronger industrial competition. China and India remained huge in total output but did not close the per capita income gap with industrial economies.

Migration and settlement economies remained powerful. Canada, Argentina, Australia, and the United States continued to absorb migrants and capital. Commodity-export economies benefited from global demand, but their vulnerability to credit and price cycles increased.

### 9. DATA CONFIDENCE & GAPS

**Reliable areas.** GDP and population estimates are strongest for the United States, United Kingdom, Germany, France, Belgium, Netherlands, Australia, Canada, and Japan relative to the global sample. Even there, the figures are reconstructed historical national accounts, not modern national accounts produced in 1906. Bank of England Bank Rate, U.S. reconstructed CPI, Paris wheat prices, and Dow Jones year-end data are relatively well documented.

**Moderate confidence areas.** Italy, Spain, Argentina, Mexico, Brazil, Russia, Austria-Hungary, and the Ottoman Empire have useful historical data but more uncertainty. Boundary changes, imperial structures, fiscal fragmentation, informal production, and incomplete price data reduce comparability.

**Low confidence areas.** China, India, Indonesia, and other colonial or semi-colonial economies have large reconstructed GDP uncertainties. Estimates for China and India are especially sensitive to population, agricultural output, regional prices, and assumptions about non-market and household production. GDP estimates for non-market or weakly monetized sectors should be treated as broad orders of magnitude.

**Ranking conflicts.** The top six are relatively stable as large economies, though the internal ordering of Germany, the United Kingdom, and Russia can shift with PPP method, boundaries, and aggregation. Ranks 9-20 are much less secure. Austria-Hungary as an imperial aggregate is not directly equivalent to modern-country Maddison observations. India and Indonesia are listed as economic territories under colonial rule. Ottoman estimates are approximate. A modern-territory ranking might differ from an imperial-sovereign ranking.

**Unavailable or weak data.** Harmonized annual real GDP growth, CPI inflation, unemployment, policy rates, government debt to GDP, current account balance to GDP, budget balance to GDP, stock index returns outside New York, bond yield curves, corporate market capitalization, corporate revenue rankings, household debt, consumer confidence, real estate price indices, and sector-level employment are not available for many countries in 1906. Where not verified, this report uses "Data not available."

**Weakest claims.** The weakest quantitative claims are the exact GDP levels and order for ranks 11-20, the GDP growth rates by country, and any implied cross-country comparison of unemployment or fiscal balances. The strongest claims are the broad gold-standard regime, sterling-centered finance, the Bank of England late-1906 tightening, the importance of San Francisco insurance gold flows, the role of Brazil's coffee valorization, and the broad industrial pattern of U.S.-German ascent.
