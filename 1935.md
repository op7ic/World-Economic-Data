# MACRO/MICRO GLOBAL ECONOMIC ASSESSMENT - YEAR: 1935

Data basis and caution: GDP, population, GDP per capita, and growth are computed from the Maddison Project Database 2023. GDP is billions of 2011 international dollars at PPP, computed from GDP per capita times population. The ranking was cross-checked against Our World in Data's Maddison-based GDP series and the GDP Histomap project. No single official nominal-dollar GDP ranking exists for 1935. Soviet, Chinese, colonial, and other weak-statistics economies are especially uncertain. For Soviet and other non-market estimates, assume rough error of about 20 to 30 percent. Unless stated otherwise, growth means MPD real PPP total-GDP change from 1934 to 1935.

### 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

1935 was a recovery year, but not a return to the pre-Depression world economy. Output rose in many major economies, equity markets improved, commodity stocks fell, and unemployment indicators moved down from extreme Depression levels. Yet world trade remained far below 1929, exchange controls were widespread, international capital flows were weak, and the economy was increasingly split among the sterling area, the dollar area, the remaining gold bloc, exchange-control economies, and planned economies.

The dominant macro divide was between countries that had escaped gold-standard deflation and those still defending gold parities. The United States, United Kingdom, Japan, Sweden, Canada, and Australia benefited from easier money, currency flexibility, public works, or cheap credit. France and the Netherlands remained under gold-bloc pressure. Belgium left gold in 1935 and recovered more strongly, foreshadowing the broader 1936 gold-bloc break.

State direction was the second defining force. In the United States, the Second New Deal turned recovery policy into permanent institutions for banking, labor, social insurance, and work relief. In Germany, Japan, Italy, and the USSR, growth was tied more directly to state allocation, public procurement, heavy industry, and strategic sectors. In colonial and developing economies, exchange controls, commodity schemes, and trade preferences mattered more than open-market adjustment.

The top-20 GDP ranking was led by the United States, former USSR, China, Germany, United Kingdom, India, France, Japan, and Italy. Large colonial or agrarian economies ranked high because of population. Financial markets improved, but recovery remained fragile and fragmented.

### 2. KEY THEMES OF 1935

**1. Uneven recovery from the Great Depression.** League of Nations indices with 1929 equal to 100 put world industrial production in 1935 at 95 including the USSR and 84 excluding it. The quantum of world trade was only 79, showing that production recovered faster than trade. Stronger recovery economies included the United States, Germany, Japan, USSR, Britain, Canada, Australia, Sweden, and Belgium.

**2. The gold bloc weakened.** France and the Netherlands still defended gold, while Belgium left gold and devalued. This mattered because gold defense transmitted external pressure into domestic deflation. The forward link was the broader 1936 break by France and the Netherlands.

**3. State-led demand replaced older laissez-faire assumptions.** The United States expanded institutions, while Germany, Italy, Japan, and the USSR used more direct state allocation. Britain relied more on cheap money and housing. Across systems, public policy became a growth mechanism.

**4. Trade recovery was slow and managed.** Tariffs, quotas, clearing agreements, exchange controls, and colonial preferences shaped flows. Germany's clearing system, sterling-area preferences, China's currency reform, and Italy's sanctions crisis show how trade became more political and administrative.

**5. Cheap money supported recovery, but finance was not normal.** The U.S. prime loan rate was recorded at 1.5 percent, date uncertain, and the UK Bank Rate was 2 percent. Low rates helped housing, debt service, and equities, but international lending and capital mobility remained impaired.

**6. Micro recovery ran through construction, durable goods, heavy industry, and import substitution.** Housing in Britain, autos and durables in the United States, metals and engineering in Germany and Japan, and import substitution in Latin America were major transmission channels.

Sterling remained central in the sterling area, the dollar gained importance after gold revaluation, gold-bloc currencies preserved parity at high domestic cost, and exchange-control currencies relied on administrative allocation.

**3B. Monetary & Financial Conditions:**

The main monetary divide was flexible-currency recovery versus gold defense. The United States operated after the 1934 gold revaluation. Britain held Bank Rate at 2 percent. Germany used exchange controls and bilateral clearing. Japan benefited from earlier yen depreciation. France and the Netherlands remained under gold-bloc pressure. Equity markets rose where recovery and reflation were credible; the Dow Jones Industrial Average ended 1935 at 144.13, up 38.53 percent. Comparable verified annual-return data for most non-U.S. exchanges were not found.

**3C. Trade & Geopolitics:**

Trade remained fragmented. Clearing agreements, quotas, tariffs, import licensing, exchange controls, and imperial preferences mattered more than pre-1914-style open flows. Italy's late-1935 sanctions crisis affected raw-material and energy expectations. China's currency reform changed trade finance. There was no Bretton Woods framework and no postwar-style regional integration. The relevant blocs were sterling, dollar, gold, German exchange-control, Soviet planning, and colonial trade systems.

**3D. Commodities & Energy:**

Commodity conditions improved but stayed fragile. League indices show stocks of staple commodities falling, while metals benefited from industrial recovery and state demand. Agriculture remained uneven: exporters such as Argentina, Australia, Canada, Brazil, and India were sensitive to prices, weather, debt, and exchange controls. A verified harmonized annual oil price for 1935 was not found in the source set.

**3E. Labor & Demographics:**

Labor markets improved from the Depression trough, but unemployment remained high and definitions were not comparable. Migration was constrained by restrictions and weak labor markets. Urbanization continued in the USSR, Japan, Latin America, and colonial Asia, but household welfare depended on rural prices, food costs, and employment recovery.

Capital controls make simple exchange-rate comparisons misleading.

### 7. RISKS, CRISES & BLACK SWANS

The biggest active risks were monetary fragmentation, geopolitical sanctions, incomplete labor recovery, and fragile trade. Gold-bloc countries risked deflation and capital flight. Exchange-control countries risked scarcity and misallocation. Commodity exporters risked renewed price declines. Banking was safer than in 1931 to 1933, but still depended on confidence and public backstops.

Italy's sanctions crisis showed how political conflict could reshape trade. China's currency reform created implementation and confidence risk. Germany's recovery hid foreign-exchange bottlenecks behind controls. Soviet growth was difficult to compare with market economies. Hindsight: 1935's recovery was achieved through national systems that reduced international integration and increased late-1930s instability.

### 8. YEAR-OVER-YEAR DELTA (1935 vs. 1934)

The main change was acceleration. MPD-covered economies grew by about 6.5 percent in aggregate. The United States, USSR, China, Germany, Canada, Belgium, Australia, Italy, Sweden, and Argentina all posted strong or moderate gains. Industrial output, employment, and commodity indicators improved.

Rank changes were concrete. The former USSR overtook China for second place. Germany overtook the United Kingdom for fourth. Australia overtook Czechoslovakia. No country entered or exited the top 20. Policy changes were also large: the United States institutionalized the Second New Deal, Canada and India began central-bank operations, Argentina created its central bank, Belgium devalued, China reformed its currency, and Germany and Italy deepened controlled-economy policies.

Risks also changed. Immediate deflation and banking-collapse risks receded in many economies, but geopolitical, exchange-control, and trade-fragmentation risks increased. Recovery was broader than in 1934, but less internationally liberal.

### 9. DATA CONFIDENCE & GAPS

Highest confidence: MPD 2023 GDP per capita, population, computed total GDP, and ranking calculations. These are still historical estimates, not official 1935 national accounts. Confidence is highest for the United States, United Kingdom, France, Germany, Canada, Australia, Sweden, Netherlands, and Belgium. Confidence is lower for China, India, Indonesia, Brazil, Poland, and Argentina. Confidence is lowest for the former USSR because market GDP concepts must be reconstructed from planned-economy data.

Country-level unemployment, CPI inflation, current-account balances, debt-to-GDP ratios, budget balances, and policy rates are not comparable without national-source reconciliation. Many countries lacked modern CPI definitions, labor-force surveys, fiscal reporting, or market-based policy rates. This file uses "Data not available" unless the searched source set verified the value.

Data conflict in three main ways: Maddison versions differ by benchmark and PPP choices; exchange rates may be official, market, gold-parity, black-market, or clearing rates; and unemployment may mean insured unemployment, union unemployment, relief rolls, or something else entirely. Weakest evidence areas are non-U.S. equity returns, global corporate rankings, exact commodity prices, fiscal ratios, and country-level current accounts.
