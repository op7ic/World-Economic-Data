# MACRO/MICRO GLOBAL ECONOMIC ASSESSMENT - YEAR: 2018

### 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

2018 was the year in which the synchronized post-crisis upswing began to lose coherence. World output was still expanding at a solid rate: nominal world GDP was roughly USD 86 trillion in current dollars, while the IMF's PPP-weighted real growth measure remained near the high point of the post-2008 expansion. Yet the year ended with a clear change in tone. The WTO recorded world merchandise trade volume growth of 3.0 percent, down from 4.6 percent in 2017, and BIS analysis described a late-year slowdown in trade, manufacturing, and financial risk appetite. The shift was not a recession, but it was a transition from globally synchronized expansion toward a more fractured, policy-sensitive cycle.

The dominant economic forces were tighter US monetary policy, the escalation of US-China tariffs, China's domestic deleveraging campaign, the late-2018 oil-price reversal, and the repricing of global risk assets. The United States accelerated because of fiscal stimulus, strong consumption, and a tight labor market, but the Federal Reserve raised the federal funds target range four times and finished the year at 2.25 to 2.50 percent. China continued to grow rapidly by global standards but slowed under the combined pressure of credit restraint, property-sector caution, weaker autos, and trade conflict. Europe lost momentum, especially Germany and Italy, while Japan stayed constrained by demographics and low inflation.

In microeconomic terms, 2018 was a year of visible divergence between digital, energy, and traditional industrial sectors. Semiconductors reached a record sales year, internet penetration crossed the symbolic threshold of more than half the world's population, and China dominated global patent filing by volume. At the same time, smartphone shipments fell for the first time after years of expansion, autos slowed in China and Europe, and global equities corrected sharply in the fourth quarter. Oil producers benefited for most of the year from higher Brent prices, but prices collapsed in the final quarter as supply and demand expectations shifted.

The most important structural change was the normalization of geopolitical risk inside routine commercial decision-making. The trade war did not yet dominate all flows, but it changed purchasing patterns, redirected soybean trade toward Brazil, and encouraged supply-chain hedging. The year also showed the fragility of leveraged emerging markets in a stronger-dollar world. Turkey and Argentina were the clearest stress cases, but tighter external financing conditions affected a much wider group of economies. Hindsight shows 2018 as the bridge between the post-2016 reflation phase and the weaker, more fragmented 2019 global economy.

### 2. KEY THEMES OF 2018

**1. From synchronized expansion to synchronized slowdown risk.** In 2017, the global economy enjoyed a broad-based trade and manufacturing rebound. In 2018, that momentum faded. Services, labor markets, and consumption remained relatively resilient, but manufacturing PMIs, export orders, and merchandise trade slowed. The most affected economies were Germany, China, Japan, Korea, Italy, and other trade-intensive economies. The forward linkage was direct: by 2019 the global policy debate had moved from inflation normalization toward recession insurance and central-bank easing.

**2. The US-China trade war became macro-relevant.** The United States imposed tariffs on successive tranches of Chinese imports, including USD 34 billion, USD 16 billion, and USD 200 billion categories. China retaliated, and the December 2018 G20 truce delayed but did not resolve the conflict. Economically, the dispute mattered not only because of tariff costs but because it raised uncertainty for investment, inventories, sourcing, and global value chains. China, the United States, Mexico, Brazil, Korea, Germany, Japan, and ASEAN economies were all affected through direct trade or supply-chain channels.

**3. Monetary divergence tightened global financial conditions.** US growth outperformed and the Fed tightened, while the European Central Bank ended net asset purchases but kept policy rates at zero or below, the Bank of Japan maintained yield-curve control, and the Bank of England raised rates only cautiously amid Brexit uncertainty. The stronger dollar and higher US yields pressured emerging-market balance sheets, especially where current-account deficits and foreign-currency liabilities were large. Turkey and Argentina were crisis cases; Indonesia, India, South Africa, Brazil, and Mexico faced pressure but had more varied outcomes.

**4. China shifted from deleveraging to selective stabilization.** Chinese policymakers entered 2018 focused on financial-risk control, shadow-banking restraint, and debt discipline. As trade conflict and domestic weakness intensified, policy moved toward reserve-requirement cuts, infrastructure support, and targeted credit easing. The change mattered because China's import demand, construction cycle, autos, and commodity consumption were central to global manufacturing and resource exporters. The forward link was the 2019 policy mix of partial stimulus without a full return to the post-2008 credit surge.

**5. Commodities had a two-phase year.** Oil rose through the first three quarters, supported by demand, OPEC and non-OPEC supply discipline, US sanctions on Iran, and limited spare capacity concerns. Brent averaged about USD 72 per barrel, but it fell sharply in the fourth quarter as supply grew and demand expectations weakened. Metals and agricultural prices became more vulnerable to Chinese demand concerns and trade restrictions. Oil exporters such as Saudi Arabia, Russia, Canada, and parts of Latin America initially gained, while importers such as India and Turkey faced inflation and current-account pressure.

**6. Digitalization became a majority-population phenomenon.** ITU estimates showed that more than half of the world population was using the internet by the end of 2018. Semiconductors recorded a peak revenue year, patent filing continued to shift toward Asia, and platform companies remained central to equity-market valuation. Yet the sector also began to show cyclical limits: smartphone shipments declined, Chinese internet equities were volatile, and data/privacy regulation became more economically significant after the EU General Data Protection Regulation took effect.

**7. Politics and economics became more tightly coupled.** Brexit uncertainty constrained UK investment; Italy's budget dispute raised sovereign-spread risk; US fiscal stimulus expanded deficits during full employment; the Khashoggi killing damaged Saudi investor sentiment; Brazil's election reset policy expectations; Mexico's election and airport cancellation affected investor confidence; and Turkey's policy credibility crisis turned into a currency and inflation shock. These episodes show how political risk moved from a background variable to a direct driver of asset prices and investment plans.

### 3. MACROECONOMIC OVERVIEW

#### 3A. Global Aggregates

World nominal GDP was about USD 86 trillion in current dollars. The IMF's PPP-weighted real world growth measure was roughly 3.6 percent, while WTO's market-exchange-rate GDP measure used in trade analysis was 2.9 percent. These figures are not contradictory: they use different weighting conventions. The key direction was the same across sources: output growth remained positive, but the expansion lost momentum after the 2017 rebound.

World merchandise trade volume grew 3.0 percent, down sharply from 4.6 percent in 2017. The slowdown reflected higher tariffs, rising uncertainty, tighter financial conditions, and weaker late-year industrial orders. Global FDI fell 13 percent to about USD 1.3 trillion, partly because US multinationals repatriated earnings after the 2017 US tax reform. International capital flows moved toward the United States and away from more vulnerable emerging markets during stress episodes.

Reserve-currency structure changed only gradually. The US dollar remained dominant, with about 61.7 percent of allocated official reserves in late 2018, while the euro's share was about 20.7 percent. The renminbi's role was rising but still small. The dollar's reserve dominance helped transmit US monetary conditions globally.

Global inflation was moderate but uneven. Advanced-economy inflation rose with energy prices but generally remained close to or below central-bank objectives. Emerging-market inflation diverged sharply, with Turkey experiencing a currency-driven inflation surge, while China and much of East Asia remained contained. Oil prices were the key commodity swing: Brent averaged about USD 72 per barrel and WTI about USD 65 per barrel, but both benchmarks ended below their starting levels after a steep fourth-quarter fall. Gold averaged roughly USD 1,268 per troy ounce, supported late in the year by risk aversion. Copper and other industrial metals weakened as China demand and trade-war concerns mounted.

#### 3B. Monetary & Financial Conditions

The Federal Reserve was the only major central bank clearly tightening through the year. It raised the federal funds target range to 2.25 to 2.50 percent by December and continued balance-sheet runoff. The European Central Bank ended net asset purchases under the Asset Purchase Programme in December, but policy rates remained unchanged at zero for refinancing operations and negative for the deposit facility. The Bank of Japan maintained negative short-term rates and yield-curve control around a zero percent 10-year JGB target. The Bank of England raised Bank Rate to 0.75 percent in August but remained constrained by Brexit uncertainty.

Exchange rates reflected this divergence. The US dollar strengthened against many emerging-market currencies. The Turkish lira and Argentine peso suffered crisis-level declines. The renminbi weakened against the dollar after spring 2018 as tariff risks and domestic easing expectations rose. The euro weakened from its early-year levels as euro-area growth disappointed. Commodity-linked currencies such as the Canadian and Australian dollars were mixed, with late-year oil weakness and China concerns weighing.

Equity markets had a strong start, a volatility shock in February, and a severe fourth-quarter selloff. Global exchange data show that domestic market capitalization fell from about USD 87.3 trillion at the end of the third quarter to USD 74.4 trillion at the end of the fourth quarter. US equities outperformed most markets but still ended down. China, Germany, Korea, Turkey, and several European exchanges experienced deeper losses. Bond markets moved from higher-yield pressure to safety demand late in the year as investors questioned the durability of global growth.

#### 3C. Trade & Geopolitics

The trade regime changed materially in 2018. The US-China tariff exchange was the central event, but it occurred alongside US steel and aluminum tariffs, disputes with the EU, NAFTA renegotiation into the USMCA, and a broader rise in trade-policy uncertainty. The Comprehensive and Progressive Agreement for Trans-Pacific Partnership entered into force at the end of 2018 among its initial parties, signaling that some regional liberalization continued even as the US-China conflict intensified.

Geopolitics affected energy, finance, and investment. US withdrawal from the Iran nuclear deal and renewed sanctions lifted oil-risk premiums in the first half. Sanctions on Russia and political risk in Turkey influenced capital flows. Brexit negotiations created a persistent investment overhang in the United Kingdom and uncertainty for European supply chains. Saudi Arabia faced investor-sentiment damage after the killing of Jamal Khashoggi, even as oil prices supported fiscal revenue.

#### 3D. Commodities & Energy

Oil was the most important commodity story. The year began with strong demand expectations and continued OPEC and non-OPEC supply management. US shale output rose rapidly, with US crude production reaching record levels. Brent peaked near USD 86 per barrel in early October, then fell to about USD 50 by late December. The price path created a split year: producers enjoyed fiscal and current-account support for much of 2018, but the late collapse exposed the fragility of oil-price-dependent budgets.

Metals weakened as the trade war raised concerns about China-centered industrial demand. World Bank commodity analysis showed metals falling in the third quarter on weaker demand expectations and tariff concerns. Agricultural markets were shaped by weather, inventories, and trade diversion. Soybeans became the clearest tariff-linked agricultural market: Chinese purchases shifted away from the United States and toward Brazil.

Energy transition themes remained present but were not yet the dominant macro story. Electric vehicles, batteries, and renewables continued to attract capital, but oil and gas price dynamics still drove short-term macro outcomes for major exporters and importers.

#### 3E. Labor & Demographics

Global labor markets improved in headline terms. ILO estimates placed global unemployment at about 172 million people and a 5.0 percent unemployment rate in 2018. However, this aggregate hides major differences in labor-force participation, informality, underemployment, and statistical definitions. Advanced economies generally saw tight labor markets, especially the United States, Germany, Japan, and the Netherlands. Wage growth improved in some economies but remained modest relative to the depth of labor-market tightening.

Demographics were increasingly visible in macro performance. Japan's aging population limited labor supply and domestic demand. Europe's aging and slower labor-force growth raised productivity concerns. China faced the longer-term consequences of low fertility and an aging workforce even while its labor force remained large. India and Indonesia benefited from younger demographics, but job quality, skills, informality, and urban infrastructure constrained the conversion of demographic potential into income growth.

Urbanization and digital connectivity deepened the microeconomic impact of growth. Urban consumers, logistics networks, mobile payments, and platform services gained importance in China, India, Indonesia, Brazil, and Mexico. Migration remained economically significant in Europe, North America, the Gulf, and parts of Asia, but political resistance increasingly shaped policy.

The US economy was the strongest major advanced-economy performer in 2018. Tax cuts and higher federal spending supported demand at a time when unemployment was already low. Consumption was robust, business investment was initially strong, and shale energy expanded rapidly. This made the US both a growth engine and a source of global tightening through higher rates and a stronger dollar.

Policy was the defining macro story. The Fed tightened four times and continued balance-sheet normalization, while fiscal policy turned expansionary late in the cycle. Tariffs became a major uncertainty for importers, exporters, retailers, and manufacturers. The US economy did not experience a crisis in 2018, but the fourth-quarter equity correction showed that valuations and credit conditions were sensitive to the combination of monetary tightening and trade uncertainty.

Micro/sectoral highlights: technology platforms, cloud computing, software, healthcare, and shale oil were leading sectors. Housing cooled as mortgage rates rose. Retail continued its shift toward e-commerce. Banks were profitable and well capitalized, but leveraged loans and corporate debt drew increasing concern. The Tax Cuts and Jobs Act encouraged repatriation of foreign earnings and share buybacks, affecting capital flows and equity markets.

Automobile sales weakened, property remained a key macro stabilizer and risk, and local-government financing continued to be a data-confidence challenge.

Japan remained a low-growth, low-inflation economy despite very tight employment. The Bank of Japan kept extraordinary monetary accommodation in place, using negative short-term rates and yield-curve control. Domestic demand was modest, exports slowed with China and electronics, and natural disasters disrupted activity.

Structural constraints were more important than cyclical weakness. Aging reduced labor supply, while productivity improvements, female labor-force participation, and automation partially offset demographic pressure. Fiscal policy remained constrained by high public debt and the planned 2019 consumption-tax increase.

Micro/sectoral highlights: autos, robotics, machine tools, electronics, and convenience retail remained globally significant. Banks faced pressure from very low yields. Real estate in major urban centers was supported by low rates and tourism, but population decline shaped regional property markets. Germany was the clearest advanced-economy example of the 2018 manufacturing slowdown. Growth decelerated sharply as auto production was disrupted by new emissions-testing procedures, diesel-related uncertainty, softer China demand, and weaker export orders. The labor market remained strong and fiscal policy was conservative, but the industrial cycle turned.

Germany's high current-account surplus remained a point of international debate. Supporters viewed it as the result of competitiveness and savings behavior; critics argued that it reflected underinvestment and weak domestic demand. In 2018 the vulnerability of export dependence became more visible.

Micro/sectoral highlights: autos, machinery, chemicals, and capital goods were the core sectors under pressure. Renewable energy and grid transition remained important but complex. #### 5B. Corporate landscape

The largest global companies by revenue in the 2018 Fortune Global 500 were still dominated by retail, energy, autos, and diversified conglomerates. Walmart ranked first, followed by State Grid, Sinopec, China National Petroleum, Royal Dutch Shell, Toyota, Volkswagen, BP, Exxon Mobil, and Berkshire Hathaway. This distribution shows that revenue scale still came from mass retail, state-linked infrastructure, hydrocarbons, and autos, even though equity-market valuation increasingly favored technology platforms.

By market capitalization, technology and platform companies were central. Apple briefly crossed a USD 1 trillion market value in 2018 before falling sharply; Microsoft ended the year as one of the world's most valuable listed firms; Amazon, Alphabet, Facebook, Tencent, and Alibaba remained major valuation leaders despite late-year declines. This split between revenue leaders and market-cap leaders is important: the revenue economy was still energy, autos, and retail, while the valuation economy was software, platforms, cloud, and data.

Major corporate events included Walmart's acquisition of Flipkart, Disney's agreement to acquire major assets of 21st Century Fox, Broadcom's failed attempt to acquire Qualcomm after US national-security intervention, and continued consolidation in telecom, media, and pharmaceuticals. IPO activity was strong in Asia but volatile as equity markets weakened. Bankruptcies and restructurings were most visible in retail, overleveraged emerging-market corporates, and firms exposed to old-economy disruption.

#### 5C. Innovation & technology

2018 was a milestone year for digital diffusion. ITU estimated that 51.2 percent of the world population, or 3.9 billion people, were using the internet by year-end. Active mobile broadband continued to expand, allowing platform businesses to scale in emerging markets. The economic effect was visible in payments, e-commerce, logistics, advertising, and cloud infrastructure.

Global semiconductor sales reached a record level in 2018, helped by memory, data centers, smartphones, industrial electronics, and cloud infrastructure. Yet this was also close to the top of the memory cycle. The later downturn in 2019 was visible in late-year equity pricing and order caution. Smartphone shipments declined, signaling saturation in mature markets and slower replacement cycles.

Patent activity continued shifting toward Asia. China received about 1.54 million patent applications in 2018, nearly half of the global total by filings. Filing volume is not the same as patent quality or productivity, but the direction showed the scale of China's innovation system. Korea, Japan, the United States, and Europe remained critical in high-value technology, semiconductors, pharmaceuticals, software, and advanced manufacturing.

#### 5D. Labor markets & human capital

The global labor market looked healthy in aggregate but uneven in quality. Advanced economies had falling unemployment, but wage gains were still moderate in many countries. Emerging economies faced informality, youth unemployment, and skill mismatch. Labor-force growth was slowing in aging economies and remained high in South Asia, Southeast Asia, and parts of Africa.

Sectoral employment shifted toward services, health, education, logistics, and digital work. Manufacturing employment was increasingly shaped by automation, supply chains, and China-centered trade. Unions and labor movements remained important in Europe and parts of Latin America, while gig work and platform-mediated employment raised new regulatory questions.

Migration affected labor supply and politics. Gulf economies depended on expatriate labor while trying to increase citizen employment. Europe faced continuing political tension around migration. The United States benefited from high-skilled immigration in technology but tightened immigration politics. Japan cautiously expanded selected foreign-worker channels because of demographic pressure.

#### 6B. Fixed income

US Treasury yields rose through much of 2018 and the yield curve flattened. The 10-year Treasury yield peaked above 3 percent before falling late in the year as risk aversion increased. German Bund yields remained low because euro-area inflation and growth disappointed. Japanese government bond yields stayed anchored by the BOJ's yield-curve control. Italy was the main advanced-economy sovereign-spread story after the budget dispute with the EU. Emerging-market debt came under pressure in countries with external deficits and credibility problems.

Corporate credit spreads widened late in the year. US leveraged loans and BBB-rated corporate debt drew attention as potential vulnerabilities. Bank capital positions were generally stronger than before 2008, but non-bank credit channels had grown.

#### 6C. Currencies

The US dollar strengthened broadly as US rates rose and growth outperformed. The euro weakened after early-year strength. Sterling was dominated by Brexit headlines. The yen served as a risk-off currency at times, while the Swiss franc remained managed through negative-rate policy and intervention risk.

Emerging-market currencies diverged. Turkey and Argentina had crisis-level depreciations. The Indian rupee, Indonesian rupiah, Brazilian real, South African rand, and Mexican peso all faced pressure at points, but policy responses and external positions differed. China's renminbi depreciation was important because it interacted with tariff expectations and capital-flow management.

#### 6D. Alternative assets

Gold was relatively subdued for much of the year but gained defensive interest during the fourth-quarter equity selloff. Real estate was highly local. US housing cooled as mortgage rates rose; Canada and Australia saw housing-price pressure; parts of Europe had affordability strains; Chinese property remained a policy-managed cycle; and Gulf real estate was uneven. Cryptoassets collapsed from the 2017 bubble peak, with Bitcoin and related tokens losing most of their speculative market value during 2018. That collapse was financially contained but important for the history of digital-asset cycles.

### 7. RISKS, CRISES & BLACK SWANS

Active crises included the Turkish currency crisis, the Argentine balance-of-payments crisis, the Italian sovereign-spread scare, the late-year global equity correction, and multiple trade-war escalations. These were not isolated events. They shared a common backdrop of tighter dollar funding, high leverage in selected sectors, and declining confidence in frictionless globalization.

Near-misses included a broader emerging-market sudden stop, a disorderly Brexit before year-end, a sharper China hard landing, and an oil-price spike after renewed Iran sanctions. These did not fully materialize in 2018, but they shaped policy and investment behavior.

Systemic vulnerabilities building in hindsight included high US corporate leverage, weak European bank profitability, China's property and local-government debt exposure, geopolitical fragmentation of supply chains, and the dependence of many economies on very low real interest rates. Hindsight also shows that 2018 was a warning year for global policy coordination: the rules-based trade system was still functioning, but its political support had weakened.

Policy mistakes visible in retrospect are debated. Some economists argued that the Fed tightened too far in 2018 given global fragility; others argued that tightening was justified by US labor-market strength and fiscal stimulus. Some argued China should have eased earlier; others saw deleveraging as necessary after years of credit excess. Italy's budget confrontation and Turkey's delayed monetary tightening were clearer cases where credibility costs were high.

### 8. YEAR-OVER-YEAR DELTA (2018 vs. 2017)

The biggest delta from 2017 was the loss of trade momentum. 2017 looked like a return to pre-crisis-style synchronized trade expansion; 2018 showed that the rebound was vulnerable to tariffs, China slowdown, and tighter financial conditions. World merchandise trade growth fell from 4.6 percent to 3.0 percent.

Financial conditions tightened. In 2017, global equities rose broadly and volatility was low. In 2018, volatility returned, US rates rose, the dollar strengthened, and emerging markets split between resilient economies and crisis economies. The fourth quarter was the clearest break from the prior year's optimism.

Policy regimes shifted. The Fed moved deeper into normalization; the ECB ended net asset purchases; the BOJ remained committed to yield-curve control; and China moved from deleveraging toward stabilization. Fiscal policy diverged: the United States stimulated late in the cycle, Germany and the Netherlands ran surpluses, Italy challenged euro-area fiscal rules, and Brazil debated pension reform.

Economic power continued shifting toward Asia, but not smoothly. China remained the second-largest economy and innovation filings surged, while India remained among the fastest-growing large economies. Yet China's slowdown, Korea's semiconductor-cycle exposure, and Japan's demographic constraints showed that Asia's rise was not a simple straight-line story.

New risks emerged or intensified: tariff escalation, supply-chain fragmentation, Turkey-style currency crises, European populist fiscal conflict, and household-debt sensitivity in Canada and Australia. Some 2017 risks resolved partially: USMCA reduced North American trade uncertainty, Brazil's election clarified the policy direction, and Spain's recovery continued despite political fragmentation.

### 9. DATA CONFIDENCE & GAPS

Data confidence is highest for advanced economies with mature statistical systems: the United States, Canada, Japan, Germany, France, the United Kingdom, Italy, Spain, the Netherlands, Switzerland, Australia, and Korea. Even there, fiscal and debt comparisons require care because general government, central government, gross debt, and net debt differ.

Data confidence is moderate for China, India, Indonesia, Brazil, Mexico, Russia, Saudi Arabia, and Turkey. The main issues are not absence of data but comparability, revisions, and hidden balance-sheet risks. China has official GDP and fiscal data, but local-government financing, augmented fiscal deficits, property exposure, and state-sector credit are harder to measure. India has debated labor-market and national-account comparability issues. Saudi unemployment is not meaningfully comparable without separating Saudi nationals, expatriates, gender, and participation. Russia's macro data are generally available, but sanctions, state-sector activity, and defense-related detail limit transparency. Turkey's 2018 data are available, but high inflation and exchange-rate volatility make current-dollar comparisons unstable.

Unemployment is the weakest cross-country indicator in this report. Definitions differ across informal economies, advanced welfare states, Gulf labor markets, and countries with large discouraged-worker or underemployment issues. Saudi Arabia is specifically flagged as not directly comparable. India's unemployment data are also flagged because official survey timing and labor-force definitions were contested.

GDP rankings are reliable for the broad top-20 set but not precise to the last dollar. World Bank WDI and IMF WEO data are cross-referenced, but current-dollar GDP rankings are sensitive to exchange rates and revisions. The countries near each other in rank should be interpreted as clusters rather than exact economic hierarchy. Canada, Korea, and Russia were close; the United Kingdom, France, and India were also close. Turkey's rank was especially exchange-rate sensitive.

Commodity, equity, and corporate data are generally reliable for headline values, but annual averages and end-year levels must not be mixed without labeling. Oil prices in this report use annual averages and selected peak/trough values. Equity returns are local-currency price-index approximations and may differ from total-return indexes. Corporate rankings by revenue differ from market-cap rankings because they measure different economic concepts.

Important gaps include comparable household debt by country, consistent small-business distress indicators, informal-sector income changes, detailed supply-chain relocation data, non-bank financial-sector exposures, and comparable real-estate valuations across major cities. Historical data conflict most often in fiscal balances, debt, unemployment, and GDP revisions. Where values were not verifiable at the level needed for a table entry, this report uses "Data not available" or a qualitative direction rather than inventing precision.
