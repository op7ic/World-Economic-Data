# MACRO/MICRO GLOBAL ECONOMIC ASSESSMENT - YEAR: 1920

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

1920 was the hinge year between the First World War economy and the unstable interwar economy. The world had not returned to a coherent gold-standard order, fiscal systems were still carrying war debts, and several states were either newly created, territorially revised, or in civil conflict. In the Maddison Project Database 2023, the global regional aggregate for 1920 is about **4.825 trillion 2011 international dollars** and world population is about **1.935 billion**. This should be read as a PPP reconstruction, not a current-dollar national-account estimate.

The dominant macroeconomic force was the turn from postwar inflation and restocking toward deflationary pressure and recession. The United States, already the largest economy by a very wide margin, tightened monetary policy sharply in 1920. NBER dates the U.S. recession peak to January 1920 and the trough to July 1921. The U.S. downturn mattered globally because the country had emerged as the major creditor economy and because U.S. credit, commodity demand, and trade conditions affected exporters in Canada, Latin America, Europe, and Asia.

Europe’s story was reconstruction under debt and monetary disorder. France and Belgium rebounded from wartime damage, but with high public debt and external financing needs. Germany recovered from the immediate postwar trough but faced unstable money, reparations uncertainty, and territorial disruption. Britain remained a financial power but was weaker in relative industrial terms and had not yet returned to gold. New states such as Poland and Czechoslovakia had to construct currencies, tax systems, and administrative capacity while absorbing the legacy of empire.

Microeconomically, 1920 was not only a macro slump. It was a sectoral sorting process. Agriculture and commodity producers moved from wartime scarcity pricing toward a price collapse. Japanese banks and trading firms were hit by a postwar financial panic. U.S. mass-production industries, petroleum, electrical equipment, and automobiles remained structurally important even as the business cycle turned down. The year therefore marks both a cyclical break and a structural reallocation toward the sectors that would define the 1920s.

## 2. KEY THEMES OF 1920

### Theme 1: Postwar inflation turned into deflationary recession

The year began with many economies still feeling the price effects of wartime finance, supply bottlenecks, and restocking demand. By the end of 1920, the policy and market direction had shifted toward contraction. The United States is the best documented case: Federal Reserve tightening in 1920 coincided with a recession that NBER dates from January 1920 to July 1921. Countries most affected included the United States, Canada, Japan, and commodity-exporting economies. Forward linkage: this adjustment created the agricultural depression and credit stress that persisted into 1921 and shaped debates about central bank responsibility.

### Theme 2: Reconstruction and state balance sheets dominated Europe

France, Belgium, Germany, Italy, and the United Kingdom all faced the fiscal legacy of war, but their positions differed. France and Belgium had physical reconstruction needs. Britain had a large debt overhang and a policy debate about returning to monetary orthodoxy. Germany had reparations uncertainty and weak fiscal legitimacy. Italy had debt, inflation, and social conflict. Forward linkage: the same debt, currency, and reparations problems fed the European stabilization crises of the early 1920s.

### Theme 3: The old gold standard had not been restored

The world of 1920 was not Bretton Woods and not the classical pre-1914 gold standard. The United States had restored gold convertibility in 1919, but Britain returned only in 1925 and Japan delayed until 1930. Many currencies floated, were controlled, or were subject to severe instability. Reserve-currency dynamics were therefore transitional: sterling still mattered as a trade and financial currency, but the dollar had gained importance through U.S. creditor status. Forward linkage: attempts to restore gold at old parities later transmitted deflationary pressure across countries.

### Theme 4: New borders and successor states changed economic geography

Poland and Czechoslovakia appear in the top 20 GDP list, but their 1920 data are not comparable to long-established national accounts. These countries were integrating territories that had belonged to different empires with different currencies, railways, laws, tax systems, and industrial structures. Germany, Austria, Hungary, and Russia lost or reconfigured economic space. Forward linkage: border changes altered trade routes, minority politics, industrial ownership, and state capacity throughout the 1920s.

### Theme 5: Commodity producers moved from boom to vulnerability

Wartime demand had lifted prices for oil, metals, food, wool, and other raw materials. In 1920, some spot prices remained high, but the direction of travel changed. U.S. crude oil first purchase prices were $3.07 per barrel in 1920, up from $2.01 in 1919, but the commodity-price bust was already forming. Agriculture was especially vulnerable because many farmers had borrowed and expanded during wartime demand. Forward linkage: rural distress became a persistent 1920s problem even where urban industrial economies later recovered.

### Theme 6: Finance moved from wartime allocation to crisis management

Banks, bond markets, and central banks had to move from wartime finance to peacetime discipline. Japan’s 1920 financial panic illustrates how wartime expansion created leveraged balance sheets that could fail when expectations turned. In Europe, governments needed to refinance debt while stabilizing currencies. In the United States, regional farm credit stress complicated national tightening. Forward linkage: the decade produced new forms of central bank coordination and statistical work under the League of Nations, although the Bank for International Settlements would not be founded until 1930.

**Key commodity prices.** U.S. crude oil first purchase price was **$3.07 per barrel** in 1920. Comparable annual world prices for copper, wheat, cotton, and other staples are not all available in one harmonized source; USGS notes that copper prices moved back toward prewar levels by late 1920.

**International capital flow direction.** The United States was the central creditor economy. Europe needed reconstruction finance, debt rollover, and reparations-related settlement. Latin American and dominion economies depended on commodity receipts and foreign capital. Soviet Russia was largely isolated from normal capital markets.

### 3B. Monetary & Financial Conditions

The monetary environment was restrictive where inflation-fighting credibility dominated policy. The Federal Reserve’s 1920 tightening is the most important case. U.S. short-rate proxies in JST are high for the period, and NBER dates the recession as beginning after the January 1920 peak. Britain also maintained high rates as policymakers looked toward eventual monetary normalization. Japan’s short-rate proxy was even higher, coinciding with the postwar collapse and banking stress.

The exchange-rate system was unsettled. The U.S. dollar was linked to gold; Britain was not yet back at prewar gold convertibility; Japan postponed restoration of gold; and several European currencies were unstable. Germany’s mark data for 1920 are not easily comparable with later hyperinflation-era measures, so harmonized exchange-rate entries are left unavailable where the source convention is unclear.

Credit conditions tightened sharply in the United States and became more fragile in Japan. European credit conditions were shaped by public debt, reconstruction, and currency weakness. Bond markets absorbed large war-debt overhangs. ### 3C. Trade & Geopolitics

The Treaty of Versailles entered into force in January 1920, and the League of Nations began operating in the same year. Reparations, war debts, mandates, and border revisions were therefore not side issues; they shaped trade routes, fiscal expectations, and currency credibility. Germany’s final reparations schedule was not yet fixed, which created uncertainty rather than settlement.

Regional integration in the modern sense was limited. The European Economic Community, ASEAN, and Bretton Woods institutions did not exist. The relevant institutions were empires, customs systems, reparations bodies, the League of Nations, and national central banks. Trade disputes and protectionist pressures were rising, although the dramatic tariff escalation associated with the 1930s was still in the future.

Cold War dynamics did not yet exist in the post-1945 sense, but the Russian Civil War and the international isolation of Bolshevik Russia had major economic effects. Poland’s war with Soviet Russia shaped Eastern European trade and fiscal conditions. Colonial systems continued to channel production and trade in India, Indonesia, Africa, and parts of the Middle East.

### 3D. Commodities & Energy

Oil was becoming a strategic industrial commodity rather than only a lighting or niche transport fuel. The United States remained a dominant producer and consumer, while Mexico was an important oil economy. The 1920 U.S. first purchase price of $3.07 per barrel captures the postwar price peak before the subsequent decline.

Coal was still central to European industry, shipping, railways, and household heating. Coal disruptions and labor disputes mattered particularly for Britain, Germany, France, Belgium, and Czechoslovakia. Metals such as copper and steel reflected the transition from war production to peacetime demand. Agricultural staples were entering a downturn as European production recovered, wartime procurement ended, and credit tightened.

### 3E. Labor & Demographics

Labor markets were shaped by demobilization, urbanization, migration disruption, and labor militancy. Soldiers returned to civilian economies while factories converted from war to peace. Comparable unemployment rates across countries are unreliable because definitions, registration systems, and coverage differed sharply.

The global population was about 1.935 billion in the MPD regional estimate. India, China, the former Russian Empire, and Europe formed the main demographic centers. Urbanization continued, but most people in the world still worked in agriculture or rural informal activities. The main labor-market shift was demobilization combined with industrial reconversion. Workers moved out of armies and war industries, while firms reduced inventories and adjusted to peacetime demand. ### 6C. Currencies

1920 currency markets were transitional. The dollar’s gold link gave it relative strength. Sterling was below its prewar parity and would not return to gold until 1925. Japan postponed gold restoration. Germany’s mark was under severe pressure, and later hyperinflation makes simple comparisons hazardous. France and Italy also faced currency weakness tied to debt and reconstruction finance.

Capital controls were common in practice, but a harmonized global inventory of 1920 controls is **Data not available**. Reserve-currency composition statistics in the modern IMF COFER sense did not exist. The best description is qualitative: sterling remained institutionally important, the dollar had risen materially, and gold-standard restoration was incomplete.

Real estate data for major cities are **Data not available** in a harmonized global source. Farmland values in North America and other commodity regions were vulnerable because wartime agricultural expectations had encouraged borrowing and expansion.

Other alternative assets included inventories, shipping capacity, livestock, and commodity stocks. In 1920, these were economically important because many firms and farmers were caught with high-cost inventories as prices turned down.

## 7. RISKS, CRISES & BLACK SWANS

The largest active crisis was the 1920 to 1921 downturn, already underway in the United States and spreading through commodity and credit channels. At the time, policymakers could see inflation, tight money, and falling demand; with hindsight, the systemic importance of agricultural debt and regional banking pressure is clearer.

Japan’s 1920 postwar financial panic was a major national financial event. It showed that wartime boom profits had encouraged leverage and weak credit practices. The Bank of Japan extended special loans, but the system remained fragile through the decade. With hindsight, the 1920 panic was an early signal of the banking weaknesses that later produced larger crises.

Germany’s risk was fiscal-monetary instability. Hyperinflation had not yet reached its 1923 peak, so describing 1920 as full hyperinflation would be inaccurate. The visible problem was a weaker fiscal base, reparations uncertainty, and reliance on money creation and debt instruments. The tail risk was a currency collapse, which later materialized.

Eastern Europe faced border, currency, and war risks. Poland’s conflict with Soviet Russia was economically central. The former Russian Empire faced civil war, war communism, transport breakdown, and food shortages. These were not ordinary business-cycle risks; they were state-capacity and survival risks.

Agricultural depression was the underappreciated global vulnerability. Farmers in the United States, Canada, Australia, Argentina, India, and parts of Europe were exposed to falling prices and debt. This risk materialized over the following years and left rural economies weaker even where urban industry recovered.

## 8. YEAR-OVER-YEAR DELTA (1920 vs. 1919)

The clearest delta was the turn from postwar boom to contraction. In 1919, many economies were still restocking, demobilizing, and absorbing wartime inflation. In 1920, credit tightening, price reversals, and inventory liquidation became dominant. The United States moved from postwar expansion to recession. Japan moved from wartime boom to panic. Britain and several European economies faced weak exports and fiscal strain.

The balance of economic power continued shifting toward the United States. This was not a sudden 1920 event, but the year made the shift more visible: U.S. credit conditions affected the world, and the dollar’s relative role grew. Britain still had financial institutions and empire, but its industrial and debt position had weakened.

Europe’s reconstruction became more concrete but also more fiscally constrained. France and Belgium rebounded from depressed wartime and immediate postwar levels. Germany’s recovery was politically and monetarily fragile. Italy’s economic strains increasingly translated into social and political conflict.

New state formation became an economic issue, not merely a diplomatic one. Poland and Czechoslovakia had to construct national economic systems. Their appearance in the top 20 is partly a real economic fact and partly a measurement problem created by the breakup of empires.

Commodity risk shifted from scarcity to surplus and debt stress. Oil still showed high 1920 prices, but the broader rural and raw-material cycle was turning. This year-over-year delta explains why some economies with superficially strong 1920 GDP still carried serious forward risks.

## 9. DATA CONFIDENCE & GAPS

**High-confidence relative ranking:** The United States, United Kingdom, Germany, France, Japan, Italy, Canada, Australia, Netherlands, Belgium, Switzerland, and Spain have relatively better historical series, especially when MPD and JST overlap. Even for these, 1920 GDP is reconstructed and PPP-adjusted, not a modern national-account observation.

**Medium-confidence data:** Argentina, Brazil, Mexico, India, and Indonesia have credible long-run estimates in MPD, but contemporary statistical systems were less complete, and colonial or commodity-economy structures complicate interpretation. GDP levels are useful for scale, but CPI, unemployment, fiscal balance, and current-account data are often unavailable or not harmonized.

**Low-confidence data:** China, the former USSR, Poland, and Czechoslovakia are the weakest entries. China is included by a regional residual estimate rather than a direct country GDPpc cell. The former USSR was in civil war and war communism. Poland and Czechoslovakia were new states integrating different imperial territories. GDP figures are estimates with large uncertainty. Growth rates for China, Poland, Czechoslovakia, and the former USSR are **Data not available** in the MPD continuity used here.

**Conflicting historical data:** GDP rankings can differ depending on whether the source uses 1990 international dollars, 2011 international dollars, territorial boundaries, colonial treatment, or national series. Maddison’s older tables and MPD 2023 are broadly consistent for the dominance of the United States and the large positions of Britain, India, Germany, France, Japan, and Russia/Soviet territories, but exact ranks and values can differ.

**Weakest claims in this document:** Global trade volume, global CPI, exact exchange rates for unstable currencies, equity index levels, corporate revenue rankings, and sectoral employment shares are weakly evidenced for 1920. They are therefore either described qualitatively or marked **Data not available**.

**Important missing data:** Harmonized 1920 unemployment rates, current-account balances, fiscal balances, CPI inflation, stock-index returns, corporate rankings, household balance sheets, and real-estate prices do not exist for all top 20 economies in a reliable modern format. This document avoids filling those gaps with false precision.
