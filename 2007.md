# MACRO/MICRO GLOBAL ECONOMIC ASSESSMENT - YEAR: 2007

## Scope and data basis

This reference note assesses calendar year 2007, with top-economy rankings based on nominal GDP in current US dollars at market exchange rates. The main ranking basis is IMF World Economic Outlook country GDP in current US dollars, cross-checked against World Bank World Development Indicators and UN national accounts series where available. PPP rankings are deliberately not used for the top-20 list because the requested ranking is by GDP for that year, and market-dollar nominal rankings capture the international financial weight most visible in 2007 capital markets. Values are rounded, and later statistical revisions can shift exact amounts and border cases. The country indicator tables are not meant to imply perfect comparability, especially for unemployment, fiscal balance and government debt, where definitions vary.

### 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

2007 was the last full year of the long pre-crisis expansion and the first year of the global financial crisis. The first half still looked like the high-growth world of the mid-2000s: world output expanded at roughly 5 percent, emerging Asia and commodity exporters grew rapidly, global trade volumes rose, corporate profits remained strong and major equity markets outside Japan often reached record highs. By late summer, however, US subprime mortgage losses had migrated into structured credit, interbank funding and bank balance sheets. The result was not yet the full 2008 collapse, but the direction of the cycle had changed.

The dominant macro tension was the coexistence of strong real activity and deteriorating financial plumbing. The IMF still described world growth as solid, with China, India, Russia, Brazil and many oil exporters growing well above advanced-economy rates. At the same time, BIS and central-bank accounts show that risk premia, bank funding costs and asset-backed securities markets deteriorated sharply from August. This was a classic late-cycle year: leverage, maturity transformation and confidence were still high enough to support output, but not high enough to survive a sustained fall in housing collateral and structured-credit liquidity.

The year also marked a visible shift in economic power. China overtook Germany in the IMF nominal GDP ranking used here, becoming the third-largest economy by market-dollar GDP. Brazil and Russia rose with commodities, India crossed into the trillion-dollar group, and South Korea remained slightly ahead of India in the IMF nominal series. The United States was still by far the largest economy, but its growth slowed, housing investment contracted and the Federal Reserve cut rates after September. Europe appeared resilient early in the year, but Northern Rock in the United Kingdom and stresses in euro-area money markets showed that the credit cycle was global.

Inflation risks were also building. Oil prices moved higher through the year, with WTI and Brent ending 2007 around the low-90-dollar range per barrel after much lower prices at the start of the year. Food and agricultural prices rose sharply, pushed by poor harvests, low stocks, energy-linked input costs, biofuel demand and rising Asian consumption. This mattered because the initial 2007 financial shock was disinflationary in credit-sensitive sectors, while commodities were inflationary for households, especially in emerging and lower-income economies.

With hindsight, 2007 was the hinge between the Great Moderation and the Great Recession. At the time, many policymakers still expected turbulence to be containable. In retrospect, the year exposed systemic vulnerabilities: originate-to-distribute mortgage credit, thinly capitalized structured investment vehicles, excessive reliance on wholesale funding, procyclical ratings, housing bubbles in several advanced economies, and large global imbalances. The microeconomic mechanisms were concrete: households rolled over mortgages less easily, mortgage lenders failed, banks hoarded liquidity, construction slowed, securitization froze and confidence weakened.

### 2. KEY THEMES OF 2007

#### 1. Financial turbulence moved from subprime to systemic liquidity risk

The defining economic development of 2007 was the transition from a US mortgage credit problem into a global funding-market problem. The immediate micro triggers included rising delinquencies in US subprime mortgages, the April bankruptcy of New Century Financial, rating downgrades of mortgage-backed securities and the August suspension of redemptions or net-asset-value calculations by BNP Paribas funds exposed to asset-backed securities. By September, Northern Rock required Bank of England liquidity support because its business model depended heavily on wholesale funding.

The significance in economic history is that 2007 revealed that modern banking risk had moved outside traditional deposit-funded banking. Structured investment vehicles, asset-backed commercial paper, collateralized debt obligations, repo markets and wholesale funding were central. The most affected economies were the United States, United Kingdom, Germany, France, Switzerland and other banking centers, but the shock affected global dollar funding and confidence. The forward link was direct: the liquidity crisis of 2007 became the solvency and capital crisis of 2008.

#### 2. Emerging markets decoupled briefly, but not permanently

Emerging economies were still expanding rapidly in 2007. China grew at double-digit rates, India remained fast-growing, Russia and Brazil benefited from commodities and capital inflows, and many Asian exporters saw strong demand. Equity markets in China, India, Brazil and Korea rose sharply. For part of the year, investors treated emerging markets as less exposed to US housing.

The historical significance is that 2007 was the peak of the early-2000s emerging-market boom before the global crisis tested the idea of decoupling. Countries most affected positively in 2007 were China, India, Russia, Brazil, Turkey and resource exporters. The later linkage was that emerging markets did not cause the crisis, but in 2008 they absorbed it through trade, commodity prices, capital-flow reversals and exchange-rate pressure.

#### 3. China entered the top tier of nominal GDP economies

China's nominal GDP in current US dollars rose above Germany's in the IMF ranking used here. This was driven by very fast real growth, renminbi appreciation, industrial expansion, export strength and urban investment. The shift mattered because nominal dollar GDP is a proxy for market size, corporate revenue opportunity, financial weight and geopolitical bargaining power.

The countries most affected were China, Germany, Japan, the United States and resource suppliers that fed Chinese demand. The forward linkage was the 2010s reorientation of global supply chains, commodity markets and corporate strategy around China. In 2007, however, China's model was still highly investment- and export-intensive, with a very large current-account surplus and rising inflation pressure.

#### 4. Commodity inflation and food-price stress intensified

Oil, metals and food prices rose sharply. Oil climbed through 2007, gold benefited from dollar weakness and safe-haven demand, copper remained historically high, and grains rose because of weather shocks, low stocks, rising feed demand and biofuel-related demand. The FAO and World Bank documented the food-price swing that accelerated from 2007 into 2008.

This mattered because commodity inflation redistributed income from consumers to producers and from importers to exporters. Oil exporters, Russia, Brazil, Australia, Canada and Middle Eastern producers benefited, while food and fuel importers faced household pressure. The forward link was the 2008 peak in oil and food prices, followed by collapse as the crisis hit demand.

#### 5. Central banks faced a split mandate problem

Before August, the policy environment was still concerned with inflation and overheating. The ECB raised its main refinancing rate to 4 percent in June, the Bank of England increased Bank Rate to 5.75 percent in July, the Bank of Japan kept a cautious normalization stance after a February increase to 0.5 percent, and the Reserve Bank of Australia raised the cash rate. After August, the Federal Reserve cut rates from 5.25 percent to 4.25 percent by December and several central banks supplied liquidity.

The significance is that 2007 exposed the difference between price stability and financial stability. Inflation was not collapsing, but money markets were stressed. The countries most affected were advanced economies with large financial systems, especially the United States, United Kingdom and euro area. The forward link was the shift to unconventional monetary policy in 2008-2009.

#### 6. Housing and construction turned from growth engines into vulnerabilities

Housing was already weakening in the United States by early 2007, but the issue broadened. Spain, the United Kingdom and parts of Europe had large housing and construction exposures. In the United States, residential investment contracted and mortgage credit tightened. In Spain, construction still supported GDP growth in 2007, but the current-account deficit and property exposure pointed to a hard adjustment ahead.

The historical significance is that construction booms are powerful growth engines but can reverse through credit channels. Countries most affected were the United States, Spain, United Kingdom, Ireland, parts of Eastern Europe and later the Gulf. The forward link was severe employment and banking stress in construction-heavy economies during 2008-2012.

#### 7. Global imbalances persisted

The United States still ran a large current-account deficit, while China, Germany, Japan, oil exporters and some northern European economies ran surpluses. Capital flowed from surplus economies into dollar assets and global credit products. This supported low long-term yields and balance-sheet expansion, but it also made the system vulnerable when confidence in structured dollar assets fell.

The significance is debated. Some economists emphasize global savings and capital flows as a cause of low yields and risk-taking; others put more weight on domestic regulation, incentives and financial engineering. Both channels mattered in 2007. The forward link was a crisis response in which surplus countries remained central to reserve accumulation, while deficit countries adjusted through recession and currency movements.

The global expansion remained broad, but momentum changed after mid-year. The IMF still described growth as strong in the first half, while downgrading prospects because of financial turbulence. WTO trade data show a slowdown from 2006's unusually strong trade expansion. World trade did not collapse in 2007, but the turn was already visible.

Reserve currency dynamics remained dollar-centered. IMF COFER and ECB international-role materials show that the US dollar was still the leading reserve currency and the euro the second. The dollar weakened against the euro in 2007, and the euro's role in reserves and international debt issuance remained significant. The renminbi was not yet a reserve currency in the modern sense, but China's reserve accumulation and current-account surplus were central to the global monetary system.

The interest-rate environment was therefore not uniformly easy. Short rates were high in the United States and United Kingdom by post-2008 standards, euro rates had risen, and Japan remained a low-rate anchor for carry trades. As the credit shock spread, central banks separated liquidity provision from policy rates. They supplied liquidity to strained markets even where inflation concerns limited rate cuts.

Exchange rates reflected both fundamentals and risk appetite. The US dollar weakened against the euro; the Canadian dollar reached parity with the US dollar; sterling remained strong early but weakened as UK credit and housing risks rose; the yen remained low by some effective-exchange-rate measures, supporting carry trades until risk aversion triggered reversals.

Equity markets were bifurcated. Emerging-market and commodity-linked markets often delivered large gains. Japan's Nikkei fell. US equities ended the year up modestly after reaching highs in October. Bank and mortgage-finance shares performed poorly as credit concerns intensified. Government bond markets rallied in stressed periods, especially in the United States, as investors sought safety.

#### 3C. Trade & Geopolitics

The Doha Round remained unresolved. WTO trade continued to grow but with increasing tension around subsidies, industrial competitiveness and exchange-rate complaints. China's export boom generated political attention in the United States and Europe, especially around the renminbi, trade deficits and product-safety concerns. The United States still ran a large goods deficit with China.

Regional integration continued. The European Union enlarged to include Bulgaria and Romania on 1 January 2007, increasing labor mobility and deepening the eastern expansion of the single market. ASEAN economic integration advanced toward the ASEAN Economic Community goals, while intra-Asian supply chains continued to thicken around China. NAFTA remained central to North American trade, but Mexico's growth slowed with US manufacturing and housing.

Geopolitical risks affected energy and capital flows. The Iraq war, Iran-related sanctions concerns, Russian energy politics, Venezuela's oil-sector policy and Middle East uncertainty all contributed to risk premia in energy markets. Russia's economy benefited from energy income while state influence over strategic sectors remained high.

#### 3D. Commodities & Energy

Energy was one of 2007's clearest inflation channels. EIA spot data show WTI prices rising from the mid-50s per barrel early in the year to the 90s late in the year. Brent also rose sharply. OPEC remained important, but demand growth from emerging markets, geopolitical risk, low spare capacity perceptions and financial flows into commodities all mattered.

Metals remained supported by China, infrastructure demand and supply constraints. Copper prices were lower than the 2006 spike at times but still historically elevated. Iron ore, coal and bulk commodities supported Australia and Brazil. Gold benefited from financial stress, dollar weakness and inflation worries.

Agricultural commodities became a macro issue rather than just a farm-sector issue. Wheat prices jumped in 2007 as poor weather hit crops in several exporting regions. Maize and soy markets were tied to feed, biofuel and land-use shifts. Food-price pressure carried into 2008 and contributed to social and fiscal stress in lower-income import-dependent economies.

#### 3E. Labor & Demographics

Global labor markets were strong relative to the post-crisis years. ILO estimates put global unemployment near 6 percent in 2007, with youth unemployment much higher than adult unemployment and with regional unemployment especially high in parts of the Middle East and North Africa. The labor-market picture was uneven: advanced economies had low or falling unemployment, while many emerging economies had large informal sectors and underemployment not captured by headline unemployment.

Migration had significant economic effects. EU enlargement increased east-west labor flows, especially to the United Kingdom, Ireland and other open labor markets. Remittances remained important for Mexico, India, the Philippines, Turkey and many smaller economies. Urbanization in China and India continued to shift labor from agriculture toward construction, manufacturing and services.

Wage growth lagged productivity in some advanced economies, while emerging-market wages rose from low levels in tradable sectors. Household debt, not wages, carried much of the consumption cycle in the United States, United Kingdom, Spain, Australia and parts of Europe. That made household balance sheets sensitive to house prices and credit access.

The US economy slowed as the housing downturn deepened. Residential investment fell, mortgage delinquencies rose and securitized mortgage markets began to fail. #### 5B. Corporate landscape

The world's largest corporations by revenue were dominated by retail, oil, autos and finance. Fortune's 2008 Global 500, which reflects 2007 results for many firms, placed Wal-Mart at the top and showed the scale of oil companies such as Exxon Mobil, Royal Dutch Shell, BP, Chevron, Total and ConocoPhillips. Toyota's rise reflected the globalization of auto production and the strength of Japanese manufacturing before the crisis.

M&A activity reached extremely high levels in 2007, with global announced volumes reported around the USD 4 trillion range despite a second-half slowdown. Major deals included the ABN AMRO battle and sale to the RBS-led consortium, Rio Tinto's Alcan acquisition, Thomson's Reuters transaction announcement, Danone's Numico acquisition and private-equity deals that were increasingly difficult to finance after the credit-market turn.

IPOs reflected the late-cycle peak. Blackstone went public in June 2007, near the top of the private-equity cycle. In Europe, VTB Bank was one of the largest IPOs of the year. Alibaba.com listed in Hong Kong in a major internet offering, highlighting China's rising digital economy. These offerings illustrate how capital markets were still receptive before the full crisis but were already close to a regime change.

Failures were concentrated in credit-sensitive firms. New Century Financial's bankruptcy in April signaled the collapse of the US subprime origination model. Hedge funds and vehicles exposed to asset-backed securities struggled with valuation and liquidity. Northern Rock did not fail in 2007 in the ordinary bankruptcy sense, but it required official liquidity support and became the symbol of wholesale funding risk.

#### 5C. Innovation & technology

The iPhone launched in 2007, which was microeconomically important even though its macro effect was not yet visible. It marked the beginning of the smartphone platform era, with long-run implications for semiconductors, telecom networks, software, advertising, app ecosystems, retail and labor productivity. Cloud computing was still early, but Amazon Web Services and similar infrastructure models were beginning to reshape enterprise IT.

Emerging-market telecom was one of the largest productivity stories. Mobile penetration expanded quickly in India, China, Africa, Latin America and Eastern Europe. Lower communication costs improved market information, payments potential and service-sector productivity.

R&D remained concentrated in the United States, Japan, Germany, Korea, France, the United Kingdom, China and Switzerland. China was rapidly increasing R&D spending, though still transitioning from imitation and scale manufacturing toward higher-value innovation. Patent activity was rising in Asia.

Infrastructure megaprojects were concentrated in China, the Gulf, India and resource-exporting economies. Ports, rail, power, highways and urban transit were central to the 2007 investment cycle. Bottlenecks were a major constraint in India, Brazil and parts of commodity-producing economies.

#### 5D. Labor markets & human capital

Labor demand was strong in many economies, but skill mismatch was already visible. Commodity regions in Australia, Canada, Russia and Brazil needed engineers, miners, construction workers and logistics workers. China and India were moving millions into urban labor markets, while advanced economies faced aging populations and demand for higher-skill services.

Wage dynamics varied. In the United States, productivity and profit growth had not translated into broad wage acceleration, and households used credit to support consumption. In China, migrant labor and manufacturing wages were rising from low levels. In Europe, labor-market reforms in Germany improved employment but were linked to wage restraint. In Spain and Ireland, construction absorbed large amounts of labor, including migrants.

Unions and labor movements faced different pressures. In advanced economies, globalization and services weakened some traditional industrial unions. In emerging economies, labor organization was often informal or state-mediated. The global labor story was therefore not just employment growth, but a shift in bargaining power toward mobile capital, tradable supply chains and high-skill labor.

Government bond markets reacted to credit stress by lowering long yields in safer markets. US Treasuries rallied in the second half as investors priced slower growth and Fed cuts. Sovereign debt stress was not yet the main crisis theme in advanced economies. Spain, Ireland, Greece and Portugal still financed easily in 2007. With hindsight, low sovereign spreads in the euro area masked private-sector imbalances and banking risk that later migrated to public balance sheets.

#### 6C. Currencies

The US dollar weakened against the euro and the Canadian dollar. The euro rose as euro-area growth and interest-rate differentials supported it, while the United States cut rates after September. The Canadian dollar reached parity with the US dollar, supported by commodities and fiscal strength. The Australian dollar was strong on the commodity cycle and high rates. The yen remained weak for much of the year because of low rates and carry trades, although risk aversion periodically caused reversals.

China maintained a managed appreciation of the renminbi. The currency regime was central to US-China tensions, because China's current-account surplus and reserve accumulation remained very large. Many emerging markets received capital inflows and faced appreciation pressure until risk sentiment deteriorated.

#### 6D. Alternative assets

Gold rose as the dollar weakened and financial stress increased. Real estate was the crucial alternative asset class. US housing had already turned down; UK and Spain were late-cycle; Australian and Canadian housing remained firm; Chinese urban property was strong; Gulf and Russian real estate were buoyed by commodity income. Private equity peaked in activity and public visibility, but its funding model was impaired by the leveraged-loan market freeze.

### 7. RISKS, CRISES & BLACK SWANS

Active crises in 2007 included the US subprime mortgage crisis, the asset-backed commercial paper freeze, the BNP Paribas fund suspension, the Northern Rock liquidity support event and widening global credit spreads. These were not isolated accidents. They were connected through securitization, maturity mismatch, ratings dependence, mark-to-market uncertainty and leverage.

Near-misses included broader bank runs and disorderly failure of large cross-border banks. In 2007 these did not fully materialize, partly because central banks supplied liquidity and because many losses were not yet recognized. The year also featured significant commodity and food-price risks, which became more politically visible in 2008.

Hindsight-only systemic vulnerabilities: the depth of bank off-balance-sheet exposure, the fragility of monoline insurers, the weakness of structured-product ratings, the scale of US housing overvaluation, the vulnerability of euro-area periphery private credit booms, and the speed with which dollar funding stress could become global. These were visible to some specialists in 2007 but not fully accepted by consensus forecasts.

Policy mistakes visible in retrospect include overconfidence in market liquidity, underestimation of housing spillovers, reliance on microprudential rather than system-wide supervision, and delayed recognition of capital needs. However, it is too simplistic to say policymakers ignored all warning signs. The harder problem was that the system had many individually rational contracts that were collectively fragile.

### 8. YEAR-OVER-YEAR DELTA, 2007 vs. 2006

The most important change from 2006 was the shift from synchronized expansion to financial fragility. In 2006, the world economy was still characterized by strong trade, easy credit, low risk spreads and rapid housing or asset-price growth in many countries. In 2007, real growth remained strong, but market plumbing changed after August.

US housing moved from slowdown to systemic trigger. Subprime delinquencies rose, lenders failed and securities linked to mortgages became difficult to value. This transformed a sectoral correction into a global credit event. The Federal Reserve moved from a hold stance to rate cuts, while the ECB and Bank of England had to balance inflation concerns with financial stress.

China's relative weight increased. In nominal-dollar IMF data, China moved above Germany, while India crossed the trillion-dollar threshold and Brazil and Russia approached the middle of the top-10 group. Commodity exporters gained influence because oil, metals and agricultural prices rose. The shift in economic power was not only about GDP but also about reserve accumulation, trade bargaining and corporate strategy.

Trade growth decelerated from 2006. WTO figures show world merchandise trade volume growth slowed, although it remained positive. This mattered because the export channel had supported Germany, Japan, China, Korea and many smaller open economies. The 2008 trade collapse was not yet present, but the slowdown had begun.

Risks that emerged: wholesale funding risk, structured-credit valuation risk, food-price instability, global inflation from commodities, and the possibility that advanced-economy housing downturns would infect banks. Risks that seemed to recede: classic emerging-market sovereign debt crisis risk, at least temporarily, because many emerging economies had stronger reserves and fiscal positions than in the 1990s.

### 9. DATA CONFIDENCE & GAPS

The highest-confidence data in this document are nominal GDP, real GDP growth, population and GDP per capita for major market economies, because they are covered by World Bank WDI, IMF WEO, UN national accounts and national statistical offices. Still, exact GDP rankings can differ because of later revisions, treatment of territories, exchange-rate conversion and whether a source uses calendar-year or fiscal-year data. The South Korea-India rank is close in the IMF current-dollar series, and Belgium-Indonesia is a border case for rank 20.

Moderate-confidence data include inflation, current accounts and fiscal balances. They are generally available through IMF, World Bank and OECD sources, but comparability is imperfect. Fiscal balances can refer to central government, general government, cyclically adjusted government or public sector. Government debt can be gross or net and can include different layers of government.

Lower-confidence or less-comparable data include unemployment, policy rates in economies with changing monetary frameworks, and sectoral employment shares. Unemployment is particularly difficult across countries because of informal work, survey definitions, registration rules and discouraged-worker effects. China's registered urban unemployment rate is not comparable with modeled ILO unemployment. India's unemployment data for 2007 are not presented as a clean annual comparable figure in this document because informality and survey timing make a single number misleading.

Financial-market index returns are approximate because exact return depends on price return versus total return, local currency versus US dollar, and index closing-date convention. Commodity prices are more reliable when sourced from EIA, World Bank Pink Sheet, FAO and exchange data, but annual averages can differ across benchmark definitions.

Soviet bloc uncertainty rules are not central for 2007, because the Soviet Union no longer existed and most top-20 economies had modern statistical systems. However, Russia and China still require caution in interpreting state-sector liabilities, quasi-fiscal obligations and banking risks. Non-market or state-influenced sectors can make official debt and credit risk look smaller than the broader public-sector exposure.

Important data gaps: harmonized 2007 household debt across all top-20 economies; comparable bank off-balance-sheet exposures; complete structured-credit exposure by country; consistent current-dollar sector value added for every top-20 country; and full distributional data linking 2007 commodity inflation to household welfare.
