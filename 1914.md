# MACRO/MICRO GLOBAL ECONOMIC ASSESSMENT - YEAR: 1914

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

1914 was the year in which the late nineteenth-century global economy broke. In the first half of the year, the world still operated largely within the classical gold-standard order: sterling was the dominant international currency, London was the main clearing center, cross-border capital moved with few peacetime restrictions, and long-distance trade connected industrial Europe, North America, Latin America, and Asian colonial economies. By year-end, the same system was under emergency controls. Major exchanges had closed, gold convertibility was suspended or restricted in many belligerent countries, government war finance began to displace private capital markets, and trade routes were redirected by wartime risk and state priority.

The dominant shock was the outbreak of World War I after the July crisis. The shock was financial before it was fully industrial. Banks and investors tried to repatriate funds, gold moved toward perceived safe centers, sterling came under stress, and securities markets closed in London, New York, Paris, Berlin, and elsewhere. The newly created Federal Reserve System was not yet operating when the crisis began. The United States relied first on Treasury action, emergency currency arrangements, gold management, private clearing-house cooperation, and the closing of the New York Stock Exchange. The twelve Federal Reserve Banks opened only on November 16, 1914.

The real economy split quickly into three zones. First, belligerent Europe entered war mobilization, with civilian consumption squeezed and government demand rising. Germany, France, Austria-Hungary, Russia, Belgium, and parts of the Balkans faced direct military disruption, mobilization of labor, and transport bottlenecks. Second, the neutral and overseas economies experienced trade and financial shocks but also new demand for food, raw materials, shipping, credit, and later munitions. Third, colonial and semi-colonial economies were exposed through imperial procurement, shipping constraints, commodity price volatility, and the redirection of external trade.

Statistically, 1914 is difficult. Modern national accounts did not yet exist for many countries. GDP figures below are therefore historical reconstructions, mainly in 1990 international dollars from Maddison legacy series, cross-checked against the Maddison Project 2023 documentation and Broadberry-Harrison wartime estimates. For China, Russia, Austria-Hungary, the Ottoman sphere, and many developing economies, the uncertainty is high. Exact 1914 observations are missing in some historical series, so adjacent-year or index-adjusted estimates are explicitly labeled rather than hidden.

The most important structural change was the shift in economic power and financial leadership. London remained central in 1914, but the crisis exposed the fragility of sterling-centered finance when Britain became a belligerent. The United States was still formally neutral, but its combination of output scale, gold inflows, and creditor potential made it the natural financial backstop for the Entente over the next few years. In hindsight, 1914 was not just the first year of a war. It was the opening of the long unwinding of the first age of globalization.

## 2. KEY THEMES OF 1914

### Theme 1: Collapse of the classical gold-standard operating environment

Before the war, the gold standard linked major currencies through fixed gold parities and helped stabilize exchange rates. In July and August 1914, the system faced a sudden scramble for liquidity and gold. Britain, Germany, France, Russia, and others imposed restrictions, suspended payments, declared moratoria, or shifted note issue to wartime purposes. The significance is that the gold standard did not simply stop because policymakers changed theory. It stopped because war finance and private liquidity demands became incompatible with peacetime gold convertibility.

Countries most affected were Britain, Germany, France, Russia, Austria-Hungary, Belgium, and the United States. Britain had to protect London bill markets and sterling while financing war. Germany and France rapidly moved toward war controls. Russia entered the war with a large agrarian economy and limited fiscal capacity. The United States stayed on gold but had to manage panic-driven gold exports. The forward link is direct: wartime inflation, blocked convertibility, and postwar attempts to restore prewar parities contributed to interwar monetary instability.

### Theme 2: Financial-market closure as crisis management

The financial crisis arrived through securities liquidation, exchange-rate pressure, bill-market distress, and fear of gold outflows. The New York Stock Exchange closed on July 31 and did not reopen for regular stock trading until December 12. London and continental exchanges also closed or operated under restrictions. This was not a normal bear market. It was a market-function crisis in which authorities stopped trading to prevent forced liquidation and preserve payment systems.

The countries most affected were the United States and Britain because of the scale of cross-border securities and sterling-dollar settlement. The forward link was the rise of New York as a wartime and postwar capital market. With European borrowers increasingly looking outside London, American financial institutions and Treasury policy gained importance. The crisis also showed the value of central-bank liquidity tools just as the Federal Reserve was being born.

### Theme 3: Total war begins to replace liberal globalization

The prewar economy was not fully free-trade, but it relied heavily on private finance, shipping insurance, commodity exchanges, and imperial trade networks. From August 1914, governments began to prioritize military supply, shipping access, food, coal, metals, rail capacity, and foreign exchange. War demand changed relative prices and business risk. Firms producing steel, chemicals, transport equipment, uniforms, foodstuffs, and financial services connected to government procurement became strategically important.

Germany, France, Britain, Russia, Austria-Hungary, Italy, Japan, and the United States were most affected. The forward link was the emergence of wartime planning institutions, price controls, rationing, and public debt expansion. Some of this apparatus disappeared after 1918, but the precedent for state direction of industrial economies remained.

### Theme 4: Resource mobilization depended on structure, not just size

Russia, China, India, and the Ottoman lands were large in population and aggregate economic weight, but much of their output was agricultural and difficult to mobilize for industrial warfare. Richer economies had greater fiscal capacity, rail density, industrial capital, banking systems, and skilled labor. Broadberry and Harrison stress that development level influenced military and industrial mobilization, not just population or total GDP.

The most affected countries were Russia, Austria-Hungary, Germany, Britain, France, India, and the United States. The forward link was decisive: industrial capacity, credit access, and food logistics helped determine wartime endurance. The same pattern later shaped interwar debt settlements, reparations disputes, and debates about economic planning.

### Theme 5: Commodity and shipping channels became strategic

Oil, coal, grain, copper, steel, nitrates, rubber, and shipping insurance moved from commercial variables to strategic constraints. Oil was still far smaller than coal in total energy use, but naval fuel, motor transport, and industrial demand made it increasingly important. U.S. crude first purchase prices were about $0.81 per barrel in 1914, and wartime demand pushed prices higher later in the conflict. Gold remained fixed in the U.S. at $20.67 per troy ounce under the gold standard.

The countries most affected included the United States, Britain, Germany, Mexico, the Dutch East Indies, Japan, Russia, and major agricultural exporters such as Argentina, Canada, Australia, and India. The forward link was the wartime rise of state procurement and the later strategic importance of oil and raw materials in diplomacy.

### Theme 6: The United States moved from peripheral reserve participant toward financial leader

The U.S. economy was already the largest in aggregate GDP by Maddison-style estimates, but London was still the leading international financial center. The 1914 crisis changed the trajectory. The United States had to defend gold convertibility and stop market panic, but its neutrality, productive capacity, and gold position gradually made it the supplier and creditor for European demand.

The countries most affected were the United States, Britain, France, Canada, Argentina, and other economies tied to Atlantic finance. The forward link was the wartime expansion of dollar finance, the later emergence of New York as a rival to London, and the long-run reserve-currency transition that became clearer after World War I and the interwar crises.

## 3. MACROECONOMIC OVERVIEW

### 3A. Global Aggregates

Exact global GDP for 1914 is data not available in the legacy raw Maddison series used here, because several large economies have missing 1914 observations. The nearest complete Maddison legacy baseline is 1913, with world GDP around 2.733 trillion 1990 international dollars. A strict 1914 world-growth rate is data not available. Major belligerent estimates show sharp early declines for Germany, France, Russia, and Austria-Hungary, while the United States and overseas economies were less directly disrupted.

Global trade volume for exact 1914 is data not available in a single comparable annual aggregate in the sources used. Qualitatively, trade was interrupted by war-risk insurance problems, naval blockade pressures, shipping diversion, capital controls, and commodity reallocation. The prewar trade system did not disappear instantly, but the direction changed from private arbitrage toward government priority.

Reserve-currency dynamics were still sterling-centered at the beginning of 1914. The pound was the dominant international settlement currency because London was the central money market, especially for bills of exchange and trade finance. By late 1914, sterling's role was stressed by war finance and market closures. The dollar did not become the dominant reserve currency in 1914, but the year began the process through which New York gained financial weight.

Global inflation trends are not available as a reliable comparable world index. Country-level prices began to diverge as governments issued notes, borrowed, imposed controls, and disrupted trade. Inflation became much more visible after 1915. Commodity prices were uneven. U.S. crude oil first purchase price was $0.81 per barrel in 1914. Gold was fixed in the United States at $20.67 per troy ounce. Copper and grain prices varied by market, contract, and shipping condition; robust global annual figures for exact 1914 are data not available in this assessment.

International capital flows reversed suddenly in late July. European investors sought liquidity and gold, including by liquidating U.S. securities. Britain and France moved from creditor-market normality toward war borrowing. The United States, initially a debtor and securities market exposed to foreign selling, began moving toward a creditor role as European demand for dollars, food, raw materials, and finance increased.

### 3B. Monetary & Financial Conditions

The United States entered 1914 without an operating central bank. The Federal Reserve Act had been passed in 1913, but the Reserve Banks opened on November 16, 1914. During the panic, Treasury Secretary William G. McAdoo used gold-management measures, emergency currency authority, and exchange closure to protect convertibility and prevent disorderly foreign liquidation of U.S. securities. U.S. policy was therefore crisis management rather than modern central-bank open-market policy.

Britain faced the center of the global liquidity shock. The Bank of England's Bank Rate moved from 3 percent to 10 percent during the crisis, then was reduced as emergency measures stabilized markets. The Currency and Bank Notes Act of 1914 permitted Treasury currency notes and effectively withdrew gold coin from ordinary circulation. London remained powerful but no longer looked like a frictionless global clearing center.

Germany, France, Russia, and Austria-Hungary shifted rapidly to wartime finance. Gold convertibility and normal discount-market conditions were interrupted. Central banks became fiscal-war institutions as much as monetary stabilizers. Japan, still a rising industrial and imperial power, joined the war on the Allied side and occupied German positions in Asia, but its financial system was less central to the global liquidity panic than London and New York.

Exchange rates were formally anchored by the gold standard at the start of the year, but crisis-market exchange rates and capital controls made simple parity quotes misleading. Credit conditions tightened sharply in July and August, then loosened selectively where governments and central banks supported bills, banks, or war finance. Stock markets closed in multiple countries, preventing a normal annual equity-return measure. Bond markets became increasingly dominated by war finance.

### 3C. Trade & Geopolitics

The defining geopolitical event was World War I. Austria-Hungary declared war on Serbia in late July, and escalation brought Germany, Russia, France, Britain, Belgium, Japan, and the Ottoman Empire into the conflict by late 1914. Trade was affected by blockades, naval risk, embargoes, insurance disruption, and the militarization of shipping.

There were no major liberalizing trade agreements in 1914 comparable to later multilateral agreements. The key change was the opposite: governments began to regulate exports, shipping, finance, and procurement. Regional integration projects such as the later European Community did not exist. Empires, not free-trade blocs, were the key economic regions.

Bilateral economic tensions were inseparable from military alliances. Germany's trade access was increasingly constrained by British sea power. Britain and France needed overseas supply and finance. Russia needed external credit and war materials. Japan used the conflict to expand its position in East Asia. Neutral economies such as the United States, Netherlands, Sweden, Switzerland, Spain, Argentina, and Brazil faced the commercial problem of trading amid belligerent pressure.

### 3D. Commodities & Energy

Coal remained the dominant industrial energy source, especially in Britain, Germany, France, and the United States. Oil was rising in strategic importance but was not yet the primary energy base of the world economy. U.S. crude prices were low by later standards, about $0.81 per barrel in 1914, before wartime demand contributed to later increases.

Metals became more important because of military procurement and industrial conversion. Steel, copper, lead, and zinc were essential for transport equipment, communication systems, construction, and war industry. Exact global 1914 metal-price aggregates are data not available in this assessment; market quotations differed by location and were affected by exchange closures.

Agriculture was central. Russia, India, Argentina, Canada, Australia, and the United States were important food or raw-material suppliers. War changed shipping availability and credit settlement. Poorer agrarian economies had large populations but lower mobilizable surplus because subsistence agriculture was less responsive to state procurement than commercial farming.

### 3E. Labor & Demographics

Global employment data are not available in a comparable annual format. Labor markets changed through military mobilization, transport disruption, and the early reallocation of male labor from civilian industry and agriculture into armies. In Europe, the effect was immediate. In neutral economies, the first effect was uncertainty, then demand shifts toward exports and war-related production.

Migration patterns were interrupted. The prewar Atlantic migration system was heavily affected by shipping disruption and wartime controls. Urbanization and industrial labor growth continued in the United States, Japan, and parts of Europe, but war redirected labor markets toward state demand. Wage-productivity data for exact 1914 are data not available on a comparable basis.

Britain began 1914 as the core of the international financial system. The outbreak of war forced London to defend payment mechanisms, support the bill market, and manage liquidity under extreme stress. ### 5B. Corporate landscape

A verified global top 10-20 company ranking by revenue or market capitalization for 1914 is data not available. The concept of standardized global corporate ranking was not yet produced in the way it is today, and national equity-market closure makes market-cap comparisons unreliable.

Important corporate and industrial actors included U.S. Steel, Standard Oil successor companies, General Electric, major U.S. railroads, J.P. Morgan-linked financial institutions, Krupp, Siemens, AEG, BASF, Bayer, Royal Dutch Shell, British shipping and insurance firms, Vickers, Armstrong Whitworth, Mitsui, Mitsubishi, Sumitomo, and large colonial trading houses. This is not a ranked list.

Major M&A and IPO data for exact 1914 are data not available in a comparable global dataset. The more important corporate change was functional: firms with access to steel, coal, chemicals, shipbuilding, arms, banking, oil, and shipping became strategic suppliers to states.

### 5C. Innovation & technology

The economic significance of technology in 1914 came from applied systems rather than a single consumer invention. Railways, steam shipping, telegraph cables, wireless communication, electrical equipment, chemicals, oil refining, internal combustion engines, and early aviation were all economically relevant. The war accelerated demand for aircraft, radio, vehicles, field medicine, and chemical production.

R&D spending data are not available in a comparable global format. Patent activity existed, but cross-country annual patent totals were not reconciled in this assessment. Infrastructure megaprojects included rail networks, ports, canals, and urban utilities, but war quickly redirected capital away from civilian expansion in belligerent economies.

### 5D. Labor markets & human capital

Labor markets shifted through mobilization. Belligerent countries withdrew millions of men from civilian work, creating shortages in agriculture, transport, heavy industry, and skilled trades. Women, older workers, colonial labor, and neutral-country suppliers became more important later in the war.

Wage data by skill level are not available in a comparable global format. Union movements were significant in Britain, Germany, France, the United States, Australia, and parts of Latin America, but wartime emergency reduced normal bargaining in many belligerent sectors. Migration was disrupted by shipping risk and wartime controls.

## 6. FINANCIAL MARKETS

### 6A. Equity markets

The main fact about 1914 equity markets is closure. The NYSE closed on July 31 and reopened for regular stock trading on December 12. London closed at the crisis and reopened in January 1915. Paris, Berlin, Vienna, and other exchanges also suspended or restricted trading. Because markets were closed for long periods, normal annual index levels and total returns are data not available or not comparable.

The closures were policy tools. Authorities wanted to prevent forced liquidation, gold drains, and panic pricing in securities. In the United States, closing the NYSE was especially tied to the fear that European investors would sell U.S. assets and demand gold.

### 6B. Fixed income

Government bond markets moved toward war finance. Britain, France, Germany, Russia, and Austria-Hungary began issuing or preparing instruments for war borrowing. Exact 10-year yield series for all major economies in 1914 are data not available in this assessment. Credit spreads are also data not available in a comparable global format.

The important fixed-income shift was institutional rather than numerical. Sovereign borrowing moved from ordinary public finance toward mass war finance, bank-supported debt placement, and later patriotic bond campaigns.

### 6C. Currencies

At the start of 1914, major currencies were linked by gold parities. By late 1914, convertibility restrictions, moratoria, note issues, and exchange-market disruption made official parities insufficient to describe actual market access. Sterling remained central, but the dollar's role increased as U.S. gold defense succeeded and European borrowing needs rose.

Major devaluations in the formal modern sense were not the main 1914 mechanism. Instead, countries suspended or limited gold payments, restricted markets, and used fiscal-monetary instruments to finance war.

### 6D. Alternative assets

Gold's official U.S. price remained $20.67 per troy ounce. This was not a market free-float price in the modern sense; it was the statutory gold parity. Real estate indicators for major cities in exact 1914 are data not available in comparable form. Commodity assets such as oil, copper, wheat, and shipping capacity became strategically valuable, but prices differed by market and insurance conditions.

## 7. RISKS, CRISES & BLACK SWANS

The active crisis was World War I and the associated financial crisis. The near-miss was a broader collapse of payment systems and gold convertibility before governments established emergency mechanisms. The NYSE closure, British bank holiday measures, Bank of England market support, Treasury note issue, and U.S. emergency currency all show how close the system came to disorderly liquidation.

Systemic vulnerabilities visible in hindsight included overreliance on London-centered finance, dependence on gold convertibility during geopolitical shock, high European military commitments, fragile agrarian mobilization in Russia and Austria-Hungary, and the absence of a fully operational U.S. central bank in July 1914.

Policy mistakes visible in retrospect include underestimating the duration and economic scale of the war. Many governments acted as if short-war finance and temporary market controls would be enough. Hindsight shows that 1914 opened a multi-year fiscal, monetary, and industrial transformation.

Tail risks that materialized included exchange closures, gold restrictions, fiscal explosions, invasion of industrial regions, and breakdown of ordinary trade finance. Tail risks that did not fully materialize in 1914 included a permanent collapse of the U.S. banking system and immediate famine in major Western economies.

## 8. YEAR-OVER-YEAR DELTA (1914 vs. 1913)

The structural break from 1913 to 1914 was extreme. In 1913, the global economy was still near the peak of the first age of globalization. In 1914, war ended the assumption that capital, shipping, gold, and securities markets would remain continuously open. The change was not gradual; it happened in weeks.

The biggest trend reversal was from private international capital mobility toward state-controlled war finance. Britain, France, Germany, Russia, and Austria-Hungary moved from ordinary fiscal management toward emergency borrowing and monetary accommodation. The United States shifted from being exposed to European liquidation to becoming a potential supplier of credit and goods.

New risks emerged in food supply, shipping, commodity access, and industrial labor. Risks that had seemed manageable in 1913, such as gold outflows or exchange pressure, became system-level crises in 1914. Economic power also shifted subtly. The United States' industrial scale became more valuable because it was outside the European battlefield, while Britain's financial dominance became more vulnerable because Britain was a belligerent.

Country-level GDP deltas show wartime disruption, though source conflicts remain. Maddison legacy rows suggest 1914 declines for the United States, Germany, France, Canada, Belgium, Argentina, Netherlands, Brazil, Spain, and Japan; small gains for the United Kingdom, India, Mexico, and Sweden; and severe reconstructed declines for Russia and Austria-Hungary. These values should not be read as fully comparable modern national accounts.

## 9. DATA CONFIDENCE & GAPS

Reliable statistics are strongest for the United States, United Kingdom, Germany, France, Canada, Australia, Netherlands, Sweden, and Belgium, but even for these countries, 1914 wartime disruption creates conflicts across sources. The biggest conflict in this assessment is the difference between Maddison legacy annual rows and Broadberry-Harrison wartime index tables for some early-war GDP changes.

Data confidence is weakest for China, British India, Russian Empire, Austria-Hungary, Mexico, Brazil, Dutch East Indies, and other colonial or developing economies. The problem is not that the economies were unimportant. The problem is that modern national accounting systems did not exist, borders changed, and later historians had to reconstruct output from fragmentary fiscal, production, trade, population, and price evidence.

The following figures are estimates rather than official national accounts: nearly all GDP, GDP per capita, and growth values; Russia's 1914 GDP; Austria-Hungary's 1914 GDP; China's use of a 1913 adjacent-year estimate; and colonial territory estimates. For Soviet bloc or pre-Soviet Russian territories and developing economies, uncertainty of plus or minus 20-30 percent is plausible.

Important data that simply does not exist in a modern comparable format for 1914 includes global unemployment, global CPI, exact global GDP growth, comparable policy rates for all top 20 economies, current-account balances for many countries, government debt/GDP for many developing and colonial economies, corporate market-cap rankings, standardized real estate prices, and comparable consumer confidence data.
