# MACRO/MICRO GLOBAL ECONOMIC ASSESSMENT - YEAR: 1986

### 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

1986 was defined by a rare combination of lower oil prices, falling inflation, large exchange-rate movements, and policy reform. The collapse in crude oil prices after Saudi Arabia shifted toward defending market share changed global terms of trade in a matter of months. Oil importers received an income transfer, inflation in industrial economies dropped, and central banks gained room to ease. Oil exporters, especially Mexico, Indonesia, Iran, Nigeria, Venezuela, and the Soviet bloc, faced immediate fiscal and external pressure.

The year also showed how exchange rates could reshape measured economic power. After the 1985 Plaza Accord, the dollar continued to fall, the yen and major European currencies appreciated, and nominal GDP in current US dollars rose sharply for Japan and Western Europe even when real growth was modest. This is why this document separates two rankings: a Maddison real-output ranking in 2011 international dollars, which includes the former USSR, and a World Bank current-dollar cross-check for market economies. On Maddison's real-output basis the United States remained first, followed by the former USSR, Japan, China, and Germany. On current-dollar World Bank data, the United States, Japan, Germany, France, and Italy led the ranking among countries with comparable market-exchange-rate data.

The most important structural policy changes were forward-looking rather than immediately visible in GDP. The Uruguay Round of GATT negotiations began in September 1986 and eventually produced the WTO framework. The Single European Act moved the European Community toward the 1992 internal market. The United States enacted the Tax Reform Act of 1986. The United Kingdom implemented the London financial market "Big Bang" and privatized British Gas. France began a privatization cycle under the Chirac government. Vietnam launched Doi Moi. These changes helped define the late-1980s shift toward liberalization, financial integration, and market-oriented reform.

The year was not simply a benign disinflation story. Latin American debt stress remained severe, Mexico contracted, Brazil grew but with very high inflation, and oil-exporting economies faced a sudden loss of export revenue. Japan's monetary easing and asset-market acceleration were positive at the time but, in hindsight, helped create conditions for the asset bubble that peaked in 1989. The Soviet system faced low oil revenues, Chernobyl-related costs, and deeper evidence of productivity weakness. 1986 therefore sits at an inflection point: the world economy was expanding, but the distribution of gains and risks was changing quickly.

### 2. KEY THEMES OF 1986

**1. Oil-price collapse as a global terms-of-trade shock.** The most important price event of 1986 was the oil crash. World Bank analysis later described the 1985-86 episode as a 61 percent fall in oil prices between January and July 1986, following Saudi Arabia's December 1985 policy change. GATT reporting also described a fall from about $30 per barrel in November 1985 to a temporary spot-market low around $10 in mid-1986. The shock lowered inflation and import bills for oil importers, but it weakened oil exporters' fiscal revenues, external balances, and debt-service capacity. The countries most affected included Mexico, Indonesia, Iran, the USSR, Venezuela, Saudi Arabia, Nigeria, and other producers. The forward linkage was durable: lower oil prices persisted for years and changed the bargaining power inside OPEC.

**2. Disinflation and monetary easing in advanced economies.** Lower energy costs reduced inflation in industrial economies and allowed monetary authorities to ease without reigniting the inflation psychology of the 1970s. The Federal Reserve lowered reserve pressure and discount rates, with federal funds trading around the 6.25 to 6.375 percent area after the July 1986 discount-rate cut. The Bank of Japan cut the official discount rate repeatedly between January 1986 and February 1987. Lower rates supported asset prices and housing, but the easing cycle had uneven consequences. It supported US demand and Japan's domestic stimulus, while raising concerns about financial excess in the years that followed.

**3. Exchange-rate realignment after the Plaza Accord.** The dollar's nominal effective exchange rate fell by about 17 percent in 1986 compared with its 1985 average, according to GATT reporting. The yen and major European currencies appreciated sharply. This mechanically inflated the current-dollar GDP of Japan and Western Europe and squeezed exporters in Japan and Germany. It also changed trade-value statistics: a large part of the rise in dollar-denominated world trade value was a valuation effect rather than a pure volume effect. The forward linkage was the Louvre Accord of 1987 and Japan's subsequent policy dilemma between exchange-rate stability and domestic asset inflation.

**4. Market liberalization, trade diplomacy, and institutional reform.** 1986 began policy processes whose full impact came later. The Uruguay Round was launched in Punta del Este and sought to go beyond manufactured-goods tariffs into agriculture, services, investment measures, and intellectual property. The Single European Act amended the European Community treaties and accelerated the internal-market program. The United Kingdom's Big Bang changed the City of London's structure, while privatizations in Britain and France changed state ownership models. Vietnam's Doi Moi announced a new reform path. These moves connected microeconomic institutional change with future macro performance.

**5. Debt, inflation, and adjustment in Latin America.** Brazil and Argentina grew in 1986, but inflation remained a central macro constraint. Mexico contracted sharply under debt pressure and lower oil revenue. The region's policy debate still revolved around external debt, exchange-rate credibility, fiscal adjustment, and heterodox anti-inflation plans. The forward linkage was the continued search for debt restructuring, culminating later in Brady-style approaches.

**6. East Asian industrial acceleration.** Japan remained the dominant Asian industrial economy, but South Korea's 1986 growth above 10 percent and current-account swing into surplus showed the rise of export-led late industrialization. China maintained very high real growth while reforming through township and village enterprises, special zones, and decentralized incentives. This theme foreshadowed the larger Asia-centered production-network shift of the 1990s.

**7. Non-market economy stress.** The former USSR still ranked as the world's second-largest real-output economy in Maddison estimates, but the data are highly uncertain and not directly comparable to market-price GDP. Low oil revenues, administrative inefficiency, Chernobyl-related costs, and the early Gorbachev reform agenda exposed a widening gap between measured scale and economic flexibility. The issue mattered for Eastern Europe as well, including Poland, where output recovered modestly but inflation and external constraints persisted.

### 3. MACROECONOMIC OVERVIEW

**3A. Global Aggregates:** World GDP in current US dollars was about **$15.35 trillion** in World Bank WDI data, up from about **$13.02 trillion** in 1985. This large nominal increase reflected real growth, inflation, and especially dollar depreciation. World real GDP growth was about **3.25 percent**, down from about **3.77 percent** in 1985. World population was about **4.94 billion**.

World merchandise trade volume grew by about **3.5 percent** in GATT estimates, while GATT noted that the IMF estimate was higher at about **4.9 percent**. World merchandise trade value rose to about **$2.12 trillion**, but the value increase was heavily affected by exchange-rate translation. Reserve-currency dynamics remained dollar-centered, yet the dollar's depreciation against the yen and major European currencies dominated trade-value and GDP-value comparisons. Global inflation generally declined in advanced economies, especially because oil prices collapsed. Inflation remained much higher in several developing and debtor economies, notably Brazil, Mexico, Turkey, and Poland.

Capital flows continued to run from surplus economies toward the United States, which recorded a large current-account deficit. Japan and Germany ran substantial surpluses. Latin American debtors remained constrained by debt-service requirements and limited voluntary private lending. Oil exporters experienced a negative income shock and had less capacity to finance imports or recycle surpluses.

Key commodity markers were: crude oil, sharply lower, with a mid-year spot trough near $10 per barrel; gold, around the high-$300s per troy ounce on annual-average private-source estimates; non-oil commodities, generally weak after the 1984-86 downturn; agriculture, mixed, with weak trade growth and policy distortions from subsidies; copper and other metals, near the beginning of a later 1987-89 rebound, but exact annual spot data are not included in the compiled source set.

**3B. Monetary & Financial Conditions:** In the United States, monetary conditions eased as inflation fell and growth moderated. The Federal Reserve's August 1986 policy record described federal funds trading around 6.25 to 6.375 percent after the July discount-rate cut. In Japan, the Bank of Japan moved through a major easing cycle as yen appreciation hurt exports and wholesale prices weakened. In West Germany, long-term yields moved in the 6 percent range in OECD/FRED data, while the Deutsche mark's strength restrained inflation and complicated export performance. The United Kingdom moved toward a lower-rate environment but still had materially higher nominal rates than Germany and Japan.

Exchange rates were managed floats rather than Bretton Woods-style fixed parities. The dominant FX story was the post-Plaza depreciation of the dollar. The yen's appreciation transformed Japan's current-dollar GDP and export incentives. The Deutsche mark and other European currencies also appreciated, making European GDP look much larger in dollars and reinforcing disinflation through cheaper imports.

Credit conditions were easier in the core advanced economies than in the early 1980s, but credit was not uniformly available. Highly indebted developing countries remained constrained. Equity markets were strong: the S&P 500 rose about 14.6 percent on a price basis and about 18.7 percent on a total-return basis; the Dow Jones Industrial Average rose about 22.6 percent; Japan's Nikkei 225 rose more than 40 percent; and the FTSE 100 rose about 18.9 percent. Lower inflation, lower interest rates, and currency effects supported equities.

**3C. Trade & Geopolitics:** The Uruguay Round began in September 1986. Its agenda was historically important because it moved the trade regime toward agriculture, services, investment measures, and intellectual property, not just tariffs on manufactured goods. The European Community moved toward deeper integration through the Single European Act. Cold War structures still shaped trade flows, technology restrictions, defense spending, and the planned-economy trading area. The Iran-Iraq War continued to affect oil markets and regional risk. Trade tensions remained high around the US deficit and Japanese surpluses, especially in autos, electronics, and semiconductors.

**3D. Commodities & Energy:** Oil was the dominant commodity. The shift in Saudi output strategy and the OPEC market-share struggle lowered prices dramatically. Importers such as Japan, Germany, India, and much of Western Europe benefited through lower energy import bills and lower inflation. Producers such as Mexico, Indonesia, Iran, and the USSR suffered revenue losses. Mining trade volumes rose because lower prices encouraged consumption and stockbuilding, but dollar unit values fell. Agriculture remained shaped by subsidy competition and weak international demand growth. Metals were weak in 1986 but began to move toward the 1987-89 boom.

**3E. Labor & Demographics:** Labor markets were not comparable across countries. OECD unemployment remained high in several European economies: Spain was above 20 percent in IMF WEO data, the United Kingdom above 11 percent, Belgium about 10 percent, France near 9 percent, and West Germany near 8 percent. Japan and Switzerland had very low measured unemployment, while China and several developing economies used definitions that do not compare directly with OECD labor-force surveys. Demographically, the world remained young, urbanization continued in Asia and Latin America, and migration flows into North America, Western Europe, and the Gulf remained economically significant. Wage growth generally lagged productivity in some tradable sectors exposed to import competition, while high-inflation economies faced unstable real wages.

The US economy expanded at a moderate pace, with lower inflation and easier monetary policy supporting demand. The dollar's fall helped future export competitiveness, but the current-account deficit remained large. **Micro/sectoral highlights:** consumer demand surged under stabilization optimism; inflation hedging shaped finance; industry grew but faced shortages and controls; agriculture and mining remained important export earners; debt overhang limited long-term investment.

### 6. FINANCIAL MARKETS

**6A. Equity markets:** The United States had a strong year: the S&P 500 price index rose about 14.6 percent and the Dow Jones Industrial Average rose about 22.6 percent. Japan's Nikkei 225 rose about 42 to 44 percent depending on the source and closing convention, reaching roughly the high-18,000 range by year-end. The UK's FTSE 100 closed around 1,679, up about 18.9 percent. Germany's modern DAX did not launch until 1988, so 1986 DAX data are not applicable. For France's CAC 40 and several emerging-market benchmarks, data in the compiled source set are not available.

**6B. Fixed income:** US 10-year Treasury yields fell sharply during the year, from around 9.19 percent in January to around 7.11 percent in December in FRED monthly data. German 10-year yields were around the 6 percent range in OECD/FRED data. Lower inflation and lower oil prices supported bonds. Credit spreads for developing-country borrowers remained affected by debt-crisis conditions. Sovereign-debt stress remained concentrated in Latin America and oil exporters.

**6C. Currencies:** The dollar depreciated significantly. GATT estimated a 17 percent fall in the dollar's nominal effective exchange rate in 1986 compared with the 1985 average. The yen's appreciation was one of the year's largest macro shocks, causing Japan's nominal current-dollar GDP to surge while real growth slowed. The Deutsche mark and other European currencies also appreciated. Many developing economies used managed rates, exchange controls, or frequent adjustments, making direct comparison difficult.

**6D. Alternative assets:** Gold traded around the high-$300s per ounce on annual-average private estimates. Japanese land and equities rose strongly, forming part of the asset-price boom. US and UK real estate benefited from lower rates and financial liberalization, although local outcomes differed. Commodity-linked real assets in oil regions weakened.

### 7. RISKS, CRISES & BLACK SWANS

The main active crisis was the oil-price collapse and its fiscal impact on exporters. Mexico was the clearest macro casualty among the top economies, while Indonesia, Iran, and the USSR faced major terms-of-trade losses. Brazil and Argentina had high inflation and fragile stabilization plans. The Chernobyl disaster was a black-swan event with long-lived health, environmental, and economic costs, particularly for Ukraine, Belarus, Russia, and the Soviet fiscal system.

Systemic vulnerabilities were also building. In hindsight, Japan's 1986 easing cycle and asset-market boom were early warning signs of the late-1980s bubble. Financial liberalization in London improved efficiency but also increased the complexity and speed of securities markets. The US current-account deficit and Japan-Germany surpluses showed persistent global imbalances. Debt overhang in Latin America remained unresolved.

Tail risks that did not fully materialize in 1986 included a generalized advanced-economy recession after the oil shock and exchange-rate realignment, a disorderly dollar collapse, and a complete breakdown of OPEC coordination. These risks were contained, but only temporarily. Policy coordination continued into 1987 through efforts to stabilize exchange rates.

### 8. YEAR-OVER-YEAR DELTA (1986 vs. 1985)

World real GDP growth slowed from about 3.77 percent in 1985 to about 3.25 percent in 1986, while current-dollar world GDP rose sharply because of dollar depreciation. The biggest structural break was oil: 1985 still looked like a weak oil-market environment, but 1986 became a full price crash. Inflation in industrial economies fell, and central banks had more room to ease.

The exchange-rate regime shifted from Plaza Accord announcement to real economic adjustment. In 1985, currency realignment was a policy objective. In 1986, it became visible in trade volumes, corporate profits, and GDP rankings. Japan's current-dollar GDP rose dramatically, but exporters faced a real squeeze. Germany's exports also slowed relative to prior years.

Trade policy moved from concern over imbalances to formal negotiation. The Uruguay Round launch made 1986 a starting point for the next global trade regime. European integration moved from programmatic ambition to treaty change through the Single European Act. Domestic policy also shifted, with US tax reform, UK financial deregulation, UK privatization, French privatization, and Vietnam's Doi Moi.

In the top-20 Maddison ranking, there were no entries or exits versus 1985. Canada overtook Mexico, Korea overtook Australia, and Argentina overtook Poland. These changes reflected the oil shock, East Asian export acceleration, and rebound dynamics in parts of Latin America.

### 9. DATA CONFIDENCE & GAPS

**Highest confidence:** World Bank WDI current-dollar GDP, WDI real GDP growth, WDI population, IMF WEO inflation and current-account indicators for OECD and major market economies, Federal Reserve policy records, GATT trade-volume estimates, and World Bank oil-price analysis.

**Medium confidence:** IMF WEO fiscal and debt indicators for 1986, especially where national accounting standards changed later. Current-account data are more reliable than fiscal-balance data for several countries, but still subject to later revision.

**Low confidence:** GDP ranking for the former USSR and other planned economies. Maddison estimates are the best consistent long-run source used here, but they are PPP-style historical reconstructions, not market-dollar GDP. Germany is also a special case because WDI/IMF pre-1990 data generally refer to West Germany, while Maddison's Germany series is a historical reconstruction. Unemployment rates are not comparable across OECD, developing, and planned economies. Exchange rates for countries with redenominations, capital controls, or legacy euro conversion are not reliable from WEO GDP-ratio methods.

**Important missing data:** comparable 1986 current-dollar GDP for the USSR; harmonized unemployment for China, India, Indonesia, Poland, and the USSR; exact 1986 policy rates for many non-OECD economies; harmonized corporate revenue and market-cap rankings; global real-estate price series; comparable household debt and savings by country; consistent sectoral productivity data; exact annual prices for several commodities in the compiled source set.

**Weakest claims:** corporate landscape rankings, non-US and non-Japan equity returns outside major benchmark sources, real-estate conditions outside Japan/US/UK, and micro-level household behavior in planned or high-inflation economies. These are included only as qualified historical interpretation, not as precise statistical claims.
