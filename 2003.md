# MACRO/MICRO GLOBAL ECONOMIC ASSESSMENT - YEAR: 2003

### 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

2003 was the year in which the world economy moved from fragile post-2001 repair to a broad but uneven expansion. The first half was shaped by the Iraq war, high oil-price uncertainty, the SARS shock in East Asia and Canada, and lingering balance-sheet damage from the dot-com bust. By the second half, global activity had accelerated, equity markets had rebounded, trade volumes were improving, and policy stimulus was clearly feeding through. The IMF later estimated world real GDP growth at about 3.9 percent for 2003, while the WTO reported a 4.5 percent rise in real merchandise exports.

The recovery was not synchronized. The United States led through fiscal stimulus, mortgage refinancing, defense outlays, and extremely accommodative monetary policy after the Federal Reserve reduced the federal funds target to 1.00 percent in June. Japan finally showed signs of export-led recovery under quantitative easing, though deflation and bank weakness remained central problems. The euro area was the weak link among advanced economies, with Germany contracting, France barely growing, and Italy close to stagnation. The United Kingdom, Spain, Australia, Canada, and several emerging economies had stronger domestic demand, often accompanied by rising household credit or housing activity.

China was the central structural story. Two years after WTO accession, China was becoming both a major export platform and a demand engine for oil, metals, machinery, semiconductors, and Asian intermediate goods. The SARS outbreak caused a sharp but temporary services shock, yet the Chinese economy still grew around 10 percent. East Asian trade increasingly revolved around Chinese final assembly and intra-Asian supply chains. This mattered for commodity exporters such as Australia, Brazil, Russia, and parts of the Middle East, and for manufacturing exporters in Japan, Korea, Taiwan, and Germany.

Financially, 2003 marked the turn from post-bubble risk aversion to a search for yield. Equity markets rallied strongly, credit spreads tightened, emerging-market capital flows recovered, and the U.S. dollar depreciated against the euro and several other currencies. At the time, this looked like recovery. In hindsight, 2003 also helped build vulnerabilities: global imbalances, U.S. household leverage, housing-market froth in several countries, underpriced credit risk, and heavy dependence on Chinese investment demand.

### 2. KEY THEMES OF 2003

### Theme 1: Policy-stimulus-led recovery after the 2001-2002 slowdown

The defining cyclical theme was recovery powered by policy support rather than by a fully self-sustaining private investment cycle. The U.S. Federal Reserve, ECB, Bank of Japan, Bank of England, Bank of Canada, and several emerging-market central banks either eased policy or kept conditions accommodative. Fiscal policy was also supportive in the United States and, to a lesser extent, Japan and some European economies. The IMF described the 2003 recovery as underpinned by policy stimulus, improving corporate profits, favorable financing conditions, IT revival, equity-market wealth effects, and inventory rebuilding.

Most affected countries: United States, Japan, United Kingdom, Canada, Australia, Korea, Taiwan, Brazil, Russia, China, and the euro area. Forward linkage: the same low-rate environment that supported recovery in 2003 helped fuel credit expansion, housing-market strength, carry trades, and global search-for-yield behavior in 2004-2007.

### Theme 2: China became a global demand and supply-chain anchor

China's WTO-era acceleration was no longer only a domestic development story. In 2003 China became a larger import market for Asian neighbors, a major buyer of metals and oil, and a growing exporter of office, telecom, textile, and consumer goods. WTO trade analysis for 2003 emphasized China's role in regional trade flows, including China's growing importance as an Asian market and its expanding share of exports in office and telecom equipment and textiles.

Most affected countries: China, Japan, Korea, Taiwan, Australia, Brazil, Russia, Germany, and Southeast Asia. Forward linkage: China's investment and export surge became a defining force of the 2000s commodity supercycle and the reorganization of manufacturing value chains.

### Theme 3: Dollar depreciation, reserve accumulation, and global imbalances

The U.S. current account deficit remained large, while East Asian and oil-exporting economies accumulated reserves. The dollar weakened substantially against the euro during 2003. This helped dollar-based exporters outside the United States in valuation terms, increased the current-dollar size of euro-area economies, and contributed to debate over whether the world was entering a de facto new dollar standard with Asian central banks recycling surpluses into U.S. securities.

Most affected countries: United States, China, Japan, euro-area members, Korea, Taiwan, oil exporters, and emerging markets with dollar debt. Forward linkage: the imbalance between U.S. deficit demand and Asian reserve accumulation became a central macroeconomic debate before the global financial crisis.

### Theme 4: The euro area lagged despite monetary easing

The ECB cut its main refinancing rate to 2.00 percent in June, but the euro area remained sluggish. Germany was close to or in recession, France and Italy were weak, and unemployment remained high. A stronger euro weighed on exports, while fiscal deficits in Germany and France put pressure on the Stability and Growth Pact.

Most affected countries: Germany, France, Italy, Spain, Netherlands, Belgium, and Sweden through regional trade. Forward linkage: weak euro-area demand fed into structural reform debates, including Germany's Agenda 2010 and labor-market reforms, while Spain's stronger credit and construction cycle diverged from the core.

### Theme 5: SARS and Iraq were large shocks but not durable global recession triggers

At the start of 2003, economists worried that Iraq-war uncertainty, oil prices, and SARS could derail recovery. SARS hit tourism, aviation, retail, hospitality, and transport in East Asia and Canada. The Iraq war created oil-price uncertainty and disrupted Iraqi production but major hostilities were short. By late 2003, these shocks were mostly treated as transitory, though they revealed the fragility of globally integrated services, travel, and energy markets.

Most affected countries: China, Hong Kong SAR, Taiwan, Singapore, Canada, Japan, Korea, United States, United Kingdom, and oil producers. Forward linkage: SARS became a reference point for later pandemic-risk analysis; Iraq shaped oil geopolitics and fiscal burdens for years.

### Theme 6: Financial-market rebound and corporate repair

Global equities recovered strongly after three difficult years. The S&P 500, Nasdaq, DAX, CAC 40, FTSE 100, Nikkei, KOSPI, Bovespa, and many emerging-market indices rose. Corporate profits recovered, technology spending stabilized, banks rebuilt capital in several systems, and merger activity revived. However, bankruptcies and scandals such as Parmalat showed that accounting and governance risks had not disappeared.

Most affected countries: United States, Japan, Germany, France, Italy, Brazil, Russia, Korea, Taiwan, and global financial centers. Forward linkage: improved risk appetite set the stage for stronger M&A, emerging-market issuance, and leverage growth in 2004-2007.

### Theme 7: Early commodity-cycle acceleration

Oil averaged around $28.90 per barrel on the World Bank's three-crude measure, higher than 2002. Gold, copper, and several metals rose as geopolitical risk, dollar weakness, and Chinese demand interacted. Mining output grew faster than agriculture and manufacturing output in WTO sector data for 2003.

Most affected countries: Russia, Brazil, Australia, Canada, Mexico, China, India, and oil exporters outside the top 20. Forward linkage: 2003 was one of the early years of the 2000s commodity boom.

### 3. MACROECONOMIC OVERVIEW

### 3A. Global Aggregates

World GDP in current U.S. dollars was about $38.9 trillion in World Bank WDI data. Real world output growth was about 3.9 percent on the IMF WEO measure. Global merchandise exports rose 4.5 percent in volume terms, significantly faster than world merchandise production, while trade growth was strongest in Asia and in commodity-related flows. The rebound was uneven: advanced economies were still recovering from weak investment and low inflation, while emerging Asia was already expanding rapidly.

Reserve currency dynamics still centered on the dollar, but 2003 was a year of dollar weakness. The euro appreciated strongly, partly reflecting U.S. current-account concerns, lower U.S. short rates, and improving European currency credibility. East Asian central banks and oil exporters continued to accumulate reserves, supporting demand for U.S. fixed-income securities even as the dollar fell.

Inflation was subdued in advanced economies. The IMF described average consumer inflation in advanced economies at about 1.8 percent in 2003, with deflation still a concern in Japan and low inflation in the United States and euro area. Emerging-market inflation was mixed: Brazil and Russia were still disinflating from high levels, Turkey was falling sharply from crisis-era inflation, China moved from deflation concerns to overheating concerns, and India remained moderate by historical standards.

Key commodity prices: the World Bank Pink Sheet reported a 2003 average crude oil price of about $28.90 per barrel for the average of Brent, Dubai, and WTI; Brent averaged about $28.85 and WTI about $31.11. Gold averaged about $364 per troy ounce. Copper and several base metals rose as Chinese imports increased. Agricultural conditions were region-specific; global staple prices did not define the macro year the way energy and metals did.

International capital flows turned more favorable toward emerging markets. Risk appetite returned, emerging-market bond spreads tightened, equity issuance revived, and net private capital flows recovered. China and other Asian economies were still large reserve accumulators rather than external borrowers; Brazil and Russia benefited from stronger investor confidence and commodity-linked terms of trade.

### 3B. Monetary and Financial Conditions

The U.S. Federal Reserve cut the federal funds target to 1.00 percent in June and explicitly noted concern about an unwelcome fall in inflation. The ECB reduced the minimum bid rate on main refinancing operations to 2.00 percent in June. The Bank of Japan kept overnight rates near zero under quantitative easing and used current-account-balance targets to provide liquidity. The Bank of England cut to 3.50 percent in July, then raised back to 3.75 percent in November as housing and household credit concerns increased.

Exchange-rate regimes in 2003 were mixed. The major advanced economies operated under floating rates. The euro, yen, sterling, Canadian dollar, Australian dollar, Swiss franc, and Swedish krona all moved under floating or managed-floating arrangements. China kept the renminbi tightly pegged near 8.277 per U.S. dollar. Taiwan, Korea, Mexico, Brazil, Russia, and India used managed floats or market-based systems with varying degrees of intervention.

Credit conditions eased materially. Corporate spreads narrowed, equity markets rallied, and mortgage credit expanded in the United States, United Kingdom, Spain, Australia, and Canada. Japan's bank system remained fragile but improved as equity prices recovered and policy support continued. In Brazil, the Selic rate was cut sharply from very high levels as inflation expectations improved under the new Lula administration.

### 3C. Trade and Geopolitics

The Doha Round suffered a major setback when the WTO Cancun ministerial failed in September 2003, largely over agriculture, industrial tariffs, and the so-called Singapore issues. The United States removed its 2002 steel safeguards in December 2003 after WTO rulings and retaliation threats. The European Union signed the Treaty of Accession with ten countries in April 2003, setting up the May 2004 enlargement and deepening Europe's eastward economic integration.

The Iraq war was the largest geopolitical event. Its economic effects included oil-price volatility, defense spending, reconstruction planning, and uncertainty over Middle East supply. Sanctions and postwar institutional disruption meant Iraq itself was not a normal market economy in 2003. The war also affected fiscal balances in the United States and United Kingdom.

Regional integration continued outside Europe. ASEAN economic integration advanced gradually, NAFTA trade remained central for North America, and China's WTO accession continued to reshape tariff, investment, and supply-chain decisions.

### 3D. Commodities and Energy

Oil prices rose on Iraq-war uncertainty, OPEC supply management, and demand recovery. Venezuelan output was recovering from the 2002-2003 strike, Iraq's output was disrupted by war and postwar instability, and global demand was strengthening. The commodity story was not only oil: copper, steel inputs, coal, and bulk commodities began to show the pull of Chinese fixed investment and urbanization.

Mining and extraction outperformed many other real sectors. Russia benefited from oil and gas; Australia from bulk commodities and Asian demand; Brazil from iron ore, agriculture, and a weaker real early in the year; Canada from energy and metals. Energy-importing economies such as Japan, Korea, Taiwan, India, and much of Europe faced higher import bills.

Agriculture was less globally synchronized. Brazil's agribusiness continued to expand. North American agriculture faced a mix of stronger prices and currency effects. Europe remained shaped by Common Agricultural Policy arrangements. India had a strong agricultural rebound, supporting its high real GDP growth.

### 3E. Labor and Demographics

Labor markets lagged the financial recovery. U.S. unemployment averaged around 6 percent and payroll recovery was slow. Euro-area unemployment remained high, with Germany and France under particular pressure. Japan's unemployment rate was high by its historical standards but stabilized. Spain and Australia created jobs alongside domestic-demand strength.

Demographic trends were already visible. Japan and much of Europe faced aging populations and low fertility. China still had abundant labor migration from rural to urban areas, but the demographic base for later aging was already set. India had a younger labor force and expanding services employment. Migration mattered for construction, care services, and agriculture in North America, Europe, and the Gulf, though definitions and official data vary widely.

Wage growth generally remained restrained relative to productivity in many advanced economies, especially the United States and Germany. The link between wage restraint, low inflation, and profit recovery was one reason corporate margins improved while household debt expanded.

**5B. Corporate landscape:**

The largest global corporations by revenue in the early-2000s rankings were concentrated in retail, oil, autos, diversified industrials, and finance. Wal-Mart was the leading revenue company, while BP, ExxonMobil, Royal Dutch/Shell, General Motors, Ford, DaimlerChrysler, Toyota, General Electric, and other energy or auto groups occupied the top tier in most contemporary revenue lists. Exact ordering depends on fiscal year and ranking publication, so this report does not present a fabricated exact top-10 revenue table.

Major corporate events included Bank of America's announced $47 billion acquisition of FleetBoston, the largest acquisition announced in 2003; News Corp's acquisition of a controlling stake in DirecTV's parent assets; BP's TNK-BP transaction in Russia; Biogen's merger with Idec; Oracle's hostile bid for PeopleSoft, which was still unresolved in 2003; and major tobacco, media, and pharmaceutical transactions. Parmalat's collapse in December exposed a major European accounting scandal. Air Canada restructured under creditor protection. United Airlines, which entered bankruptcy in late 2002, remained in restructuring.

The IPO market reopened but remained cautious. Google was still private in 2003 and did not go public until 2004. China-related listings, insurance and telecom offerings, and emerging-market issuance helped signal the return of risk appetite.

**5C. Innovation & technology:**

The technology cycle turned from contraction to recovery. Semiconductor demand improved, wireless networking spread, broadband adoption accelerated, and enterprise IT spending stabilized. Intel's Centrino platform, launched in 2003, helped popularize Wi-Fi-enabled laptops. Mobile phones and 2.5G/early 3G networks expanded in Europe and Asia. The Human Genome Project's completion in 2003 had long-term implications for biotechnology, drug discovery, diagnostics, and research funding.

China's industrial upgrading was itself an innovation story at the production-system level. The combination of foreign direct investment, export processing zones, domestic infrastructure, and large-scale labor mobilization created a manufacturing platform with global price effects. Korea and Taiwan deepened specialization in semiconductors, displays, handsets, and components.

**5D. Labor markets & human capital:**

Employment shifted toward services in advanced economies and toward manufacturing-plus-services in emerging Asia. U.S. productivity growth was strong but payroll recovery was weak. Germany's labor-market reform debate reflected high unemployment and wage rigidity. China's rural-urban migration supplied factories and construction sites, while India expanded IT and services employment.

Wage dynamics were uneven. High-skilled technology, finance, and professional services recovered faster than low-skilled manufacturing in many advanced economies. In Europe, collective bargaining and labor-market institutions moderated wage adjustment. In Brazil and Russia, disinflation and currency stabilization affected real wages. In China and India, the labor supply was large, but regional and skill mismatches were already visible.

Commodities and emerging-market debt became more attractive as investors searched for yield.

### 7. RISKS, CRISES & BLACK SWANS

Active crises in 2003 included SARS, the Iraq war, the aftermath of Argentina's sovereign default, weak Japanese banks, Brazil's post-election confidence test, euro-area fiscal-rule conflict, and corporate scandals such as Parmalat. Most did not trigger a systemic global downturn, but each exposed fragilities.

Near-misses included the possibility of a longer Iraq conflict causing a much larger oil shock, a deeper SARS impact on Asian supply chains, renewed Japanese financial instability, and loss of market confidence in Brazil. A further risk was deflation in the United States and euro area, which central banks explicitly worried about.

Hindsight risks: U.S. household leverage, mortgage underwriting, structured credit growth, European housing divergence, and global imbalances were all building. These were not universally recognized as systemic in 2003, but the low-rate and search-for-yield environment was an important precondition for later problems.

Policy mistakes visible in retrospect include underestimating housing-credit vulnerabilities, tolerating opaque off-balance-sheet and structured-credit risks, and insufficiently addressing euro-area internal divergence. More debatable is whether central banks should have tightened earlier: at the time, inflation was low, labor markets were weak, and deflation risk was credible.

### 8. YEAR-OVER-YEAR DELTA (2003 vs. 2002)

The most important delta was the shift from hesitant recovery to visible expansion. In 2002, the world economy was still dealing with dot-com losses, corporate scandals, weak investment, and geopolitical uncertainty. In 2003, the first half remained fragile, but the second half delivered a stronger expansion, equity recovery, and trade rebound.

Policy became more forceful. The Federal Reserve moved to a 1 percent funds target and openly discussed the risk of too-low inflation. The ECB cut to 2 percent. The Bank of Japan maintained quantitative easing. Brazil moved from market panic to stabilization. The Bank of England, by contrast, began turning back toward tightening late in the year because of housing and credit.

Economic power continued shifting toward emerging Asia. China grew faster, imported more commodities and intermediate goods, and shaped supply chains more deeply than in 2002. India also accelerated. Russia and Brazil moved from crisis legacies toward stronger commodity- and confidence-supported performance.

Risks changed character. The obvious risks of 2002 and early 2003 were war, SARS, and renewed recession. By late 2003, the larger risks were less visible: credit excess, property-market overvaluation, global imbalances, and excessive confidence in stable low inflation.

### 9. DATA CONFIDENCE & GAPS

High-confidence data: nominal GDP, population, GDP per capita, and many growth/inflation series for OECD economies are relatively reliable because they come from national accounts, OECD, IMF WEO, and World Bank WDI. Interest-rate decisions for the Federal Reserve, ECB, Bank of Japan, and Bank of England are high confidence because they are in central-bank releases.

Medium-confidence data: fiscal balances, debt ratios, and current-account ratios are mostly reliable for advanced economies but differ by definition, including central government vs. general government, gross vs. net debt, cash vs. accrual budget balance, and calendar year vs. fiscal year. Emerging-market fiscal and labor data are less comparable.

Lower-confidence or non-comparable data: unemployment rates for China, India, Mexico, Brazil, and Russia should not be compared directly with U.S. or European ILO-style rates. China's registered urban unemployment excludes large parts of the labor market. India's comparable annual unemployment data are not summarized cleanly by a single 2003 headline number. Brazil's unemployment measure in 2003 depends on survey geography and methodology.

Taiwan caveat: Taiwan is not in World Bank WDI country GDP tables, so its inclusion relies on IMF/Taiwan statistical sources and secondary cross-checks. It sits extremely close to Turkey in current-dollar GDP for 2003, so rank 20 is sensitive to source choice and rounding. If the list is restricted to sovereign WDI countries, Turkey should be included instead.

GDP ranking caveat: current-dollar rankings are not real economic-size rankings in a welfare sense. Exchange-rate movements, especially euro appreciation and dollar weakness, heavily affected 2003 rankings. PPP rankings would put China and India much higher. Sector, corporate, and market data are also uneven: some exchanges publish exact total-return data, while many historical market-return summaries use price indices only.

Weakest evidence in this document: some country-level fiscal and current-account estimates in Section 4 are rounded from IMF/OECD-style macro databases and should be treated as approximate. Corporate revenue rankings are described qualitatively because exact 2003 vs fiscal-year ranking data differ by publication. Several sector assessments are synthesis judgments based on trade, commodity, and national accounts data rather than one unified global sector dataset.

Important missing data: globally comparable household debt, real estate valuations, informal employment, sectoral productivity, small-firm credit, and consumer balance-sheet data are incomplete for 2003. Comparable cross-country policy rates are also difficult because some countries did not use a single modern policy-rate framework.
