# MACRO/MICRO GLOBAL ECONOMIC ASSESSMENT - YEAR: 1924

### 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

1924 was a stabilization year in the world economy. The dominant story was not a single boom or crash, but a shift from postwar disorder toward managed reconstruction, currency stabilization, and renewed cross-border lending. The Dawes Plan was the central European event: it stabilized Germany's reparations schedule, supported the new German currency regime, helped end the Ruhr crisis, and reopened Germany to large-scale foreign lending. This mattered far beyond Germany because reparations, inter-Allied debts, and U.S. capital exports formed a circular flow that shaped the entire late-1920s international financial system.

The global ranking of economies still looked very different from modern rankings. The United States was clearly first by reconstructed real GDP. China, using an explicitly interpolated Maddison-style estimate because the 2023 database lacks a direct 1924 GDP-per-capita value, was probably second. The United Kingdom and India were nearly tied, followed by Germany, France, the former USSR, Japan, Italy, and the Dutch East Indies, now Indonesia. These rankings are real-GDP purchasing-power estimates, not current-dollar national accounts. They should not be read as official 1924 national statistical releases.

The monetary background was the incomplete interwar gold standard. The United States was on gold and held a central reserve role. Britain had not yet returned to gold at the old parity, but policy was already oriented toward that 1925 decision. Many European currencies were still recovering from wartime inflation, fiscal stress, and exchange-rate instability. Germany had just ended hyperinflation through the Rentenmark and fiscal measures, while France, Belgium, Austria, and several other states still faced tensions between budget repair, currency confidence, and social demands.

Microeconomically, 1924 was also a year of industrial consolidation and infrastructure formation. Ford-style mass production, radios, electrical equipment, tabulating machines, telephony, oil, aviation, cinema, and international shipping were important mechanisms through which macro recovery became visible. IBM adopted its modern name in 1924, Compagnie Française des Pétroles was created in France, Telefónica was established in Spain, Imperial Airways was created in Britain, and MGM was formed in the United States. These were not yet the largest global firms by modern standards, but they show the transition from wartime and postwar reconstruction toward communications, energy, data processing, aviation, and mass entertainment.

### 2. KEY THEMES OF 1924

**1. European financial stabilization after postwar disruption.** The Dawes Plan defined the year. It did not solve the reparations problem permanently, but it reduced immediate payments pressure, coordinated external supervision, encouraged evacuation of the Ruhr, and opened Germany to a large foreign loan. Its significance is that it converted a reparations crisis into a credit-dependent recovery. The countries most affected were Germany, France, Britain, Belgium, and the United States. The forward linkage was direct: late-1920s prosperity became increasingly dependent on U.S. lending and confidence in interwar debt and reparations flows.

**2. The United States as the financial center of gravity.** The U.S. economy had the largest reconstructed GDP, a deep capital market, and rising consumer-industrial strength. U.S. lending to Europe was decisive in the Dawes framework and broader capital flows. The U.S. stock market strengthened sharply, with the Dow Jones Industrial Average ending 1924 at 120.51, up about 26.16 percent for the year in common historical index series. The forward linkage was the build-up of a U.S.-centered credit cycle that became a major vulnerability by 1928-1929.

**3. Incomplete return to the gold standard.** 1924 was between the wartime suspension of old gold rules and the attempted restoration of prewar monetary order. The United States was anchored to gold. Britain was preparing to return to gold, which it did in 1925. Germany had stabilized after hyperinflation. The interwar gold standard was not simply the pre-1914 gold standard restored; it included more gold-exchange arrangements, reserve concentration, central bank coordination problems, and political constraints. Countries most affected included the United Kingdom, Germany, France, Austria, Belgium, Japan, and much of Latin America.

**4. Mass production and consumer credit in the United States.** U.S. growth was increasingly driven by automobiles, electrical goods, construction, advertising, radios, and installment credit. This was a microeconomic mechanism behind macro leadership. The Ford Motor Company remained central to autos, while General Motors was expanding a multi-brand, finance-supported competitive model. Household durable consumption and securities-market participation linked industrial output, consumer confidence, and finance.

**5. Colonial and export-economy dependence.** India, Indonesia, Argentina, Brazil, Canada, Australia, Mexico, and many smaller economies were still tied to commodity exports, imperial finance, shipping, and sterling or dollar markets. Their growth was sensitive to agricultural prices, harvests, freight rates, and capital access. The forward linkage was vulnerability to the commodity-price and trade collapse of 1929-1933.

**6. Soviet NEP recovery and statistical uncertainty.** The former USSR was recovering under the New Economic Policy after civil war and war communism. Agriculture, small trade, and light industry revived, but the statistical basis is weaker than for Western Europe or North America. The GDP figures should be read as approximate reconstructed values, not contemporary market-based national accounts. The forward linkage was the policy turn toward forced industrialization later in the decade.

**7. Communications, aviation, oil, and cinema as emerging network industries.** 1924 saw important corporate formation in data processing, telecommunications, oil, civil aviation, and entertainment. IBM, Telefónica, CFP, Imperial Airways, and MGM indicate a broader shift toward network-scale firms. The effect was not immediate macro dominance in 1924, but these sectors became strategic in later decades.

**3B. Monetary & Financial Conditions:**

The international monetary system was in a transition phase. The United States operated with gold convertibility and deep capital markets. Britain was trying to restore sterling credibility ahead of the 1925 return to gold. Germany had stabilized the currency after the 1923 hyperinflation, supported by domestic fiscal measures and the Dawes framework. France remained under fiscal and exchange-rate strain, with the franc still vulnerable until later stabilization.

Precise comparable policy rates are difficult because central-bank instruments differed. Bank Rate in Britain, Federal Reserve discount rates in the United States, Reichsbank operations in Germany, and Bank of Japan instruments are not directly equivalent. The safe conclusion is that credit was easing compared with crisis conditions, but international lending remained highly confidence-sensitive.

Major stock-market data are uneven. The Dow Jones Industrial Average ended 1924 at 120.51, a 26.16 percent annual rise in common historical index series. Unofficial Nikkei-style retrospective calculations put Japan's market up about 7.10 percent for 1924, but the modern Nikkei 225 was not officially calculated until after World War II. For London, Paris, Berlin, and other exchanges, comprehensive annual index data are not consistently available in this document.

**3C. Trade & Geopolitics:**

Trade policy remained shaped by postwar debts, reparations, exchange controls, shipping networks, and imperial preference debates. The Dawes Plan was the most important economic-diplomatic event because it changed Germany's relationship with creditors and occupied territories. The United States also passed the Immigration Act of 1924, which reduced immigration through national-origins quotas and had long-run implications for labor supply and trans-Pacific relations, especially with Japan.

Regional integration in the modern sense was limited. The European Economic Community did not yet exist. ASEAN did not exist. Trade networks were instead organized through empire, bilateral finance, shipping, commodity exchanges, and gold-standard constraints. The British Empire, French Empire, Dutch East Indies, and U.S.-Latin American relationships mattered more than formal regional trade blocs.

**3D. Commodities & Energy:**

Oil was already strategic, but OPEC did not exist. Production and concessions were shaped by U.S. oil fields, British and Anglo-Persian interests, Dutch-Shell networks, and new French efforts to secure independent oil access. France's creation of Compagnie Française des Pétroles in 1924 reflects the postwar lesson that oil was a national strategic asset.

Agricultural commodities were central for Canada, Argentina, Australia, India, Brazil, Indonesia, and much of Eastern Europe. Wheat, beef, wool, coffee, sugar, rubber, jute, rice, and cotton transmitted global price changes into rural incomes, fiscal receipts, rail freight, and bank credit. Metals and coal remained central to Britain, Germany, Belgium, France, Czechoslovakia, Mexico, and the United States.

**3E. Labor & Demographics:**

Labor data are not comparable across countries in 1924. Definitions of unemployment differed sharply, and many agrarian economies had seasonal underemployment rather than recorded unemployment. Britain and Germany had visible industrial unemployment, while the United States had a more dynamic urban-industrial labor market. Immigration restrictions in the United States altered labor-market expectations and reduced the previous model of mass European migration. Urbanization continued across the United States, Japan, parts of Europe, Latin America, and colonial port economies.

The economically relevant technologies of 1924 were not digital computing in the modern sense. They were automobiles, road systems, radios, telephones, electrification, chemical processing, oil refining, aircraft operations, tabulating machines, cinema, and mass advertising. The United States led in consumer durables and mass production. Germany remained strong in chemicals, engineering, and electrical equipment. Japan continued industrial upgrading, especially textiles and machinery. The Netherlands had important electrical and oil-linked corporate capabilities through Philips and Royal Dutch/Shell.

Infrastructure megaproject data are not available in a global comparable table. However, national patterns are clear: Japan rebuilt after the 1923 earthquake, Spain expanded state-backed infrastructure under Primo de Rivera, France and Belgium continued reconstruction, and settler economies such as Canada and Australia relied on rail, ports, and public works.

**5D. Labor markets & human capital:**

Labor statistics are among the weakest data categories for 1924. Industrial economies recorded unemployment more systematically than agrarian or colonial economies, but definitions differed. Britain had persistent unemployment in coal, shipbuilding, and textiles. Germany's labor market improved after currency stabilization but remained fragile. The United States had expanding employment in consumer durables, construction, and services. India, China, Indonesia, Brazil, and the former USSR had large rural labor forces where seasonal underemployment mattered more than recorded unemployment.

Union strength differed sharply. Britain and parts of Western Europe retained strong organized labor in heavy industry. Italy's independent labor movement was being suppressed under Fascism. The Soviet Union had state-mediated labor institutions rather than independent unions. Migration remained economically important, but the U.S. Immigration Act of 1924 changed Atlantic migration incentives and reduced the earlier model of mass European immigration to the United States.

**6B. Fixed income:**

Comparable 10-year government bond yield tables for 1924 are not available in this document. The key fixed-income event was the Dawes loan to Germany, led by international banking interests and heavily subscribed. More broadly, U.S. and British investors supplied capital to Europe and commodity economies, while domestic public debts from World War I continued to shape fiscal policy. Sovereign debt risk was not evenly priced because exchange-rate regimes, war debts, reparations, and political risk differed by country.

**6C. Currencies:**

Sterling was still below the old prewar gold parity in 1924, with historical dollar-pound data placing the annual dollar value of the pound below the later 1925 parity. Britain returned to gold at the old parity in 1925, not in 1924. Germany stabilized after hyperinflation through the Rentenmark and then the Reichsmark framework. The French franc remained under pressure. Many colonial or commodity economies were linked to sterling, gold, silver, or managed local arrangements. China lacked a unified national currency regime, and silver remained important.

**6D. Alternative assets:**

Gold's official U.S. price was $20.67 per troy ounce. Real-estate data by major city are not available in comparable form. The most economically relevant property conditions were urban reconstruction in Japan, housing and construction in the United States and Britain, and land-credit dynamics in Argentina, Australia, Canada, Brazil, and Mexico. Commodity-linked land values were exposed to export prices and credit conditions.

### 7. RISKS, CRISES & BLACK SWANS

The active crisis of 1924 was the European stabilization problem. The Dawes Plan reduced immediate danger but also created a dependence on U.S. lending. This was not necessarily visible as a crisis in 1924 because the arrangement improved confidence, but hindsight shows it as a structural vulnerability. A sudden stop in U.S. capital flows would later become catastrophic for Germany and the wider reparations-debt system.

Currency risk remained high. Germany had stabilized, but the memory of hyperinflation shaped behavior. France and Belgium still had currency pressures. Britain's attempt to restore gold at the old parity was already a looming policy risk, visible to contemporary critics and later widely judged as costly for employment and export competitiveness.

Commodity risk was another systemic vulnerability. Many top-20 economies depended on wheat, coffee, wool, beef, rubber, sugar, oil, coal, or metals. A broad fall in commodity prices would hit rural incomes, fiscal receipts, rail freight, banks, and external balances. This was not a black swan; it was a built-in exposure.

Financial-market optimism in the United States was a near-term strength and a hindsight vulnerability. The 1924 equity rise and expanding consumer credit did not make a crash inevitable, but they formed part of the financial culture that later intensified leverage, speculation, and asset-price sensitivity.

### 8. YEAR-OVER-YEAR DELTA (1924 vs. 1923)

The most important change from 1923 to 1924 was Germany's move from hyperinflation and Ruhr disruption toward stabilization. The Maddison reconstructed growth rate for Germany was about 17.06 percent, reflecting a rebound from the 1923 breakdown. France, Austria, Czechoslovakia, the Netherlands, and the former USSR also show strong reconstructed growth, but these gains partly reflect recovery from earlier disruption rather than a stable long-run trend.

The U.S. economy moved from recovery into a stronger financial-market phase. The Dow's positive 1924 performance and the role of U.S. lending in the Dawes framework show the country's increasing importance as the marginal supplier of international capital. Compared with 1923, the United States became even more central to European stabilization.

The policy regime also shifted. In 1923, the international problem was acute disorder: German hyperinflation, Ruhr occupation, reparations conflict, and postwar currency instability. In 1924, the problem became managed fragility: stabilization through international loans, central-bank credibility, fiscal discipline, and gold-standard expectations. That was an improvement, but it also created a more interconnected financial system.

Microeconomically, 1924 showed more visible consolidation in new network sectors than 1923. IBM, CFP, Telefónica, Imperial Airways, and MGM all point to an economy in which scale, systems, communications, and distribution mattered increasingly. These changes were not the main macro story of 1924, but they became major mechanisms of 20th-century growth.

### 9. DATA CONFIDENCE & GAPS

The highest-confidence GDP data in this document are the Maddison Project Database 2023 entries for countries with direct 1924 GDP-per-capita and population observations: the United States, United Kingdom, Germany, France, Japan, Italy, Canada, Belgium, Netherlands, Australia, and several European or settler economies. These are still reconstructed real purchasing-power estimates, not official 1924 nominal GDP figures.

The lowest-confidence top-20 entries are China, the former USSR, India, Indonesia, Mexico, Brazil, and other countries where contemporary national accounts were incomplete, colonial, fragmented, or non-market. China is especially important: it is included through a clearly labeled interpolation because MPD 2023 lacks a direct 1924 GDP-per-capita value while reporting population. The former USSR is a reconstructed aggregate for a non-market economy under NEP, and a reasonable uncertainty band may be large. Colonial economies such as India and Indonesia are not fully comparable to independent states because fiscal, monetary, and trade policy were partly controlled externally.

Unemployment data are not comparable across countries and are therefore marked Data not available in country tables. CPI inflation, government debt-to-GDP, current account balances, budget deficits, and policy interest rates are also mostly marked Data not available unless a reliable, comparable country-year value was directly verified. This is intentional. Presenting modern-looking tables with invented 1924 values would be misleading.

The weakest claims in the document are sectoral rankings, corporate scale rankings, and global aggregate values. No verified global top-company-by-revenue or market-cap table for 1924 is included. No single official world GDP or world trade volume number is reported. The qualitative claims are grounded in the cited historical sources, but many precise microeconomic indicators simply do not exist in a comparable form for 1924.
