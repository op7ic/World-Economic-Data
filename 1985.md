### 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The world economy in 1985 was defined by disinflation, a maturing recovery from the early-1980s recession, a peaking U.S. dollar, and the approach of an oil-market break. Global nominal GDP was about $12.6 trillion in IMF current-dollar series, while a World Bank-sourced historical table places world real GDP growth near 3.1 percent. The recovery continued, but contemporary IMF and World Bank commentary described it as uneven: industrial-country growth slowed from 1984, European unemployment stayed high, trade growth weakened, and debtor developing countries remained constrained.

The dominant policy event was the Plaza Accord of September 22, 1985. The United States, Japan, West Germany, France, and the United Kingdom agreed that major non-dollar currencies should appreciate in an orderly way. This did not restore Bretton Woods; it was coordinated management inside a floating-rate system. The goal was to reduce the U.S. external deficit, ease Japanese and West German surplus pressures, and lower the risk of a protectionist trade spiral.

Energy was the other regime-shift story. Oil prices did not collapse during most of 1985, but OPEC discipline weakened as Saudi Arabia moved from price defense toward market-share defense. This mattered for every top economy. Oil importers such as Japan, West Germany, South Korea, India, and much of Europe stood to gain from lower inflation and better terms of trade. Oil exporters such as Saudi Arabia, Mexico, Iran, and the Soviet Union faced revenue and foreign-exchange risks that became severe in 1986.

At the micro level, 1985 was a year of divergent adjustment. U.S. finance, services, technology, and defense grew in influence, while tradable manufacturing suffered from the strong dollar. Japan and West Germany remained highly competitive exporters but were exposed to currency revaluation. China and South Korea showed rapid industrialization. Brazil and Mexico illustrated the debt-overhang problem: large productive economies where inflation, external debt, and scarce foreign exchange distorted firm behavior.

### 2. KEY THEMES OF 1985

**1. Coordinated dollar reversal.** The Plaza Accord was the year's most important macro policy event. It showed that the major floating-currency economies could still coordinate when exchange rates became politically dangerous. The United States wanted relief from a large trade deficit; Japan and West Germany faced surplus pressure; France and the United Kingdom joined to stabilize expectations. The forward link was major: yen and deutsche mark appreciation reshaped 1986 to 1987 trade, monetary policy, and asset markets.

**2. Durable advanced-economy disinflation.** The inflationary regime of the 1970s had largely broken in the United States, Japan, and Western Europe. BIS commentary tied the improvement to lower commodity prices, monetary credibility, and reduced inflation expectations. Yet disinflation did not solve unemployment. Western Europe still faced high joblessness, and the social cost of early-1980s stabilization shaped later debates over labor-market reform.

**3. The oil-price regime was cracking.** OPEC had lost market share after conservation, recession, and non-OPEC supply growth. Saudi Arabia's late-1985 shift toward market share changed producer incentives. The full collapse arrived in 1986, but the causal turn belongs to 1985. The countries most exposed were Saudi Arabia, Mexico, Iran, the USSR, Nigeria, Indonesia, and Venezuela.

**4. Debt and commodity weakness constrained developing economies.** Latin America was still inside the post-1982 debt crisis. Brazil and Mexico had large industrial bases, but inflation, indexation, external debt service, and weak access to voluntary finance shaped investment and consumption. Commodity-exporting countries faced weaker purchasing power and tighter external accounts.

**5. East Asian industrialization accelerated.** Japan was already an advanced industrial leader, but China and South Korea showed the next layer of Asian growth. China expanded through reform-era rural industry and local enterprises. South Korea grew through export manufacturing, state-guided finance, and chaebol scale. This was an early stage of the supply-chain shift that later transformed global manufacturing.

**6. Financial liberalization increased both efficiency and risk.** The United States saw strong equity returns and more restructuring finance. The United Kingdom was preparing for the 1986 Big Bang. Australia operated with a newly floated currency. Sweden's credit liberalization began changing bank lending. These moves improved financial flexibility but also created later leverage, property, and banking vulnerabilities.

**7. European integration and exchange-rate discipline strengthened.** The European Monetary System constrained policy choices, while Spain and Portugal were preparing to join the European Community in 1986. Europe's core problem was balancing disinflation, competitiveness, and high unemployment. The forward link was the Single European Act period and, eventually, monetary union.

### 3. MACROECONOMIC OVERVIEW

**3A. Global Aggregates:** World nominal GDP was about $12.6 trillion in IMF current-dollar data. World real GDP growth was about 3.1 percent in a World Bank-sourced historical table. Global trade momentum slowed sharply from the 1984 rebound, but an exact verified 1985 global trade-volume growth figure was not retrieved. The U.S. dollar remained the central reserve and invoice currency, but its overvaluation became a coordinated policy target. Inflation fell in most advanced economies, while some debtor and developing economies retained high inflation. Commodity prices were generally weak; exact 1985 annual averages for gold, copper, and major agricultural staples are Data not available in this version. Capital flows favored the United States, while debt-crisis countries faced restricted market access.

**3B. Monetary & Financial Conditions:** Major advanced-economy rates generally eased as disinflation became credible. BIS described U.S. monetary relaxation, lower U.S. rates, falling European money-market rates, and lower bond yields. West Germany retained Bundesbank credibility; EMS countries faced exchange-rate constraints. Japan faced a surplus and currency-appreciation shock after Plaza. The United Kingdom combined high unemployment, North Sea oil, anti-inflation policy, and pre-Big Bang financial reform. Equity markets were strong, with the S&P 500 returning about 31.7 percent in 1985.

**3C. Trade & Geopolitics:** U.S. trade deficits and Japanese and German surpluses drove trade tension. Plaza can be read as an anti-protectionist currency agreement as much as a monetary event. The Cold War shaped defense spending, technology controls, and Soviet-bloc trade. The Iran-Iraq War affected oil, shipping risk, and Middle Eastern budgets. Europe prepared for Spain and Portugal's 1986 EC accession. South Africa faced rising international pressure under apartheid; exact sanction-flow values are Data not available.

**3D. Commodities & Energy:** Oil was the key commodity. U.S. crude first-purchase prices stayed around the mid-$20s per barrel during much of 1985, but the market was unstable. OPEC discipline weakened, and Saudi Arabia's market-share strategy prepared the 1986 price collapse. Importers gained disinflation and income benefits; exporters faced fiscal and external stress. North Sea oil supported the UK, Soviet oil and gas supported hard-currency earnings, and Middle Eastern producers faced a strategic price-versus-volume tradeoff.

**3E. Labor & Demographics:** Labor markets diverged. U.S. employment improved after the early-1980s recession. Japan retained low unemployment by OECD standards. Western Europe faced high and persistent unemployment. China and India had very large young populations and needed labor absorption outside low-productivity agriculture. Gulf economies relied on expatriate labor tied to oil revenue. Because definitions vary sharply, unemployment is not treated as a directly comparable cross-country statistic.

### 7. RISKS, CRISES & BLACK SWANS

Active risks included Latin American debt stress, the Iran-Iraq War, high European unemployment, U.S. trade imbalance, oil-market instability, and rising protectionist pressure. The Plaza Accord reduced the risk of a disorderly dollar fall or protectionist spiral, but did not remove global imbalances.

The oil-price collapse was the main crisis building in plain sight. OPEC's loss of market share and Saudi Arabia's frustration with price defense were visible, but the full 1986 price fall was not yet realized. The Soviet exposure to oil revenue, planning inefficiency, and technological lag is a hindsight-labeled vulnerability. Financial liberalization was another hindsight risk: in 1985 it looked like modernization, but later leverage, property, and banking problems showed the downside.

### 8. YEAR-OVER-YEAR DELTA (1985 vs. 1984)

Compared with 1984, 1985 was slower, less balanced, and more policy-coordinated. The United States moved from exceptional rebound toward moderate growth and larger external imbalance. The dollar shifted from a tolerated market outcome to a coordinated policy problem. Oil moved from unstable price defense toward market-share conflict. East Asian industrialization accelerated, especially in China and South Korea. Europe remained constrained by unemployment and exchange-rate discipline, while Spain prepared for EC entry. Brazil and Mexico remained trapped by debt, inflation, and external financing constraints.

The structural breaks were clear: the Plaza Accord changed currency expectations; the OPEC/Saudi shift changed energy expectations; Gorbachev's accession changed the Soviet reform horizon; financial liberalization increased the role of asset prices and credit; and developing-country debt problems persisted rather than resolving naturally with global recovery.

### 9. DATA CONFIDENCE & GAPS

High-confidence figures are the WDI-style current-dollar GDP observations for most market economies and the broad qualitative claims supported by IMF, World Bank, BIS, UNCTAD, OECD, and central-bank-era sources. Medium-confidence areas include West Germany and UK current-dollar GDP where WDI-style and IMF historical tables differ, China because of transition-economy measurement, Iran because of war and exchange controls, and Saudi Arabia because oil price and production changes dominate nominal GDP.

The weakest figure is the Soviet Union's nominal GDP. It is useful for rank context but not directly comparable with market GDP because of administered prices, non-market exchange rates, secrecy, and planning. A 20 to 30 percent uncertainty band should be assumed.
