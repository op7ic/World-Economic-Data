# MACRO/MICRO GLOBAL ECONOMIC ASSESSMENT - YEAR: 1960

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

1960 was a late-postwar expansion year with two conflicting signals. On the surface, world output, industrial production, and trade were still expanding rapidly after the 1958 downturn. The World Bank WDI current-dollar series reports world GDP of about USD 1.37 trillion in 1960. IMF reporting for the year emphasized that world exports rose by roughly 12 percent in value from 1959 to 1960, with volume rising by almost as much. Yet this expansion was uneven. The United States entered a recession that began after the April 1960 business-cycle peak and ran into early 1961, while Western Europe and Japan were still growing quickly.

The dominant macro regime was Bretton Woods. Major currencies were fixed or managed against the U.S. dollar, and the dollar was formally linked to gold at USD 35 per troy ounce. This produced low headline inflation in most advanced economies, but it also exposed a growing structural weakness: the United States was simultaneously the reserve-center country and a deficit country. Gold outflows, balance-of-payments pressure, and debates about international liquidity were already visible in 1960, even though the 1971 breakdown of gold convertibility was still in the future.

The year was also a turning point in energy and resource politics. OPEC was created at the Baghdad Conference in September 1960 by Iran, Iraq, Kuwait, Saudi Arabia, and Venezuela. At the time, the institutional move looked modest beside the power of the international oil companies, but in retrospect it marked the start of producer-state coordination that would reshape the oil market in the 1970s. Commodity prices were generally not in a boom. IMF reporting described average primary-product prices as roughly near 1959 levels, with industrial raw materials firmer than food early in the year but weaker by year-end.

At the country level, the United States remained by far the largest economy. The Soviet Union was almost certainly second in real or purchasing-power terms, but its nominal current-dollar ranking is not directly comparable because the ruble was not a convertible market currency. Germany, the United Kingdom, France, China, Japan, Italy, Canada, and India formed the next tier. Japan's high-growth model accelerated under the political and policy setting that produced the Income Doubling Plan, while China was in the Great Leap Forward crisis, with severe disruption to agriculture and household welfare. The contrast between Japan's market-industrial takeoff and China's centrally planned crisis is one of the sharpest microeconomic contrasts of 1960.

## 2. KEY THEMES OF 1960

### 1. Bretton Woods maturity and early strain

**Significance in economic history:** 1960 sat inside the fixed-exchange-rate system that shaped postwar trade, capital controls, and monetary policy. The dollar served as the reserve anchor, while gold convertibility at USD 35 per ounce was the symbolic foundation of confidence.

**Specific data/events in 1960:** The United States faced recession and balance-of-payments pressure at the same time. Monetary policy could not be set only for domestic output because low U.S. short-term rates risked worsening dollar and gold outflows.

**Countries most affected:** United States, United Kingdom, Germany, France, Japan, Switzerland, Canada, and major dollar-area commodity exporters.

**Forward linkage:** The liquidity problem, dollar overhang, and gold drain of the early 1960s led to the Gold Pool, wider capital controls, special drawing rights, and ultimately the collapse of dollar-gold convertibility in 1971.

### 2. Divergence between U.S. cyclical weakness and non-U.S. industrial expansion

**Significance in economic history:** The postwar growth leadership of Western Europe and Japan became more visible. The U.S. was still dominant, but the recovery economies were catching up through manufacturing productivity, export expansion, and high investment.

**Specific data/events in 1960:** NBER dates show a U.S. recession beginning after the April 1960 peak. IMF reporting, however, shows world exports rising about 12 percent from 1959 to 1960 and trade among manufacturing countries rising especially quickly.

**Countries most affected:** United States, Germany, France, Italy, Japan, Netherlands, Belgium, Sweden, Canada.

**Forward linkage:** The U.S. slowdown gave way to the Kennedy-Johnson expansion, while Europe and Japan continued high growth into the 1960s. The catch-up process became a defining feature of the decade.

### 3. European trade integration and the EEC/EFTA divide

**Significance in economic history:** 1960 deepened Western Europe's institutional split between the European Economic Community, the "Inner Six," and the European Free Trade Association, the "Outer Seven." This shaped tariff policy, production networks, and business location decisions.

**Specific data/events in 1960:** EFTA was established by the Stockholm Convention, signed on 4 January 1960, with the objective of liberalizing trade among member states. IMF reporting noted that EEC countries' purchases from each other rose strongly in 1960.

**Countries most affected:** United Kingdom, Sweden, Switzerland, Austria, Denmark, Norway, Portugal, Germany, France, Italy, Netherlands, Belgium.

**Forward linkage:** The EFTA/EEC divide influenced British trade strategy, later British applications to join the EEC, and the reorganization of European industry around common-market access.

### 4. Energy sovereignty begins to institutionalize

**Significance in economic history:** OPEC's creation was the clearest 1960 marker of the long shift from oil-company dominance toward producer-state bargaining power.

**Specific data/events in 1960:** OPEC was created in Baghdad in September 1960 by five founding states. Posted crude prices were still low by later standards, roughly around USD 3 per barrel for leading benchmark crude oils.

**Countries most affected:** Iran, Iraq, Kuwait, Saudi Arabia, Venezuela, United States, United Kingdom, oil-importing Western Europe, Japan.

**Forward linkage:** OPEC's early years were institution-building years. Its full price-setting importance emerged later, especially in the 1970s oil shocks.

### 5. Developmentalism, planning, and import substitution

**Significance in economic history:** Many large developing economies used state planning, import controls, industrial policy, and public investment as core growth tools. This was not a single model. India, Brazil, Mexico, Argentina, Turkey, and Spain all used different mixes of stabilization, protection, and infrastructure spending.

**Specific data/events in 1960:** India was in the Second Five-Year Plan period, Brazil was completing the Kubitschek developmental push and Brasilia era, Mexico was inside the "stabilizing development" model, Spain was emerging from the 1959 stabilization program, and Turkey was moving through political and macroeconomic stress after its 1958 stabilization.

**Countries most affected:** India, Brazil, Mexico, Argentina, Turkey, Spain, South Africa.

**Forward linkage:** These policies produced mixed legacies: rapid industrialization in some cases, but also inflation, external bottlenecks, and later debt vulnerabilities.

### 6. Centrally planned economies: high measured output, low statistical comparability

**Significance in economic history:** The Soviet bloc remained a huge share of global industrial capacity, but GDP measurement is particularly difficult because plan prices, administered exchange rates, and hidden quality differences distort comparisons.

**Specific data/events in 1960:** The Soviet Union ranked near the top globally under Maddison/PWT-style estimates, but nominal current-dollar conversion is not reliable. China was still recorded as a large economy in current-dollar series, but the Great Leap Forward caused major economic dislocation.

**Countries most affected:** Soviet Union, China, Eastern Europe.

**Forward linkage:** Retrospective estimates show that the apparent strength of central planning in the late 1950s and early 1960s masked large inefficiencies. China later moved toward reforms after 1978, while Soviet growth slowed sharply.

The main macro surprise was not a crash but a divergence. U.S. demand softened, imports fell, and the U.S. trade balance improved, while Europe and Japan maintained stronger industrial momentum. Primary-producing countries saw a less favorable pattern: import demand recovered, but export prices did not strengthen enough to prevent trade-balance pressure in many cases.

### 3B. Monetary & Financial Conditions

The United States entered 1960 with policy constrained by both domestic slowdown and external defense of the dollar. The Federal Reserve had tightened earlier and then faced the problem of easing into recession without intensifying gold outflows. The United Kingdom raised Bank Rate to 5 percent in January 1960, reflecting the classic sterling-area tension between growth, external balance, and exchange-rate credibility.

Germany's Bundesbank operated from a position of strength. The deutsche mark was undervalued relative to Germany's export competitiveness, and the DM was still at roughly 4.20 per U.S. dollar until the March 1961 revaluation. Japan maintained the fixed yen parity of 360 yen per U.S. dollar, which supported export planning and industrial investment. France operated with the new franc from January 1960, replacing 100 old francs with 1 new franc.

Bond markets were still mostly domestic, regulated, and policy-influenced. Yield-curve data for many countries are either not available or not directly comparable. Equity-market data are much better for the United States and Japan than for most other markets. The DJIA closed 1960 down about 9 percent, while the Nikkei 225 rose sharply, reflecting Japan's accelerating growth cycle.

### 3C. Trade & Geopolitics

World trade was a central growth engine. IMF reporting shows a strong 1960 increase in world exports, especially trade among manufacturing countries. The EEC countries increased intra-bloc purchases, while EFTA created a rival free-trade framework among the United Kingdom, Sweden, Switzerland, Austria, Denmark, Norway, and Portugal.

Cold War structure affected technology trade, capital flows, and aid. Soviet bloc trade was institutionally separate from most market-economy trade, although not completely isolated. Decolonization also mattered economically. 1960 was the "Year of Africa" in political history, with many new African states entering international trade and aid systems. Belgium faced major political and economic uncertainty connected to Congo's independence.

### 3D. Commodities & Energy

Oil was cheap and abundant by later standards, but bargaining power was changing. OPEC's creation was a structural event, not a 1960 price shock. The immediate oil market remained dominated by international oil companies and posted prices. Producer governments, however, were beginning to coordinate on tax, royalty, and price issues.

Gold was fixed at USD 35 per ounce under Bretton Woods. That price was not a free-market indicator of inflation expectations in the modern sense because official convertibility and exchange controls shaped the market. Primary commodities were mixed. IMF reporting suggests average primary-product prices for 1960 were roughly near 1959 levels, with weakness later in the year.

### 3E. Labor & Demographics

Labor markets were structurally tight in much of Western Europe and Japan, supporting migration, overtime, and productivity-driven wage growth. West Germany relied increasingly on foreign labor recruitment in the early 1960s. In the United States, the recession weakened labor markets and unemployment rose into 1961.

Demographically, the postwar baby boom was still reshaping school demand, housing, and consumer durables in advanced economies. Urbanization continued across Latin America, India, China, and parts of Africa. The microeconomic effect was a shift in consumption toward housing, appliances, transport, and education in richer countries, and toward basic infrastructure and food security in poorer economies.

The recession that began after the April peak reflected weaker durable-goods demand, inventory adjustment, and earlier monetary tightening. ### 5B. Corporate landscape

A globally comparable 1960 ranking of the largest companies by revenue or market capitalization is not available in this assessment. Large corporations by economic importance included General Motors, Standard Oil of New Jersey, Royal Dutch/Shell, Texaco, U.S. Steel, IBM, AT&T, Unilever, Siemens, Volkswagen, Fiat, Sony, Matsushita, Toyota, Hitachi, and major state-linked energy or industrial firms. This list is illustrative, not a ranked table.

Corporate finance was less globalized than today. Mergers and acquisitions occurred, but the giant cross-border M&A market of later decades did not exist. National champions, regulated utilities, banks, and vertically integrated manufacturers dominated. In developing economies, state enterprises and licensed private firms were major mechanisms of industrial policy.

### 5C. Innovation & technology

The economic frontier in 1960 was shaped by computing, electronics, jet aviation, chemicals, nuclear energy, and materials science. Mainframe computing moved deeper into banking, insurance, government, and large manufacturing firms. The first laser demonstration in 1960 had long-term implications, although its immediate commercial impact was limited. Integrated circuits were still early, but the pathway toward miniaturized electronics was becoming visible.

Infrastructure megaprojects mattered more immediately than laboratory breakthroughs in many countries. Highways, ports, power generation, steel plants, dams, and new cities created demand for construction, heavy machinery, cement, and engineering services.

### 5D. Labor markets & human capital

Sectoral employment continued to move from agriculture into manufacturing and services. This shift was fastest in Japan, Italy, Spain, Brazil, Mexico, and parts of Western Europe. In the United States, the recession produced cyclical labor weakness but did not reverse the long-term move toward services and white-collar work.

Human-capital investment expanded through universities, technical institutes, and vocational training. Japan's Income Doubling Plan explicitly linked growth to education and living standards. Europe's social-market and welfare-state models treated skilled labor as a core industrial asset.

### 6B. Fixed income

Government bond yield data for 1960 are uneven and not directly comparable across countries. The United States and United Kingdom had developed government bond markets, but official rate management, capital controls, and banking regulations shaped yields. In the United Kingdom, short rates rose after Bank Rate moved to 5 percent. In the United States, monetary easing was constrained by concern over gold outflows.

### 6C. Currencies

The key currency fact was fixed parity. The U.S. dollar was the reserve anchor. The pound was at USD 2.80. The yen was at 360 per dollar. The deutsche mark was about 4.20 per dollar until the 1961 revaluation. The Italian lira was about 625 per dollar. The Mexican peso was fixed at 12.50 per dollar. Many developing economies used exchange controls, multiple rates, or administered parities, so published rates often understate scarcity of foreign exchange.

Gold was officially USD 35 per ounce. Real estate was important for household balance sheets, especially in the United States, Western Europe, Australia, and Japan, but comparable major-city price data for 1960 are not available in this assessment. Art, collectibles, and private equity were not macro-scale asset classes in the modern sense.

## 7. RISKS, CRISES & BLACK SWANS

1. **U.S. recession:** Knowable in real time as industrial and labor weakness. Its global effect came through weaker imports, policy uncertainty, and dollar concerns.

2. **Bretton Woods dollar-gold vulnerability:** Partly visible in 1960. Hindsight shows it was the beginning of a long systemic problem, but the exact 1971 endpoint was not inevitable from the 1960 perspective.

3. **China's Great Leap Forward crisis:** Severe disruption was already occurring. The depth of the demographic and welfare consequences became clearer later, and statistical uncertainty remains high.

4. **Oil producer coordination:** OPEC did not immediately create a price shock, but hindsight identifies 1960 as a major institutional turning point.

5. **Decolonization and political risk:** Congo's independence crisis affected Belgium and mining-finance expectations. Wider African decolonization changed aid, trade, and investment relationships.

6. **Latin American inflation and external constraints:** Brazil and Argentina showed the vulnerability of development models financed by credit expansion and protected markets.

7. **European bloc fragmentation:** The EEC/EFTA split created uncertainty about Britain's long-term trade strategy and European industrial organization.

## 8. YEAR-OVER-YEAR DELTA (1960 vs. 1959)

World trade accelerated sharply relative to 1959, with IMF reporting about 12 percent value growth in exports from 1959 to 1960. However, momentum weakened late in the year as activity slowed in several industrial countries.

The United States shifted from recovery into recession. This changed the pattern of world trade: U.S. imports fell, while U.S. exports to some markets improved. Europe and Japan were still expanding, so the global economy did not move in lockstep with the U.S. cycle.

Europe's institutional landscape changed. EFTA was created in 1960, deepening the contrast with the EEC. Intra-EEC trade became increasingly important, while the United Kingdom's position outside the EEC became a larger strategic issue.

Energy politics changed structurally with OPEC's formation. The 1960 oil market itself remained calm, but producer coordination was new and important.

China's Great Leap Forward moved from ambitious mobilization toward visible economic crisis. The shift was especially severe in agriculture and household welfare.

Spain moved from the immediate shock of its 1959 stabilization toward the conditions for the 1960s growth acceleration. Brazil moved toward the end of the Kubitschek era with infrastructure achievements but rising inflation and external stress.

## 9. DATA CONFIDENCE & GAPS

**High-confidence data:** World Bank WDI/API current-dollar GDP for market economies with national accounts coverage; IMF world trade aggregates for 1958 to 1960; OPEC founding date and founding members; NBER U.S. business-cycle dates; Bretton Woods institutional rules; EFTA founding date and objective.

**Medium-confidence data:** Population and GDP per capita figures used in country headers, because they are rounded and in some cases depend on postwar territorial definitions. Latin American GDP and inflation comparisons are medium-confidence because exchange controls and data revisions can change the interpretation.

**Low-confidence data:** Soviet Union current-dollar GDP, China GDP and sectoral performance during the Great Leap Forward, Eastern bloc GDP rankings, unemployment comparisons across almost all non-U.S. countries, government debt/GDP, budget balance/GDP, and current-account balance/GDP for many countries.

**Historical data conflicts:** WDI current-dollar GDP, Maddison Project historical GDP, and Penn World Table real-output measures answer different questions. WDI current-dollar GDP reflects official national accounts and exchange-rate conversion; Maddison and PWT are designed for long-run real-income comparisons. For non-market economies, current-dollar conversion can be misleading.

**Weakest claims in this document:** Borderline top-20 ranking near positions 18 to 20; exact sectoral growth claims outside the United States, Japan, and Western Europe; corporate-size comparisons; financial-market returns outside the U.S. and Japan.

**Important missing data:** Comparable 1960 unemployment rates; validated 1960 policy rates for all top 20; complete global corporate market-cap rankings; standardized government-debt and budget-balance ratios; reliable real-estate prices; high-frequency bond-market yields for most countries; and consistent Soviet bloc national accounts in market terms.
