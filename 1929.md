# MACRO/MICRO GLOBAL ECONOMIC ASSESSMENT - YEAR: 1929

### 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

1929 was the last year of the interwar expansion and the first year of the Great Depression. The year did not begin as a synchronized global collapse. Several economies were still expanding in real terms, including the United States, France, Japan, Italy, India, Spain, Argentina, and the USSR in the Maddison Project Database estimates. Yet the system's vulnerabilities were already visible: high U.S. interest rates, stock-market leverage, Germany's dependence on foreign capital, commodity-exporter exposure, and the rigidity of the international gold-exchange standard.

The defining event was the Wall Street crash of October 1929, but the deeper economic mechanism was monetary and financial contraction under gold-standard rules. The Federal Reserve's anti-speculation tightening helped pull interest rates upward in the United States and, through gold-standard reserve defense, pressured other central banks. The U.S. recession is dated from August 1929 in the later NBER chronology, so the crash should be treated as an amplifier and confidence shock rather than the only starting point.

The country ranking in this file is historical, not a retroactive modern ranking. It uses Maddison Project Database 2023 GDP per capita and population, multiplied to produce real PPP GDP in 2011 international dollars. On that basis, the top five economies in 1929 were the United States, China, Germany, the United Kingdom, and India. The USSR, France, Japan, Italy, and the Dutch East Indies completed the top ten. These estimates are less certain for China, India, the USSR, the Dutch East Indies, Brazil, Mexico, and Poland than for the United States and Western Europe.

Microeconomically, 1929 was a year of divergent sector stories. U.S. autos, radio, utilities, and consumer durables were still icons of modernity even as securities speculation broke. Germany's chemicals and machinery base was world-class but externally financed. Japan's zaibatsu and textile-export system were vulnerable to silk-price declines. Australia, Canada, Argentina, Brazil, the Dutch East Indies, and India were exposed to falling world commodity prices. The USSR was not hit through stock markets in the same way, but 1929 marked a violent institutional break through forced collectivization and the First Five-Year Plan.

The main structural break from 1928 to 1929 was not that all output immediately collapsed. It was that the policy and financial regime changed from expansion with manageable imbalances to contraction under brittle institutions. The year converted sectoral and financial vulnerabilities into a global depression mechanism that would become unmistakable in 1930-1932.

### 2. KEY THEMES OF 1929

**1. The gold-standard constraint turned local tightening into international tightening.** The interwar gold-exchange standard linked monetary policy through reserves and exchange-rate defense. U.S. tightening aimed partly at speculation, but other central banks faced pressure to follow or risk gold outflows. This was historically significant because it showed how a fixed-exchange-rate regime can transmit deflation when the anchor economy tightens. The countries most affected in 1929 were the United States, Germany, Britain, Japan, Canada, Australia, and European gold-standard economies. The forward linkage was the deflation of 1930-1932 and the later recovery advantage of countries that left gold earlier.

**2. The Wall Street crash was a financial accelerator, not the sole cause.** Equity prices broke in October after a speculative boom in brokers' loans, investment trusts, utilities, radios, and other growth narratives. The crash reduced wealth, damaged bank and investor balance sheets, and changed expectations. Yet industrial production and U.S. business conditions had already softened by late summer. The countries most directly affected were the United States and Canada, but the shock spread through London, Berlin, Paris, Tokyo, Brussels, and commodity-exporting economies. The forward linkage was the banking and debt-deflation spiral of the early 1930s.

**3. Commodity exporters faced an external-income shock.** Agricultural and raw-material prices weakened in 1929 and then fell sharply in 1930-1932. The shock hit Australia through wool and wheat, Canada through wheat and resource exports, Argentina through grains and meat, Brazil through coffee, the Dutch East Indies through sugar, rubber, copra, oil, and tin, and India through jute, cotton, and agricultural incomes. The significance is that the Depression was not only a factory and finance crisis; it was also a terms-of-trade crisis for primary producers.

**4. Germany was the key European financial fault line.** Germany's late-1920s recovery depended heavily on foreign lending under the Dawes Plan environment. By 1929, the Young Plan reshaped reparations, while U.S. capital-market tightening made external funding more fragile. German GDP was already slightly negative in MPD's estimate, and short rates were high in JST data. The forward linkage was the 1931 Central European banking crisis and the collapse of the reparations-credit architecture.

**5. Non-market and colonial economies moved through different channels.** The USSR's 1929 transformation was driven by policy coercion, not by private credit contraction. China used a silver standard and had fragmented political-economic institutions. India and the Dutch East Indies were colonial economies where trade, tax, currency, and corporate structures were shaped by imperial systems. This matters because a single depression narrative does not fit every top-20 economy.

**6. Modern consumer and technology sectors were real, but overcapitalized.** Radio, autos, electrical goods, aviation, chemicals, and branded consumer goods were not imaginary. They represented real productivity and household changes. The problem was that asset prices, holding-company structures, and margin finance extrapolated growth too aggressively. The forward linkage was a 1930s investment collapse that did not erase the long-run importance of these technologies.

**7. Fiscal orthodoxy dominated before mass stabilization policy.** Most governments still prioritized balanced budgets, currency defense, and debt service. The United States had a federal surplus in JST data, Britain was burdened by very high debt, Australia faced external-debt pressure, and Germany faced reparations and foreign borrowing constraints. The forward linkage was the policy debate over austerity, public works, exchange controls, and eventually Keynesian ideas.

### 3. MACROECONOMIC OVERVIEW

**3A. Global Aggregates:**

- **World GDP:** Data not available as a single official nominal world GDP aggregate for 1929. A coverage-limited Maddison Project 2023 country sum for the 58 entities with a 1929 observation is about 5.654 trillion 2011 international dollars. This is not a complete world total. The top 20 economies in this file sum to about 5,078.2 billion 2011 international dollars.
- **Growth rate:** Data not available for a fully consistent all-country world growth rate. On the intersection of countries with both 1928 and 1929 MPD observations in the downloaded data, aggregate real GDP rose about 4.0 percent. This likely overstates the stability of the world economy because 1929 is a transition year and country coverage changes.
- **Global trade volume:** Data not available as a single verified 1929 global volume index in the compiled sources. The year is best treated as the peak before collapse. Later research reports that import and export volumes in industrialized nations fell about 30 percent from 1929 to 1932, while the value of world trade fell even more because prices also collapsed.
- **Reserve currency dynamics:** The world was on the interwar gold-exchange standard, not Bretton Woods. Sterling and the U.S. dollar were the key reserve and settlement currencies, with gold as the ultimate reserve asset. France accumulated gold after franc stabilization, adding to asymmetry in the system.
- **Global inflation trends:** Advanced-economy CPI patterns were mixed. JST reports U.S. CPI inflation at 0.0 percent, U.K. inflation at about -0.9 percent, Germany at about +1.3 percent, France at about +6.4 percent, Japan at about -2.3 percent, and Australia at 0.0 percent. Commodity prices were already weakening and then fell steeply after 1929.
- **Key commodity prices:** Gold was fixed at USD 20.67 per troy ounce under the U.S. gold standard. A USGS copper series reports U.S. producer copper at 18.35 cents per pound in 1929, falling to 13.23 cents in 1930. One historical crude-oil series reports average crude around USD 1.27 per barrel in 1929. Agricultural staples were highly exposed to falling world demand and inventories; precise global wheat, coffee, jute, and wool price series differ by source.
- **International capital flows:** Directionally, capital moved away from the easy late-Dawes environment. U.S. foreign lending became less reliable, Germany faced a sudden-stop risk, and Latin American and commodity exporters faced reduced external finance. Capital-flow data by country are incomplete, but the direction of stress is clear from German, Argentine, Brazilian, Australian, and Canadian evidence.

**3B. Monetary & Financial Conditions:**

The main regime was the gold-exchange standard. Bretton Woods did not exist. Exchange rates were either formally or informally anchored to gold, sterling, the dollar, or silver. The United States tightened financial conditions to restrain speculation. Britain raised Bank Rate in 1929, including a move to 6.5 percent in September, while Germany and Japan operated with high short rates in the JST series. France had lower interest rates and gold inflows after stabilization.

Major interest-rate environment in 1929, using JST annual short rates where available: United States 7.61 percent, United Kingdom 5.26 percent, Germany 8.27 percent, France 3.47 percent, Japan 6.68 percent, Italy 6.69 percent, Spain 5.50 percent, Netherlands 4.82 percent, Belgium 4.35 percent, and Australia 4.50 percent. Canadian short-rate data are not available in the JST entry used here; Canadian long government yields were about 4.93 percent.

Equity markets reversed sharply. JST capital-gain series show 1929 equity-price declines of about 7.6 percent in the United States, 18.7 percent in the United Kingdom, 26.4 percent in Germany, 16.6 percent in Japan, 15.0 percent in Italy, 31.9 percent in Belgium, and 10.1 percent in Australia. France's market was far less negative in this annual series, and Spain's equity capital-gain decline was small. These annual figures understate the drama of the U.S. October crash because they smooth across the full year.

Bond markets signaled divergent risks. Long government yields in JST were about 3.32 percent in the United States, 4.60 percent in Britain, 6.48 percent in Germany, 3.61 percent in France, 6.25 percent in Japan, 6.50 percent in Italy, 4.34 percent in Spain, 4.93 percent in Canada, 4.01 percent in the Netherlands, 4.53 percent in Belgium, and 5.23 percent in Australia. Germany and Italy therefore carried high long-rate burdens, while France and the United States had lower long yields.

**3C. Trade & Geopolitics:**

Trade policy was becoming more defensive, although the most famous U.S. tariff shock, Smoot-Hawley, was enacted in 1930 rather than 1929. The forward linkage is important: 1929 was the year in which the political economy of protection hardened as prices and employment weakened. Reparations were also a major trade and payments issue. The Young Plan negotiated in 1929 altered Germany's reparations schedule, but it did not remove the external debt structure that later failed.

Regional integration in the modern postwar sense was limited. There was no EEC, no ASEAN, and no Bretton Woods. Imperial preference, colonial trade channels, and sterling-area relationships mattered more. British India, Australia, Canada, and the Dutch East Indies were all tied to imperial or colonial trade and monetary systems, though in different ways.

Geopolitically, 1929 sat between the First World War settlement and the crises of the 1930s. The USSR was undertaking a domestic command-economy revolution. China was politically fragmented despite Nationalist consolidation. Latin American economies were exposed to U.S. and British capital-market conditions. These structures determined how the financial shock traveled.

**3D. Commodities & Energy:**

Oil was not yet an OPEC-era market. OPEC did not exist. Production and pricing were shaped by U.S. fields, Royal Dutch/Shell, Anglo-Persian, Standard Oil successors, and colonial concessions. Crude prices were low by later standards, around USD 1 to USD 1.50 per barrel in commonly cited historical series, and the sector faced overcapacity tendencies.

Metals were cyclical. Copper's 1929 U.S. producer price of 18.35 cents per pound was near the end of the late-1920s strength and then fell sharply. Gold's fixed U.S. official price of USD 20.67 per ounce made gold flows, not gold-price changes, the key monetary variable. Silver mattered especially for China and Mexico because their monetary systems and export sectors were sensitive to silver prices.

Agriculture was the central social shock absorber. Wheat, wool, coffee, sugar, rubber, jute, cotton, and meat were tied to rural incomes and government revenues across Canada, Australia, Argentina, Brazil, India, China, and the Dutch East Indies. Falling agricultural prices turned a financial crisis into a rural-debt and political crisis.

**3E. Labor & Demographics:**

Labor-market data are not comparable across countries. JST gives standardized historical unemployment estimates for some advanced economies: United States 3.2 percent, United Kingdom 7.3 percent, Germany 5.9 percent, France 1.0 percent, Italy about 1.65 percent, Canada about 3.05 percent, Netherlands about 5.66 percent, Belgium 0.8 percent, and Australia 6.7 percent. These figures use historical definitions that vary, so they should not be ranked as if they were modern labor-force surveys.

Demographic scale shaped the GDP ranking. China and India ranked second and fifth in total GDP because of population, despite low per-capita income. The United States ranked first because it combined a large population with very high productivity. Japan's population and industrialization made it eighth. Urbanization continued in the United States, Japan, the USSR, and parts of Europe, while most of China, India, Brazil, Mexico, and the Dutch East Indies remained heavily rural.

Migration mattered economically. The U.S. downturn soon affected Mexican workers and cross-border labor markets. Soviet industrialization pulled and coerced labor into cities. Rural distress in Japan, India, China, and Eastern Europe intensified the social consequences of price deflation.

**Economic narrative:**

The United States was the largest economy in the 1929 MPD ranking and the main transmitter of the year's financial shock. Real output was still high, mass production industries were near their late-1920s peak, and nominal GNP in the JST series was roughly USD 104.6 billion. The recession began in August according to the later NBER chronology, before the October stock market crash, which means the financial break did not start the entire downturn but sharply worsened confidence, wealth, and credit conditions.

Policy was central to the U.S. story. The Federal Reserve tightened in response to stock-market speculation and broker-loan growth, with short rates high by late-decade standards. In a gold-standard world, that tightening radiated outward because other central banks had to defend reserves. Fiscal policy was not yet countercyclical in the later New Deal sense. The federal budget was still in surplus in the JST data, so the first response was orthodoxy rather than stabilization.

**Micro/sectoral highlights:**

- Automobiles, electrical equipment, utilities, chemicals, and consumer durables were the emblematic boom sectors, although autos had already become cyclical and replacement-driven.
- Radio was a genuine technology adoption story as well as a speculative equity story; radio-equipment sales reached late-1920s highs.
- Broker loans, investment trusts, and utilities holding companies were central financial-market mechanisms behind the bubble and crash.
- The largest U.S. revenue companies included Standard Oil of New Jersey, General Motors, Ford, and U.S. Steel; comparable global revenue rankings are not complete.

**5B. Corporate landscape:**

Comparable global company rankings by revenue or market capitalization for 1929 are not available in the compiled sources. For the United States, revenue lists identify Standard Oil of New Jersey, General Motors, Ford, and U.S. Steel among the largest firms. This U.S. list should not be treated as a global list because European, Japanese, colonial, and private-company reporting conventions differed.

The most important 1929 corporate combination was the agreement between Margarine Unie and Lever Brothers to create Unilever, signed in September 1929 and implemented as a unified group in 1930. This was a major consumer-goods consolidation across food fats, soap, branding, distribution, and colonial markets. Other large corporate systems included Royal Dutch/Shell, I.G. Farben, Siemens, AEG, Mitsui, Mitsubishi, Standard Oil successors, General Electric, RCA, and major railway and shipping groups.

Bankruptcies in 1929 were not yet the defining story at global scale, but failures and forced restructurings rose after the crash. The important 1929 micro-financial mechanisms were margin loans, investment trusts, holding-company leverage, commodity inventories, and foreign-credit dependence.

**5C. Innovation & technology:**

Radio was the clearest new mass technology. It had real adoption, advertising, household, and entertainment value, but also became a vehicle for speculative valuations. Automobile production and road infrastructure transformed labor markets, suburbs, oil demand, steel, rubber, and consumer credit. Aviation was commercially small but strategically important. Chemicals, electrical equipment, telephony, and mechanized production continued to diffuse.

The USSR's innovation pattern was different: it emphasized imported machinery, forced industrial learning, electrification, and heavy-industry capacity. Japan's zaibatsu supported industrial diversification. Germany remained a world leader in chemicals, optics, electrical machinery, and applied engineering. France, Britain, Czechoslovakia, and Italy had important industrial clusters but faced different capital and demand constraints.

Patent and R&D data comparable across the top 20 are not available in the compiled sources. Infrastructure megaproject data are also fragmented, though roads, electrification, railways, ports, and Soviet industrial construction were the most economically important categories.

**5D. Labor markets & human capital:**

The main labor-market shift was from late-cycle tightness or moderate employment toward rising insecurity. Britain and Australia already had high unemployment by contemporary advanced-economy standards. Germany had significant unemployment before the deepest crisis. The United States still looked relatively strong in annual unemployment data, but the recession had started by late summer and job losses would rise rapidly in 1930.

Sectorally, agriculture still employed most workers in China, India, the USSR, Brazil, Mexico, the Dutch East Indies, and parts of Eastern Europe. Manufacturing employment mattered most in the United States, Germany, Britain, France, Japan, Italy, Belgium, Czechoslovakia, and the Netherlands. Union and labor-movement dynamics were strong in Britain, Germany, Italy under state control, Australia, and parts of Latin America, but definitions and data differ substantially.

Education and skill formation were uneven. The United States, Germany, Britain, France, Japan, and Czechoslovakia had deeper industrial skill bases. China, India, Brazil, Mexico, and the Dutch East Indies had much lower mass education levels and more rural underemployment. The USSR was mobilizing literacy and technical education, but with coercive labor allocation and severe welfare costs.

**6B. Fixed income:**

JST long government bond yields for 1929 were: United States 3.32 percent, United Kingdom 4.60 percent, Germany 6.48 percent, France 3.61 percent, Japan 6.25 percent, Italy 6.50 percent, Spain 4.34 percent, Canada 4.93 percent, Netherlands 4.01 percent, Belgium 4.53 percent, and Australia 5.23 percent. Germany, Italy, Japan, and Australia therefore faced relatively expensive long-term funding compared with the United States and France.

Sovereign debt events in 1929 were mostly vulnerabilities rather than defaults. Germany's reparations framework was renegotiated under the Young Plan. Australia faced external debt and current-account pressure. Latin American borrowers faced the loss of easy foreign credit. The major sovereign and banking crises materialized more fully in 1931-1933.

**6C. Currencies:**

The main currency regime was gold-standard rigidity. The U.S. dollar was fixed to gold at USD 20.67 per troy ounce. Sterling was fixed to gold at the prewar parity until the United Kingdom left gold in 1931. France had stabilized the franc in 1928 and accumulated gold. Japan was preparing to return to gold in January 1930, a policy that increased deflationary pressure. Argentina abandoned gold convertibility in December 1929, making it an early mover away from strict gold orthodoxy.

Major exchange-rate series in JST include RM 4.184 per USD for Germany, £0.2059 per USD for Britain, ¥2.17 per USD for Japan, ₤19.108 per USD for Italy, Pta 6.82 per USD for Spain, C$1.009 per USD for Canada, ƒ2.4899 per USD for the Netherlands, and BFr 35.939 per USD for Belgium. China is better understood through silver rather than gold exchange-rate conventions.

**6D. Alternative assets:**

Gold was a monetary anchor rather than a floating alternative asset. Real estate data by major city are not available in standardized form in the compiled sources. U.S. urban and Florida real-estate speculation had already weakened before 1929, while farm land and rural collateral became increasingly stressed as commodity prices fell. Silver mattered for China and Mexico, but comparable investment-return data are not available.

### 7. RISKS, CRISES & BLACK SWANS

**Active crises during 1929:** The major active crisis was the U.S. stock-market crash, especially the late-October break. Germany faced a foreign-credit and reparations vulnerability. Australia, Brazil, Argentina, Canada, India, and the Dutch East Indies faced commodity-price and external-income shocks. The USSR experienced a state-driven social and economic crisis through collectivization and forced industrialization.

**Near-misses and vulnerabilities:** Banks had not yet failed in the massive numbers of 1930-1933, but the ingredients were present: falling collateral values, broker-loan exposure, maturity mismatch, foreign-currency debt, and commodity inventories. The gold standard reduced policy flexibility. Fiscal orthodoxy made public deficits politically difficult even as private demand weakened.

**Policy mistakes visible in retrospect:** Hindsight identifies tight monetary policy, defense of gold parities, delayed lender-of-last-resort action, and fiscal austerity as mechanisms that deepened the Depression. This is a hindsight judgment. In 1929, many policymakers viewed currency defense, balanced budgets, and anti-speculation tightening as prudent rather than dangerous.

**Tail risks that materialized:** The equity crash materialized. The sudden-stop risk to Germany began materializing. Commodity-price collapse began materializing. Soviet collectivization's human and production risks began materializing. The full banking crisis, sovereign debt crisis, and world-trade collapse materialized later, especially in 1931-1932.

### 8. YEAR-OVER-YEAR DELTA (1929 vs. 1928)

The main structural change from 1928 to 1929 was the move from late-cycle expansion to contraction risk. In 1928, the world economy still looked broadly functional: U.S. finance was buoyant, Germany still benefited from the Dawes-era capital-flow model, France had stabilized, and commodity exporters could still rely on world demand. In 1929, those same mechanisms became transmission channels for crisis.

Monetary conditions tightened. The Federal Reserve's anti-speculation stance and gold-standard pressures raised the cost of defending currencies. Britain and other central banks faced the dilemma of supporting domestic activity or defending gold. Germany shifted from heavy capital import dependence toward sudden-stop vulnerability.

Financial-market psychology changed abruptly. Before October, U.S. equities and investment trusts were still treated by many investors as high-growth assets. After October, liquidity preference, balance-sheet defense, and distrust of leverage became dominant. The effect was not confined to Wall Street because global capital markets were connected through London, Berlin, Paris, and New York.

Commodity exporters moved from price risk to income crisis. Brazil's coffee system, Australia's wool and wheat exports, Argentina's beef and grain complex, Canada's wheat economy, India's jute and cotton channels, and the Dutch East Indies' plantation exports all became exposed to a synchronized fall in global purchasing power.

Political-economic risk increased. In the USSR, 1929 was the decisive year of forced collectivization and heavy-industry mobilization. In Spain, the dictatorship was approaching collapse. In Brazil and Argentina, external shocks weakened existing political arrangements. In India, economic distress fed the context for intensified anti-colonial mobilization in 1930.

### 9. DATA CONFIDENCE & GAPS

**Most reliable statistical coverage:** The United States, United Kingdom, Germany, France, Japan, Italy, Spain, Canada, Netherlands, Belgium, and Australia have relatively strong long-run macrofinancial coverage in the JST database, although even these series are reconstructed and may differ from national statistical-office revisions.

**Moderate confidence:** Argentina, Czechoslovakia, Brazil, Mexico, Poland, and the Dutch East Indies have usable historical estimates for GDP in MPD, but fewer standardized annual macro indicators. Sectoral and trade claims are stronger than unemployment, fiscal, or CPI claims.

**Lowest confidence:** China, India, and the USSR require special caution. China and India have large population-based GDP estimates but limited standardized annual macro indicators. Soviet output estimates are especially uncertain because prices, incentives, and output reports were not market-based. A plus or minus 20 to 30 percent uncertainty range is reasonable for Soviet-bloc and non-market comparisons.

**Historical data conflicts:** MPD real GDP growth and JST real GDP-per-capita growth do not always match, as seen in Canada, Japan, and the Netherlands. This reflects different underlying series, population treatment, benchmark methods, and national-account reconstructions. The country ranking in Section 4 uses MPD 2023 consistently, while macro tables use JST only for indicators that MPD does not provide.

**Unavailable data:** A single official world nominal GDP for 1929 is not available. A fully consistent global trade-volume index for 1929 is not available in the compiled sources. Comparable unemployment rates for all top-20 economies are not available. Corporate global revenue and market-cap rankings are not available. Real-estate returns by city, patent counts by country for the top 20, household debt by country, and many current-account and fiscal balances are not available.

**Weakest claims:** The weakest quantitative areas are commodity prices outside gold and copper, corporate rankings outside the United States, unemployment outside JST economies, and macro indicators for colonial and non-market economies. These have been marked as Data not available when the compiled source base does not support a figure.
