### 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

1940 was the year in which the world economy became visibly subordinated to war. The Second World War was no longer a regional European conflict. The fall of France, the occupation of the Low Countries, the Battle of Britain, the tightening of shipping constraints, and the intensification of state procurement shifted the global economy from Depression recovery toward command, rationing, and military production. The United States was still formally outside the war, but its industrial base was already moving into an armaments-led expansion. Canada, the United Kingdom, and Australia became larger suppliers in the Allied production network, while Germany, Italy, Japan, and the Soviet Union operated under increasingly militarized economic systems.

The global economy in Maddison's 2010 reconstruction reached 4,502,584 million 1990 international Geary-Khamis dollars in 1940. That figure is not nominal world GDP and should not be converted mechanically into current dollars. It is a purchasing-power historical reconstruction intended to compare real output over time. The United States alone accounted for roughly one fifth of the Maddison world total, while the Soviet Union, Germany, the United Kingdom, China, India, and Japan formed the next tier. China's exact 1940 level is uncertain in the extracted annual table, but its population and surrounding GDP observations make clear that it was too large to omit analytically.

The biggest structural change from 1939 was the collapse of normal international trade. Continental Europe was cut off from many prewar suppliers and customers. Britain became more dependent on the Empire and the United States. South America and parts of Asia faced the disappearance of important European markets. Gold and capital flowed toward the United States, while exchange controls and bilateral clearing arrangements became more important than free international payments. The world was not yet in the Bretton Woods system; that institutional framework would be negotiated only in 1944.

At the micro level, 1940 was a story of sectoral divergence. Aircraft, shipbuilding, steel, chemicals, machine tools, transport logistics, and state-financed construction expanded in mobilizing economies. Civilian retail, imported consumer goods, travel, luxury services, and many small open-economy sectors contracted. Households experienced the same macro shock through rationing, shortages, employment shifts, price controls, and changing access to credit and imports.

### 2. KEY THEMES OF 1940

**1. War mobilization replaced ordinary recovery.** The significance of 1940 lies in the transition from post-Depression recovery to planned production. In the United States, real output rose strongly while idle labor and capacity still existed. In the United Kingdom and Canada, procurement and war finance drove faster industrial output. In Germany, Italy, Japan, and the USSR, state allocation was already central. The forward link is clear: by 1941-1943, the scale of mobilization would transform employment, public debt, industrial organization, and technology.

**2. The collapse of open European trade.** Germany's occupation of much of Western Europe and Britain's maritime war severed the prewar structure of commerce. The BIS described the disappearance of Continental European demand for many raw materials and foodstuffs from extra-European countries and the shift of British trade toward the Empire and the United States. Latin America was especially exposed because Europe had been a major customer. The forward linkage was import substitution, U.S.-led hemispheric finance, and the postwar reordering of trade institutions.

**3. Financial power shifted toward the dollar and gold center in the United States.** Gold imports into the United States were very large in 1940, and capital flowed from insecure Europe toward North America and parts of South America. This did not mean a Bretton Woods dollar order already existed, but it did mean the U.S. financial position strengthened as Europe's private and public balance sheets became more stressed. The forward linkage was the postwar dollar shortage and U.S. dominance in international reconstruction finance.

**4. The world economy became more autarkic and controlled.** Exchange controls, rationing, commodity stockpiling, price controls, capital controls, and bilateral trade arrangements became normal tools. This was not only a fascist or communist pattern. Liberal economies also directed credit, imports, shipping, and production. The forward linkage was the persistence of state planning instruments after 1945, even in market economies.

**5. Commodity markets split between scarcity and glut.** Strategic materials such as oil, rubber, tin, copper, and shipping capacity became crucial. At the same time, loss of Continental European demand created gluts for some exporters. Governments responded with purchase programs, stockpiling, and price support. The forward linkage was the strategic resource logic that shaped Japan's policy, Allied stockpiling, and postwar commodity agreements.

**6. Labor markets moved from unemployment toward allocation.** The United States still had high unemployment in 1940, but the direction of travel was toward absorption into industrial and military-linked work. In belligerent countries, the problem became less simple unemployment and more allocation of skilled labor, women workers, transport workers, and engineers. The forward linkage was the wartime labor-market transformation of 1941-1945.

### 3. MACROECONOMIC OVERVIEW

**3A. Global Aggregates:** Maddison 2010 reports world GDP in 1940 at 4,502,584 million 1990 international Geary-Khamis dollars. A comparable global 1939 Maddison observation is not available in the horizontal file used here, so a precise world growth rate is **Data not available**. Global trade volume for 1940 in a single comparable series is **Data not available** in the sources consulted, but contemporary BIS and League of Nations reporting describe a severe disruption of European and intercontinental trade. Reserve dynamics favored the United States, with large gold inflows and a stronger net creditor position. Global inflation was not measurable as a single comparable CPI; country experiences differed between price controls, shortages, and still-low U.S. inflation. Oil in the U.S. first-purchase price series averaged about $1.02 per barrel in 1940, while the official U.S. gold price remained $35 per troy ounce. Comparable global copper, wheat, and rice prices for 1940 are **Data not available** in the source set used for this file.

**3B. Monetary & Financial Conditions:** The United States had abundant banking reserves and low interest rates. Federal Reserve open-market operations were minor relative to later wartime finance, while fiscal spending rose. The United Kingdom maintained war finance under exchange control and a low Bank Rate environment. Germany, Italy, Japan, and the USSR operated in controlled systems in which posted interest rates were less meaningful than administrative allocation of credit. There was no Bretton Woods system in 1940. Sterling, the dollar, gold, exchange controls, and bilateral clearing arrangements formed the practical payments system. Equity markets were unstable: the Dow Jones Industrial Average closed 1940 down about 12.7 percent. Comparable annual returns for most occupied or controlled exchanges are **Data not available**.

**3C. Trade & Geopolitics:** The decisive trade fact was the split of the world into war blocs and controlled trade zones. Britain lost access to many European markets and concentrated more heavily on the Empire, North America, and shipping-protected routes. Germany's continental system absorbed occupied economies and redirected production. Latin America lost parts of its European demand and became more connected to U.S. finance and trade. Japan's yen-area policy and resource needs became more important in East Asia. Regional integration institutions such as the later EEC or ASEAN did not yet exist.

**3D. Commodities & Energy:** Energy and strategic materials mattered more than market price alone. Oil, rubber, tin, copper, aluminum, steel, coal, and shipping capacity became strategic bottlenecks. The Dutch East Indies was economically important because of oil and other commodities. U.S. agencies accumulated strategic materials, while Britain used bulk purchase arrangements. Agricultural markets were split between food-security demand, shipping shortages, and lost European demand.

**3E. Labor & Demographics:** Global employment data are **Data not available** in a comparable statistical sense. The direction was clear in industrial economies: unemployment was falling where mobilization increased demand, while labor allocation and skill bottlenecks became more important. Migration was shaped by refugees, military mobilization, colonial labor systems, and restrictions. Demographic trends were overshadowed by war, although the long-run urbanization trend continued in North America, Japan, the USSR, and parts of Latin America.

**Economic narrative:** The United States was the largest economy by a wide margin and moved from recovery toward mobilization. Private demand was no longer the whole story: aircraft, shipbuilding, machine tools, steel, chemicals, and transport equipment became the industrial hinge between the civilian recovery and the later war economy. Housing was recovering but not yet in a postwar boom.

**6A. Equity markets:** The U.S. Dow Jones Industrial Average ended 1940 at 131.13 and recorded an annual price decline of about 12.7 percent. Paris, Amsterdam, Brussels, and other continental markets were disrupted by occupation or political control. ### 7. RISKS, CRISES & BLACK SWANS

The active crisis was total war. The largest realized shocks were the fall of France, the occupation of the Low Countries, the isolation of Britain, the disruption of shipping, and the fracture of normal trade. Near-misses and systemic vulnerabilities included British import dependence, Japanese resource constraints, Soviet exposure to a future German attack, and the possibility that U.S. mobilization would remain too slow.

With hindsight, the most important economic vulnerability was the mismatch between military ambition and resource access. Japan's dependence on imported oil and raw materials would become decisive. Germany's inability to convert early military victories into a sustainable global resource base would also matter. For the Allies, the main risk was that shipping losses, financial strain, or delayed U.S. production would prevent effective mobilization. These are hindsight judgments, not all of which were equally visible to policymakers in 1940.

Policy mistakes visible in retrospect include underestimating the duration and material intensity of the war, assuming occupied continental production could be smoothly integrated, and relying on coercive extraction that damaged productivity. The risks that did not fully materialize in 1940 included a complete British financial collapse or immediate U.S. entry into war. Those outcomes remained possible but occurred differently in later years.

### 8. YEAR-OVER-YEAR DELTA (1940 vs. 1939)

The structural break from 1939 to 1940 was the move from war shock to war system. In 1939, the war had begun but much of the world still operated with partial continuity. In 1940, the fall of France and occupation of the Low Countries destroyed that continuity. Britain became more dependent on transatlantic support. The United States shifted toward active industrial preparation. Canada and Australia became more important suppliers. Germany's continental position expanded, while Italy joined the war.

The biggest output changes in the Maddison top 20 were war-linked. France, the Netherlands, Belgium, Sweden, and Turkey fell or were constrained by invasion, occupation, trade disruption, or mobilization costs. The United Kingdom, Canada, the United States, Australia, Indonesia, and Spain show increases, though Spain's increase should be read partly as post-civil-war rebound. China cannot be measured year-over-year in the extracted table because 1939 and 1940 GDP observations are missing.

New risks emerged around shipping, raw materials, gold flows, and the fiscal capacity of belligerents. Risks that seemed to resolve in the short run, such as Germany's access to continental resources, later proved unstable. Economic power shifted further toward the United States because it combined huge domestic capacity, creditor status, gold inflows, and still-limited physical war damage.

### 9. DATA CONFIDENCE & GAPS

The highest confidence data in this document are the Maddison 2010 GDP, population, and GDP per capita observations extracted directly from the Groningen Excel workbook for countries with a 1940 cell. U.S. real GDP and unemployment are also relatively well supported because modern U.S. statistical agencies and FRED preserve long historical series.

Medium confidence applies to wartime narratives from BIS, League of Nations, and economic-history syntheses. These sources are strong for trade disruption, gold flows, capital movements, and policy context, but they do not always provide harmonized national-account statistics. Low confidence applies to Soviet GDP, occupation economies, China, and colonial economies. Soviet and other non-market GDP estimates can easily carry errors of 20-30 percent or more. China is explicitly low confidence because the Maddison 2010 annual GDP sheet has no 1939 or 1940 GDP observation; the value shown is a linear interpolation from 1938 and 1950 only to avoid an analytically false omission.

The weakest evidence concerns country-level unemployment, inflation, government debt/GDP, current account balances, budget balances, and policy interest rates across all top 20 economies. Definitions varied sharply, wartime controls distorted measurement, and many sources do not offer comparable annual data. Where this document says **Data not available**, it means no sufficiently reliable 1940 value was found in the consulted source set.

Potential conflicts across sources include GDP unit conventions. Maddison 2010 uses 1990 international Geary-Khamis dollars; Maddison Project 2023 and OWID use later processing and 2011 international-dollar conventions for many charts; OWID's long-run global GDP chart also uses 2021 international dollars in its latest presentation. These are not interchangeable. This file therefore keeps the ranking table in the unit of the extracted Maddison 2010 workbook.
