# MACRO/MICRO GLOBAL ECONOMIC ASSESSMENT: YEAR 1938

### 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

1938 was a hinge year between the Great Depression and the war economies of the early 1940s. The world economy was no longer in the free-fall of 1929 to 1933, but recovery was fragile, uneven, and increasingly political. The United States experienced a sharp recession inside the broader Depression recovery, Europe accelerated rearmament, and East Asia absorbed the economic shock of the Sino-Japanese War. Global activity was therefore pulled in two directions: cyclical weakness in trade and private investment, and state-led demand from public works, defense preparation, and command-style allocation.

The defining macroeconomic feature was the incomplete restoration of international economic integration. The classical gold standard had collapsed earlier in the decade, but many countries still behaved as if external reserves, exchange stability, and capital controls constrained policy. Currency blocs, exchange controls, imperial preference, bilateral clearing, and managed trade replaced much of the pre-1914 liberal order. The dollar and sterling both mattered, but no Bretton Woods system yet existed. Gold still anchored important parts of the system, with the United States maintaining the $35 per ounce official gold price established after the 1934 Gold Reserve Act.

The largest economies were not the same as today's ranking. The United States was clearly first. The former USSR, Germany, China, the United Kingdom, India, Japan, France, and Italy followed in varying order depending on PPP methodology and border treatment. Several very populous low-income economies, especially China, India, and the Dutch East Indies, ranked high in aggregate GDP but low in GDP per capita. Soviet, Chinese, colonial, and civil-war data should be treated as estimates, not ordinary national accounts.

Microeconomically, 1938 was a year in which state priorities increasingly overrode normal market allocation. In Germany, Japan, the Soviet Union, Italy, Mexico, and Brazil, state direction was central. In the United States, the policy mix turned again toward stimulus after the 1937 to 1938 recession. In Britain and France, rearmament and strategic imports became more important. Across much of the world, households faced weak labor markets, constrained consumption, and limited access to modern consumer finance. The strongest sectors were those tied to public infrastructure, raw materials, heavy industry, import substitution, and strategic production.

### 2. KEY THEMES OF 1938

**1. The 1937 to 1938 recession as a warning about premature tightening.** The United States downturn, lasting from May 1937 to June 1938, showed that recovery from the Great Depression was not self-sustaining. The combination of monetary restraint, gold sterilization, fiscal tightening, and private-sector caution remains debated, but the economic lesson was clear: policy reversal could trigger severe contraction before full employment returned. The global spillover was limited compared with 1929 to 1930, partly because countries held more reserves and because some economies used controls.

**2. Rearmament and state demand replaced private-led recovery in Europe and Japan.** Germany's Four-Year Plan, Japan's National Mobilization Law, British and French defense expansion, Soviet heavy-industrial planning, and Italian autarkic policy all shifted demand toward steel, chemicals, engineering, transport, energy, and state procurement. The significance is that GDP growth in several economies increasingly reflected mobilization rather than household welfare improvement. This theme linked directly to 1939 to 1945, when national accounting and industrial capacity became strategic instruments.

**3. Trade fragmentation and currency blocs persisted.** World trade had recovered from the worst Depression collapse but remained below the old liberal trajectory. Bilateral clearing, imperial preference, sterling-area management, exchange controls, and quota systems prevented a full return to integrated global markets. The most affected countries were export-dependent economies such as Canada, Argentina, Australia, the Netherlands, Belgium, Brazil, and the Dutch East Indies. The forward linkage was the wartime breakdown of trade routes, followed by the postwar effort to rebuild a rules-based trading system.

**4. Colonial and developing economies remained large but poor.** India, China, Indonesia, Brazil, and Mexico demonstrate the difference between aggregate GDP and living standards. Large population made them major economies in total output, but low productivity, rural employment, weak infrastructure, and limited domestic industry kept per capita income low. This distinction is crucial for interpreting power in 1938: military-industrial capacity and fiscal capacity were concentrated in a smaller group of industrialized economies.

**5. Non-market and controlled economies make GDP comparability fragile.** The former USSR, Germany, Japan, and parts of China used prices, quotas, controls, and state-directed production in ways that weaken direct comparison with market economies. Soviet GDP is especially uncertain because official prices and plan accounting do not represent market valuation. For these cases, figures should be interpreted as broad scale estimates, not precise measures.

**6. Commodity weakness and strategic raw materials shaped policy.** Oil, copper, rubber, tin, wheat, cotton, coal, and iron ore mattered both commercially and strategically. Cheap oil and weak commodity prices helped importers but hurt many exporters. At the same time, governments increasingly cared about access to raw materials for security reasons. This explains why colonies, resource frontiers, and trade routes became economically and politically sensitive.

**7. Household welfare often diverged from headline production.** In several countries, GDP-relevant output rose or held up because of public works, rearmament, or forced allocation, while consumers faced shortages, unemployment, inflation, rationing, or low rural incomes. Germany and Japan are the clearest cases, but the same divergence appears in Spain's civil-war economy, China's wartime economy, and the Soviet planned economy.

### 3. MACROECONOMIC OVERVIEW

#### 3A. Global Aggregates

**World GDP and growth:** A defensible single world-GDP level for 1938 in current dollars is not available from the sources used. Maddison/OWID long-run data provide PPP estimates in constant international dollars, but the accessible data did not yield a directly verified world total for 1938. A modern economic-history estimate cited in the literature places the world GDP change in 1938 at roughly negative 0.4 percent, far milder than 1930 but still showing global weakness. This figure should be treated as an estimate.

**Global trade volume:** Data not available as one verified annual number in this assessment. The historical trade literature and League of Nations sources show that world trade had not returned to a fully integrated pre-Depression pattern. Volumes and values were distorted by tariffs, quotas, exchange controls, bilateral clearing, and colonial preference systems.

**Reserve currency dynamics:** The dollar and sterling were the key reserve and settlement currencies, but 1938 was not a Bretton Woods world. Sterling remained central through the sterling area and imperial links. The dollar gained relative importance because of the scale of the U.S. economy, gold inflows, and New York's financial position. Gold retained monetary significance, with the U.S. official gold price at $35 per troy ounce.

**Global inflation trends:** Global inflation is not available as a comparable single figure. Broadly, commodity prices and manufactured-goods prices were still influenced by Depression-era slack, but countries under war pressure or currency stress, especially China and Japan, faced rising inflationary risk. In the United States, the 1937 to 1938 recession was disinflationary.

**Key commodity prices:** Oil was cheap by later standards. One historical price series reports U.S. crude oil near $1.13 per barrel in 1938. Gold was officially $35 per troy ounce in the United States. Copper, rubber, tin, wheat, and cotton remained cyclical commodities with weak demand relative to late-1920s expectations, though strategic buying began to matter for metals and energy.

**International capital flows:** Capital favored reserve centers and safe havens, especially the United States and Britain, while continental Europe faced rising political risk. Capital controls in Germany, Japan, Italy, the Soviet bloc, and many developing or colonial economies meant that official flows cannot be interpreted as free-market capital allocation.

#### 3B. Monetary & Financial Conditions

The United States maintained very low short-term policy rates, with the Federal Reserve discount rate at 1.00 percent after the August 1937 change. The key policy issue was less the nominal rate than the reserve-requirement and gold-sterilization debate. The Bank of England held Bank Rate at 2.00 percent through 1938. Germany's official bank discount rate was 4.0 percent in late 1938, but credit allocation and exchange controls mattered more than the published rate. Bank of Japan historical discount-rate data exist, but a comparable modern headline policy rate is not used here.

Exchange regimes were mixed. The United States was on a managed gold-dollar system after devaluation. Britain managed sterling rather than returning to a classical gold standard. Germany and Japan relied heavily on exchange controls. France had devalued in 1936 and continued to face confidence problems. Many colonial currencies were tied to metropolitan currencies, especially sterling, the guilder, or the franc.

Equity markets were volatile. In the United States, the Dow Jones Industrial Average fell sharply in 1937 and rebounded in 1938, with common historical-return tables showing about a 28 percent rise in the Dow for calendar 1938. This should be read as a recovery from depressed prices rather than proof of a healthy real economy. Bond markets in major financial centers reflected low rates and strong demand for safer assets, but yields are not available here in a comparable global table.

#### 3C. Trade & Geopolitics

The trade system was fragmented. The United States continued reciprocal trade agreements but still operated in a world shaped by the earlier Smoot-Hawley shock. Britain relied on imperial preference and sterling-area links. Germany used bilateral clearing agreements and import controls. Japan's war in China created direct disruption and raised the importance of resource security. Italy and the Soviet Union used controlled trade and state priorities. The Munich Agreement, the annexation of Austria, the Spanish Civil War, and the Sino-Japanese War all affected investment expectations and commercial routes.

Regional integration in the modern sense was limited. The European Economic Community did not exist. ASEAN did not exist. Instead, the main regional economic structures were empires, currency blocs, trade preference zones, and state-directed bilateral arrangements. The most important bilateral tensions were U.S. and British disputes with Mexico after the oil expropriation, U.S. and British concerns over Japan in China, and European tensions around Germany's expansion.

#### 3D. Commodities & Energy

Oil was not organized around OPEC, which did not exist. Production and pricing were shaped by U.S. production controls, private international oil companies, colonial concessions, and state policy. Mexico's 1938 expropriation was the year's most important microeconomic energy event. The Dutch East Indies, the United States, Mexico, Venezuela, and the Soviet Union were strategically important producers or resource holders.

Metals and minerals gained strategic importance even when market prices were weak. Steel demand rose in mobilizing economies. Iron ore, coal, copper, aluminum inputs, rubber, and tin became more sensitive to geopolitical risk. Agriculture remained vulnerable to low incomes and price volatility. Wheat exporters such as Canada and Australia, beef and grain exporters such as Argentina, coffee exporters such as Brazil, and plantation exporters in Southeast Asia were exposed to demand weakness and trade barriers.

#### 3E. Labor & Demographics

Labor markets were not comparable across countries. The United States still had mass unemployment, with the 1937 to 1938 recession pushing unemployment near 20 percent at its peak. Germany reported near-full employment, but the figure was distorted by conscription, public works, rearmament, discrimination, and controls. Britain had regional unemployment, especially in older industrial areas. The Soviet Union and Japan used administrative labor allocation. Spain and China suffered conflict-driven displacement.

Demographically, the biggest economies by population were China and India, but most of their labor forces remained rural. Urbanization continued in Japan, the United States, parts of Europe, Brazil, Mexico, and colonial Southeast Asia. Migration was restricted compared with the pre-1914 world, and refugees from European political persecution and Asian war became economically significant even when not captured well by GDP statistics.

#### 5B. Corporate landscape

A defensible global top-10 to top-20 list by revenue or market capitalization for 1938 was not verified. Comparable global corporate rankings were not standardized, and many major firms were privately held, state-owned, imperial concessions, or embedded in zaibatsu and state-directed systems. The largest and most economically consequential enterprises included major U.S. industrial and oil firms, British and Dutch trading and oil interests, German chemical and steel combines, Japanese zaibatsu groups, Soviet state trusts, and colonial concession firms.

Major corporate and ownership events were more important than IPOs. Mexico's oil expropriation created a state oil monopoly through Petróleos Mexicanos. Italy's IRI remained central to state ownership of banks and industry. Germany intensified state direction of large private firms. Japan strengthened state control over credit, labor, materials, and major industrial groups. In the Soviet Union, enterprise identity was administrative rather than shareholder-based.

#### 5C. Innovation & technology

The economically important technologies of 1938 were not computers or software. They were electrification, radio, aviation, chemicals, synthetic materials, mechanized transport, improved machine tools, and industrial process control. Corporate laboratories and state procurement shaped innovation. Germany emphasized synthetic fuels and materials because of import constraints. Japan emphasized heavy industry and logistics for war. The United States retained deep capabilities in automobiles, chemicals, electricity, and mass production.

Infrastructure megaprojects and public works mattered. The United States continued New Deal infrastructure. Germany expanded highways and strategic industrial capacity. The Soviet Union invested in heavy industry, rail, energy, and urban-industrial projects. Colonial infrastructure in India and the Dutch East Indies remained oriented toward trade, extraction, and administrative control rather than universal domestic development.

#### 5D. Labor markets & human capital

Labor markets diverged sharply. The United States still had Depression-scale unemployment. Germany and the USSR moved labor through state priorities. Japan expanded labor controls through the National Mobilization Law. Britain, France, Belgium, and the Netherlands had skilled industrial workforces but faced regional and sectoral unemployment. Developing and colonial economies had large rural labor forces with low productivity, limited schooling, and weak bargaining power.

Union and labor movements were politically important. In democratic economies, labor policy was negotiated through parties, unions, and employers. In authoritarian economies, unions were controlled or replaced by state bodies. In Mexico, labor conflict in the oil sector helped set the stage for expropriation. In Spain and China, war disrupted ordinary labor relations. Human capital accumulation continued, but it was overshadowed by mobilization, displacement, and fiscal stress.

### 6. FINANCIAL MARKETS

#### 6A. Equity markets

Reliable annual-return data are available for the U.S. Dow Jones Industrial Average, which rebounded strongly in 1938 after the 1937 decline. Other major exchanges existed in London, Paris, Berlin, Amsterdam, Brussels, Tokyo, Toronto, Sydney, Buenos Aires, and Milan, but comparable annual index levels and returns were not verified in the sources used. In controlled economies, market prices often reflected policy intervention, capital controls, and limited investor freedom.

The year's equity-market pattern was a recovery from panic, not a clean bull market. U.S. equities responded to policy reversal and expectations of recovery after mid-1938. European markets were constrained by geopolitical risk. German and Japanese financial markets were increasingly subordinated to state goals. Commodity exporters' markets depended on external prices and sterling or dollar liquidity.

#### 6B. Fixed income

Ten-year government bond yields for all major economies were not verified in comparable 1938 form. Core financial centers had low short-term rates, with the U.S. discount rate at 1.00 percent and Bank of England Bank Rate at 2.00 percent. In Germany, the official bank discount rate was 4.0 percent, but state financing and controls dominated the yield environment. Sovereign risk was political as much as financial.

#### 6C. Currencies

The currency system was a patchwork. The dollar was tied to gold at $35 per ounce. Sterling was managed and central to the sterling area. The Reichsmark, yen, lira, and ruble were heavily controlled, making official rates poor measures of real convertibility. China faced severe wartime currency pressure. Colonial currencies were linked to metropolitan systems, embedding India, Australia, and many colonies in sterling-linked finance, and the Dutch East Indies in guilder-linked structures.

#### 6D. Alternative assets

Gold was the key alternative reserve asset, not a free-floating investment asset in the modern sense. Real estate markets varied: U.S. and British housing finance recovered unevenly, while war-risk areas in Europe and China became highly uncertain. Farmland values depended on commodity prices and credit conditions. Art, collectibles, and private stores of value became relevant for capital flight, but reliable aggregate data are not available.

### 7. RISKS, CRISES & BLACK SWANS

Active crises in 1938 included the U.S. recession, the Spanish Civil War, the Sino-Japanese War, the Munich crisis, the annexation of Austria by Germany, currency stress in China, and rising pressure on European capital markets. These were not isolated events. They interacted through trade expectations, capital flight, raw-material demand, shipping insurance, and fiscal planning.

The main systemic vulnerability was the absence of a stable international monetary and trade order. Countries had escaped the strictest gold-standard constraints, but many still feared reserve loss and currency instability. Trade barriers and capital controls protected some domestic economies in the short term but reduced global adjustment capacity. Hindsight makes clear that rearmament demand was not a normal recovery engine: it increased measured output while preparing the institutional and industrial structure for war.

Policy mistakes visible in retrospect include premature U.S. tightening, underestimation of geopolitical risk in investment planning, and the failure to rebuild a cooperative trade system before conflict escalated. Tail risks that materialized shortly afterward included the outbreak of European war in September 1939, further fragmentation of trade, rationing, capital controls, and large-scale state direction of production. Tail risks that did not fully materialize in 1938 included a second synchronized global depression on the scale of 1929 to 1933.

### 8. YEAR-OVER-YEAR DELTA (1938 vs. 1937)

The biggest change from 1937 to 1938 was the shift from fragile recovery to renewed recession in the United States, followed by policy reversal. In 1937, many observers could still imagine a gradual return to private-led recovery. By mid-1938, that optimism was damaged. The recession made it clearer that aggregate demand support, monetary expansion, and fiscal policy remained central.

In Europe, 1938 marked a sharper transition from ordinary interwar economics to crisis economics. Germany's annexation of Austria and the Munich Agreement changed borders, resource expectations, and investor psychology. Britain and France accelerated defense planning. Italy and Germany deepened autarkic pressure. Neutral economies such as Sweden, the Netherlands, Belgium, and Switzerland faced growing uncertainty about trade routes and capital safety.

In East Asia, 1938 was a full wartime year after the 1937 expansion of the Sino-Japanese War. Japan's National Mobilization Law formalized wider state control over economic life. China shifted more activity inland, and the Nationalist state faced growing inflationary and fiscal pressure. These changes were structural, not cyclical.

In Latin America and the developing world, the year reinforced state-led development. Mexico's oil expropriation was a decisive resource-nationalization event. Brazil's Estado Novo consolidated a more interventionist path. Argentina continued balancing export dependence with import-substitution pressure. Commodity exporters remained vulnerable to weak world demand and trade barriers.

### 9. DATA CONFIDENCE & GAPS

**Most reliable areas:** The United States and United Kingdom have relatively strong financial and macro records, especially for monetary-policy rates and financial-market series. Germany has some high-quality historical financial series, but ordinary macro indicators are distorted by controls, rearmament, and political exclusions. Western European population and output estimates are generally stronger than those for colonial and conflict economies.

**Moderate confidence:** France, Italy, Canada, Australia, Belgium, the Netherlands, Sweden, Argentina, Brazil, and Mexico have usable historical estimates, but annual macro indicators such as unemployment, CPI, debt/GDP, budget balances, and current account data are not consistently verified in a comparable 1938 framework.

**Low confidence or special caution:** The former USSR is a non-market economy with plan prices and administrative allocation, so GDP should be treated as a rough PPP reconstruction with error plausibly in the plus or minus 20 to 30 percent range. China in 1938 was at war, with fragmented administration and currency stress. India and Indonesia were colonial economies where national accounts are retrospective estimates. Spain was in civil war, making almost every macro indicator fragile. Czechoslovakia is a key gap because 1938 border events make a clean annual national account difficult; it may belong in a broader top-20 discussion, but a direct verified 1938 GDP figure was not inserted.

**Conflicting historical data:** Maddison Project, Bairoch, national historical accounts, and League of Nations data use different borders, currencies, price bases, and concepts. Maddison/OWID estimates use PPP international dollars and modern processing, while Bairoch's Europe estimates use 1960 U.S. dollars and historical borders. Nominal GDP rankings would differ from PPP rankings. Colonial territories may appear either as separate economic units or as parts of empires, changing rankings.

**Weakest claims in this document:** Exact country rank near positions 14 to 21 is the weakest part because several economies are close in size and data gaps are significant. Country-level annual growth rates, inflation, budget balances, current account balances, and unemployment are frequently unavailable or non-comparable. Corporate global rankings by revenue or market value were not verified and are therefore not fabricated.

**Important data that does not exist in clean form for 1938:** Comparable global unemployment, global CPI, global current account balances, worldwide corporate market capitalization rankings, globally comparable household debt, standardized real-estate prices, and a single official world GDP level in current U.S. dollars.
