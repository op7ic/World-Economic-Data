# MACRO/MICRO GLOBAL ECONOMIC ASSESSMENT - YEAR: 1975

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

1975 was the trough year of the first postwar global stagflation cycle. The oil shock of 1973-1974 had already changed relative prices, but 1975 revealed its full macroeconomic cost: industrial economies entered the sharpest synchronized recession since 1945, inflation remained high by postwar standards, and unemployment reached levels that many advanced-economy policy systems had not been designed to handle. IMF reporting for the period placed world output growth at about -0.5 percent in 1975, with industrial countries at about -1.5 percent, while developing countries and oil-exporting economies continued to grow, though under very different balance-of-payments conditions.

The dominant economic story was not simply recession. It was a regime break. The old postwar combination of fixed exchange rates, cheap energy, fast manufacturing growth, and confidence in demand management had been replaced by managed floating currencies, oil-cartel pricing power, wage-price conflict, and a deeper debate over inflation control. Central banks in the United States, Japan, Germany, and the United Kingdom generally eased from the severe monetary tightness of 1973-1974, but they did so with inflation still elevated. That produced a policy dilemma that would define the late 1970s.

The year also redistributed economic power. OPEC exporters, especially Iran and Saudi Arabia outside the top-20 PPP list used here, accumulated large surpluses and placed them through international banks. Deficit oil importers, including Italy, Turkey, India, Brazil, and parts of southern Europe, faced external financing pressure. Commercial banks became a major channel for recycling oil surpluses into sovereign and quasi-sovereign borrowing. With hindsight, this was an early phase of the debt cycle that culminated in the Latin American debt crisis of the 1980s.

At the microeconomic level, households delayed purchases, especially autos and durable goods, because inflation, unemployment risk, and credit conditions damaged confidence. Heavy industry, autos, construction, shipping, and energy-intensive manufacturing were under stress. Oil, gas, petrochemicals, and parts of mining retained strategic importance. Technology was still small in macro terms, but 1975 marked a seed year for personal computing: the Altair 8800 entered public attention, and Microsoft was founded to supply software for that new microcomputer market.

For non-market economies, especially the former USSR, China, and Poland, figures are historical estimates rather than national-account measurements directly comparable to OECD data. A practical uncertainty band of plus or minus 20 to 30 percent is appropriate for Soviet-bloc aggregate output levels.

## 2. KEY THEMES OF 1975

### Theme 1: Stagflation became the defining advanced-economy problem

1975 joined recession and inflation in a way that challenged postwar Keynesian policy assumptions. In many industrial economies, output contracted while consumer prices rose rapidly. IMF and BIS evidence from the period describes a deep downturn, record postwar unemployment, and inflation that eased from 1974 peaks but remained historically high. The United Kingdom and Italy were among the most visibly affected, while Germany and Japan had lower inflation but still faced real output weakness. The forward link was direct: the late 1970s became a struggle between incomes policies, monetary restraint, fiscal stimulus, and later disinflation.

### Theme 2: Floating exchange rates replaced Bretton Woods as the operating reality

By 1975, the Bretton Woods fixed-rate system was no longer the anchor of major-currency policy. The dollar, Deutsche mark, yen, sterling, lira, and franc moved in a volatile managed-float environment. This changed how external adjustment worked. Countries could no longer assume fixed parities would absorb trade and capital shocks. Currency weakness intensified inflation in import-dependent countries, while stronger currencies and tighter monetary credibility helped Germany and, later, Japan contain inflation. The forward linkage was the development of modern foreign-exchange intervention, reserve management, and exchange-rate surveillance.

### Theme 3: Oil-price redistribution reshaped current accounts and banking flows

The oil shock did not end in 1974. In 1975 the macro adjustment continued. Oil exporters accumulated surpluses while oil importers either compressed demand or borrowed. International banks became intermediaries in petrodollar recycling. Energy importers such as Italy, Turkey, India, and Brazil faced a difficult mix of higher import bills, inflation, and growth objectives. Oil exporters such as Iran used revenue to expand state spending and industrial projects, often creating domestic bottlenecks. With hindsight, the same recycling mechanism helped build sovereign-debt vulnerabilities in Latin America and parts of the developing world.

### Theme 4: The industrial cycle was synchronized, but recovery timing differed

The United States and Japan began to recover earlier in the second half of the year, while much of Europe lagged. Inventory liquidation, weak capital expenditure, and depressed consumer confidence defined the trough. Germany cut policy rates as recession deepened, Japan reduced the discount rate in steps, and the United States saw the federal funds rate fall from its 1974 highs. The forward link was a 1976 cyclical rebound, but the rebound did not restore the pre-1973 growth regime.

### Theme 5: Developing economies split between commodity winners and oil-importing borrowers

Oil exporters and some primary producers had more favorable external positions, while many oil-importing developing economies relied on debt, import controls, or domestic austerity. Brazil and Mexico maintained high headline growth through state-led investment and external finance. India benefited from a rebound and agricultural improvements, but remained heavily controlled and import-constrained. Indonesia, although an oil exporter, faced the Pertamina debt crisis, proving that commodity revenue did not eliminate financial fragility.

### Theme 6: Microeconomic adjustment appeared before macro indicators normalized

Consumers cut or postponed durable-goods spending; firms reduced inventories; construction slowed; and banking systems faced new credit-risk patterns. Autos, steel, shipbuilding, and energy-intensive industry were particularly exposed. The household sector became more cautious, while labor conflict intensified where real wages were squeezed by inflation. These micro adjustments were not secondary details. They transmitted the oil shock and monetary tightening into output, jobs, corporate profits, and public budgets.

### Theme 7: Technology was economically small but historically decisive

The large 1975 economy was still oil, autos, steel, agriculture, finance, utilities, and construction. Yet the microcomputer appeared as a commercially meaningful object. The Altair 8800 and the founding of Microsoft did not change 1975 GDP, but they signaled a future shift from hardware-centered mainframe computing toward software, personal computing, and platform economics. This was a weak short-run macro event but a high-significance structural event.

The global economy was in recession, but not all regions moved together. Industrial economies bore the main output contraction. Developing economies as a group still grew, but the average hid sharp differences between oil exporters, commodity producers, and oil-importing borrowers. Trade was a major transmission channel: the IMF described the volume of world trade as falling about 4 to 5 percent from 1974 to 1975.

Key commodity prices were unusually important. International oil marker prices remained far above pre-1973 levels, while U.S. domestic crude first-purchase prices averaged about $7.66 per barrel under domestic pricing arrangements. Gold averaged roughly $161 per troy ounce in commonly used LBMA-based historical price tables, down from its 1974 peak but still high compared with the Bretton Woods gold price of $35. Copper and other industrial commodities weakened with manufacturing demand; exact 1975 values from the World Bank Pink Sheet were not retrieved in this extraction, so country-specific claims about those prices are not made here.

### 3B. Monetary & Financial Conditions

The broad pattern was easing from the very tight conditions of 1974, but not a return to easy money in the pre-oil-shock sense. The U.S. effective federal funds rate averaged about 5.82 percent in 1975, falling from 7.13 percent in January to 5.20 percent in December. The Bank of Japan cut its discount rate in several steps from 9.0 percent to 6.5 percent by October. The Bundesbank discount rate fell from 8.0 percent in January to 4.5 percent by late 1975. The Bank of England moved in a more complicated path, with Bank Rate at 11.25 percent in January, 12.0 percent in October, and 11.25 percent at year-end.

Equity markets rebounded in the United States as investors anticipated recovery; secondary market-return tables place the S&P 500 1975 price return at roughly 31.6 percent. This rebound should not be confused with macro health. Corporate profits, employment, and capital spending remained under pressure in much of heavy industry. Government bond markets reflected recession, inflation uncertainty, and shifting expectations about policy credibility. ### 3C. Trade & Geopolitics

1975 sat inside the Cold War, decolonization aftershocks, and the first oil-shock adjustment. Trade disputes were less defined by modern tariff wars than by balance-of-payments measures, capital controls, import deposits, exchange controls, and energy policy. The European Economic Community continued to matter as a regional trade and policy framework, but European economies were divided by exchange-rate pressures and different inflation outcomes. ASEAN remained young and economically smaller but strategically important in Southeast Asia. The United States remained the central reserve and trading economy, while Japan continued to grow as an export and manufacturing power despite the oil shock.

The Helsinki Final Act in 1975 symbolized détente, but economic integration across the Cold War divide remained limited. Soviet-bloc trade was still heavily planned, with significant intra-bloc flows and selected hard-currency trade for energy, grain, machinery, and technology. The USSR imported grain when harvest conditions and planning outcomes required it, and energy exports were increasingly important for hard-currency capacity.

### 3D. Commodities & Energy

Oil dominated the economic meaning of commodities. OPEC's new pricing power forced oil importers to reallocate income abroad or borrow. The energy shock changed sector profitability, public investment priorities, and industrial competitiveness. Countries with domestic energy resources, including the USSR, Iran, Indonesia, Canada, the Netherlands through gas, and Australia through resource exports, had strategic advantages, though not always macro stability.

Metals and industrial raw materials faced weaker demand because manufacturing and construction slowed. Agriculture remained volatile. Food-price stress from earlier years eased in some places, but national experiences varied sharply. India, for example, recorded strong overall Maddison growth in 1975 after weak years, while the USSR continued to face the productivity limits of its agricultural system.

### 3E. Labor & Demographics

Labor markets deteriorated most visibly in industrial economies. Unemployment definitions differed widely, so this document does not present a single cross-country unemployment ranking. The key comparative fact is qualitative but strongly supported by contemporary IMF and BIS descriptions: postwar unemployment highs and weak consumer confidence appeared across many advanced economies.

Demographics still supported labor-force expansion in many developing economies, including India, Indonesia, Mexico, Brazil, Iran, and Turkey. Urbanization continued to shift employment from agriculture toward industry and services, but in many countries the formal labor market could not absorb the full demographic increase. ## 7. RISKS, CRISES & BLACK SWANS

The active crisis was global stagflation. It combined recession, inflation, unemployment, and external imbalances. The direct shock came from oil and earlier monetary tightening, but the persistence came from wage-price dynamics, indexation, weak confidence, and currency instability.

The second crisis was balance-of-payments fragility. Oil importers needed adjustment or financing. Petrodollar recycling made financing available, but it also shifted risk into banks and sovereign balance sheets. With hindsight, the borrowing of the mid-1970s was an early warning for the 1980s debt crisis, especially in Latin America.

The third crisis was political economy. Argentina's Rodrigazo, Canada's wage-price controls, Britain's inflation and labor conflict, Spain's transition, and Indonesia's Pertamina crisis all showed that macro shocks became institutional stress tests. In planned economies, the visible risk was different: weak consumer supply, hidden inefficiency, and unreliable price signals.

The main near-miss was that the 1975 recession did not become a depression. Policy easing, automatic stabilizers, and the inventory cycle helped generate recovery in 1976. The unresolved vulnerability was that recovery occurred before inflation had been structurally defeated.

## 8. YEAR-OVER-YEAR DELTA (1975 vs. 1974)

1974 was the year when the oil shock and inflation surge hit with full force; 1975 was the recession trough and the first phase of policy response. The major delta was from inflation acceleration to output contraction with still-high inflation. In industrial economies, the question shifted from how to contain overheating to how to restart growth without validating inflation.

World trade moved from expansion to outright decline. The IMF described global trade volume as down roughly 4 to 5 percent from 1974 to 1975. Industrial production and durable-goods consumption were weaker than in 1974, while inventories were liquidated. This made 1975 more severe for manufacturing and logistics than the first oil-shock year alone.

Monetary policy pivoted toward easing in the United States, Japan, and Germany. The U.S. federal funds rate fell through the year; the Bank of Japan cut the discount rate; the Bundesbank lowered its discount rate. The United Kingdom had less room because inflation and sterling pressure remained severe.

Economic power shifted further toward energy producers and surplus holders. The policy risks that emerged in 1975 were not all immediate: bank recycling of oil surpluses, sovereign borrowing, and state-enterprise contingent liabilities became more important. In hindsight, Brazil's continued debt-financed growth, Mexico's external imbalance before 1976, and Indonesia's Pertamina bailout were all warning signs.
