
# MACRO/MICRO GLOBAL ECONOMIC ASSESSMENT - YEAR: 1918

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

1918 was an economic hinge year: the final year of World War I, the year of the armistice of 11 November, and the first year in which the global economy began to feel the overlapping shock of demobilization and the influenza pandemic. The world economy was not operating under normal market conditions. Large parts of Europe were under war planning, rationing, price controls, shipping controls, capital controls, and military procurement systems. International trade was shaped less by comparative advantage than by blockades, troop logistics, shipping scarcity, convoy systems, foreign exchange controls, and government allocation.

The main macroeconomic pattern was a transfer of economic weight toward the United States and, to a lesser extent, Japan, while continental Europe and Russia were physically, fiscally, and institutionally weakened. The United States entered 1918 with industrial momentum from Allied demand and war mobilization, while Britain remained financially central but heavily indebted and dependent on wartime borrowing. Germany, Austria, France, Belgium, and Russia were deeply distorted by war damage, labor mobilization, food shortages, and the exhaustion of public finance. Japan benefited from an export and manufacturing boom as European competitors were displaced from Asian markets.

Inflation was one of the dominant facts of the year. Wartime borrowing, monetary expansion, shortages, shipping bottlenecks, and price controls created very high price pressure in many countries. Gold remained officially fixed at the prewar U.S. parity of $20.67 per troy ounce, but that figure did not represent a freely functioning international gold standard. The classical gold standard had effectively been suspended by wartime finance, capital controls, and exchange controls. The postwar international monetary order was therefore unsettled, and it would remain so until failed restoration attempts in the 1920s and eventual abandonment in the 1930s.

The top 20 economies in this assessment are built primarily from Maddison Project style historical GDP estimates in 2011 international dollars. For countries with missing 1918 observations, especially China, the former Russian Empire and early Soviet area, and Poland, the assessment uses explicit, low-confidence bridging estimates and flags them. This matters because a country ranking for 1918 is not a modern nominal GDP ranking. It is a historical real-output ranking under conditions of war, empire, colonial administration, and weak national accounts. Many country-level unemployment, current account, debt, and budget figures are marked "Data not available" because consistent official annual national accounts did not exist for many economies in 1918.

## 2. KEY THEMES OF 1918

### Theme 1: War economy reached its peak, then turned toward demobilization

The final year of World War I saw the peak of state-directed production in the belligerent economies. Governments directed steel, coal, railways, shipping, food, credit, and labor toward military production and logistics. In the United States, the war economy expanded sharply even though direct U.S. participation was late relative to Europe. Britain, France, Germany, Austria, Italy, Canada, Australia, and Japan were already deeply integrated into wartime procurement and public borrowing systems.

The economic significance was not simply the level of military output. The deeper change was institutional. War boards, ministries of munitions, food administrations, exchange controls, and government purchasing systems changed the relationship between firms and the state. When the armistice arrived, a large part of demand that had been guaranteed by the state suddenly faced cancellation, creating a demobilization problem that would shape 1919 and 1920.

### Theme 2: Inflation, debt, and monetary disorder replaced the prewar gold standard order

By 1918, the pre-1914 monetary regime was broken in practice. Gold parities still existed on paper in some cases, but convertibility, capital mobility, and market exchange rates were restricted by war controls. Governments financed war through taxation, borrowing, and central bank accommodation. The result was a broad inflationary environment, especially in Europe but also in the United States.

This theme mattered because it created the postwar policy problem: how to normalize money, debt, and exchange rates after years of emergency finance. The unresolved choice between austerity, inflation, devaluation, and restoration of gold parity became one of the central policy conflicts of the 1920s. In hindsight, it also foreshadowed later debt and monetary crises, especially in Central Europe.

### Theme 3: The United States moved closer to global financial leadership

The United States was already the largest economy in real output terms before 1918, but the war accelerated its financial rise. European allies relied heavily on U.S. production, credit, and supplies. Britain remained an indispensable financial and imperial power, but war borrowing weakened its balance sheet. The U.S. dollar gained relative importance because U.S. banks, Treasury finance, and industrial exports were central to the Allied war effort.

The forward linkage is direct: the 1920s world economy would be marked by a larger U.S. role in international credit, commodity demand, and industrial technology. The shift did not instantly displace sterling, but 1918 made clear that the prewar London-centered order had been weakened.

### Theme 4: The influenza pandemic created a global labor and demand shock

The 1918 influenza pandemic overlapped with the final military phase of the war and demobilization. It reduced labor supply, disrupted production and transport, increased health costs, and damaged household welfare. The shock was global, but mortality and economic effects were uneven. India experienced especially severe mortality. Military camps, ports, troop movements, and dense cities were important transmission environments.

The economic significance of the pandemic is difficult to measure because it coincided with war and postwar transition. Still, the pandemic was a real macro and micro shock: it affected labor availability, consumer demand, local services, health systems, and household savings. The recessionary effects in the United States were brief relative to the scale of mortality, but global welfare losses were enormous.

### Theme 5: Empires collapsed and statistical geography became unstable

1918 was a year of political-economic boundary change. The Russian Empire had already collapsed into revolution and civil war. The German, Austro-Hungarian, and Ottoman empires were moving toward dissolution. Poland re-emerged as a state late in the year. These events created direct economic dislocation through border changes, currency fragmentation, fiscal breakdown, transport disruption, and loss of administrative continuity.

This theme matters for data. A 1918 GDP estimate for "Russia," "former USSR," "Austria," or "Poland" is not the same kind of object as a modern national accounts observation. It is often a territorial or reconstructed estimate. Rankings involving these economies carry very high uncertainty.

### Theme 6: Japan and parts of Asia gained from wartime trade displacement

Japan experienced a wartime export and manufacturing boom as European competitors could not supply Asian and Pacific markets as before. Japanese shipbuilding, textiles, chemicals, and light manufacturing expanded, while wartime profits and credit growth helped produce speculative pressures. India, Indonesia, and the Philippines were still colonial or colonial-linked economies with large agricultural bases and external trade shaped by imperial controls and wartime shipping scarcity.

The forward linkage was uneven. Japan entered the 1920s with expanded industrial capacity but also inflation, social tension, and financial fragility. Many colonial economies did not receive the same development benefits because gains from commodity production were constrained by imperial trade structures, unequal bargaining power, and limited domestic industrial policy autonomy.

### Theme 7: Food, fuel, and shipping were binding constraints

The year was defined by shortages of food, coal, oil, shipping, and rail capacity. Germany and Austria faced severe supply shortages under blockade and war exhaustion. Britain and the Allies had to manage U-boat risk, convoy systems, shipping allocation, and food imports. Oil demand rose with mechanized war, naval fuel, transport, and industrial needs, while coal remained the dominant energy source.

The forward linkage was the postwar adjustment problem. Food shortages, transport bottlenecks, and fuel constraints did not disappear when fighting stopped. They fed social unrest, strikes, and inflation in 1919 and 1920.

## 3. MACROECONOMIC OVERVIEW

### 3A. Global Aggregates

**World GDP and growth rate:** Data not available as a precise official 1918 global national accounts aggregate. Maddison Project style estimates allow reconstruction of many country totals in 2011 international dollars, but coverage gaps and wartime borders limit confidence. The top 20 estimates in Section 4 sum to approximately 3.35 trillion 2011 international dollars when the explicitly flagged bridging estimates for China, the former USSR/Russia area, and Poland are included. This is not a complete world GDP estimate.

**Global trade volume:** Data not available as a single consistent global volume series for 1918 in the sources used here. Qualitatively, trade was heavily distorted by war, naval blockades, shipping shortage, submarine risk, convoy allocation, and state procurement. Trade among the Allied powers and their suppliers was prioritized. Central Powers trade was sharply restricted by blockade and the loss of normal shipping access.

**Reserve currency dynamics:** The prewar sterling-centered system was weakened, but not replaced overnight. The U.S. dollar became more important because the United States was the leading supplier and creditor to the Allies. Gold parities existed formally, but the classical gold standard was not functioning normally because convertibility, capital mobility, and free exchange markets were constrained by war controls.

**Global inflation trends:** Inflation was widespread. It reflected wartime borrowing, monetary expansion, shortages, rationing, shipping bottlenecks, military demand, and reduced civilian supply. U.S. consumer inflation was very high by historical standards, and British inflation was also severe. Comparable CPI data for many countries are not available or not comparable.

**International capital flows:** Capital flowed toward war finance. The United States became a central creditor and supplier to Allied economies. Britain remained financially important but was heavily burdened by war borrowing. Central Powers were largely cut off from normal international capital markets and financed war internally through borrowing and money creation.

### 3B. Monetary & Financial Conditions

**United States:** The Federal Reserve was still a young institution. The Federal Reserve Bank of New York discount rate was 4.25 percent early in 1918 and moved to about 4.56 percent from May through December. The policy stance supported war finance and the market for government securities. Credit conditions were shaped by Treasury borrowing, Liberty Loans, and bank participation in public finance.

**United Kingdom:** Bank Rate was 5 percent throughout 1918. Britain used public borrowing, taxation, exchange controls, and imperial financial networks to sustain the war effort. Public debt was very high by the end of the war.

**Germany and Austria:** Data not available for directly comparable modern policy-rate series in this assessment. Monetary conditions were dominated by war finance, Reichsbank and Austro-Hungarian Bank accommodation, debt issuance, shortages, and exchange controls. The financial effects became more visible after the war through inflation and currency instability.

**Japan:** Data not available for a single directly comparable policy rate in this assessment. Monetary and credit conditions were expansionary in the sense that wartime export profits, industrial growth, and speculative activity expanded rapidly. The Bank of Japan and financial authorities faced rising prices and credit pressures.

**Exchange rate regime context:** Bretton Woods did not exist in 1918. The prewar classical gold standard had been suspended or heavily constrained by war controls in practice. Exchange rates were affected by capital controls, official support, wartime finance, and limited convertibility rather than free market gold flows.

**Stock markets and bonds:** Major stock exchanges existed in New York, London, Paris, Berlin, Amsterdam, Tokyo, Toronto, and elsewhere, but annual index-level and return data are not available in a sufficiently consistent cross-country form for this assessment. Government bond markets were dominated by war loans. Yield data for 10-year government bonds are marked "Data not available" unless a specific official series is cited in future work.

### 3C. Trade & Geopolitics

There were no institutions such as the EEC or ASEAN in 1918. The central geopolitical economic fact was World War I. The Allied blockade restricted Central Powers access to food, raw materials, and shipping. German submarine warfare had already forced major adjustments in Allied shipping and convoy systems. U.S. participation changed the balance of resources, supplies, and credit.

Major trade tensions were not modern tariff disputes so much as wartime control systems. Governments determined shipping priorities, import licensing, exports of strategic goods, and access to foreign exchange. The armistice did not instantly normalize trade. Ports, railways, credit lines, and shipping capacity had to be redirected from military to civilian use.

### 3D. Commodities & Energy

Oil was becoming more economically important, though coal still dominated energy. U.S. oil production and pricing mattered because the United States was the leading oil producer and a central supplier. Wartime logistics, naval fuel, trucks, aircraft, and industrial production increased demand for liquid fuels.

Metals such as steel, copper, nickel, and aluminum were strategic inputs, but wartime controls and government procurement make market price comparisons difficult. Agricultural conditions were severe in many regions. Food shortages and inflation were major causes of social unrest, especially in Germany, Austria, and parts of the collapsing Russian imperial economy.

### 3E. Labor & Demographics

Labor markets were disrupted by mobilization, casualties, troop movements, women entering war industries, colonial labor mobilization, and pandemic mortality. Unemployment rates are not comparable across countries and are often unavailable. The more relevant labor indicators were labor scarcity in war industries, strikes, real wage pressure, food access, and demobilization risk.

The influenza pandemic created an additional global labor shock. It removed workers temporarily through illness and permanently through mortality. The burden was uneven, and India was among the worst affected regions. Demographically, 1918 also intensified the economic consequences of wartime deaths, disability, orphanhood, and household income loss.

## 4. COUNTRY-BY-COUNTRY: TOP 20 ECONOMIES OF 1918

### Data basis and ranking method

The ranking below uses real GDP totals reconstructed mainly from Maddison Project Database 2020 style data in 2011 international dollars. For most countries, total GDP is calculated as real GDP per capita multiplied by population. This is the same conceptual approach used by Our World in Data when it derives total GDP from Maddison Project GDP per capita and population. A Maddison 2010 series in 1990 international dollars was used as a cross-check for available countries; it broadly confirms the largest economies among the United States, the United Kingdom, Germany, India, Japan, France, and Italy, but it has important country and year gaps.

Three entries require explicit low-confidence bridging estimates: China, the former USSR/Russia area, and Poland. China lacks a 1918 GDP per capita observation in the MPD 2020 file used here, so the estimate uses its 1918 population and the nearest prewar GDP per capita value available in that file. The former USSR/Russia area uses an interpolated population and available 1918 GDP per capita. Poland uses an approximate territorial interpolation between available prewar and early postwar observations and should be treated as a territorial estimate rather than a normal national account for an independent state. These entries are included because excluding them would produce a misleading top-20 ranking.

## 7. RISKS, CRISES & BLACK SWANS

The active crises of 1918 were unusually severe and overlapping. The first was the final military and economic phase of World War I. The second was the influenza pandemic. The third was state collapse and civil conflict in the former Russian Empire. The fourth was the fiscal and monetary overhang created by war finance. The fifth was food and fuel scarcity.

Near-misses and systemic vulnerabilities included the risk of Allied shipping failure, food shortages in urban Europe, social unrest from inflation, fragile banking systems exposed to government debt, and the difficulty of demobilizing millions of soldiers without mass unemployment or political instability. Some risks materialized immediately, such as revolution and state collapse in Central and Eastern Europe. Others unfolded later, such as the 1920-21 recession, postwar debt disputes, reparations conflict, and currency crises.

With hindsight, 1918 also shows the danger of restoring prewar monetary and fiscal assumptions after a war that had changed debt levels, social expectations, and international creditor positions. That hindsight should be used carefully. Policymakers in 1918 did not have modern national accounts, modern pandemic data, or knowledge of the exact path of the 1920s.

## 8. YEAR-OVER-YEAR DELTA (1918 vs. 1917)

The largest change from 1917 to 1918 was the transition from total war to armistice. In 1917, the economic system was still adapting to U.S. entry into the war, Russian revolution, and continuing military escalation. In 1918, U.S. mobilization reached much greater intensity, Russia's economic collapse deepened, Germany and Austria reached exhaustion, and the Allied resource advantage became decisive.

The United States accelerated from a supplier and new belligerent into the central industrial and financial support of the Allied side. Britain remained central but became more visibly debt-burdened. France and Belgium faced severe damage and reconstruction needs. Germany and Austria moved from constrained war economies into defeat, regime change, and monetary vulnerability.

The influenza pandemic was the most important new global non-military shock. It changed labor availability, mortality, public health conditions, and household welfare. Unlike many economic shocks, it was not limited to industrial or financial centers. It hit agrarian and colonial economies hard, with India especially affected.

In sector terms, 1918 was the peak and break point for war production. Military procurement, steel, shipbuilding, chemicals, railways, and logistics remained central, but the armistice created sudden uncertainty for firms built around state demand. Agriculture and food distribution remained politically sensitive because inflation and shortages persisted after fighting stopped.

The distribution of global economic influence shifted further toward the United States and Japan. The shift did not mean immediate U.S. global monetary dominance or Japanese regional dominance without limits, but it did mark a structural weakening of prewar European supremacy.

**Most reliable areas:** U.S. and British monetary-policy rates, selected U.S. and British inflation estimates, U.S. oil and gold price data, and Maddison-style GDP estimates for countries with direct 1918 observations are relatively stronger. Even here, modern comparability should not be overstated.

**Moderate-confidence areas:** Western European GDP estimates, Japan's aggregate GDP estimate, and settler-economy estimates for Canada and Australia are usable but affected by wartime controls, mobilization, and historical reconstruction methods. France, Belgium, Germany, Italy, and Austria require special caution because war damage and military allocation distort welfare comparisons.

**Weakest ranking areas:** China, the former USSR/Russia area, and Poland are the weakest entries in the top-20 table. China lacks a direct 1918 GDP per capita observation in the MPD 2020 file used here. The former USSR/Russia area requires population interpolation and is affected by revolution and civil war. Poland is a territorial estimate for lands that became a state only at the end of 1918.

**Unemployment comparability:** Unemployment rates are mostly marked Data not available because mobilization, forced labor, underemployment, colonial labor structures, and missing labor-force surveys make modern comparison misleading.

**Fiscal and external accounts:** Government debt/GDP, budget balances, and current account balances are often Data not available. Where historical fiscal data exist, they may refer to empires, colonies, or successor states rather than the exact economy ranked here. Current account data are especially difficult under war controls and imperial financial systems.

**Corporate and financial-market data:** A global top-company ranking by revenue or market capitalization is Data not available. Equity index levels and annual returns for 1918 are Data not available in a consistent global format in this assessment. Bond yields and credit spreads are also incomplete.

**Conflicting sources:** Maddison 2010 and MPD 2020 use different price bases and methods, so their GDP levels are not directly comparable. Rankings for countries with observations are broadly similar at the top, but lower-ranked economies and missing countries can change depending on series choice and territorial definitions.
