# MACRO/MICRO GLOBAL ECONOMIC ASSESSMENT - YEAR: 1916

### 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

1916 was the year in which the world economy fully became a war-mobilized economy. The First World War had already broken the pre-1914 gold-standard trading order, but by 1916 the dominant economic problem was no longer short-term disruption. It was the ability of states to direct labor, credit, shipping, food, coal, metals, and industrial output toward a prolonged conflict.

The central structural change was the replacement of market-led globalization with state-managed allocation. Many belligerents had suspended or restricted gold convertibility, capital controls expanded, and central banks increasingly supported government borrowing. Sterling remained a major international currency, but wartime finance strained its role. New York and the U.S. dollar gained importance because the United States supplied credit, food, raw materials, and industrial goods before entering the war.

Output patterns were uneven. The United States, Japan, Canada, and some neutral suppliers grew strongly. Britain and France maintained or recovered output through mobilization and external finance. Germany and Austria-Hungary faced worsening shortages and trade isolation. Russia is the largest data problem: older estimates show a major fall by 1916, while later reconstruction suggests the pre-1917 decline was smaller.

Microeconomically, 1916 was defined by bottlenecks. Coal, shipping, rail transport, copper, steel, grain, and credit constrained production. Heavy industry, shipbuilding, mining, chemicals, food distribution, and finance became more important, while consumer goods, housing construction, and normal international commerce were squeezed. Labor markets changed as mobilization pulled women, colonial workers, rural migrants, and older workers into new roles.

This document uses historical GDP estimates, mostly Maddison-style 1990 international Geary-Khamis dollars, not modern nominal U.S. dollar rankings. Several rankings include territorial or colonial units because historical datasets often use modern or colonial territorial boundaries. If data are unavailable, the entry says "Data not available" rather than guessing.

### 2. KEY THEMES OF 1916

**1. Total-war mobilization became the macroeconomic regime.** Government expenditure, procurement, rationing, borrowing, and direct labor allocation became central in Britain, France, Germany, Austria-Hungary, Russia, Italy, and the Ottoman Empire. The forward linkage was postwar debt, inflation, demobilization, and the unstable early-1920s recovery.

**2. The classical gold standard was no longer the operating system.** The 1916 world was pre-Bretton Woods and also no longer the free gold-standard world of 1913. Gold exports, capital movements, and exchange rates were increasingly controlled. The pound remained important, but official support and borrowing replaced normal gold-market discipline.

**3. The United States moved toward the center of global finance and supply.** U.S. neutrality did not mean economic detachment. War demand raised exports, industrial production, commodity prices, and lending. This strengthened New York's role and foreshadowed the larger shift after U.S. entry in 1917.

**4. Blockade, shipping scarcity, and raw-material controls reshaped trade.** The Allied blockade and neutral-trade restrictions limited Central Powers access to food and imported inputs. Neutral economies such as the Netherlands, Sweden, Switzerland, Denmark, and Spain faced both profit opportunities and diplomatic pressure.

**5. Inflation and household strain intensified.** Prices rose in many countries because military demand, shipping costs, money creation, supply disruption, and food scarcity interacted. Modern CPI comparisons are limited because many national price indexes were new, local, or reconstructed.

**6. Japan, Canada, and selected suppliers boomed.** Wartime demand favored exporters with access to shipping, finance, and raw materials. Japan expanded textiles, shipping, and regional trade. Canada benefited from grain, food, manufacturing, and imperial procurement.

**7. Statistical visibility was unequal.** U.S., U.K., Canadian, and several Western European data series are relatively strong. China, Russia, the Ottoman Empire, colonial territories, and many developing economies require larger uncertainty bands. This is especially important around the lower half of the top-20 ranking.

### 3. MACROECONOMIC OVERVIEW

#### 3A. Global Aggregates

A fully comparable official world GDP figure and world growth rate for 1916 are data not available. Maddison-style estimates place world GDP in 1913 at about 2.73 trillion 1990 international Geary-Khamis dollars, which is the safest global benchmark used here. By 1916, the key fact was not a uniform global recession but a redistribution of output toward economies able to supply war demand.

Global trade-volume growth is data not available. Directionally, trade became more expensive, controlled, and politically routed. Shipping shortages, contraband rules, insurance costs, blockade policy, and state procurement shifted flows toward Allied supply chains and away from the Central Powers. Sterling remained central, but U.S. credit and dollar-linked finance gained importance.

Commodity evidence is partial. A historical crude-oil series reports U.S. crude oil around 1.10 U.S. dollars per barrel in 1916. U.S. copper producer prices were about 28.46 cents per pound in a USGS historical table. Gold remained officially fixed in the United States at 20.67 dollars per troy ounce, but wartime restrictions meant this was not a fully free global market price.

#### 3B. Monetary & Financial Conditions

The world monetary system was pre-Bretton Woods and under wartime control. In the United States, the Federal Reserve System was new, and there was no single modern federal funds rate. U.S. 10-year Treasury yields are reported near 4.05 percent at the start of 1916 in later historical compilations. In Britain, government borrowing, Bank of England support, capital controls, and exchange management dominated; a Bank of England historical series places long consol yields near 4.32 percent in 1916.

Germany, Austria-Hungary, France, Italy, and Russia relied heavily on wartime borrowing, monetary support, and financial controls. In these economies, normal market rates and equity prices are less informative because state direction, exchange restrictions, and captive domestic bond markets shaped pricing. The Dow Jones Industrial Average ended 1916 down roughly 4.2 percent, showing that U.S. real growth and equity returns did not move together mechanically.

#### 3C. Trade & Geopolitics

The main trade issue was not a new free-trade agreement but the wartime reorganization of commerce. The Allied blockade, neutral trade quotas, and shipping controls constrained German and Austrian access to overseas inputs. U.S. trade and finance leaned increasingly toward the Allies. Japan used European distraction to expand export markets in Asia. The 1916 Sykes-Picot Agreement did not immediately change production, but it signaled future economic reorganization of former Ottoman territories.

#### 3D. Commodities & Energy

Coal was the dominant industrial energy source. Railways, factories, cities, mines, and ports depended on coal allocation. Oil was still smaller in aggregate energy use but increasingly strategic for shipping, transport, and industry. Copper, steel, iron ore, nitrates, rubber, cotton, wool, and grain were key inputs. Agriculture was not secondary: food supply, fodder, rural labor, and transport capacity were central to price stability and political legitimacy.

#### 3E. Labor & Demographics

Mobilization pulled working-age men out of civilian labor forces and raised demand for industrial, transport, clerical, and public-administration workers. Women expanded wage work in several belligerent economies, while colonial and migrant labor became more important in imperial systems. Unemployment rates are not comparable across 1916 economies, and many national series are missing or limited to unions, cities, registered workers, or selected sectors.

#### 5B. Corporate landscape

A comparable global top-10 or top-20 company ranking by revenue or market capitalization for 1916 is data not available. Corporate reporting standards, private ownership, exchange closures, empire structures, and wartime controls make such a list unreliable. Important corporate groups included U.S. Steel, Standard Oil successor companies, major U.S. and British banks, German chemical and electrical firms, French industrial groups, and Japanese zaibatsu such as Mitsui and Mitsubishi. The larger story was not normal M&A but the rise of government contracting, public borrowing, procurement controls, and resource allocation.

#### 5C. Innovation & technology

The economic impact of innovation in 1916 was mostly applied and organizational. Production engineering, chemical processes, electrical equipment, transport coordination, telegraph and radio communication, medical logistics, and large procurement systems mattered more than consumer inventions. Comparable R&D spending and global patent rankings for 1916 are data not available.

#### 5D. Labor markets & human capital

Labor shifted toward industry, transport, public administration, and war-supply sectors. Women expanded paid work in several countries, although permanence varied. Skilled metalworkers, miners, railway workers, engineers, clerks, and transport workers gained importance. Nominal wages rose in some sectors, but inflation often reduced real purchasing power. Comparable global data for wages by skill, productivity, and education are data not available.

### 6. FINANCIAL MARKETS

#### 6A. Equity markets

New York is the best-documented major market in this compilation. Later historical series place the Dow Jones Industrial Average at about 95.00 at the end of 1916, down roughly 4.2 percent for the year. For London, Paris, Berlin, Vienna, Tokyo, Amsterdam, Madrid, Buenos Aires, Toronto, and Sydney, comparable annual index levels and returns are data not available here. In Europe, war controls, closures, low liquidity, and government borrowing priorities reduce comparability.

#### 6B. Fixed income

Government bond markets were the core financial markets of 1916. Britain, France, Germany, Austria-Hungary, Italy, Russia, Canada, and Australia all relied on wartime borrowing. U.K. long consol yields were near 4.32 percent in a Bank of England historical series. U.S. 10-year yields are reported near 4.05 percent at the start of 1916. Modern-style credit-spread comparisons are data not available.

#### 6C. Currencies

Gold-standard discipline was suspended or restricted across many belligerents. Exchange rates were managed through controls, borrowing, gold-export restrictions, and official stabilization. The dollar gained importance because U.S. supply capacity and credit mattered more, but the full reserve-currency transition was still incomplete.

#### 6D. Alternative assets

Gold remained officially fixed in the United States at 20.67 dollars per troy ounce, but this official parity did not represent a fully free wartime world gold market. Comparable real-estate prices for major cities and other alternative-asset series are data not available.

### 7. RISKS, CRISES & BLACK SWANS

The active crisis was the economic strain of prolonged war. The main systemic vulnerabilities were fiscal exhaustion, inflation, food scarcity, transport bottlenecks, exchange instability, and political legitimacy. These risks were visible in 1916, even if the exact outcomes were not.

For the Central Powers, the key risk was inability to access enough food and imported raw materials. For the Allies, the key risk was growing dependence on U.S. supply and credit. For Russia, hindsight shows that transport, prices, fiscal strain, and political instability were moving toward the 1917 rupture, but economic historians disagree about how far output had already fallen in 1916.

Neutral economies faced the risk of being squeezed between profitable trade and political coercion. The Netherlands, Sweden, Switzerland, Spain, and others had opportunities from scarcity but also sanctions risk, shipping constraints, price pressure, and diplomatic limits.

### 8. YEAR-OVER-YEAR DELTA (1916 vs. 1915)

The main 1915 to 1916 change was the normalization of state-managed wartime economy. In 1915, governments were still adapting to a longer war than expected. By 1916, procurement, public debt, exchange controls, labor allocation, and rationing were more systematic.

The United States accelerated as supplier and creditor. Britain and France deepened fiscal mobilization and external finance. Germany and Austria-Hungary moved further into shortage economics. Russia's transport, fiscal, and urban-supply strains worsened, even though exact output change is disputed. Japan's export boom strengthened, creating both current growth and later postwar adjustment risk.

### 9. DATA CONFIDENCE & GAPS

Relatively reliable statistics exist for the United States, United Kingdom, Canada, Australia, Japan, France, Germany, Italy, the Netherlands, and Spain, although even these require reconstruction for GDP, CPI, debt, and labor-market measures. Medium-confidence estimates cover India, Dutch East Indies, Argentina, Brazil, Mexico, and Belgium. Low-confidence estimates cover China, the Russian Empire, Austria-Hungary, and the Ottoman Empire.

The weakest data categories are comparable unemployment, current-account balances as percent of GDP, budget balances as percent of GDP, debt-to-GDP ratios, real-estate prices, credit spreads, household debt, consumer confidence, and global company rankings. GDP values are not nominal U.S. dollars. They are 1990 international Geary-Khamis purchasing-power estimates or transparent derivatives from those estimates. Under wartime gold restrictions and exchange controls, nominal dollar conversions would be misleading.

The most important source conflict is Russia. Older wartime estimates place 1916 output far below 1913, while later reconstruction suggests the decline before 1917 was smaller. This document therefore reports uncertainty rather than a single false-precision figure.
