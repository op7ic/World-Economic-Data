# MACRO/MICRO GLOBAL ECONOMIC ASSESSMENT — YEAR: 1911

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

1911 sat near the high point of the first globalization. The classical gold-standard order was still functioning, London still intermediated a large share of world trade finance and cross-border capital, and industrial production was expanding in the United States, Germany, Japan, and parts of the British Empire. The year was not a single global crisis year, but it was a year in which several structural weaknesses became visible: fragile monetary systems without modern lenders of last resort, imperial fiscal stress, dependence on commodity and capital flows, and political instability in large agrarian economies.

The biggest structural event was China's 1911 Revolution. Economically, the revolution grew out of a fiscal, railway, and sovereignty crisis as much as a constitutional crisis. The Qing state's attempt to nationalize locally financed railway ventures and negotiate foreign financing helped trigger provincial opposition. China remained one of the world's largest economies in aggregate output, but its fiscal capacity and per-capita income were very low, and the collapse of imperial authority created a shock to taxation, debt expectations, and infrastructure planning.

In the industrial core, the United States remained the largest economy in the Maddison historical series. The 1911 Supreme Court decisions against Standard Oil and American Tobacco marked a major turn in the microeconomic governance of large corporations. Germany continued to deepen its heavy industrial, chemical, electrical, and banking strengths. Britain remained the center of global finance, shipping, and sterling credit, even as its relative industrial lead narrowed. Japan continued its Meiji-era industrial ascent.

This document uses historical GDP concepts rather than modern nominal GDP rankings. The principal numerical base is the Maddison Database 2010 in million 1990 international Geary-Khamis dollars, cross-checked against the Maddison Project Database 2023 and secondary Maddison ranking summaries. Exact 1911 annual values are not available for several large empires and non-market or weak-statistical economies. Where this happens, the table states "Data not available" or uses a clearly labeled 1913 benchmark proxy. The weakest parts of the ranking are China, Russia, Austria-Hungary, the Ottoman Empire, and colonial/partitioned territories.

## 2. KEY THEMES OF 1911

### Theme 1: The classical gold-standard order still disciplined policy

The monetary system was built around gold convertibility, fixed exchange parities, and credibility in external payments. This mattered because countries with gold-standard commitments could not freely pursue independent monetary policy without risking reserve losses. Britain, France, Germany, Japan, the United States, and many colonial or sterling-linked economies were inside or tied to this system. The gold standard helped stabilize exchange rates and encouraged cross-border capital flows, but it also transmitted shocks through gold flows, interest-rate changes, and reserve pressure.

In 1911, the system still appeared durable. Hindsight changes the interpretation: the gold standard's success depended on cooperation, confidence, and peace. The war beginning in 1914 showed how quickly convertibility and international capital markets could be suspended or redirected when geopolitical constraints changed.

### Theme 2: Industrial scale and corporate governance moved to the center of microeconomics

The most visible corporate-governance development was the United States' antitrust action against Standard Oil and American Tobacco. These cases showed that the state could challenge dominant trusts even in a generally pro-business age. The economic significance was not only legal. Large-scale industrial organization, vertical integration, transport networks, pricing power, and market access were now central questions for policymakers.

The forward link is obvious: twentieth-century capitalism would be shaped by the tension between scale efficiency and monopoly control. The 1911 trust decisions became reference points for later competition policy, while Germany's cartels, Britain's older industrial firms, and Japan's zaibatsu showed that countries handled industrial concentration differently.

### Theme 3: China revealed the economic fragility of late imperial state finance

China's 1911 Revolution was not simply a political event. It exposed a fiscal state that had difficulty financing railways, servicing obligations, balancing provincial interests, and defending sovereignty over infrastructure concessions. The Railway Protection Movement is therefore a macro-micro bridge: local shareholders, provincial tax levies, foreign loans, railway concessions, and central government authority all collided.

The forward link was the Republican era's fiscal fragmentation. What was knowable in 1911 was state breakdown; what became clearer later was that weak central fiscal capacity would limit national development and complicate monetary unification for decades.

### Theme 4: Commodity exporters prospered but remained exposed

Argentina, Canada, Australia, Brazil, the Dutch East Indies, India, and many African and Latin American economies were tied to global demand for wheat, meat, wool, rubber, coffee, sugar, cotton, metals, and oil. The first globalization rewarded economies that could connect land, railways, ports, and foreign capital to European demand.

The vulnerability was concentration. Commodity booms supported railways and urban growth, but price declines, capital-flow reversals, or war could cut export revenue and credit. This vulnerability became much more obvious after 1914 and again in the interwar period.

### Theme 5: Imperial and military competition began to crowd into economic life

1911 was not yet a world-war year, but it was deeply shaped by imperial rivalry. Italy's war against the Ottoman Empire over Libya began in September 1911. Naval spending, arms industries, railway strategy, and colonial finance were already economically relevant. Germany and Britain competed in naval and industrial capacity, Russia expanded rail and military infrastructure, and Japan's imperial sphere widened after the annexation of Korea in 1910.

The forward link is 1914. Military-industrial capacity, fiscal space, gold reserves, railway networks, and access to capital markets would determine how states mobilized once war began.

### Theme 6: Labor, migration, and urbanization were changing domestic political economy

Mass migration and urbanization were major economic forces. Canada, Argentina, Australia, and the United States absorbed immigrants; European economies experienced both emigration and internal migration; Japan moved labor from agriculture into textiles and industry; India and China remained largely agrarian but increasingly linked to railways, ports, and migrant labor circuits.

Labor movements and social legislation became more important. Britain's National Insurance Act of 1911 was a signal that industrial economies were building social-policy institutions to manage health, unemployment, and working-class insecurity. This trend would accelerate in the twentieth century.

## 3. MACROECONOMIC OVERVIEW

### 3A. Global Aggregates

**World GDP:** Data not available for exact 1911 in the extracted annual Maddison table. The Maddison 2010 horizontal file gives a **1913 world total of about 2,733,190 million 1990 international Geary-Khamis dollars**, but that is a benchmark near 1911, not a 1911 value. The Maddison Project Database 2023 and the Our World in Data processing indicate that historical GDP estimates are reconstructed from GDP per capita and population series, adjusted for inflation and purchasing-power differences. Therefore, global aggregate values should be treated as reconstructed PPP estimates, not official national accounts.

**World growth rate:** Data not available. The available annual Maddison rows allow growth calculations for many countries, but not for all major economies or the world aggregate in 1911. A defensible global growth figure cannot be produced from the extracted data without interpolation.

**Global trade volume:** Data not available for a single exact 1911 world trade volume in this file. The broader evidence points to a highly integrated prewar trade system, with historical trade databases covering exports and imports from 1800 to 1938. The economically important point is that international trade and capital flows were near their pre-1914 peak, but exact 1911 volume should not be guessed.

**Reserve currency dynamics:** Sterling was the main reserve and trade-finance currency of the classical gold-standard era. The exact 1911 reserve-currency composition is data not available here. A near benchmark is that by 1913 sterling accounted for a very large share of known foreign-exchange reserves. Gold remained the central reserve asset, and countries defended convertibility through interest-rate and reserve policies.

**Global inflation trends:** Data not available as a comparable global CPI measure. The gold-standard environment limited long-run inflation in the core economies but did not prevent relative-price swings in commodities, harvest-dependent food prices, or country-specific price movements.

**Key commodity prices:** Gold was fixed in the United States at $20.67 per troy ounce. Historical oil-price tables report a nominal U.S. crude oil price around $0.61 per barrel in 1911, but this is indicative rather than a global spot price. Copper and wheat price series exist for specific markets; a global 1911 price table is data not available in this file. OPEC did not exist in 1911.

**International capital flow direction:** Capital moved primarily from Britain, France, Germany, the Netherlands, and other European centers toward settler economies, railways, sovereign borrowers, colonies, and infrastructure. London was the key node. Exact 1911 capital-flow totals are data not available.

### 3B. Monetary & Financial Conditions

The monetary context was the classical gold standard. Exchange rates among gold-standard currencies were fixed by gold parities and only moved within narrow gold points under normal conditions. This reduced exchange-rate risk but made monetary policy subordinate to reserve defense. Countries with weaker fiscal credibility or political instability faced higher borrowing costs and external pressure.

The United States had no central bank in 1911. The Panic of 1907 had already exposed the fragility of a decentralized banking system, and the National Monetary Commission's work was part of the institutional path toward the Federal Reserve Act of 1913. For the United Kingdom, the Bank of England Bank Rate was in the range of roughly 3.0-4.0% during 1911, with quarterly values around 3.5%, 3.0%, 3.33%, and 4.0%. For Germany, France, Japan, and most other countries, exact policy-rate figures are data not available in this file.

Stock-market evidence is uneven. The Dow Jones Industrial Average ended 1911 nearly unchanged at about 81.68. London remained the world's central securities market, but modern FTSE-style index data do not exist for 1911. Historical reconstructions exist for British equities, but exact annual all-market returns are not extracted here. Bond-yield and spread data are data not available except where specialized historical datasets are consulted.

### 3C. Trade & Geopolitics

The main global trade regime was not a single multilateral institution but a network of gold-standard finance, imperial preferences, bilateral treaties, colonial tariffs, and national tariff systems. The United States remained tariff-protected; Canada debated reciprocity with the United States and rejected it politically in the 1911 election. European powers combined trade openness in some areas with strategic protection in others.

Geopolitically, the Italo-Turkish War began in 1911, exposing the economic strain of imperial conflict. China entered revolution. Mexico's revolution disrupted railways, mining, oil, and foreign-investment expectations. These were not yet systemic global financial crises, but they were warnings that political risk could interrupt the first globalization.

### 3D. Commodities & Energy

Energy remained coal-centered in most industrial economies. Oil was rising in importance, especially in the United States, the Caucasus, Mexico, the Dutch East Indies, and parts of the Middle East, but the world oil system was not yet governed by OPEC or modern posted-price institutions. Electricity, petroleum, and internal-combustion technologies were beginning to change industrial organization and transport, but coal and steam still dominated heavy industry and shipping.

Metals demand was driven by railways, armaments, electrical networks, construction, and machinery. Copper mattered for electrification and communications; iron and steel were core inputs for railways, ships, bridges, and military equipment. Agricultural commodities remained macro-critical because harvests affected trade balances, rural incomes, and food prices.

### 3E. Labor & Demographics

The world labor force remained overwhelmingly agricultural, but the leading economies were urbanizing. Industrial labor markets expanded in the United States, Germany, Britain, Japan, and parts of continental Europe. Immigration supported growth in the United States, Canada, Argentina, and Australia. Emigration from Italy, Eastern Europe, and other regions affected wages, remittances, and household strategies.

Comparable unemployment rates are data not available. Even where unemployment data existed, definitions varied. Wage growth and productivity are also not comparable globally for 1911 without specialized country datasets. The central qualitative pattern was structural: workers moved from farms and rural crafts toward factories, ports, mines, railways, construction, and urban services.

**Economic narrative:** The United States was the largest economy in the available historical accounts and, in the Maddison annual series, expanded strongly from 1910 to 1911. The economy combined mass manufacturing, rail transport, steel, petroleum, meatpacking, farm exports, and an increasingly national consumer market. The absence of a central bank remained a central institutional weakness. After the Panic of 1907, the Aldrich-Vreeland framework and National Monetary Commission work were still feeding into the reform debate that produced the Federal Reserve System in 1913.

The dominant microeconomic event was trust-busting. The Supreme Court's 1911 Standard Oil and American Tobacco decisions were not merely legal milestones; they reorganized large pools of capital, management, and industrial assets. The breakup of Standard Oil left successor firms with valuable refineries, pipelines, brands, and technical capacity, so the ruling changed ownership structure more than the underlying demand for petroleum. Financial markets were calmer than in 1907: the Dow Jones Industrial Average ended 1911 almost flat, with a reported year-end close of 81.68 and annual change of about +0.39%.

**Micro/sectoral highlights:** Leading sectors: steel, petroleum, railroads, electrical equipment, meatpacking, farm machinery, and consumer staples. Contracting or stressed areas: parts of rail finance still faced regulation and debt pressure. Consumer dynamics: rising urban consumption, catalog retail, and automobile diffusion were visible but still early. Banking health: fragmented and seasonally strained, with no lender of last resort.

### 5B. Corporate landscape

Exact global top-10 or top-20 company rankings by revenue or market capitalization for 1911 are data not available in the sources extracted for this file. However, the corporate landscape can be described qualitatively. Large firms and groups were already central in petroleum, steel, railways, chemicals, banking, shipping, and electrical equipment. The United States had giant trusts and integrated corporations; Germany had large industrial combines and universal banks; Britain had global finance, shipping, mining, and imperial trading houses; Japan had zaibatsu groups; colonial economies had plantation, mining, and trading companies linked to metropolitan capital.

Major corporate/legal events included the U.S. breakup orders against Standard Oil and American Tobacco. These were economically important because they tested whether the law would tolerate vertically integrated dominance in national markets. The breakup did not destroy the assets or technologies of the firms; it redistributed control and created successor companies that remained major players.

M&A, IPO, and bankruptcy data are not globally available in a comparable 1911 dataset. London and New York listings were active, but exact worldwide corporate issuance tables are beyond the verified sources in this file.

### 5C. Innovation & technology

Technologies with economic impact included electrical power systems, telephones, telegraphs, internal-combustion engines, automobiles, chemicals, steelmaking improvements, refrigeration, and railway technologies. Aviation was still commercially tiny but strategically visible. Refrigerated shipping supported Argentina, Australia, and New Zealand by linking meat and dairy to European demand. Electrification supported copper demand and reorganized factories, urban utilities, and transport.

R&D spending data and patent totals for 1911 are data not available in this file. Qualitatively, Germany and the United States were leaders in applied industrial research, while Britain retained strengths in finance, shipping, and engineering. Japan's technology absorption was increasingly successful in textiles, shipbuilding, and military production.

### 5D. Labor markets & human capital

The major structural shift was the movement of labor out of agriculture and into industry and services in richer countries. Germany, the United States, and Japan showed rapid industrial labor absorption. Britain had a mature industrial working class and rising labor politics. Canada, Argentina, Australia, and the United States relied on immigration and frontier settlement. India and China remained predominantly agrarian, with modern industrial work concentrated in ports, treaty ports, railway zones, and specific manufacturing centers.

Wage data by skill level are data not available globally. Education and technical training mattered increasingly, especially in Germany and Japan. Unions and labor movements were economically relevant in Britain, the United States, continental Europe, and parts of Latin America, but comparable membership and wage-bargaining data are not available for all countries.

### 6B. Fixed income

Government bond-yield tables for 1911 across major economies are data not available in the extracted sources. The qualitative pattern is clearer: gold-standard credibility, fiscal reputation, and political stability shaped yields. Britain and France were core creditors; Germany had a strong domestic capital market; Russia, Argentina, Ottoman borrowers, and many colonial or infrastructure projects relied on foreign capital. Sovereign debt crises were not the central global event of 1911, but several states carried vulnerabilities that would matter after 1914.

### 6C. Currencies

Gold-standard currencies traded near fixed parities. Sterling, the dollar, the mark, the franc, the yen, and other gold-linked currencies were constrained by convertibility and gold points. Silver and mixed-currency areas, including parts of China, were harder to summarize with one exchange rate. Colonial currency systems often linked local money to sterling or metropolitan finance.

Devaluations/revaluations in 1911: no globally systemic devaluation event identified in the verified sources for this file. Capital controls in the modern sense were not central in gold-standard core economies, though many colonial and weak-state systems had administrative constraints and concessionary finance.

## 7. RISKS, CRISES & BLACK SWANS

**China's 1911 Revolution:** This was the largest political-economic shock of the year. It disrupted central authority, railway finance, debt expectations, and provincial revenue systems. It was not yet clear in 1911 how long fragmentation would last.

**Mexican Revolution:** Mexico's political upheaval affected railways, oil, mining, land, and foreign investment. In 1911 the full economic cost was not yet visible, but the risk to property rights and infrastructure was already clear.

**Italo-Turkish War:** The conflict over Libya added fiscal and geopolitical pressure to Italy and the Ottoman Empire. It also signaled that imperial conflict could interrupt trade, debt, and regional investment.

**Gold-standard fragility:** Hindsight risk. In 1911 the gold standard looked stable; retrospectively, it was vulnerable to war, banking panics, sudden stops, and reserve loss. The absence of a U.S. central bank was a known vulnerability; the systemic fragility of the whole prewar order was less obvious to contemporaries.

**Commodity concentration:** Export economies such as Argentina, Canada, Brazil, Australia, and the Dutch East Indies benefited from global demand but were exposed to price swings and capital-flow reversals. This was a visible risk, though its full implications became clearer after the prewar globalization broke.

**Military-industrial buildup:** Defense spending and naval rivalry were not yet a macro crisis in 1911, but they were absorbing fiscal and industrial resources. With hindsight, the military-industrial base of 1911 was a precondition for the scale of mobilization after 1914.

## 8. YEAR-OVER-YEAR DELTA (1911 vs. 1910)

The most important delta from 1910 to 1911 was political rather than a synchronized global business-cycle break. China moved from late Qing reform crisis to revolution. Mexico moved deeper into revolutionary uncertainty. Italy moved from peace into colonial war. Canada moved from a trade-policy debate to rejection of U.S. reciprocity through election politics.

In the Maddison annual series where exact data exist, several major economies grew strongly: the United States, Germany, the United Kingdom, France, Italy, Japan, Canada, Spain, and the Dutch East Indies. India shows a small decline in the annual series. These growth rates are reconstructed historical estimates, not modern national-account releases.

The policy-regime delta was also institutional. The United States had not yet created the Federal Reserve, but the post-1907 reform process was advancing. Britain passed major social-insurance legislation. Corporate-governance policy in the United States shifted sharply through antitrust enforcement.

In global power terms, the United States and Germany continued to rise relative to Britain in industrial output, while Britain retained financial centrality. Japan remained the most important non-Western industrializer. China's aggregate size remained immense, but the state crisis reduced its ability to mobilize resources effectively.

## 9. DATA CONFIDENCE & GAPS

**Most reliable in this document:** Maddison annual GDP/population/per-capita rows for the United States, Germany, United Kingdom, India, France, Italy, Japan, Dutch East Indies/Indonesia, Spain, Canada, Belgium, Argentina, Mexico, Netherlands, Australia, and Brazil. Bank of England Bank Rate values, the U.S. gold price, and the Dow Jones annual close are also relatively high confidence.

**Moderate confidence:** The top-20 ranking as a historical-economy ranking. It is directionally defensible, but exact positions depend on whether one ranks sovereign states, empires, colonies, or modern-territory reconstructions. Austria-Hungary, British India, the Dutch East Indies, and Ottoman/Turkey require explicit classification choices.

**Low confidence or proxy-only:** China, Russia, Austria-Hungary, and the Ottoman Empire for exact 1911 GDP. For China and Russia, benchmark values around 1913 exist, but exact 1911 values in the extracted annual table are not available. For Austria-Hungary, the empire aggregate comes from Schulze/Maddison cross-reference rather than a direct 1911 annual country row. For the Ottoman Empire, a modern Turkey proxy is not the same as the full empire.

**Historical data conflicts:** Secondary Maddison ranking summaries differ on Spain's 1913 value. Some tables show Spain around 45,686 million 1990 int-$; the Maddison 2010 workbook row used here shows 41,653 million for Spain in 1913 and 40,332 million for 1911. Austria-Hungary is excluded from some main Maddison top-ten tables but appears in notes or Schulze comparisons. Russia's 1913 figure also differs depending on whether a Russian Empire or former-USSR territorial proxy is used.

**Unavailable or not comparable:** CPI inflation, unemployment, government debt/GDP, current-account balance, budget balance, exact policy rates, bond yields, credit spreads, and real-estate price indices for most of the top 20 are data not available in this file. These may exist in specialized national archives, but they are not consistently available or comparable across countries for 1911. Unemployment is especially problematic because definitions and registration systems differed widely.

**What does not exist in modern form:** A globally consistent 1911 national-accounts dataset equivalent to post-1990 World Bank/IMF data does not exist. Historical GDP is reconstructed from later national accounts, population series, commodity output, fiscal records, and scholarly estimates. For non-market, colonial, and weak-statistical economies, uncertainty can be large, and for some Soviet-bloc or developing-country histories the error bands can plausibly be 20-30% or more.
