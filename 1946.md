## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

1946 was the first full postwar year, but not a normal peace economy. The dominant force was reconversion: labor, factories, public finance, and trade shifted away from total war. In the U.S., measured GDP fell as military output collapsed even while civilian demand revived. In Europe and Japan, destruction, broken transport, food shortages, fuel bottlenecks, inflation, and weak currencies dominated.

The international monetary system existed more as blueprint than functioning market order. Bretton Woods had created the IMF and World Bank, and currencies were meant to be fixed but adjustable around the dollar-gold anchor. In practice, exchange controls, dollar rationing, sterling-area constraints, and bilateral finance dominated.

Food scarcity defined welfare in 1946. FAO warned of a serious grain import gap and shortages of fats, meat, sugar, and essentials. Truman described the world food shortage as an emergency. Germany and Japan relied on rationing and black markets, while the Soviet Union suffered famine in 1946-1947.

The year also embedded the postwar state. The U.S. passed the Employment Act, Britain nationalized the Bank of England and legislated coal nationalization, France built the Monnet planning framework, the Soviet Union launched the Fourth Five-Year Plan, Argentina created IAPI, and Japan’s occupation reforms attacked zaibatsu power.

## 2. KEY THEMES OF 1946

### Theme 1: Reconversion and the GDP measurement trap

GDP changes often reflected composition rather than welfare. U.S. output fell as military procurement ended, while civilian channels revived. Several European economies rebounded from low baselines; Germany and Japan remained trapped by occupation, currency disorder, and input shortages. Forward linkage: the U.S. avoided depression, Western Europe needed aid, and Germany/Japan required later stabilization.

### Theme 2: Bretton Woods before convertibility

The dollar-gold system was legally important but operationally incomplete. Exchange controls and dollar scarcity mattered more than convertibility. Britain’s sterling system remained important but weak. Forward linkage: this gap helped lead to the Marshall Plan and later convertibility.

### Theme 3: Food, coal, and transport bottlenecks

Food shortages affected Europe and Asia, while coal constrained European power, steel, rail, and heating. Transport damage made both worse. Key affected areas included Germany, Japan, the Soviet Union, Britain, France, the Netherlands, India, and Eastern Europe. Forward linkage: shortages made reconstruction a security issue.

### Theme 4: Inflation under controls

Inflation was widespread but hard to measure. Official prices often missed black-market premiums or queues. Japan had extreme inflation, Germany had suppressed inflation and barter, and the U.S. saw post-control price pressure. Forward linkage: later German and Japanese stabilizations addressed 1946-era disorder.

### Theme 5: State-led reconstruction and mixed economies

State intervention expanded across systems. Soviet planning, French indicative planning, British nationalization, U.S. macro institutions, Argentine trade control, and Japanese occupation reform were different answers to postwar scarcity. Forward linkage: these institutions shaped growth models.

### Theme 6: Early Cold War economic separation

Blocs were not fully formed, but the split was visible. The Soviet Union prioritized planned heavy industry; Western Europe depended on U.S. finance and imports; Germany’s division deepened. Forward linkage: the Truman Doctrine, Marshall Plan, German currency reform, and Comecon followed.

## 3. MACROECONOMIC OVERVIEW

### 3A. Global Aggregates:

World GDP: Data not available as a complete 1946 total from the country-level MPD 2020 file used here. The top 20 listed economies sum to about $5.345 trillion in 2011 international PPP dollars, but coverage is incomplete.

World growth rate: Data not available. Available evidence diverges: U.S. measured real GDP contracted, Western Europe rebounded unevenly, Germany and Japan stayed in emergency conditions, and Soviet/developing-country estimates are less comparable.

Global trade volume: Data not available. Trade reopened but was constrained by exchange controls, damaged ports, shipping shortages, dollar scarcity, and import licensing.

Reserve currency dynamics: The dollar was the core reserve currency, with official gold convertibility at $35 per troy ounce for foreign monetary authorities. Sterling remained important but weakened.

Global inflation trends: Data not available as a unified global CPI. Inflation pressure was broad, but rationing and black markets weaken comparability.

Commodity prices: Gold was fixed officially at $35 per troy ounce. Exact 1946 annual average oil, copper, wheat, rice, meat, and sugar prices are Data not available in the sources used. The key commodity fact was scarcity, especially grain, coal, fats, meat, sugar, and shipping capacity.

Capital flows: Relief, loans, and reconstruction finance flowed mainly from the U.S. and other less-damaged exporters toward Europe and Asia, but 1946 flows were insufficient relative to needs.

### 3B. Monetary & Financial Conditions:

The U.S. Federal Reserve still maintained wartime yield support: Treasury bills around 3/8 percent and long Treasury bonds around 2.5 percent. The U.K. Bank Rate was 2.00 percent. Germany and Japan lacked normal market monetary systems, while France, Italy, India, Australia, Argentina, Brazil, Spain, and Turkey relied heavily on exchange and credit controls. U.S. equities fell in 1946; many other stock markets were closed, thin, controlled, or not comparable.

### 3C. Trade & Geopolitics:

There was no GATT yet; trade was governed by controls, relief systems, occupation policy, sterling-area arrangements, and foreign-exchange rationing. The dollar shortage defined European policy debates. Spain was isolated, colonial conflicts affected Dutch, French, British, and Indian economic settings, and Germany’s occupation zones became more economically distinct.

### 3D. Commodities & Energy:

Coal was Europe’s critical energy bottleneck. It constrained electricity, steel, railways, heating, and industrial recovery. Oil mattered but was not yet the global macro-shock commodity it became after 1973, and OPEC did not exist. Food commodities were a macroeconomic issue because import gaps affected survival, politics, and external balances.

### 3E. Labor & Demographics:

Demobilization, refugees, displaced persons, and reconstruction needs reshaped labor markets. Modern unemployment rates are often not comparable. In rural economies, hidden underemployment mattered more than registered unemployment. 1946 also began or intensified baby booms in several Western countries, increasing future demand for housing, schools, and consumer goods.

## 7. RISKS, CRISES & BLACK SWANS

Active crises included the global food shortage, the Soviet famine, Germany’s black-market and currency breakdown, Japan’s inflation and food emergency, and Europe’s dollar shortage. These were not isolated events; each linked household welfare to foreign exchange, logistics, and state capacity.

Near-miss: U.S. policymakers feared demobilization could recreate Depression-scale unemployment. That risk did not materialize at the feared scale because private demand, savings, housing needs, and reconversion absorbed labor better than pessimists expected.

Hindsight risk: Bretton Woods solved interwar instability partly by creating a dollar-centered order, but the same asymmetry later produced the 1960s dollar-overhang problem. In 1946, however, the immediate issue was shortage of dollars, not excess dollars.

## 8. YEAR-OVER-YEAR DELTA (1946 vs. 1945)

The structural break was the move from victory to reconstruction. In 1945, ending war and restoring administration were the core tasks. In 1946, governments had to allocate scarce food, fuel, dollars, labor, credit, and equipment.

The U.S. shifted from military procurement to civilian demand and created new macroeconomic institutions. Britain moved nationalization from platform to law. France moved planning from idea to apparatus. The Soviet Union began the Fourth Five-Year Plan while entering famine conditions. Germany and Japan moved from defeat to occupation administration but not yet to monetary stabilization. Argentina began the Peronist policy turn; Brazil entered the Dutra period; Mexico moved toward the Alemán development model.

New risks included post-control inflation, dollar shortage, food crisis, occupation currency breakdown, and Cold War economic separation. Risks that eased included fears of a U.S. demobilization depression and the total paralysis of trade and shipping seen in the final war phase.

## 9. DATA CONFIDENCE & GAPS

Reliable statistics: MPD-style GDP per capita and population for listed countries; broad GDP ranking for the largest economies; Bretton Woods context; U.S. yield peg; U.S. Employment Act; UK nationalizations; Tokyo Stock Exchange closure and 1949 reopening; FAO and Truman food-crisis evidence.

Estimated or weaker data: Soviet GDP and famine interpretation; Japan’s extreme inflation; French planning effects; Argentina’s policy shift; lower-ranked GDP positions around ranks 19-23. Soviet and other non-market estimates may have very wide error bands.

Unavailable data: complete 1946 world GDP and growth in the extracted file; world trade volume; comparable unemployment; current accounts; budget balances; debt/GDP for most countries; corporate rankings; most non-U.S. equity returns; city property prices; and many commodity prices.

Historical data conflict: MPD 2020 and Maddison 2010 agree on the broad top group but differ near the cutoff. MPD 2020 ranks Denmark and Turkey 19-20; Maddison 2010 places Turkey and Venezuela inside the top 20 and Denmark outside. The cutoff is uncertain.

Most important missing case: China. The sources used do not provide a usable 1946 country-level China GDP entry, so China is not ranked. That is a data gap, not a claim about economic size.
