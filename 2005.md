# MACRO/MICRO GLOBAL ECONOMIC ASSESSMENT - YEAR: 2005

### 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

2005 was a late-cycle expansion year. The world economy remained strong after the unusually rapid rebound of 2004, but the mix changed. Growth was still led by the United States, China, India, commodity exporters, and recovering Japan, while the euro area remained comparatively weak. World GDP in current U.S. dollars was about $47.9 trillion in World Bank data. IMF and World Bank growth measures differ because of weighting methods, but both point to a broad expansion rather than recession. The big macro question of the year was not whether the world economy was growing, but whether it was growing on a stable foundation.

Three forces defined the year. First, oil and metals prices rose sharply, benefiting Russia, Brazil, Canada, Australia, Saudi Arabia, and other resource exporters while raising inflation pressure in oil-importing economies. Second, the U.S. Federal Reserve continued its tightening cycle, taking the federal funds target to 4.25 percent by December, while Japan remained under quantitative easing and the euro area only began to lift rates at year-end. Third, global current-account imbalances widened: the United States absorbed savings from Asia and oil exporters, while China, Japan, Germany, Russia, Switzerland, and oil exporters accumulated surpluses.

The most important structural event was China’s July 2005 renminbi reform. The immediate revaluation was small, about 2.1 percent against the U.S. dollar, but the move away from the decade-old tight dollar peg signaled a new phase in Asian exchange-rate management. It did not close global imbalances, but it changed the policy debate. 2005 was also the first year of the European Union Emissions Trading System, a major microeconomic policy innovation that created a tradable carbon allowance market and altered cost structures in power and energy-intensive industry.

With hindsight, 2005 also sits close to the peak of the pre-crisis credit and housing cycle. Household leverage, structured credit, low long-term yields, and U.S. housing finance looked manageable to many observers at the time. In retrospect, these were major vulnerabilities. The year therefore belongs to two histories at once: the high-growth globalization cycle of the mid-2000s and the build-up to the 2007-2008 financial crisis.

### 2. KEY THEMES OF 2005

**1. Expansion with late-cycle inflation pressure.** Growth stayed broad, but the composition became less comfortable. Oil, metals, and construction costs rose, putting pressure on real incomes and margins in fuel-importing economies. Advanced economy core inflation was still contained, which allowed central banks outside the United States and some commodity-linked economies to remain cautious.

**2. Global imbalances became a central policy problem.** The United States ran a very large external deficit, funded by capital inflows from surplus economies. China, Japan, Germany, Switzerland, Russia, and oil exporters were the major surplus poles. At the time, policy debates focused on exchange-rate flexibility, U.S. fiscal saving, Asian reserve accumulation, and the recycling of oil revenues. The forward link is direct: these imbalances shaped the international policy debate through 2006 and interacted with the credit boom before the financial crisis.

**3. The commodity super-cycle accelerated.** Copper, iron ore, zinc, energy, and gold rose on strong Asian demand, low inventories, and supply constraints. The strongest beneficiaries among the top 20 economies were Russia, Brazil, Australia, Canada, and to a lesser degree Mexico. The forward linkage was the investment boom in mining, oilfield services, shipping, and resource-linked infrastructure that continued into 2007-2008.

**4. China moved from fixed peg to managed flexibility.** China’s July 2005 currency reform was modest in magnitude but large in policy meaning. It changed expectations for Asian currencies and became part of a broader argument over trade balances, reserve accumulation, and export-led growth. China also remained an enormous source of demand for metals, energy, shipping, machinery, and electronics supply chains.

**5. Japan’s recovery became more credible.** Japan remained under deflationary pressure and the Bank of Japan kept quantitative easing in place, but corporate profits, bank balance-sheet repair, equity prices, and investment all improved. With hindsight, 2005 was the last full year before the Bank of Japan exited quantitative easing in 2006.

**6. Housing, credit, and leverage looked benign but were becoming systemic.** In the United States, Spain, the United Kingdom, Australia, and parts of Europe, property markets and household credit were major demand supports. At the time, low long-term rates and financial innovation were widely treated as signs of market depth. Hindsight shows that in the United States, the same conditions helped build the mortgage-credit vulnerabilities that surfaced in 2007.

**7. The microeconomics of globalization deepened.** Cross-border M&A, global supply chains, outsourcing, container trade, emerging-market consumer growth, and Web 2.0 platforms all advanced. P&G-Gillette, Sprint-Nextel, eBay-Skype, the growth of Google services, and the founding of YouTube illustrate the corporate and technology layer underneath the macro story.

#### 3B. Monetary & Financial Conditions

The United States tightened steadily. The Federal Reserve raised the federal funds target to 4.25 percent by December 2005, continuing the “measured pace” cycle that started in 2004. Long-term yields rose much less than short rates, producing the famous yield-curve “conundrum.” Credit stayed easy enough to support corporate borrowing, private equity, housing finance, and structured credit growth.

The euro area remained accommodative for most of the year. The ECB kept the main refinancing rate at 2.0 percent until December, then raised it to 2.25 percent. The Bank of England moved in the opposite direction, cutting Bank Rate from 4.75 percent to 4.50 percent in August after softer consumption and housing signals. The Bank of Japan maintained quantitative easing and near-zero short-term rates, targeting large current-account balances at the central bank.

Equity markets outside the United States were strong. Japan’s Nikkei 225 rose about 40 percent, Germany’s DAX about 27 percent, and the FTSE 100 about 17 percent. The U.S. market was much more subdued, with the broad market posting only a small positive return. Bond markets were shaped by low term premia, foreign official demand, pension demand, and credibility of inflation-targeting central banks.

#### 3C. Trade & Geopolitics

The WTO Doha Round remained unresolved. Trade growth stayed strong, but the multilateral system showed signs of strain over agriculture, industrial tariffs, and services. China’s WTO accession effects continued to reshape manufacturing supply chains, especially in electronics, textiles, machinery, and consumer goods. 2005 was also the first full year after the end of the WTO Agreement on Textiles and Clothing quota system, intensifying pressure on textile producers in some developing and advanced economies.

Geopolitics affected oil, defense budgets, and risk premia. The Iraq war continued, tensions around Iran affected oil-risk pricing, and Hurricane Katrina exposed the vulnerability of U.S. Gulf energy infrastructure. Sanctions and embargoes were not the dominant global growth driver, but energy geopolitics was a major price driver.

Regional integration advanced unevenly. The European Union absorbed the 2004 enlargement economically, while its constitutional treaty suffered political rejection in France and the Netherlands. ASEAN and China continued deepening trade linkages. In the Americas, NAFTA supply chains remained mature, while broader hemispheric trade liberalization was politically constrained.

#### 3D. Commodities & Energy

Oil was the defining commodity. WTI and Brent moved into price ranges that were high by early-2000s standards, with mid-year and late-summer spikes around refinery constraints, strong demand, and Hurricane Katrina. OPEC operated in a context of reduced spare capacity and high uncertainty about non-OPEC supply growth.

Metals were the second major story. Chinese fixed-asset investment and global manufacturing demand pushed copper, zinc, iron ore, nickel, and aluminum markets higher. Mining exporters benefited through income, tax receipts, investment, and currency appreciation pressure. Agricultural staples were less explosive than energy and metals, though sugar and some soft commodities strengthened. The broad food crisis of 2007-2008 had not yet arrived.

#### 3E. Labor & Demographics

Labor conditions improved in the United States, Japan, Germany, and several emerging economies, but unemployment definitions were not comparable across countries. Europe’s unemployment remained high in Germany, France, Spain, Belgium, and Italy by U.S. or Japanese standards. China’s official urban registered unemployment rate understated migrant and informal labor-market dynamics. India’s labor-market data were not directly comparable with OECD unemployment statistics.

Demographics were already economically important. Japan’s working-age population decline was an explicit policy concern. Europe faced aging and pension sustainability problems. China still benefited from a large working-age population, but rural-to-urban migration and household registration rules shaped labor supply. India’s demographic dividend was visible in services, construction, and domestic demand, but job quality and informality remained major constraints.

#### 5B. Corporate landscape

The largest companies by revenue were still dominated by retail, oil, autos, and industrial conglomerates. Fortune’s 2005 Global 500 ranking placed Walmart first, followed by BP, Exxon Mobil, Shell, General Motors, DaimlerChrysler, Toyota, Ford, General Electric, and Total. The ranking captures the energy-price effect: oil companies climbed because crude prices lifted revenue even where output growth was modest.

Major M&A included Procter & Gamble’s acquisition of Gillette, valued around $56.9 billion, one of the largest consumer-products deals of the period. Sprint and Nextel combined in telecommunications. eBay bought Skype for about $2.6 billion, a transaction that later became a cautionary example of strategic overreach but also signaled the perceived value of internet communications networks. Banking, telecoms, lodging, and consumer goods all saw deal activity supported by low financing costs.

Bankruptcies and failures were not yet systemic. U.S. airlines and auto suppliers were under pressure from fuel, pensions, and competition. General Motors and Ford faced deteriorating credit perceptions, while Japanese banks and firms looked healthier after years of restructuring.

#### 5C. Innovation & technology

2005 was a foundational year for platform internet. YouTube was founded, Google Maps launched, broadband adoption expanded, and online advertising became a more mature business model. These innovations were not yet large enough to dominate macro aggregates, but they changed the microeconomics of media, advertising, search, mapping, and user-generated content.

Semiconductors, mobile handsets, LCD displays, broadband equipment, and enterprise software were the core technology value chains. Korea, Japan, Taiwan, China, the United States, India, Sweden, and Germany were the main nodes. India’s IT services and business-process outsourcing sectors grew as firms globalized back-office functions.

The EU ETS was an institutional innovation. Its first phase created carbon allowances for power and industrial installations. Early design problems, especially over-allocation, later became clear, but 2005 marked the beginning of carbon pricing as a large-scale market mechanism.

#### 5D. Labor markets & human capital

The global labor market was defined by skill polarization, migration, and informality. China’s migrant labor force powered factories and construction. India’s high-skill services exports grew rapidly, but the majority of workers remained informal. In advanced economies, high-skill finance, technology, health care, and professional services gained income share, while lower-skill tradable manufacturing faced pressure.

Unions remained important in continental Europe, Korea, public sectors, and autos, but bargaining power was uneven. Germany’s labor reforms, Italy’s competitiveness problems, and France’s unemployment showed the policy tension between social protection and labor-market flexibility. Migration was economically significant in Spain, the United Kingdom, the United States, Gulf economies outside the top 20, and parts of Europe.

#### 6B. Fixed income

The fixed-income story was low long-term yields despite rising short rates. U.S. 10-year Treasury yields averaged roughly the low-to-mid 4 percent range, while the Fed funds target moved up steadily. German Bund yields were lower, Japan’s 10-year yield remained near the low 1 percent range, and U.K. gilt yields were around the low-to-mid 4 percent range. Credit spreads were generally tight, reflecting strong corporate profits, liquidity, and investor demand for yield.

Sovereign debt stress was not a global theme in 2005, but there were important legacies. Argentina completed a major debt restructuring after its 2001 default. Turkey continued post-crisis adjustment. Italy and Belgium carried high debt burdens inside the euro area, while Japan’s gross debt was very high but domestically financed.

#### 6C. Currencies

The U.S. dollar strengthened against the euro and yen during much of 2005 as interest-rate differentials moved in its favor. China’s renminbi reform was the key structural FX event. Asian currencies were watched for follow-on appreciation, but most moves were gradual.

Commodity currencies such as the Canadian dollar and Australian dollar were supported by high resource prices. Brazil’s real strengthened on external improvement and high interest rates. Turkey’s new lira symbolized stabilization but the economy remained externally exposed.

#### 6D. Alternative assets

Gold averaged about $444 per troy ounce and rose on liquidity, dollar concerns, and commodity-market momentum. Real estate was the more systemically important alternative asset. U.S., Spanish, British, Australian, and parts of European property markets were elevated. ### 7. RISKS, CRISES & BLACK SWANS

The most visible crisis was Hurricane Katrina. It caused large human and economic disruption, damaged Gulf Coast infrastructure, affected energy supply chains, and exposed weaknesses in disaster response and insurance systems. It was a macro-relevant shock because U.S. Gulf energy infrastructure was central to refining and distribution.

The most important non-crisis risk was the U.S. housing and credit boom. At the time, the risk was debated but not fully understood. With hindsight, lax underwriting, securitization incentives, leverage, and reliance on rising house prices were systemic vulnerabilities.

Global imbalances were an active risk, not merely a hindsight risk. Economists disagreed on whether they would unwind gradually through exchange-rate changes and saving shifts or abruptly through dollar and bond-market stress. The abrupt adjustment did not occur in 2005, but the imbalance widened.

Oil-price risk materialized. High energy prices did not cause a global recession in 2005, partly because monetary credibility was stronger than in the 1970s and because energy intensity had declined. However, oil prices redistributed income toward exporters and squeezed real purchasing power in importers.

Other vulnerabilities included euro-area political fragmentation, Japanese deflation risk, banking and public-sector contingent liabilities in China, external deficits in Turkey and Spain, and commodity-dependence in Russia and Brazil.

### 8. YEAR-OVER-YEAR DELTA (2005 vs. 2004)

2005 was slower than 2004 but still strong. Trade growth decelerated from the exceptional pace of 2004, and world output growth eased, but the expansion remained broad. The most important shift was that the expansion became more visibly late-cycle: oil was higher, inflation pressure rose, and central banks became less uniformly accommodative.

The United States moved from early tightening to sustained tightening. The Fed’s rate path became central to global markets. In contrast, Japan stayed at zero and the ECB waited until December, widening policy divergence. Long-term yields stayed surprisingly low, increasing confidence at the time but raising concern in hindsight.

China’s nominal GDP moved ahead of France in the World Bank current-dollar ranking. This was partly real growth, partly exchange-rate and price dynamics, but symbolically important. Brazil moved ahead of India in current dollars because of currency appreciation and commodities, even though India’s real growth was faster. Australia moved ahead of the Netherlands on commodity and exchange-rate strength.

The commodity boom moved from theme to macro driver. Resource exporters saw better fiscal and external positions. Importers saw margin and household-income pressure. Corporate revenue rankings shifted toward oil majors.

New risks emerged or intensified: U.S. mortgage-credit quality, Spain’s construction imbalance, global imbalances, oil dependence, and the possibility that low long-term yields were encouraging leverage. Risks that eased included Japanese banking distress, some emerging-market external fragility, and Turkey’s post-2001 stabilization credibility.

### 9. DATA CONFIDENCE & GAPS

**High-confidence data.** Nominal GDP rankings, world GDP, GDP per capita, population, and broad GDP growth are high-confidence for World Bank WDI countries. U.S., euro-area, Japan, U.K., Canada, Australia, Sweden, Switzerland, and Korea data are generally reliable and well documented.

**Medium-confidence data.** Fiscal balances, government debt, current accounts, policy-rate comparisons, and exchange rates are generally reliable for advanced economies but definitions vary. Debt can mean gross public debt, net public debt, central government debt, or general government debt. Budget balances can be central, federal, or general government. Current-account data are more comparable than fiscal data but still subject to later revision.

**Lower-confidence or non-comparable data.** Unemployment is the weakest cross-country table item. China’s registered urban unemployment is not comparable with U.S. U-3 or OECD harmonized unemployment. India’s labor-market informality makes a single unemployment rate misleading. Mexico, Brazil, Turkey, and Russia have definitional and informality issues. The document therefore flags comparability rather than pretending the rates are identical measures.

**Historical ranking caveats.** Taiwan is excluded from the World Bank country ranking because World Bank WDI does not provide standard country data for Taiwan. UNCTAD reports Taiwan GDP at about $374.1 billion in 2005, below Belgium and above Saudi Arabia, so the top 20 sovereign-country list would not change if Taiwan were added as an economy, but the next-ranked positions would.

**Data not available.** Comparable city-level real estate prices, detailed global sector-level profitability by country, full top-20 market-cap rankings, corporate bankruptcies outside major reported cases, and exact YoY changes for every country-table indicator are not available in this source packet. Some country policy-rate entries are approximations because monetary frameworks differed across countries.

**Weakest claims.** The weakest evidence in this document is in micro-sector detail for countries where national statistics are not included in the source packet, especially informal labor, real estate quality, and banking-sector health. These claims are stated as qualitative assessments and should be checked against national statistical offices or IMF Article IV reports before being used for quantitative modeling.
