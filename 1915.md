# MACRO/MICRO GLOBAL ECONOMIC ASSESSMENT - YEAR: 1915

### 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

1915 was the first full year in which the world economy operated under the conditions of World War I. The defining economic fact was not a single business cycle turn, but the conversion of major European economies from peacetime market coordination toward state-directed war economies. Finance, trade, labor allocation, shipping, food supply, and industrial production were all pulled into the war effort. The global gold standard had already been severely disrupted in 1914; in 1915 the practical monetary system became a set of emergency regimes, with gold convertibility suspended or restricted in many belligerent countries, capital controls expanding, and neutral financial centers gaining relative importance.

The largest global reallocation was away from civilian consumption and toward public procurement, shipping, munitions, food supply, and strategic materials. Britain and France relied increasingly on imports and external finance, especially from North America. Germany and Austria-Hungary faced tightening blockade pressure and import substitution problems. Russia had not yet suffered its full output collapse, but the weaknesses of transport, procurement, food distribution, and monetary finance were becoming visible. Italy entered the war in May 1915, adding another major European economy to the mobilization system.

The United States was the most important non-belligerent beneficiary. It was still neutral, but its export industries, banks, railroads, farmers, and manufacturers increasingly served Allied demand. Gold inflows and Allied borrowing strengthened the dollar and the American banking system, while also raising concerns about domestic credit expansion and inflation. Japan also gained from the war through export growth, shipping demand, and reduced European competition in Asian markets. China, India, Argentina, Brazil, Canada, Australia, and other producers were pulled into war trade channels in uneven ways.

GDP rankings for 1915 are necessarily estimates. No contemporary global system of national accounts existed. The figures below use Maddison-style reconstructed real GDP and population series, cross-checked against later economic history literature and official series where available. They should not be read as official 1915 GDP. For pre-1990 non-market or imperial economies, and for colonial economies such as British India and the Dutch East Indies, uncertainty is high. Where modern-style indicators were not available, this document states "Data not available" rather than guessing.

### 2. KEY THEMES OF 1915

**1. Total-war mobilization became the dominant macroeconomic regime.** 1914 had been a shock year; 1915 was the year in which governments accepted that the war would not be short. Ministries, treasuries, central banks, and procurement agencies expanded their roles. Britain moved deeper into munitions coordination and short-term Treasury finance. France developed a more mixed economy of state buying and private production. Germany relied on war loans, central bank support, and import control. The forward linkage was the larger command economies of 1916 to 1918.

**2. The classical gold standard gave way to wartime monetary fragmentation.** Before the war, the gold standard and sterling-centered bill market had supported international trade and capital flows. In 1915, gold convertibility, private gold exports, and cross-border payments were heavily restricted in many belligerent economies. Britain kept a managed gold and sterling system, but not the prewar free gold standard. Germany had suspended specie payments in 1914. The United States received gold inflows from Europe, strengthening its reserve position and accelerating the dollar's rise as an international currency.

**3. Neutral economies became strategic suppliers and financial intermediaries.** The United States, Japan, the Netherlands, Spain, Argentina, Brazil, and other neutrals experienced a mixture of export opportunity, shipping disruption, inflation, and diplomatic pressure. Neutrality did not mean insulation. It meant exposure to blockades, freight shortages, insurance costs, foreign exchange strain, and pressure to choose trading partners indirectly.

**4. Blockade and shipping constraints reshaped commodity markets.** Britain and its allies used sea power to restrict Central Power imports. Germany and Austria-Hungary faced growing scarcity of food, fertilizer, metals, and imported raw materials. At the same time, ocean freight rates rose and commodity prices shifted. Oil remained a small but rising strategic sector; the reported average crude oil price for 1915 was about $0.64 per barrel in nominal terms. Wheat and other staples were affected by higher demand and freight costs.

**5. Labor markets were militarized and segmented.** Millions of workers were mobilized or redirected to war industries. Women, colonial workers, older workers, and youth entered or expanded participation in industrial and support work. Standard unemployment rates are mostly not comparable or unavailable for 1915. The central labor issue was not peacetime joblessness, but the allocation of scarce labor among armies, farms, transport, factories, and households.

**6. The United States moved from debtor periphery toward creditor center.** The shift was not complete in 1915, but it was visible. European purchases of American food, raw materials, and manufactured goods generated gold inflows. Private American banks arranged large credits for Allied governments. The forward linkage was the postwar rise of New York in global finance and the weakening of London's prewar monopoly over international liquidity.

**7. Food, transport, and inflation risks were already systemic.** Russia, Germany, the Ottoman Empire, and occupied Belgium faced serious distribution problems. These were not only agricultural problems. They were failures of transport, procurement, price controls, fiscal finance, and political legitimacy. With hindsight, 1915 can be seen as an early warning year for the deeper crises of 1916 to 1918.

### 3. MACROECONOMIC OVERVIEW

#### 3A. Global Aggregates:

**World GDP and growth rate:** Data not available as a reliable contemporaneous world aggregate. Maddison-style reconstructions place world output in the low single-digit trillions of 1990 international dollars, but annual global growth for 1915 is highly uncertain because major combatants, colonies, and non-market economies were reconstructed later with different methods. A defensible statement is that global output was redistributed rather than uniformly reduced: U.S. and Japanese output rose or held up, while parts of continental Europe contracted or shifted heavily toward war production.

**Global trade volume:** Data not available as a precise annual global volume. Trade flows were reorganized by blockade, military shipping needs, freight shortages, insurance costs, and import licensing. Allied access to Atlantic shipping and American supply was a central advantage. Central Powers increasingly relied on domestic production, occupied territories, neutral intermediaries, and rationing.

**Reserve currency dynamics:** Sterling remained central to global finance, but wartime controls weakened the old gold-sterling system. The U.S. dollar gained importance because the United States was receiving gold, selling exports, and extending credit. There was no Bretton Woods system in 1915.

**Global inflation trends:** Inflation rose in many belligerent and neutral economies, but comparable CPI series are limited. Inflation was driven by monetary expansion, war borrowing, shipping bottlenecks, higher food prices, and scarcity of tradable goods. Mexico and the Ottoman Empire faced especially severe monetary instability. The United States experienced emerging inflationary pressure from gold inflows and export demand.

**Key commodity prices:** Nominal crude oil averaged about $0.64 per barrel in 1915 in historical oil price series. Gold's official U.S. mint price remained $20.67 per troy ounce, but free gold markets were distorted by controls. Wheat prices were materially higher than in 1914, with contemporary statistical commentary attributing increases to both demand and freight costs. Copper, coal, nitrates, rubber, cotton, jute, and steel-related inputs were strategic, but exact global price series are incomplete in this document.

**International capital flow direction:** Capital flowed increasingly from the United States and neutral financial channels toward the Allied powers. Britain and France sold assets, borrowed in New York, and mobilized domestic savings. Germany financed itself mainly through domestic war loans and central bank credit. Capital mobility was far below prewar norms.

#### 3B. Monetary & Financial Conditions:

**United States:** The Federal Reserve System was new and still developing. Gold inflows from Europe expanded reserves and credit. The modern federal funds rate did not exist. Discount rates at Reserve Banks were the relevant instrument, but they were not equivalent to a modern policy rate.

**United Kingdom:** Bank Rate was 5 percent through 1915 in the Bank of England historical series. In practice, war finance and Treasury bill issuance increasingly influenced money-market conditions. The pound remained central, but not under the old free gold standard conditions.

**Germany:** The Reichsbank had suspended specie payments in 1914. War finance relied on Treasury bills, central bank credit, and repeated war loans. The 1914 war loan had a 5 percent coupon; later wartime finance continued the debt-heavy model.

**France:** The Banque de France and Treasury operated under wartime finance conditions. Gold convertibility and capital movement were constrained. The state expanded purchasing, financing, and allocation functions.

**Japan:** Japan retained more normal financial conditions than European belligerents and benefited from export growth. Data not available for a single comparable policy rate in this document.

**Stock markets:** The 1914 market closures gave way to selective reopening in 1915. The New York market staged a strong rebound; the Dow Jones Industrial Average ended 1915 at roughly 99.15, with an annual gain of about 81 percent from its depressed 1914 close. London reopened in January 1915, but capital markets were increasingly subordinated to war finance. Berlin bond trading remained severely restricted.

**Bond markets and yield curves:** Standard 10-year yield comparisons are not reliable for 1915. Government borrowing dominated. Britain issued large war loans and short-term Treasury instruments. Germany relied heavily on war loans and Reichsbank-supported paper. Many private capital markets were crowded out by public borrowing.

#### 3C. Trade & Geopolitics:

World trade in 1915 was geopolitically divided. Britain expanded blockade measures, including restrictions on cargoes considered destined for enemy use. Germany faced declining imports and attempted to respond through substitutes, rationing, and pressure on neutral routes. Allied purchases in the United States became a major political and financial issue. Japan used the war to expand trade and influence in Asia. Italy's entry into the war in May 1915 changed European trade and fiscal alignments. Colonial economies were integrated into imperial supply chains through food, raw materials, labor, and finance.

No EEC, ASEAN, IMF, World Bank, or Bretton Woods institutions existed. Regional integration was imperial rather than treaty-based in the later twentieth-century sense. Tariffs mattered, but wartime licensing, blockade, shipping priority, and exchange controls were more important than normal tariff bargaining.

#### 3D. Commodities & Energy:

Oil was strategically important but still smaller than coal in the global energy system. Coal remained the core fuel for heavy industry, shipping, railways, and urban energy. Britain and Germany were major coal economies; French access to coal and steel was damaged by the occupation of industrial regions. U.S. oil production and refining gained importance as transport and mechanization needs rose. Metals, nitrates, rubber, cotton, jute, and grain moved through increasingly militarized supply chains.

Agriculture was pressured by labor mobilization, transport bottlenecks, freight rates, requisitions, and changing export demand. Wheat exporters such as the United States, Canada, Australia, and Argentina were strategically important. Food-importing and blockaded economies faced the greatest strain.

#### 3E. Labor & Demographics:

Labor market data are not comparable across countries in 1915. The dominant trends were conscription, war industry expansion, female industrial employment, colonial labor mobilization, and pressure on farm labor. Urban consumers faced rising food prices in many countries. Migration was constrained by war, but refugees, prisoners, colonial workers, and internal relocation became economically significant. Productivity statistics are weak, but it is clear that civilian productivity and welfare were often sacrificed to military output and state procurement.

The United States was the clearest macroeconomic winner of 1915. It remained neutral, but Allied demand for food, raw materials, chemicals, finance, transport, and manufactured goods lifted output and profits. The U.S. economy was not yet a fully mobilized war economy, but it was becoming the supply base for Allied Europe. Gold inflows increased banking reserves and helped transform New York into a more important international financial center.

The Federal Reserve was too new to operate like a modern central bank. It could not easily neutralize gold inflows, and credit conditions were shaped by export demand and bank lending. The main macro risk was that export-led expansion and gold inflows would later turn into inflationary pressure.

**Micro/sectoral highlights:** Agriculture benefited from grain demand. Steel, chemicals, explosives inputs, shipping, railroads, and finance gained. New York investment banks arranged large Allied credits. Consumer conditions were mixed: employment and income rose in export industries, but prices began to rise. Corporate data by revenue and market capitalization are incomplete for 1915.

China's aggregate GDP was large because of population, not per capita income. The Republic of China under Yuan Shikai had limited fiscal reach and a fragmented political economy. National accounts, unemployment, and CPI data are not available in modern form. The currency system was silver-based and regionally uneven, although 1914 to 1915 coinage reforms helped standardize silver yuan circulation.

The war had a paradoxical effect. European disruption opened markets for Chinese exports and reduced the trade deficit, but these gains were temporary and uneven. Treaty ports, coastal trade, textiles, foodstuffs, and raw material exports were more directly affected than inland subsistence agriculture.

**Micro/sectoral highlights:** Export merchants, treaty-port finance, shipping intermediaries, and some light manufacturers benefited. The household economy remained overwhelmingly agrarian. Corporate data are limited. Political fragmentation limited infrastructure and fiscal modernization.

#### 5B. Corporate landscape:

Globally comparable top-company rankings by revenue or market capitalization are not available for 1915. The strongest corporate clusters were U.S. steel, railroads, oil, banking, and chemicals; British shipping, banking, mining, and armaments supply; German chemicals, electrical equipment, coal, and heavy industry; French banking and relocated industrial production; Japanese shipping, textiles, and trading companies. Corporate profits depended heavily on war demand, access to raw materials, shipping, and state contracts.

Major M&A data are incomplete in this document. IPO data are not globally comparable. Notable failures were often not formal bankruptcies but shutdowns, requisitions, occupation losses, exchange closures, or state redirection of capital. Nationalization in the later twentieth-century sense was less common than wartime control, licensing, requisitioning, and compulsory allocation.

#### 5C. Innovation & technology:

Innovation in 1915 was less about consumer technology and more about industrial organization, materials science, communications, logistics, chemical production, and standardized procurement. Germany's chemical sector, Britain's munitions organization, U.S. mass production capabilities, Japan's shipbuilding expansion, and Allied logistics all had long-term implications. Patent and R&D spending data are not comparable globally.

Infrastructure megaprojects were constrained by war priorities. Railways, ports, telegraph networks, and shipping capacity were more important than new peacetime construction. War needs accelerated applied engineering and production methods but often at the expense of civilian investment.

#### 5D. Labor markets & human capital:

Labor shifted from civilian sectors into military service, war production, railways, shipping, ports, agriculture, and state administration. Women entered or expanded work in industrial and clerical roles in several countries. Colonial labor and goods were mobilized across empires. Wage data are fragmented; real wages depended on whether nominal pay kept up with food and rent. Union power varied, but labor unrest became more likely where inflation outpaced wages or food distribution failed.

Education and human capital were affected by mobilization and fiscal pressure. In belligerent countries, the opportunity cost of war was large: investment in schools, housing, public health, and civilian capital formation was delayed or crowded out.

### 7. RISKS, CRISES & BLACK SWANS

The largest active crisis was the continuation and deepening of World War I. The "black swan" element of 1915 was not that war existed, but that many governments had still underestimated the duration, cost, and institutional demands of industrial warfare. By 1915, the risk of a short-war assumption had already materialized.

Key systemic vulnerabilities were war finance inflation, transport bottlenecks, food distribution failures, shipping losses, blockade escalation, and capital-market crowding out. Russia, Germany, Austria-Hungary, the Ottoman Empire, Belgium, and Mexico had especially high institutional stress. Britain and France were more resilient because of empire, sea access, and access to U.S. credit, but they were building large debt burdens.

With hindsight, 1915 was an early warning year for later crises: Russia's 1917 breakdown, Germany's worsening scarcity, the Ottoman fiscal and supply crisis, postwar European debt instability, and the permanent shift in financial power toward the United States. These outcomes were not fully knowable in 1915, but the mechanisms were already visible.

### 8. YEAR-OVER-YEAR DELTA (1915 vs. 1914)

The year 1914 was defined by outbreak, panic, emergency closures, and first mobilization. The year 1915 was defined by institutional adaptation to long war. The major structural break was the acceptance that ordinary market mechanisms could not allocate food, labor, shipping, finance, and industrial inputs under war conditions.

Compared with 1914, public borrowing expanded sharply. Britain moved deeper into Treasury bill finance and war loans. Germany consolidated a debt-heavy war finance model. France reorganized production after industrial occupation. Italy moved from neutrality to belligerency. The United States moved from crisis recovery to export-led war boom. Japan moved further into export expansion and Asian market opportunity.

Risks that emerged in 1915 included food unrest, inflationary finance, and the dependence of Allied powers on U.S. credit. Risks that partly resolved included the immediate liquidity panic of 1914 and the uncertainty over whether New York could function as a major wartime financial market. The shift in economic influence from Europe toward the United States accelerated.

### 9. DATA CONFIDENCE & GAPS

**Reliable or relatively reliable areas:** U.S. nominal and real GDP series, U.S. population, Bank of England Bank Rate, some historical oil prices, Dow Jones index values, and selected war-finance institutional facts are relatively well documented. Britain, France, Germany, and Japan have strong economic history literatures, though not always modern annual macro indicators for 1915.

**Estimated areas:** GDP rankings, GDP per capita, and population for most countries are reconstructed. Maddison-style estimates are suitable for broad ranking but not precise annual policy analysis. Soviet-bloc predecessor regions, China, India, Indonesia, the Ottoman Empire, Mexico, and occupied Belgium have high uncertainty. Error margins of plus or minus 20 to 30 percent are plausible for several entries.

**Conflicting areas:** GDP rankings change depending on whether one uses modern borders, imperial polities, colonies, nominal exchange rates, 1990 international dollars, 2011 PPP dollars, or 2021 international dollars. China, India, Russia, the United Kingdom, and Germany can shift order depending on method. Colonial economies such as British India and the Dutch East Indies are not sovereign states but were major economic units.

**Unavailable data:** Comparable unemployment, CPI, central bank policy rates, government debt/GDP, current account/GDP, budget balance/GDP, and exchange-rate series are not available for many 1915 economies. Corporate revenue, market capitalization, M&A, IPO, bankruptcy, household debt, and real estate data are generally not globally comparable.

**Weakest claims:** The exact lower-bound top-20 ranking, country-specific GDP growth rates outside the United States, and sectoral performance rankings for colonial and neutral economies have the weakest evidence. They should be treated as historically informed estimates, not official statistics.
