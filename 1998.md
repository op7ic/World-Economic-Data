# MACRO/MICRO GLOBAL ECONOMIC ASSESSMENT - YEAR: 1998

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

1998 was the year in which the Asian financial crisis stopped being a regional balance-sheet problem and became a global financial-system stress test. Indonesia, Thailand, South Korea, Malaysia, and other Asian economies were already in crisis or adjustment after the 1997 shock. In 1998 the stress spread through trade, commodity prices, bank balance sheets, capital flows, and investor risk appetite. Russia's August default and devaluation then turned a regional-emerging-market shock into a broader liquidity event that reached Wall Street through Long-Term Capital Management and other leveraged positions.

The dominant contrast was between resilient demand in the United States and parts of Western Europe, and recession or financial distress in Japan, emerging Asia, Russia, and commodity-linked economies. World output growth fell sharply from the unusually strong 1997 pace. Contemporary World Bank estimates put world growth around 1.8 percent in 1998, while later IMF and WTO estimates also show a major slowdown in world trade. The United States grew strongly, Europe prepared for the euro, and Japan contracted. These differences created large exchange-rate and capital-flow effects: money moved toward dollar assets and high-quality government bonds while emerging-market spreads widened.

Policy regimes changed visibly. The euro's conversion rates were fixed on 31 December 1998, closing the deutsche mark, franc, lira, guilder, peseta, and other legacy-currency market era before legal euro introduction on 1 January 1999. The Federal Reserve cut rates three times in response to market stress after Russia and LTCM. Japan moved closer to zero-rate monetary policy and announced banking and fiscal measures. In emerging markets, 1998 strengthened the post-crisis consensus that short-term foreign-currency borrowing, weak banking supervision, and rigid exchange-rate regimes could be destabilizing.

At the micro level, 1998 was also a year of consolidation and technological transition. Exxon and Mobil signed a merger agreement, Citicorp and Travelers formed Citigroup, Daimler-Benz and Chrysler signed their merger contract, and Google was incorporated. Falling oil prices hurt producers and helped consumers, internet and telecommunications optimism lifted U.S. equities, and crisis-driven restructuring changed corporate governance in South Korea, Thailand, and parts of Latin America. Hindsight shows that 1998 helped shape the early-2000s world: the euro, the internet equity boom, emerging-market reserve accumulation, and a new sensitivity to global liquidity shocks.

## 2. KEY THEMES OF 1998

### 2.1 Contagion from Asia to the global financial system

The Asian crisis began in 1997 but 1998 revealed its international transmission channels. Currency depreciation and banking stress in Asia reduced import demand, compressed investment, and forced large current-account reversals. South Korea and Thailand moved from deficits to surpluses through recession and import collapse. Commodity exporters were hit because Asia had been a major source of marginal demand for oil, metals, timber, and many agricultural products.

The significance in economic history is that 1998 made capital-account vulnerability a central policy issue. Economists disagreed about emphasis: some stressed moral hazard and fixed exchange rates, some stressed premature financial liberalization, some stressed panic and self-fulfilling runs, and some stressed weak domestic bank governance. The subsequent policy linkage was large: many emerging economies accumulated more foreign-exchange reserves, lengthened debt maturities, strengthened bank supervision, and became more cautious about short-term external debt.

### 2.2 Russia's default and the repricing of leveraged finance

Russia's 17 August 1998 ruble devaluation and domestic debt restructuring shocked investors because it challenged assumptions about IMF support, sovereign risk, and liquidity in emerging-market debt. The direct size of Russia's economy was modest compared with the United States or Japan, but the financial linkages were large because hedge funds, banks, and proprietary desks held correlated positions across emerging-market, credit, and convergence trades.

The forward linkage was immediate: Long-Term Capital Management required a private-sector recapitalization organized with Federal Reserve Bank of New York involvement. The event is historically significant because it showed that leverage and crowded trades could make a shock systemic even when the originating economy was not large. Later debates around 2007-09 repeatedly returned to the 1998 lesson: funding liquidity and market liquidity can disappear together.

### 2.3 The euro's final pre-launch year

1998 was the final year before legal euro introduction. The euro conversion rates were fixed on 31 December 1998 and the European Central Bank took over monetary-policy authority for the initial euro area. Germany, France, Italy, Spain, the Netherlands, and other first-wave members completed the transition from national monetary policy toward a shared central bank.

The significance was structural rather than cyclical. Currency risk inside the first euro area fell, bond yields converged, and bank/treasury systems prepared for euro-denominated clearing and securities markets. The forward linkage was enormous: the euro reshaped European capital markets, removed exchange-rate adjustment among members, and created institutional questions that became central during the sovereign-debt crisis years after 2010.

### 2.4 Japan's recession and the move toward zero-rate policy

Japan's weakness was one of the main global macro facts of 1998. The banking system remained impaired, the Nikkei stayed depressed, and fiscal and monetary measures struggled to revive private demand. The Bank of Japan lowered the overnight call target toward 0.25 percent and policy debates increasingly focused on liquidity traps, bank recapitalization, and deflation.

This theme matters because Japan became the first modern advanced-economy case study for the zero-lower-bound problem. The forward linkage runs through later unconventional monetary policy in the United States and Europe after 2008. What seemed Japan-specific in 1998 later became a global advanced-economy policy problem.

### 2.5 Commodity deflation and oil-price collapse

Oil prices fell to levels that stressed petroleum exporters and energy companies. Brent and WTI were near the low teens by late 1998, and some spot quotations approached roughly $10 per barrel. Metals and several agricultural commodities also weakened, although individual crops varied. Low commodity prices helped consumers and lowered CPI inflation in advanced economies, but they worsened fiscal and external balances in Russia, Mexico, Venezuela, Gulf producers, and parts of Africa.

The forward linkage was visible in corporate consolidation and producer policy. Exxon and Mobil signed their merger agreement in December 1998, BP and Amoco had announced a major deal earlier, and OPEC moved toward production cuts. The oil trough of 1998 and early 1999 preceded the price recovery of 1999-2000.

### 2.6 Technology optimism and the internet-market acceleration

The crisis year did not stop the U.S. technology boom. The Nasdaq rose strongly, internet IPOs attracted attention, and Google was incorporated in 1998. Technology was not yet the dominant macro sector it would become, but it was increasingly important for U.S. productivity narratives, capital expenditure, venture funding, and equity-market leadership.

The forward linkage was double-edged. Some companies born or scaled in this period became durable giants, while equity valuations and unproven internet business models contributed to the dot-com bubble that burst in 2000. The 1998 distinction is important: the technology transformation was real, but the pricing of many related assets became speculative.

## 3. MACROECONOMIC OVERVIEW

### 3A. Global Aggregates

**World GDP and growth.** World current-dollar GDP was approximately $31.5 trillion in World Bank WDI-style current U.S. dollar terms. Real world output growth slowed sharply, with the World Bank's Global Economic Prospects 1998/99 giving an estimate near 1.8 percent for 1998. IMF and WTO materials likewise show a major slowdown from 1997, although trade-volume estimates differ by publication date and methodology.

**Global trade volume.** World trade growth fell markedly as Asian import demand collapsed and Japan contracted. Contemporary World Bank estimates made during the year were more optimistic than later ex-post estimates. WTO and IMF post-year assessments put trade-volume growth closer to the low single digits, around 3 to 3.5 percent. This discrepancy should be treated as a data-confidence issue rather than averaged away.

**Reserve currency dynamics.** The U.S. dollar remained the leading reserve and transaction currency. The future euro did not yet circulate legally, but 1998 was the last year in which the deutsche mark, French franc, Italian lira, Dutch guilder, Spanish peseta, and other first-wave euro currencies traded as separate monetary units. The euro's introduction created the second major reserve-currency bloc after 1999.

**Global inflation.** Inflation was low in the United States, Japan, Canada, and Western Europe, helped by cheaper oil, cheaper imports, and output gaps outside the United States. Inflation remained high in some emerging markets, especially Turkey and Russia after devaluation. China experienced deflationary pressure.

**Commodity prices.** Oil prices were exceptionally weak. FRED series show Brent at $10.54 per barrel and WTI at $12.14 per barrel on 31 December 1998. Average oil prices for the year were in the mid-teens, depending on benchmark. Copper and several non-oil commodities weakened as Asian demand slowed. Gold was weak, averaging around the high-$200s per troy ounce, consistent with a strong dollar and low inflation expectations.

**Capital flows.** International capital moved away from emerging-market risk and toward U.S. Treasuries, German Bunds, and other high-quality assets. Private capital flows to many developing economies fell or reversed. IMF programs and official financing became central in Asia and later Brazil, while Russia showed that official support could not overcome fiscal and confidence problems by itself.

### 3B. Monetary & Financial Conditions

**United States.** The Federal Reserve began 1998 with policy tight enough to watch inflation, then shifted toward risk management after Russia and LTCM. The FOMC cut the federal funds target by 25 basis points on 29 September, again on 15 October, and again in November, leaving the target at 4.75 percent by year-end.

**United Kingdom.** The Bank of England entered the year with restrictive settings after earlier tightening, then cut rates as external weakness and domestic slowdown signals increased. Sterling stayed relatively firm, adding pressure to manufacturing exporters.

**Germany and incoming euro area.** The Bundesbank and other European central banks converged interest rates ahead of EMU. Bond yields converged across core and periphery euro entrants, reflecting reduced currency risk and confidence in the euro framework.

**Japan.** The Bank of Japan reduced the overnight call-rate target toward 0.25 percent in September. Monetary policy was becoming constrained by near-zero rates and bank credit weakness. Long-term yields fell to very low levels as recession and deflation concerns intensified.

**Financial markets.** The U.S. equity market recovered after an autumn sell-off and ended with strong returns. European equities generally rose, supported by lower rates and euro optimism. Japan remained weak. Emerging-market equities and debt were volatile, with Russian assets suffering extreme losses. High-quality bond yields fell as investors sought safety.

### 3C. Trade & Geopolitics

The WTO system remained the central multilateral trade framework, but the Asian crisis tested whether countries would resort to protectionism. Broadly, the main trade effect in 1998 came through demand collapse and exchange-rate changes rather than a global tariff war. Regional integration deepened in Europe through EMU. NAFTA continued to support Mexico's export-manufacturing recovery. ASEAN economies faced crisis adjustment, and policy debate shifted toward financial regulation as much as trade liberalization.

Geopolitically, the Cold War had been over for less than a decade, but post-Soviet transition remained unstable. Russia's fiscal and debt crisis weakened confidence in transition-economy assets. China maintained currency stability and became more important as a regional stabilizer. In Latin America, Brazil became the next major concern after Russia, with international support efforts focused on preventing a disorderly devaluation.

### 3D. Commodities & Energy

Oil was the central commodity story. OPEC and some non-OPEC producers discussed and implemented production cuts, but prices remained weak through much of 1998 and into early 1999. Low oil prices strained producer fiscal balances and contributed to Russia's vulnerability. Energy-company consolidation accelerated, including BP-Amoco and Exxon-Mobil.

Metals were pressured by lower Asian industrial demand. Copper weakness signaled reduced construction and manufacturing momentum in emerging Asia. Agricultural commodities were mixed: cotton and some traded crops weakened, while cocoa and coffee had idiosyncratic movements. For developing economies dependent on a narrow export base, commodity weakness translated quickly into fiscal and external pressure.

### 3E. Labor & Demographics

Labor markets diverged sharply. The United States and several European economies had improving employment. Spain still had very high unemployment despite rapid growth. Japan's unemployment rose to levels that were unusually high for Japan. South Korea saw a dramatic labor-market shock as crisis restructuring lifted unemployment. Russia experienced official unemployment, wage arrears, underemployment, and informal adjustment.

Demographically, the major structural trends were urbanization in Asia, population aging in Japan and Western Europe, and large youth cohorts in India, Mexico, Turkey, and parts of Latin America. Migration had economic significance in Europe and North America, but comparable annual global migration data for 1998 are not available in the reviewed sources.

**Economic narrative**

That response reinforced the late-1990s pattern of strong asset markets, deep credit intermediation, and rising confidence in the U.S. as the world's liquidity backstop.

- Consumer balance sheets benefited from employment and equity wealth, but household leverage and equity valuation risk were building.

### 5B. Corporate landscape

The corporate landscape of 1998 was dominated by consolidation. Exxon and Mobil signed a definitive merger agreement on 1 December 1998. Economically significant innovation centered on internet search, e-commerce, mobile telecommunications, enterprise software, semiconductor equipment, and financial-market infrastructure. ### 5D. Labor markets & human capital

Labor-market shifts were crisis-specific. South Korea moved abruptly from labor shortage to high unemployment as corporate restructuring accelerated. In the United States, a tight labor market supported wages and consumption, but inequality and skill premia remained policy concerns.

Human capital investment increasingly favored information technology, engineering, finance, and internationally tradable services. India's software workforce, U.S. technology clusters, Sweden's telecom skills, and South Korea's electronics workforce were examples of micro foundations for later growth. Union power and labor regulation varied widely, so unemployment figures should not be read as fully comparable across countries.

## 7. RISKS, CRISES & BLACK SWANS

**Active crises.** The Asian financial crisis remained active across 1998, with Indonesia, South Korea, Thailand, and Malaysia experiencing severe financial, currency, or output stress. Japan's banking crisis and recession added to regional weakness. Russia's August default and devaluation became the global systemic event of the year. LTCM's near-failure was a near-miss in the core financial system.

**Near-misses.** Brazil was the most important near-miss during 1998, with the full devaluation delayed until January 1999. Argentina remained stable in narrow currency-board terms, but external and competitiveness risks accumulated. Hong Kong's currency board and equity market were under pressure, although the peg survived.

**Systemic vulnerabilities visible with hindsight.** Hindsight reveals several vulnerabilities that were not equally obvious to all observers at the time: crowded leveraged trades, dependence on short-term foreign-currency funding, weak banking supervision, fiscal fragility under fixed exchange rates, and the possibility that advanced-economy central banks would respond to market stress in ways that encouraged risk-taking. It is important not to overstate hindsight: many policymakers in 1998 were responding to genuine panic and uncertainty, not simply rewarding speculation.

**Policy mistakes debated by economists.** The Asian crisis response remains debated. Critics argue that fiscal and monetary tightening in some programs was initially too harsh. Defenders argue that credibility and external financing required strong stabilization. Russia's policy failure is less controversial at the proximate level: a weak fiscal base, short-term debt dependence, and fragile confidence made the exchange-rate and debt regime unsustainable.

## 8. YEAR-OVER-YEAR DELTA (1998 vs. 1997)

1997 was the year the Asian crisis began; 1998 was the year it became global. The year-over-year change was therefore not just slower growth but a shift in risk perception. Investors moved from believing the crisis could be contained within a handful of Asian currencies to recognizing that global leverage, commodity prices, and sovereign-debt markets were connected.

World growth slowed sharply. Japan moved from stagnation toward outright contraction. South Korea moved from high growth to deep recession. Russia moved from tentative stabilization to default. The United States remained strong, so the global cycle became more asymmetric: one large economy was booming while many emerging and commodity-linked economies were tightening or contracting.

Policy also shifted. In 1997 the main question was how to design IMF programs for Asia. In 1998 the question expanded to systemic liquidity and financial-market functioning in advanced economies. The Fed's autumn easing, the LTCM recapitalization, and the Brazil support package all reflected a broader concern that local crises could become global liquidity shocks.

Europe moved from preparation to irreversible transition. By the end of 1998, exchange rates among the first euro members were locked. This was a structural break from 1997 and from the entire postwar European monetary order.

Corporate behavior also changed. Low oil prices and financial-sector globalization produced giant mergers. Technology firms gained influence over equity markets and business expectations. The 1997 Asian crisis damaged old industrial-finance models in East Asia, while 1998 U.S. technology optimism pointed toward a new market narrative.

## 9. DATA CONFIDENCE & GAPS

**High-confidence data.** Nominal GDP rankings for the top 20 are high confidence because World Bank WDI current-dollar country series provide explicit 1998 values, and the same series is reproduced in FRED. The top-20 ranking is a nominal current-dollar ranking and should not be interpreted as PPP economic size. U.S., Japan, Germany, France, United Kingdom, Italy, China, Brazil, Canada, Spain, Mexico, Netherlands, India, Australia, South Korea, Switzerland, Argentina, Turkey, Russia, and Sweden all have WDI current-dollar GDP series.

**Moderate-confidence data.** Real GDP growth, CPI inflation, current-account balances, and unemployment rates are moderate confidence for OECD economies and major IMF-program countries, but definitions differ. Unemployment is especially non-comparable across China, India, Turkey, Russia, and several OECD countries because informal work, registration systems, and labor-force survey standards differ.

**Lower-confidence data.** Government-debt and fiscal-balance comparisons are lower confidence when not tied to a single dataset and a single definition. General government, central government, public sector, and gross versus net debt produce different results. For this reason, several country tables use Data not available rather than a guessed figure.

**Conflicting data.** World trade growth estimates differ across sources because some 1998 documents were written before final data were available. The World Bank's contemporaneous Global Economic Prospects estimate was higher than later WTO/IMF ex-post estimates. This document treats that as a documented conflict, not as an error to hide.

**Post-Soviet and crisis-economy uncertainty.** Russia's 1998 data are particularly uncertain because of the ruble collapse, barter and arrears, weak tax collection, and changing statistical systems. Unlike pre-1990 Soviet-bloc data, the 1998 Russian Federation is covered in WDI, but the economic reality behind official aggregates still had large measurement problems.

**Important missing data.** Comparable global 1998 data are not available in the reviewed sources for sector-level output by country, patent counts by country, city-level real estate returns, household debt across all top-20 economies, and comprehensive corporate revenue/market-cap rankings. Where these were unavailable, the document uses qualitative evidence or states Data not available.
