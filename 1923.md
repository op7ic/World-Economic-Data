# MACRO/MICRO GLOBAL ECONOMIC ASSESSMENT - YEAR: 1923

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

1923 was a year of uneven postwar reconstruction, monetary trauma, and early 1920s expansion. The United States was the clear global output leader and, by Maddison Project Database estimates, expanded strongly after the 1920-1921 recession. Western Europe remained split between recovery and fragility: Britain had a modest recovery but persistent unemployment and weak staple industries; France was rebuilding while pressing Germany over reparations; Germany endured the defining monetary catastrophe of the year, the Ruhr occupation and hyperinflation.

A historically accurate GDP ranking cannot be made from modern nominal GDP tables. The ranking below uses Maddison Project Database 2023 GDP per capita and population in 2011 international dollars. Where MPD 2023 has a 1923 country observation, it is used directly. Where an economically essential country lacks a 1923 GDP per capita observation, such as China and Poland, the table uses a transparent linear interpolation between the nearest MPD GDP per capita observations and MPD population for 1923. These interpolated entries are flagged. This method puts the United States first, China second, the United Kingdom third, India fourth, and Germany fifth.

The year’s dominant macro story was not a synchronized world cycle. It was a set of country-specific adjustments to the First World War settlement. Germany’s crisis was rooted in reparations conflict, fiscal weakness, the Ruhr occupation, and money creation. Japan’s economy was hit by the Great Kanto earthquake in September, with huge losses concentrated in Tokyo and Yokohama. The Soviet Union, formally created in late 1922, was recovering under the New Economic Policy. Italy consolidated early Fascist economic policy under a fiscal and business-confidence agenda. China remained politically fragmented under warlord-era conditions, with treaty-port commerce and regional finance more meaningful than unified national macro policy.

The global monetary system remained transitional. Bretton Woods did not exist. The classical gold standard had not been fully restored. The U.S. dollar was on gold, sterling was still off gold, and several European currencies were unstable or under reconstruction. International finance was shifting toward a dollar-sterling dual center, with New York gaining importance while London remained central to trade finance. Commodity markets were generally calmer than in the immediate postwar boom-bust: U.S. crude oil first purchase prices averaged $1.34 per barrel in 1923, while the official U.S. gold price remained $20.67 per troy ounce under the gold standard framework.

## 2. KEY THEMES OF 1923

### Theme 1: The German hyperinflation and the end of passive resistance

Germany’s 1923 crisis was the most dramatic macroeconomic event of the year. French and Belgian occupation of the Ruhr after Germany’s reparations default triggered passive resistance, disrupted coal and industrial production, and drove fiscal expenditures that were financed through money creation. The Papiermark collapsed, and by late 1923 the Rentenmark stabilization halted the inflationary spiral. The immediate effect was social and financial destruction: cash balances, savings, fixed incomes, and wage contracts were upended. The forward linkage is direct: stabilization in late 1923 set the stage for the Dawes Plan in 1924, renewed foreign lending, and the fragile mid-1920s German recovery.

### Theme 2: U.S. industrial expansion after the 1920-1921 recession

The United States entered 1923 with powerful industrial momentum. Maddison-based real GDP for the United States rose about 12.5 percent from 1922 to 1923 using GDP per capita multiplied by population. Autos, electrical equipment, construction, consumer durables, advertising, and installment credit were the visible microeconomic channels. The Federal Reserve’s New York discount rate moved from 4.0 percent early in the year to 4.5 percent later in the year, indicating firmer but not crisis-level monetary conditions. The forward linkage was the maturing of the U.S. “Roaring Twenties” expansion and the buildup of private credit and equity-market optimism later in the decade.

### Theme 3: A divided Europe: reconstruction, stabilization, and postwar unemployment

Europe was not a single economy in 1923. France and Belgium pushed the Ruhr strategy and were still reconstructing war-damaged regions. Britain experienced modest output growth but continued to carry high unemployment in coal, shipbuilding, textiles, and export industries. Austria was under League of Nations financial reconstruction, and Poland faced currency instability ahead of the zloty reform of 1924. This theme matters because the postwar settlement linked fiscal burdens, reparations, exchange rates, and political legitimacy. The forward linkage was the mid-1920s stabilization wave, including Germany’s Dawes Plan, Britain’s 1925 gold return, and several Eastern European monetary reforms.

### Theme 4: Non-Western economies were large but statistically uneven

China and India were among the largest economies by output volume, but their 1923 statistical base is much weaker than U.S. or Western European data. China’s MPD 2023 GDP per capita is not directly observed for 1923, so the China entry uses interpolation between 1913 and 1929 GDP per capita observations combined with the MPD 1923 population figure. India has a direct 1923 MPD entry, but it reflects a colonial economy dominated by agriculture and subject to monsoon, commodity, and administrative data limitations. This matters because modern narratives often underweight large Asian economies when using only official national accounts. The forward linkage is the long-run divergence between output scale and income per head.

### Theme 5: Disaster as macro-financial shock in Japan

The Great Kanto earthquake struck Japan on 1 September 1923. It was a localized but enormous economic shock to the Tokyo-Yokohama region, damaging firms, banks, transport, housing, and inventories. The Bank of Japan and government responded through special financial support and earthquake bills, helping prevent immediate credit collapse but leaving balance-sheet problems that contributed to later banking fragility. The forward linkage was the 1927 Japanese financial crisis and the acceleration of urban reconstruction, building standards, and state intervention in financial stabilization.

### Theme 6: Early interwar capital flows and international financial governance

The League of Nations financial reconstruction of Austria and other stabilization efforts showed an emerging model of multilateral oversight, conditional lending, and external guarantees. At the same time, U.S. capital was becoming more important in European stabilization, while London retained deep trade-finance networks. This theme is significant because it foreshadowed later international financial institutions, even though the 1920s system remained ad hoc, creditor-driven, and politically fragile.

## 3. MACROECONOMIC OVERVIEW

### 3A. Global Aggregates

**World GDP and growth.** A fully official, annually observed world GDP figure for 1923 is not available in the same sense as modern World Bank or IMF data. Using MPD 2023 country data with direct observations plus clearly labeled GDP per capita interpolation where needed, the available-country sum for 1923 is approximately **4.673 trillion 2011 international dollars** across 76 countries with usable GDP per capita and population data. This is not a complete modern national-accounts world total. It is a Maddison-style historical estimate and should be treated as approximate.

**Global trade volume.** Data not available as a single verified annual global trade-volume figure in this document. Qualitatively, global trade was recovering from the postwar slump but remained distorted by reparations, exchange instability, transport reorganization, and trade barriers.

**Reserve currency dynamics.** The world was in a partial gold-standard restoration phase. The U.S. dollar remained convertible into gold at the official parity. Sterling was not yet restored to gold and would not return until 1925. Central-bank reserve composition data for 1923 are not available in this document, but the institutional direction was toward a dollar-sterling system rather than the pre-1914 sterling-dominated gold standard.

**Global inflation trends.** No consistent global CPI aggregate is available. The dominant inflation event was Germany’s hyperinflation. Austria was stabilizing after its earlier postwar inflation. The United States and Britain were beyond the sharp 1920-1921 deflation but not in a high-inflation regime. Many developing and colonial economies lack comparable CPI data.

**Key commodity prices.** U.S. crude oil first purchase prices averaged **$1.34 per barrel** in 1923. The official U.S. gold price was **$20.67 per troy ounce**. Copper, wheat, rice, rubber, and sugar prices varied by market and contract; verified global annual prices are Data not available in this document.

**International capital flow direction.** Capital was moving from the United States and Britain toward European reconstruction, stabilization loans, and commodity-export economies. League of Nations loans and private banking syndicates were especially important for Central Europe. Capital flows to Latin America and the Dominions remained tied to railways, ports, land, mining, and public works.

### 3B. Monetary & Financial Conditions

**United States.** The New York Federal Reserve discount rate was 4.0 percent early in 1923 and 4.5 percent for much of the remainder of the year. Monetary policy was firmer than in the immediate recovery phase but not restrictive enough to stop industrial expansion.

**United Kingdom.** Bank Rate was 3 percent in the first half of 1923 and 4 percent in the second half. Britain was preparing, politically and financially, for eventual gold restoration, but the economy remained burdened by unemployment and weak exports.

**Germany.** Standard interest-rate comparisons break down under hyperinflation. The Reichsbank and money market operated in a collapsing unit of account until the late-1923 stabilization. The relevant policy shift was not a conventional rate decision but the fiscal-monetary stop to passive resistance and the creation of the Rentenmark.

**Japan.** The Great Kanto earthquake forced extraordinary liquidity and credit measures. The Bank of Japan’s emergency support helped prevent an immediate credit crunch but transferred risks into bank balance sheets and bills that later became politically and financially contentious.

**Exchange-rate regime.** Bretton Woods did not exist. Exchange rates were a mixture of gold-linked currencies, managed floats, unstable paper currencies, and colonial pegs. The German mark collapsed before the Rentenmark reform. Sterling remained off gold. The U.S. dollar was the strongest anchor among major currencies.

**Credit conditions and equities.** U.S. credit conditions supported consumer durables and construction, while Germany’s credit markets were disorganized by inflation. ### 3C. Trade & Geopolitics

The Ruhr occupation was the main geopolitical-economic event in Europe. France and Belgium occupied the Ruhr after Germany’s reparations default, seeking payment in kind and pressure on German fiscal compliance. The result was not a clean extraction of resources but a wider crisis in German production, finance, and politics.

Trade disputes were less institutionalized than in the later GATT era. Tariffs, exchange controls, reparations deliveries, colonial preferences, and bilateral debt settlements shaped flows. The United States had adopted the Fordney-McCumber Tariff in 1922, and its protectionist effects continued into 1923. Regional integration institutions such as the EEC did not exist. ASEAN did not exist. The League of Nations played a financial and technical role but not a free-trade integration role.

### 3D. Commodities & Energy

Oil markets were still pre-OPEC. The United States was a dominant producer, and Mexico remained important but was past its most spectacular early production phase. U.S. crude oil first purchase prices fell to $1.34 per barrel in 1923 from the higher postwar levels of 1920-1922. Coal remained central to European industry, making the Ruhr conflict economically decisive. Electricity, petroleum products, and autos were transforming the U.S. industrial structure, while much of Asia, Latin America, and Africa remained tied to agricultural and mining export cycles.

### 3E. Labor & Demographics

No comparable global unemployment rate exists for 1923. The best-supported general statement is that labor-market conditions differed sharply by country. U.S. employment improved with industrial recovery. Britain had persistent unemployment in staple export industries. Germany’s hyperinflation disrupted wage bargaining and household cash management. Japan’s disaster caused regional displacement and reconstruction labor demand. Urbanization continued, especially in the United States, Japan, Latin American capitals, and treaty-port China. Migration was economically important but increasingly constrained by quota regimes and postwar border controls.

The United States was the dominant economy of 1923 by a wide margin. Recovery from the 1920-1921 contraction had turned into a broad industrial upswing. Autos, petroleum products, electrical machinery, telephones, construction, advertising, and consumer finance formed the microeconomic core of the expansion. The economy was not free of weakness: agriculture remained under pressure relative to urban industry, and income gains were uneven.

Policy was not radically expansionary, but credit was available enough to support private investment and durable-goods consumption. The Federal Reserve moved rates modestly higher, and the equity market cooled after 1922. Hindsight risk: the same consumer credit, real-estate enthusiasm, and equity-market optimism that supported the 1920s boom later became vulnerabilities.

**Micro/sectoral highlights:** Ford and General Motors competed in a rapidly expanding auto market. Radio broadcasting and electrical appliances were moving from novelty to commercial industry. Chain retail, mail order, and national advertising helped create a more integrated consumer market. Banking conditions were much healthier than in 1920-1921, but rural banks remained exposed to farm distress.

Germany was the year’s central macroeconomic disaster. The Ruhr occupation and passive resistance hit the core industrial region, while the fiscal cost of supporting resistance fed money creation. Hyperinflation destroyed the currency function of money, accelerated barter and real-asset substitution, and redistributed wealth away from cash holders and creditors.

The late-1923 stabilization changed the regime. The end of passive resistance, fiscal correction, and Rentenmark introduction created the conditions for the 1924 Dawes Plan and foreign lending. At the micro level, the crisis did not affect all groups equally: debtors and owners of real assets could gain relative to fixed-income savers, while wage earners faced chaotic payment cycles.

**Micro/sectoral highlights:** Ruhr coal and steel were disrupted. Exporters and asset-heavy firms navigated the inflation differently from savers and fixed-income households. Banks processed nominally huge but economically unstable balance sheets. Real estate, inventories, and foreign exchange became stores of value.

## 7. RISKS, CRISES & BLACK SWANS

The German hyperinflation was the year’s systemic crisis. It exposed how fiscal breakdown, external claims, monetary accommodation, and political conflict could destroy a currency. The immediate crisis was resolved in late 1923, but the social memory and distributional shock persisted.

The Great Kanto earthquake was the major natural-disaster shock. It was not only a humanitarian and urban disaster but also a financial shock, because damaged firms and banks required extraordinary support. Hindsight links the earthquake-bill problem to later Japanese banking fragility.

Austria, Poland, and other Central and Eastern European states faced stabilization risk. The League of Nations model could restore confidence, but it often imposed fiscal discipline under external oversight, creating political tension.

Commodity-export economies faced price and demand volatility. Argentina, Canada, Australia, Brazil, Mexico, and the Dutch East Indies all depended on external markets. A fall in commodity prices or tighter global credit could transmit quickly into incomes and fiscal accounts.

Hindsight risk: the U.S. boom was becoming more credit-intensive. This did not make a crash inevitable in 1923, but consumer credit, real-estate expansion, and later equity speculation became more important as the decade advanced.

## 8. YEAR-OVER-YEAR DELTA (1923 vs. 1922)

The most important structural break was Germany’s collapse. MPD real GDP for Germany fell about 16.9 percent from 1922 to 1923. This was not a normal recession; it was a currency, fiscal, political, and industrial crisis tied to the Ruhr occupation and hyperinflation.

The United States strengthened dramatically, rising about 12.5 percent in the MPD real GDP estimate. The shift from post-recession recovery to consumer-industrial expansion became clearer. Autos, construction, and electrical sectors were the mechanisms.

The United Kingdom moved above India and Germany in the ranking because of modest British growth, Indian contraction, and German collapse. This ranking movement should not be misread as a return to prewar British dominance: Britain’s structural problems remained acute.

The former USSR rose above Italy due to strong recovery from a depressed post-civil-war base. This was a rebound under NEP, not yet the later Stalinist industrialization drive.

China remained second in the interpolated ranking. Its position was stable by aggregate scale, but the data are weaker than for advanced economies. Its political fragmentation meant that aggregate GDP did not translate into unified state capacity.

Japan’s aggregate GDP grew slightly in MPD data, but the year-over-year comparison hides the September earthquake’s concentrated destruction. A local shock in Tokyo-Yokohama had national financial consequences.

## 9. DATA CONFIDENCE & GAPS

**Highest-confidence data in this document:** the MPD 2023 dataset identity, GDP per capita and population method, U.S. and U.K. policy-rate series, EIA crude oil price, and the broad historical facts of Germany’s Ruhr-hyperinflation crisis and Japan’s Great Kanto earthquake.

**Medium-confidence data:** MPD-based country GDP rankings for countries with direct 1923 GDP per capita observations. MPD estimates are not official national accounts in the modern sense, but they are the appropriate source for long-run cross-country historical GDP comparisons.

**Lower-confidence data:** China and Poland GDP levels because GDP per capita is interpolated. The inclusion of China in the top ranks is robust, but the precise 1923 value is not. Poland’s top-20 inclusion is plausible but weaker than direct-observation countries.

**Very low comparability:** unemployment rates, CPI inflation, debt/GDP, current-account balances, budget balances, and policy rates across most countries. Definitions, territorial coverage, and institutions vary too much to present values as comparable without a separate statistical appendix.

**Soviet and non-market economies:** former USSR GDP is especially uncertain. Even when MPD provides a direct value, the underlying economy involved weak price signals, civil-war recovery, changing borders, and administrative data limitations. A rough uncertainty band of plus or minus 20-30 percent is appropriate.

**Colonial territories and modern labels:** India, Indonesia, and other entries use territorial labels for economies that were not sovereign in 1923. Indonesia refers to the Dutch East Indies territory. India refers to British India in the relevant historical-economic context, not the post-1947 state.
