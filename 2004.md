### 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

2004 was the strongest global expansion year of the early twenty-first century. IMF reporting described world output growth at about 5.1 percent, the fastest pace in nearly three decades, while the World Bank WITS/WDI current-dollar world GDP measure put global output at about US$44.18 trillion. The expansion was broad: the United States accelerated from the post-dot-com recovery into a credit- and housing-supported upswing; China continued double-digit industrialization; India grew rapidly in services, construction and manufacturing; commodity exporters benefited from higher prices; and Japan continued its uneven recovery from deflation and bank balance-sheet stress.

The year was defined by a rare combination of synchronized demand, easy-to-tightening financial conditions and rising commodity scarcity. The Federal Reserve began lifting the federal funds target from 1 percent in June and reached 2.25 percent by December, but long-term rates stayed low by historical standards. The ECB kept its main refinancing rate at 2 percent, the Bank of Japan remained at the zero-rate and quantitative-easing boundary, and the Bank of England tightened to 4.75 percent. Financial markets therefore faced a transition from emergency accommodation toward normalization, without yet pricing the full leverage and housing risks that would become visible later.

Trade was the second defining force. WTO data describe real merchandise export growth around 9 percent in 2004, about twice the pace of 2003. China, Korea, Singapore and Japan were central to the Asian export cycle; Germany and the Netherlands benefited from global capital-goods demand and intra-European trade; and commodity exporters from Russia to Brazil to Australia gained from energy, metals and agricultural demand. The U.S. current account deficit widened, while Asia and oil exporters accumulated surpluses, reinforcing the global imbalance pattern of the mid-2000s.

The microeconomic story was equally important. Google went public, the mobile-telecom industry consolidated, JPMorgan Chase completed the Bank One merger, and oil, autos, retail, finance and telecoms dominated global corporate rankings. Supply chains became more China-centered, European integration deepened with the May 2004 EU enlargement, and household balance sheets in several advanced economies became more exposed to real estate and credit cycles. With hindsight, 2004 looks like both a high-growth year and an incubation year for later vulnerabilities: global imbalances, low risk premia, U.S. housing leverage, European competitiveness divergence and commodity dependence.

### 2. KEY THEMES OF 2004

**Theme 1: Synchronized global expansion with emerging-market acceleration.** 2004 was historically significant because growth was strong in both advanced and emerging economies. The United States grew close to 4 percent, China above 10 percent on revised series, India near 8 percent, Russia above 7 percent and Brazil close to 6 percent. This made 2004 a bridge between the post-2001 recovery and the pre-crisis boom of 2005-2007. The countries most affected were China, India, Russia, Brazil, Korea, the United States and Germany. The forward linkage was clear: high growth encouraged risk-taking, cross-border lending and capital inflows, while also creating bottlenecks in oil, metals, electricity, shipping and skilled labor.

**Theme 2: Monetary policy normalization began, but global liquidity stayed abundant.** The Federal Reserve started a "measured pace" tightening cycle in June 2004, but policy rates remained low relative to nominal GDP growth and credit appetite. The Bank of England tightened earlier, the ECB stayed on hold, and Japan remained under quantitative easing. This mix mattered because it allowed global credit conditions to remain supportive even as short rates rose. The United States, United Kingdom, Australia, Canada and several emerging markets faced housing or credit expansion pressures. With hindsight, 2004 was not yet a crisis year, but it was a year in which cheap funding, search for yield and structured-credit growth became more embedded.

**Theme 3: China moved from export engine to macro-management challenge.** China’s economy continued rapid integration into global production networks. The problem in 2004 was not weak demand, but overheating: fixed-asset investment, energy shortages, transport bottlenecks and rising inflation pressures led authorities to tighten credit and raise benchmark lending rates. China’s expansion affected Japan, Korea, Germany, Australia, Brazil, Russia and oil exporters through trade and commodity channels. The forward linkage was China’s growing role as the marginal buyer of industrial commodities and as a driver of global manufacturing relocation.

**Theme 4: Commodity exporters regained macro leverage.** Oil prices rose sharply, with Brent averaging about US$38 per barrel and monthly averages moving much higher in the second half. Metals prices were also strong. Russia, Brazil, Australia, Canada and parts of the Middle East and Africa gained fiscal and external room. The significance is that commodity-exporting economies entered the mid-2000s with stronger current accounts, reserves and public finances than in the late 1990s. The forward linkage was the commodity supercycle, which strengthened through 2007 and reshaped capital spending in mining, energy, ports and rail.

**Theme 5: Europe deepened integration but exposed divergence.** On 1 May 2004, ten countries joined the European Union: Cyprus, Czechia, Estonia, Hungary, Latvia, Lithuania, Malta, Poland, Slovakia and Slovenia. This was economically important because it expanded the single market eastward, increased labor mobility over time and reinforced foreign direct investment into Central and Eastern Europe. At the same time, the euro area core still had weak domestic demand and fiscal-rule tensions. Germany relied heavily on exports and wage restraint; France and Italy struggled with reform and competitiveness; Spain boomed on construction and credit. The forward linkage was the widening divergence inside Europe that later mattered for the euro-area crisis.

**Theme 6: Corporate scale, platforms and consolidation advanced.** The largest global companies were still dominated by retail, oil, autos and industrial conglomerates, with Walmart, BP, Exxon Mobil, Shell, GM, Ford, DaimlerChrysler, Toyota, GE and Total among the largest by revenue. Yet 2004 also marked a platform turning point through Google’s IPO and the scaling of internet advertising. Telecom consolidation accelerated, including Cingular’s acquisition of AT&T Wireless, while U.S. banking consolidation continued with JPMorgan Chase and Bank One. The forward linkage was a shift toward platform economics, data-driven advertising, mobile scale and universal banking.

**Theme 7: Global imbalances became a central macro risk.** The United States ran a current account deficit above 5 percent of GDP, while Japan, Germany, China, Russia, Switzerland, Sweden, the Netherlands and oil exporters accumulated surpluses. The immediate 2004 interpretation was that capital flowed willingly into dollar assets and U.S. consumption supported world demand. With hindsight, the same imbalance pattern interacted with low yields, mortgage credit, securitization and reserve accumulation, contributing to systemic fragility.

### 3. MACROECONOMIC OVERVIEW

#### 3A. Global Aggregates

World GDP in current U.S. dollars was about US$44.18 trillion on the World Bank WITS/WDI measure. IMF communications put real global growth at about 5.1 percent, the strongest in decades. World trade expanded faster than output: WTO reporting describes real merchandise export growth of about 9 percent, with Asia, South and Central America, the Commonwealth of Independent States and commodity-exporting developing economies performing especially strongly.

Reserve-currency dynamics remained dollar-centered, but the dollar was under pressure. The euro traded much stronger than in 2001-2002, the Canadian dollar and several commodity currencies appreciated, and Asian reserve accumulation remained substantial. China’s renminbi stayed effectively pegged near 8.28 per U.S. dollar. Japan’s yen averaged around 108 per dollar. The international monetary system was a managed-float system dominated by the dollar, not a Bretton Woods fixed-rate system.

Commodity prices tightened materially. Brent crude averaged about US$38.26 per barrel, up from roughly US$28.83 in 2003, and monthly Brent prices moved toward US$50 in October. Gold averaged around US$410 per troy ounce, supported by dollar weakness and investor demand. Copper prices rose strongly as China-led industrial demand met constrained mine supply. Agricultural staples were mixed: grains were not in a generalized food crisis, but freight, energy and input costs were rising.

International capital flowed toward the United States and other deficit economies through bond markets, banking flows and portfolio investment, while emerging Asia and oil exporters accumulated reserves. Foreign direct investment continued to favor China, Central and Eastern Europe, and selected service and resource sectors. The broad direction was surplus-country saving into dollar and euro financial assets, plus multinational investment into supply-chain and commodity capacity.

#### 3B. Monetary & Financial Conditions

The United States began the year with the federal funds target at 1 percent and ended it at 2.25 percent after a sequence of 25 basis-point increases. The Federal Reserve judged that emergency accommodation was no longer necessary, but inflation remained contained enough to tighten gradually. Long-term Treasury yields did not rise proportionately, supporting mortgages, credit and equity valuations.

The euro area was more subdued. The ECB kept the main refinancing rate at 2 percent throughout 2004, with the deposit facility at 1 percent and marginal lending at 3 percent. Germany, France and Italy had weak domestic demand relative to the global expansion, while Spain and Ireland were more credit-sensitive. The United Kingdom was stronger: the Bank of England raised its repo rate to 4.75 percent by August, reflecting housing and demand pressures.

Japan remained exceptional. Short-term rates were held near zero under quantitative easing, and the Bank of Japan’s current-account-balance target remained the main operating framework. Deflation pressures were diminishing but not gone. Japanese banks and corporations were healthier than earlier in the decade, but the recovery still depended heavily on exports and corporate balance-sheet repair.

Equity markets rose moderately. U.S. equities posted positive returns, with the S&P 500 total return around 10.9 percent. Germany’s DAX rose about 7.3 percent, Britain’s FTSE 100 about 7.5 percent and Japan’s Nikkei 225 about 7.6 percent. Bond markets remained supported by low inflation expectations and strong demand for safe assets. Credit conditions were favorable in most advanced economies and improving in emerging markets.

#### 3C. Trade & Geopolitics

The WTO Doha Round remained unresolved, but trade volumes grew rapidly despite political frictions. The EU enlargement of 2004 was the major regional-integration event, expanding the single market and accelerating investment links between Western Europe and Central and Eastern Europe. NAFTA continued to shape North American trade, especially for Mexico and Canada. Asian production networks deepened around China, Japan, Korea, Taiwan and ASEAN economies.

Geopolitical risks were present but did not derail global growth. The Iraq war continued to affect oil risk premia and fiscal pressures. Russia’s Yukos affair raised concerns about property rights and state influence in strategic sectors, even as oil revenues strengthened Russia’s macro position. China’s WTO-era integration continued, but trade tensions around textiles, technology and exchange-rate policy were building.

#### 3D. Commodities & Energy

Oil was the central commodity story. Demand growth from China, the United States and broader global recovery met limited spare capacity, geopolitical risk and refining constraints. OPEC remained important, but the market increasingly focused on capacity limits and the ability of non-OPEC supply to respond. Russia, Canada, Brazil, Australia and Middle Eastern producers gained from higher export revenues.

Metals benefited from industrial construction, infrastructure and manufacturing demand. Copper, aluminum, iron ore and steel markets reflected Chinese fixed-asset investment and global capital-goods orders. Mining investment increased, but supply responses were slow because projects required long lead times and infrastructure.

Agriculture did not experience a 2007-2008 style price shock, but input costs, freight rates and weather remained important. Brazil expanded agribusiness exports, India had stronger rural demand after better agricultural conditions, and China’s grain policy remained important for domestic stability.

#### 3E. Labor & Demographics

Labor markets improved in the United States, Canada, Australia and parts of Asia, while unemployment remained high in France, Germany, Italy, Spain and Turkey. Definitions varied widely: official urban unemployment in China understated labor-market slack, India lacked a directly comparable annual unemployment rate, and informal work was large in emerging economies.

Demographically, advanced economies continued aging, especially Japan and Germany. China still had a large working-age expansion but was moving toward later demographic pressure. India’s young labor force was becoming a structural growth advantage, provided education, infrastructure and job creation kept pace. Migration mattered in Europe and North America, particularly in construction, services and agriculture.

The United States was the main demand engine of the world economy. Growth was supported by household consumption, residential investment, business equipment spending and defense-related federal outlays. The output gap narrowed, payrolls recovered and corporate profits improved, but growth was accompanied by a larger current-account deficit and heavy reliance on foreign capital inflows.

Policy shifted from crisis support toward normalization. The Federal Reserve began raising short-term rates in June, but the housing market and credit channels remained powerful because long-term yields stayed low. At the micro level, autos faced intense competition, energy companies benefited from oil prices, finance expanded mortgage and structured-credit activities, and technology sentiment improved as internet advertising became investable again after the dot-com collapse.

Micro/sectoral highlights: Google’s IPO signaled the rise of search advertising as a large-scale business model; JPMorgan Chase completed the Bank One merger; Cingular’s AT&T Wireless acquisition reshaped U.S. mobile telecom; household debt and mortgage exposure increased; retail scale remained symbolized by Walmart; real estate was already a major growth mechanism and a future vulnerability.

#### 5B. Corporate landscape

The largest corporations by revenue were still old-economy giants: Walmart, BP, Exxon Mobil, Shell, General Motors, Ford, DaimlerChrysler, Toyota, General Electric and Total were among the leading names. Energy companies benefited from rising prices, automakers faced both volume strength and margin pressure, and Walmart represented the global scale of retail logistics and supplier bargaining power.

Major corporate transactions reflected consolidation. JPMorgan Chase completed the Bank One merger, strengthening U.S. universal banking. Cingular’s acquisition of AT&T Wireless created a larger U.S. mobile operator. Sanofi-Synthélabo’s acquisition battle for Aventis reshaped European pharmaceuticals. Oracle’s pursuit of PeopleSoft, completed in early 2005, signaled enterprise-software consolidation. Google’s 2004 IPO was smaller in revenue terms than oil or retail giants, but more important for the long-run platform economy.

Bankruptcies and failures were less central than in 2001-2002, but balance-sheet repair remained important. Airlines still faced fuel-cost and competition pressure. In Russia, the effective dismantling of Yukos’s private control over core oil assets was a major corporate governance and state-capitalism event.

#### 5C. Innovation & technology

The biggest innovation shift was commercial rather than purely scientific: internet search advertising became a public-market growth story. Broadband diffusion, mobile telephony, enterprise software, semiconductors and outsourcing changed firm organization and consumer behavior. India’s IT services sector and Korea’s broadband ecosystem showed how human capital and telecom infrastructure translated into exportable services and digital consumption.

R&D-intensive sectors included pharmaceuticals, semiconductors, autos, aerospace and telecom equipment. China expanded manufacturing process capability; Japan and Germany retained strengths in precision engineering; the United States dominated software, internet platforms, biotech finance and venture ecosystems. Infrastructure megaprojects were concentrated in China, Spain, the Gulf, and transport-linked European corridors.

#### 5D. Labor markets & human capital

Sectoral employment shifted toward services in advanced economies and toward construction, manufacturing and urban services in fast-growing emerging economies. Wage growth was uneven: skilled technology, finance and engineering workers benefited, while tradable low-skill manufacturing faced pressure from global competition.

Union influence remained important in Europe, autos, public sectors and parts of manufacturing, but bargaining power differed sharply across countries. Migration supported construction and services in Spain, the UK, the U.S. and parts of the Gulf. India and China supplied large pools of engineers and technical workers, but both faced quality, infrastructure and regional inequality challenges.

### 6. FINANCIAL MARKETS

#### 6A. Equity markets

Major equity markets rose in 2004 but not with the explosive rebound of 2003. The S&P 500 total return was about 10.9 percent, helped by profit growth and low yields. The German DAX rose about 7.3 percent, the FTSE 100 about 7.5 percent and the Nikkei 225 about 7.6 percent. Emerging-market equities generally benefited from global growth, dollar liquidity and commodity prices, though country performance varied.

Sector rotation favored energy, materials, selected industrials, financials and technology platforms. Oil and mining equities benefited from commodity prices. Banks benefited from credit expansion and capital-market activity. Telecoms recovered selectively through consolidation and mobile growth.

#### 6B. Fixed income

Government bond yields remained low relative to growth. U.S. short rates rose, but long-term yields were contained by global savings, reserve accumulation, pension demand and anchored inflation expectations. Euro-area bond markets were supported by weak domestic demand and ECB stability. Japanese government bond yields remained low under deflation and quantitative easing.

Credit spreads were generally tight. Emerging-market borrowers benefited from stronger reserves and lower default risk perceptions. This was favorable in 2004 but contributed to risk compression that later became dangerous.

#### 6C. Currencies

The U.S. dollar weakened against the euro and several commodity-linked currencies. The euro averaged around US$1.24 and traded higher late in the year. The yen averaged around 108 per dollar. The renminbi remained pegged around 8.28 per dollar, reinforcing debate over China’s external surplus and reserve accumulation. The Canadian and Australian dollars gained support from commodities.

Capital controls and managed regimes remained important in China, India, Russia and several emerging economies. The global system was therefore mixed: freely floating G7 currencies, managed Asian exchange rates, and commodity currencies responding to terms-of-trade gains.

#### 6D. Alternative assets

Gold rose and averaged around US$410 per troy ounce, supported by dollar weakness and investor diversification. Real estate was economically important in the United States, United Kingdom, Spain, Australia and China. In 2004 these were mostly interpreted as wealth and construction supports; with hindsight, several markets were accumulating financial fragility.

### 7. RISKS, CRISES & BLACK SWANS

Active risks in 2004 included oil-price escalation, U.S. external deficits, China overheating, geopolitical risk around Iraq, euro-area fiscal-rule strains, Japan’s deflation exit risk, and institutional uncertainty in Russia. The Indian Ocean tsunami of 26 December 2004 was a humanitarian catastrophe with major local economic consequences, especially for tourism, fisheries, housing and infrastructure in affected economies such as Indonesia, Sri Lanka, Maldives and Thailand.

Near-misses included the possibility that oil prices would choke off the global recovery, that China’s tightening would cause a hard landing, or that U.S. rate increases would break housing and credit momentum. None of those risks materialized in 2004, but they did not disappear.

Hindsight-labeled vulnerabilities were substantial. The U.S. housing and mortgage-credit boom was becoming more fragile. Global imbalances were increasingly reliant on foreign official and private demand for dollar assets. European divergence was masked by low euro-area rates and sovereign-spread compression. Commodity exporters became more dependent on high prices. In Russia, state control over strategic assets increased in ways that would matter for later investment risk.

### 8. YEAR-OVER-YEAR DELTA (2004 vs. 2003)

The most important shift from 2003 to 2004 was the move from recovery to boom. World trade accelerated sharply, global output growth reached a multi-decade high, and corporate profits improved across many sectors. China’s demand became more visibly central to commodity and capital-goods markets.

Policy also changed. In 2003, the United States was still in emergency-easing mode; in 2004, the Federal Reserve began tightening. The Bank of England was already concerned enough about domestic demand to raise rates. The ECB and BOJ remained accommodative, creating an uneven but still easy global liquidity environment.

Commodity prices moved from supportive to strategically important. Oil rose enough to affect inflation, terms of trade, fiscal balances and geopolitical bargaining power. Russia, Brazil, Canada and Australia gained, while oil importers faced pressure.

Europe’s year-over-year delta was structural: the EU expanded from 15 to 25 members. That did not instantly transform GDP rankings, but it changed investment maps, labor-market expectations and long-run integration. Spain’s boom accelerated, Germany’s export model strengthened, and Italy’s competitiveness problems became more apparent.

The corporate delta was that post-dot-com technology regained public-market credibility. Google’s IPO did not yet make it a top global company by revenue, but it changed investor perception of internet platforms. Telecom consolidation and bank consolidation also showed that scale was a major strategic response to the early-2000s cycle.

### 9. DATA CONFIDENCE & GAPS

High-confidence data: nominal GDP rankings and current-dollar GDP values for the top 20 are high confidence when using World Bank WITS/WDI, with IMF WEO as a cross-check. Global GDP, world trade growth, central-bank policy decisions in the United States, euro area, United Kingdom and Japan, and Brent oil prices are also high confidence.

Medium-confidence data: real GDP growth, inflation, current-account balances, exchange rates and broad fiscal aggregates are generally reliable but may differ by vintage and revision. Some figures are calendar-year estimates, while India and some fiscal indicators use fiscal-year reporting. Government debt can mean gross, net, central-government or general-government debt depending on source.

Lower-confidence or non-comparable data: unemployment is the weakest cross-country indicator. China’s official urban unemployment is not comparable with OECD unemployment. India’s annual unemployment is not cleanly comparable for 2004. Mexico, Brazil and Turkey have large informal sectors. Russia’s transition statistics improved by 2004 but still require caution. Budget balances for emerging economies can differ materially by central-government, public-sector and general-government definitions.

GDP ranking conflicts: World Bank WITS/WDI and IMF WEO nominal GDP series can produce small ranking differences near the cutoff because of revisions, exchange-rate treatment and statistical-vintage differences. The top-20 membership in this document is based on World Bank WITS/WDI current U.S. dollar GDP. The closest ranks are Mexico vs Korea, Turkey vs Switzerland, and Sweden vs Belgium. Exact prior-year entries/exits were not fully re-extracted, so the document does not assert a definitive entry-exit list.

Important gaps: comparable household debt, housing valuation, corporate leverage, credit spreads, sectoral employment, small-business dynamics and informal-sector data are incomplete for many countries. Many microeconomic claims are therefore presented qualitatively and grounded in corporate records, central-bank reports, IMF Article IV reports and sector evidence rather than a single harmonized global dataset.
