# MACRO/MICRO GLOBAL ECONOMIC ASSESSMENT - YEAR: 1904

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

1904 sat near the high-water mark of the first modern globalization. Most leading economies operated inside the classical gold standard, international trade was tied together by steamship, railway, cable, and bill finance, and London remained the core clearing house for global capital. The year was not one of a single synchronized world boom. It was a mixed year in which the United States was still working through the 1903 industrial and financial downturn, Germany continued to deepen its industrial base, Argentina and Australia benefited from export and settlement dynamics, and Japan diverted a large share of fiscal and financial capacity into war.

The central geopolitical-economic event was the Russo-Japanese War, which began in February 1904. It mattered not only as a military conflict, but also as a test of global capital markets. Japan financed a major part of the war through foreign loans in London and New York, while Russia drew on European financial connections and domestic fiscal resources. The conflict helped prove that a non-Western industrializing state could mobilize modern finance, shipping, manufacturing, and taxation at scale. Its full political meaning would be clearer after 1905, but its capital-market consequences were already visible in 1904.

The ranking of large economies was still dominated by population-heavy agrarian empires and by the industrial North Atlantic. Using Maddison Project style historical estimates, the United States was the largest economy by real purchasing-power-adjusted output, followed by China, the United Kingdom, India, Germany, a Russian Empire proxy based on former-USSR territory, and France. China and India remained enormous in aggregate because of population, but with low output per person. The United States, United Kingdom, Belgium, Australia, and Canada were much richer per person, reflecting industrialization, resource endowments, high capital intensity, and deeper financial systems.

Microeconomically, 1904 was a year of industrial scale, commodity extraction, and infrastructure formation rather than a year of mass consumer technology. Steel, coal, railways, ports, textiles, electrical equipment, chemicals, shipbuilding, and finance were the commanding sectors. Oil was growing quickly, especially in the United States, but coal still dominated energy systems. Corporate capitalism was consolidating in the United States and Germany, zaibatsu and state-directed industrial firms were central in Japan, and colonial export systems shaped India, Indonesia, and large parts of Africa and Asia.

The most important hindsight lesson is that 1904 looked stable because the gold standard imposed predictable exchange rates and because London could intermediate global liquidity. That stability was conditional. The structure depended on confidence, gold flows, short-term credit, imperial security, and political restraint. Within three years the United States would experience the Panic of 1907, and within a decade the classical gold standard would break under the stress of World War I. Those later events should not be read back as inevitable in 1904, but the vulnerabilities were already present.

## 2. KEY THEMES OF 1904

### Theme 1: Classical gold standard stability with hidden fragility

The dominant monetary architecture was the classical gold standard. Major currencies were tied to gold at fixed legal or conventional parities, which stabilized exchange rates among gold-standard economies and reduced currency risk in trade finance. Gold convertibility also limited discretionary monetary policy. Central banks and financial authorities defended reserves through discount-rate changes, fiscal caution, and gold-market operations. In 1904, this regime made international trade and capital flows look orderly, but it also transmitted financial stress across countries.

The United Kingdom was the key node. Bank of England Bank Rate moved from 4.00 percent in the first quarter to 3.00 percent in the remaining quarters of 1904. The United States had no central bank, so liquidity was managed through the National Banking System, Treasury operations, private clearing houses, and New York money-market mechanisms. Germany, France, and Japan operated within gold-linked monetary systems, but exact comparable policy-rate histories are not equally available. The forward linkage is clear: the gold standard survived 1904 but its reliance on confidence and short-term liquidity would become more visible in the 1907 panic and then fatal in 1914.

### Theme 2: War finance as an international capital-market test

The Russo-Japanese War changed the economic conversation in 1904. Japan had industrialized rapidly after the Meiji Restoration, but war with Russia tested whether that industrialization could be converted into military procurement, shipping, taxation, and international borrowing. Japan raised loans abroad, especially in London and New York, while using domestic fiscal measures and financial institutions to sustain the war. Russia faced the costs of fighting at long distance, dependent on the Trans-Siberian Railway and European finance.

The affected economies were Japan, Russia, the United Kingdom, France, Germany, and the United States. The war linked East Asian strategy to Western capital markets. Its forward linkage was large: Japan's victory in 1905 would change the balance of power in East Asia, while Russia's defeat would contribute to domestic crisis and revolution in 1905.

### Theme 3: Uneven industrialization across the top economies

The United States, Germany, and the United Kingdom were the central industrial economies, but they were not industrializing in the same way. The United States had scale, railways, steel, petroleum, machinery, and large domestic markets. Germany had advanced chemicals, electrical engineering, coal, steel, and universal banks. The United Kingdom retained shipping, textiles, finance, coal, and imperial trade, but its relative industrial lead was narrowing.

The data show a large gap between aggregate GDP and GDP per capita. China and India were among the top economies by total output, but their per-capita output was far below the industrial economies. This contrast is one of the most important structural facts of 1904. It foreshadowed the twentieth century's divergence between industrial and agrarian economies, and later the difficult transition from agrarian labor absorption to industrial and service-sector productivity growth.

### Theme 4: Commodity, railway, and migration capitalism

The world economy in 1904 still ran through commodity frontiers. Grain, wool, meat, sugar, rubber, coffee, tea, tobacco, metals, coal, and petroleum shaped capital flows and settlement patterns. Canada, Argentina, Australia, Mexico, and the Netherlands East Indies benefited from commodity exports and foreign capital, though distributional gains were uneven. Railways connected interior production zones to ports, while steamships and refrigerated shipping integrated food and raw-material markets.

Migration was a major labor-market mechanism. The United States recorded very large immigrant inflows in 1904, mostly from Europe. Argentina, Canada, and Australia also used migration and land settlement as part of their growth models. The forward linkage was the continued expansion of settler economies before World War I, followed by later restrictions on migration and capital flows.

### Theme 5: Corporate scale and managerial capitalism

1904 belonged to the era of large-scale corporations and trusts. United States Steel, Standard Oil, major railroads, large banks, and electrical firms demonstrated the corporate scale that had emerged by the turn of the century. Germany's chemicals and electrical sectors were similarly organized around large firms and bank relationships. Japan's zaibatsu and state-linked industrial capacity showed a different institutional path.

The year also illustrates early automobile globalization. Ford Motor Company was still young, but in 1904 it formed Ford Motor Company of Canada and built its first international production presence in Ontario. Automobiles were not yet a mass-market macro driver, but the organizational and technological direction was visible.

### Theme 6: Colonial and imperial economic structures

A large share of global production was governed through empires, colonies, protectorates, and semi-colonial treaty-port systems. India was part of the British Empire, Indonesia was the Netherlands East Indies, and China was formally sovereign but constrained by foreign treaty rights, indemnities, and tariff limitations. African, Southeast Asian, and Caribbean production was deeply shaped by imperial trade, plantation systems, mining concessions, and shipping routes.

This matters for measurement. Many historical GDP series are modern-country or modern-territory estimates applied backward. They are useful but not the same as national accounts created by sovereign states. The forward linkage is that decolonization and national statistical systems would later change both economic institutions and the quality of data.

### 3B. Monetary & Financial Conditions

The monetary environment was defined by the classical gold standard, not by modern central-bank inflation targeting. Exchange-rate stability among gold currencies reduced nominal FX uncertainty, but it also meant that gold outflows and reserve pressures could force policy tightening. The Bank of England remained the key global rate-setting institution because of London's role in bills, shipping finance, and capital exports.

The United Kingdom cut or normalized its policy stance in 1904: Bank Rate was 4.00 percent in the first quarter and 3.00 percent in the second, third, and fourth quarters. The United States had no central bank and no single policy rate. Liquidity was provided or constrained through national banks, New York call money, Treasury balances, and private clearing-house arrangements. Germany and France had central banks, but exact 1904 comparable policy-rate values are Data not available in this report.

Bretton Woods was not relevant. It would not be created until 1944. The 1904 world used gold convertibility, gold points, bills of exchange, and capital controls only in limited cases compared with later wartime and interwar systems. Many colonial currencies were managed through imperial monetary boards or fixed relationships to metropolitan currencies.

Equity markets were recovering unevenly from earlier stress. In the United States, the Dow Jones Industrial Average rose strongly in 1904 after a large negative return in 1903. The Dow ended 1904 around 69.61 and recorded an annual price return of roughly 41.74 percent. ### 3C. Trade & Geopolitics

1904 was a trade-integrated but politically imperial world. Major trade flows were shaped by empire, tariffs, shipping routes, and commodity frontiers. The United Kingdom remained a global trading and financial hub. Germany's industrial exports were rising. The United States was increasingly important as an industrial exporter, while still absorbing immigrants and capital. India, Indonesia, China, and other Asian economies were tied into global markets through colonial administration, treaty-port structures, commodity exports, and imported manufactures.

The main geopolitical disruption was the Russo-Japanese War. It redirected fiscal resources, affected capital-market sentiment, and strengthened the relationship between military capacity and financial credibility. Another important infrastructure event was the transfer of the Panama Canal project to U.S. control and the start of the U.S. canal-building era. The canal was not yet operational, but the economic rationale was already clear: shorter maritime routes, cheaper intercoastal shipping, and greater strategic control.

### 3D. Commodities & Energy

Coal was the dominant industrial energy source. Britain, Germany, the United States, Belgium, and parts of the Habsburg and Russian economic spaces depended heavily on coal for industry, railways, heating, and power. Petroleum was growing but not yet the central fuel of transport and geopolitics. The United States was the leading oil producer, and Pennsylvania, Texas, and other producing regions were increasingly important. Standard Oil remained central to refining and distribution.

Metals were central to industrial growth. Steel supported railways, ships, bridges, machinery, and armaments. Copper demand was linked to electrification and communications. Gold was monetary metal as much as commodity. Agricultural commodities shaped the fortunes of Argentina, Canada, Australia, India, the United States, and the Netherlands East Indies. ### 3E. Labor & Demographics

Labor markets in 1904 cannot be compared using modern unemployment definitions. Many economies had no official unemployment rate, and agrarian underemployment was not measured consistently. Urban labor markets were increasingly organized through factories, ports, railways, mines, and construction. Wages varied by country, skill, gender, sector, and region, with strong differences between industrial cores and agrarian peripheries.

Migration was economically important. The United States recorded 812,870 alien arrivals in 1904 according to contemporary immigration reporting. Canada, Argentina, and Australia also absorbed migrants and used settlement as part of agricultural and resource expansion. Urbanization continued in industrial economies, while China and India remained predominantly rural. Demographic size strongly shaped aggregate GDP rankings, but productivity and capital intensity determined GDP per capita.

## 7. RISKS, CRISES & BLACK SWANS

The largest active crisis was the Russo-Japanese War. It created fiscal stress, capital-market exposure, shipping and insurance risk, and geopolitical uncertainty across East Asia and Europe. At the time, it was clear that the war mattered, but the full political consequences were not yet knowable. With hindsight, the war helped weaken the Russian state and strengthened Japan's regional position.

A second risk was U.S. financial fragility. The recovery in the Dow during 1904 did not mean the financial architecture was stable. The United States lacked a central bank and depended on seasonal liquidity, call-money markets, clearing houses, and Treasury actions. With hindsight, this vulnerability would become visible in the Panic of 1907.

A third risk was overconfidence in gold-standard stability. Fixed exchange rates made trade and lending easier, but they also tied economies to gold flows and market confidence. The system worked in 1904 because investors expected convertibility, fiscal discipline, and political continuity. It was not designed to absorb a general European war.

A fourth risk was colonial and agrarian vulnerability. India, China, Indonesia, Russia proxy, Mexico, and other economies had large rural populations with limited buffers against crop failure, tax pressure, debt, and price swings. Modern social insurance was minimal or absent in many places.

A fifth risk was geopolitical infrastructure competition. Railways, canals, ports, and naval power were economic assets and strategic assets at the same time. The Panama Canal project, the Trans-Siberian Railway, East Asian concessions, and naval construction all show how infrastructure and geopolitics were intertwined.

## 8. YEAR-OVER-YEAR DELTA (1904 vs. 1903)

The biggest year-over-year change was the move from financial and industrial weakness in 1903 toward partial recovery in 1904, especially in the United States equity market. The Dow's strong 1904 rebound followed a large 1903 fall. Real output for the United States, however, was still negative in the Maddison-derived estimate, showing that financial recovery and real economic recovery did not align perfectly.

The second major delta was war. In 1903, Japan and Russia were in escalating strategic tension. In 1904, they were at war. This altered fiscal priorities, capital-market flows, shipping risk, and the economic meaning of East Asian infrastructure. Japan's ability to borrow abroad became one of the defining economic facts of the conflict.

The third delta was continued industrial divergence. Germany's 1904 growth estimate was stronger than those for Britain and France, reinforcing the trend toward German industrial catch-up and specialization in chemicals, electrical equipment, steel, and machinery. The United States remained largest by output, but its short-run cycle was weaker.

The fourth delta was the continued expansion of commodity frontier economies. Argentina and Australia show strong estimated growth, and Canada remained in a settlement and resource-expansion phase. These economies were tied closely to British capital, migration, railways, and global commodity prices.

The fifth delta was infrastructure commitment. The U.S. assumption of the Panama Canal project in 1904 did not immediately change trade routes, but it marked a concrete shift in future shipping economics and U.S. strategic capacity. The canal's later opening would matter for interoceanic trade, naval deployment, and logistics.

## 9. DATA CONFIDENCE & GAPS

**Highest-confidence data in this report:** The Maddison Project style population, GDP per capita, and implied GDP estimates are the best available consistent long-run framework for comparing 1904 economies. The Bank of England Bank Rate series, the U.S. gold price parity, the Federal Reserve founding date, U.S. crude oil production, and the Dow Jones annual return are relatively well-documented.

**Medium-confidence data:** Maddison estimates for industrial economies such as the United States, United Kingdom, Germany, France, Italy, Belgium, Canada, Netherlands, Australia, and Japan are usable for broad comparisons, but they are still reconstructed historical estimates. They are not official 1904 national accounts.

**Low-confidence data:** China, the Russian Empire proxy, Poland, Czechoslovakia territory, India, Indonesia, and other colonial or imperial territories require extra caution. Some estimates are interpolated. Others refer to modern territories projected backward rather than sovereign states. The Russian Empire proxy is particularly weak because former-USSR territory does not equal exact 1904 imperial borders.

**Unavailable data:** Comparable CPI inflation, unemployment, policy rates, debt-to-GDP, current-account balances, budget balances, housing prices, corporate revenue rankings, total-return equity indexes, credit spreads, and household balance-sheet data are Data not available for most economies in this report. Where modern-style indicators appear in tables, most are explicitly marked Data not available.

**Conflicting data issues:** GDP rankings can differ depending on whether the analyst uses 1990 international Geary-Khamis dollars, 2011 international dollars, nominal exchange-rate conversion, modern-country territories, historical state borders, or empire aggregates. This report uses a consistent Maddison Project style PPP framework, but readers should not treat the numerical ranking as exact to the last digit.

**Weakest claims:** Any claim about sectoral growth rates outside the major industrial economies is weak unless backed by national historical statistics not reproduced here. Any claim about unemployment is especially weak because definitions varied or did not exist. Any claim about corporate rankings by revenue or market capitalization is weak because globally comparable 1904 corporate datasets are not available here.
