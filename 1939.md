# MACRO/MICRO GLOBAL ECONOMIC ASSESSMENT - YEAR: 1939

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

1939 was the year the late interwar economy stopped being a recovery story and became a war-economy story. The world was still living with the institutional damage left by the Great Depression: exchange controls, bilateral clearing arrangements, high tariffs, sterling-area preferences, gold movements toward the United States, and weak private international capital mobility. Industrial output had recovered in several major economies by 1937, was hit by the 1937-1938 downturn in the United States, and then shifted sharply in 1939 toward state-directed demand, inventory accumulation, military procurement, and commodity stockpiling.

The defining break was not simply the outbreak of the European war in September. Germany, Japan, Italy, and the Soviet Union had already put state-directed industrial mobilization at the center of economic policy. The United Kingdom and France entered 1939 still balancing civilian recovery with rearmament, then moved toward exchange control, import priority systems, food and shipping controls, and direct fiscal expansion after war began. The United States remained formally neutral, but its economy accelerated strongly from the 1938 recession, with real GDP growth estimated at about 8 percent by later national accounts. This gave the United States a widening industrial and financial lead before it became a belligerent.

The global economy was large but fragmented. A top-20 ranking derived from Maddison Project Database style estimates places the United States far ahead, followed by the former USSR, Germany, China, the United Kingdom, India, Japan, France, Italy, and Indonesia. These figures are real purchasing-power estimates in 2011 international dollars, not nominal dollars. They should not be read as precise current-account, fiscal, or balance-sheet statistics. For Soviet, Chinese, colonial, and war-disrupted economies, uncertainty is material. For the former USSR and other non-market or partly mobilized economies, an error band of roughly plus or minus 20-30 percent is appropriate.

Microeconomically, 1939 was a year of forced reallocation. Heavy industry, transport, energy, shipbuilding, metals, chemicals, and state-linked finance strengthened relative to civilian consumer durables and discretionary services. Agriculture remained central for India, China, Brazil, Indonesia, and much of Eastern Europe, while manufacturing and extractive sectors determined foreign-exchange availability. Households faced higher uncertainty, controlled prices in some countries, rationing preparations in others, and labor-market changes as men moved into armed services or state-directed industrial work.

A senior economist looking at 1939 would see the end of the liberal interwar recovery path. The year combined cyclical recovery in some countries with a structural turn toward command allocation, capital controls, fiscal dominance, and geopolitically segmented trade. Many of the economic facts later associated with the 1940s, including defense-led demand, public debt expansion, exchange controls, and state planning, were already visible in 1939.

## 2. KEY THEMES OF 1939

### Theme 1: The transition from depression recovery to war mobilization

The most important economic theme of 1939 was the shift from peacetime recovery to mobilized production. Germany and Japan were already operating under state-led systems that prioritized heavy industry, imports of strategic raw materials, and industrial self-sufficiency. Britain and France moved from prewar rearmament to wartime controls after September. The United States, though not at war, benefited from inventory rebuilding, recovery from the 1938 slump, and rising orders from abroad.

Significance in economic history: 1939 marks the point where fiscal policy and state procurement stopped being emergency depression tools and became structural demand-management tools in the major industrial economies. The forward linkage is direct: by 1940-1942, war finance and production planning dominated the economic systems of Europe, Japan, the USSR, and eventually the United States.

Countries most affected: Germany, Japan, the United Kingdom, France, Italy, the USSR, China, and Poland.

### Theme 2: Trade fragmentation and the retreat from pre-1914 globalization

World trade had not returned to the pre-1914 liberal pattern. The ratio of world trade to output had fallen sharply from its 1913 peak by the late 1930s. Exchange controls, import quotas, tariffs, imperial preferences, and bilateral clearing systems were central to international commerce. The outbreak of war redirected shipping routes, raised insurance costs, and shifted import priorities toward food, fuel, metals, and industrial inputs.

Significance in economic history: the global economy of 1939 was not simply smaller than the pre-Depression economy. It was institutionally different. Market-clearing international trade had been partly replaced by state-managed flows.

Countries most affected: the United Kingdom and its empire, Germany and its trading partners in Central and Eastern Europe, Japan and occupied or contested parts of Asia, the Netherlands and Dutch East Indies, Brazil, Argentina, Canada, Australia, India, and Indonesia.

Forward linkage: wartime shipping, convoy systems, import licensing, and postwar planning for new trade institutions emerged from these constraints.

### Theme 3: Gold, the dollar, and reserve asymmetry

The United States had a strong financial position in 1939. The dollar's gold price was fixed at $35 per troy ounce after the Gold Reserve Act era, and gold continued to move toward the United States as political risk rose abroad. Sterling remained central in a large currency area, but the sterling system became increasingly controlled after the war began. There was no Bretton Woods system yet. The IMF and World Bank did not exist. Reserve management was a mix of gold, dollar balances, sterling balances, and country-specific controls.

Significance in economic history: 1939 foreshadowed the postwar dollar-centered monetary order but did not yet contain its institutional architecture. The United States already had the financial depth and gold position that would later underpin the Bretton Woods settlement.

Countries most affected: United States, United Kingdom, France, Canada, Australia, India, South Africa, and countries whose reserves or payments were tied to sterling.

### Theme 4: Non-market and controlled economies gained relative weight

The top-20 list includes several economies whose output was not measured through market-price national accounts in a modern sense. The former USSR used centralized planning. Germany used extensive controls, fiscal devices, clearing arrangements, and directed investment. Japan's imperial economy involved controlled trade and war-related procurement. China was fragmented by war. India and Indonesia were colonial economies with national-account estimates reconstructed later.

Significance in economic history: standard GDP comparison becomes less reliable exactly when the world's largest economies were becoming more state-directed. Real output estimates can rank scale, but they do not fully capture consumer welfare, coercion, rationing, or capital depletion.

Countries most affected: USSR, Germany, Japan, China, India, Indonesia, Poland.

### Theme 5: Commodity stockpiling and raw-material insecurity

The 1939 economy was deeply shaped by access to oil, coal, iron ore, copper, rubber, wheat, rice, and shipping. Oil remained cheap in nominal terms by later standards, but access and transport were more important than price alone. Gold was fixed in the United States. Copper prices strengthened with industrial demand and war expectations. Agricultural staples were tied to harvest conditions, shipping availability, and import controls.

Significance in economic history: resource security moved from a commercial question to a state-security question. This reshaped trade, colonial policy, and industrial investment.

Countries most affected: United States, United Kingdom, Germany, Japan, Netherlands/Indonesia, Canada, Australia, Brazil, Argentina, India.

### Theme 6: Labor-market recovery coexisted with forced allocation

Unemployment fell in several countries, but comparability is weak. German unemployment had already been transformed by rearmament, public works, conscription, and exclusionary policy. U.S. unemployment remained high by later standards even during rapid 1939 growth. British unemployment fell but regional distress persisted. In China and Poland, war disrupted labor markets. In colonial economies, formal unemployment measures were rarely comparable or unavailable.

Significance in economic history: 1939 shows that lower unemployment under mobilization is not the same as broad household prosperity. Employment growth often came with controls, conscription, restricted consumption, and political coercion.

### 3B. Monetary & Financial Conditions

The monetary environment in 1939 was shaped by interwar institutions rather than by Bretton Woods. There was no IMF, no World Bank, and no formal global dollar exchange standard. The U.S. Federal Reserve Bank of New York discount rate was 1.00 percent throughout 1939 in the historical FRED series. Bank of England policy rate data show 2 percent in the first half, a third-quarter increase, and a return to 2 percent in the fourth quarter. German credit conditions were heavily controlled, and open-market rates in Berlin are not equivalent to a free central bank policy rate. Japanese monetary policy had moved into a more managed wartime-finance setting.

Exchange-rate regimes were mixed. The United States had a gold price rather than modern floating exchange rates. Sterling was managed and moved under wartime exchange control. Germany relied on exchange controls and clearing arrangements. Japan operated with controls linked to its war economy. Many colonial or sterling-area economies, including India and Australia, were tied to sterling arrangements.

Stock markets reflected both recovery and war risk. U.S. equity returns were roughly flat to negative in 1939 depending on the index, with Dow Jones annual return data around -2.9 percent and S&P-style total return series around -1.1 percent. Comparable verified annual index levels for London, Paris, Berlin, Tokyo, Milan, Amsterdam, Toronto, and Bombay were not available in this research pass.

### 3C. Trade & Geopolitics

The September 1939 outbreak of war transformed trade. Germany's invasion of Poland, the division of Poland between Germany and the Soviet Union, and the entry of Britain and France into war created immediate pressure on shipping, insurance, imports, and exchange management. The Soviet-German pact changed Eastern European flows and territorial control. The Sino-Japanese war already had a large effect on Chinese production, transport, finance, and trade.

Tariffs and quotas remained important, but by 1939 the larger issue was state allocation. Germany relied on bilateral clearing systems. Britain and France began wartime controls. Japan's external trade was constrained by its war in China and its need for imported raw materials. Colonial economies supplied food, minerals, rubber, and other inputs to imperial centers.

Regional integration was limited. The European Economic Community did not exist. ASEAN did not exist. The sterling area and imperial preference system were the most economically important regional currency and trade blocs in 1939. Latin American countries balanced export demand from the United States and Europe against payment restrictions and debt-service pressures.

### 3D. Commodities & Energy

Oil prices were low in nominal terms, but the key 1939 issue was secure access, refining capacity, and transport. The United States was the central oil producer among the top economies. Germany, Italy, and Japan were vulnerable to imported fuel and raw materials, which increased the strategic economic value of stockpiles, synthetic production, and controlled trade. Coal remained the dominant industrial energy source in Europe and Japan.

Metals demand strengthened with heavy industry. Copper, steel, aluminum, and industrial chemicals gained importance. Agricultural markets remained central to export earnings in Argentina, Brazil, Canada, Australia, India, and Indonesia. Shipping constraints meant that even countries with surplus food or raw materials could not rely on normal peacetime logistics.

### 3E. Labor & Demographics

The world population in 1939 was concentrated in China, India, Europe, and the former USSR. Urbanization continued, but much of Asia, Latin America, and Eastern Europe remained predominantly rural. U.S. labor markets improved in 1939 but unemployment remained high by later standards. British unemployment was lower than in the early 1930s but regional pockets of distress remained. German official unemployment was no longer a comparable market statistic because conscription, coercive labor policy, and public procurement changed the meaning of measured unemployment.

Labor migration was constrained by nationalism, border controls, and war. Refugee movements from Central Europe and population displacement from China and Poland were economically significant but cannot be summarized by a reliable global migration figure here. Wage growth and productivity data are not globally comparable for 1939.

## 7. RISKS, CRISES & BLACK SWANS

Active crises in 1939 included the European war, the Sino-Japanese war, the destruction of Poland as an independent economic unit, shipping and insurance disruptions, refugee movements, exchange controls, and commodity insecurity. Spain's civil war ended, but left economic damage and scarcity. Germany's controlled economy, Japan's raw-material dependence, and the USSR's planned mobilization all contained vulnerabilities that were not visible through GDP alone.

Systemic vulnerabilities building with hindsight: state-directed finance was raising public and quasi-public liabilities; consumer goods were being squeezed in mobilized economies; shipping bottlenecks would become a decisive constraint; and raw-material access would determine the ability of industrial states to sustain output. Hindsight also shows that the U.S. industrial rebound of 1939 was an early stage of a much larger wartime expansion, but that was not fully knowable as a peacetime forecast in 1939.

Policy mistakes visible in retrospect include the failure of the interwar system to rebuild open trade and stable capital mobility after the Depression, the tolerance of fragmented currency blocs, and the underestimation by some policymakers of how quickly geopolitical shocks would become economic shocks. At the same time, one should not overstate peacetime policy options: many constraints in 1939 were political and strategic rather than narrowly economic.

## 8. YEAR-OVER-YEAR DELTA (1939 vs. 1938)

The biggest year-over-year change was the shift from rearmament under threat to actual European war. In 1938, the world economy was already stressed by the Munich crisis, Japanese war in China, and U.S. recession. In 1939, those risks became structural realities. Poland was invaded and partitioned. Czechoslovakia disappeared as an independent state. Britain and France entered war. Germany moved from coercive revisionism to open military-economic expansion. The USSR changed its economic and territorial position through the pact with Germany and moves into Eastern Poland.

The United States shifted from the 1938 recession to rapid growth. The later national accounts estimate real GDP growth of about 8 percent in 1939 after a 1938 contraction. This was one of the major changes in global economic power: U.S. industrial slack began to convert into output just as Europe was entering a destructive conflict.

Trade risk intensified. The late-1930s trade system was already fragmented, but war turned commercial risk into direct state control. Shipping, insurance, foreign exchange, import licensing, and commodity stockpiling all became more important in 1939 than in 1938.

Financially, gold and dollar strength became more important. Sterling remained central, but exchange controls expanded. Private capital mobility was weaker than before the Depression, and official controls, reserves, and clearing systems mattered more than ordinary cross-border lending.

At the micro level, civilian consumer goods lost priority in mobilizing states. Heavy industry, transport, energy, mining, and government-linked finance gained share. In neutral or non-belligerent exporters such as Argentina, Brazil, Canada, Australia, Sweden, and Mexico, the war created both opportunity and risk: higher demand for food and materials, but greater shipping and payment uncertainty.

## 9. DATA CONFIDENCE & GAPS

Highest-confidence data in this file: U.S. real GDP growth, U.S. FRB New York discount rate, Bank of England policy-rate quarters, U.S. gold price context, and MPD-style GDP per capita and population rows for many countries.

Medium-confidence data: reconstructed GDP rankings for market economies with continuous national accounts, such as the United States, United Kingdom, France, Italy, Canada, Australia, Netherlands, Sweden, Belgium, Argentina, Brazil, and Mexico. These are still PPP estimates, not nominal GDP.

Low-confidence data: former USSR, China, Poland in 1939, colonial economies, and countries undergoing war, occupation, civil-war recovery, or severe controls. For the former USSR and other non-market economies, plus or minus 20-30 percent uncertainty is a reasonable caution. For China, the 1939 ranking uses a 1938 GDP per capita proxy because the visible MPD2020 extract did not include 1939 GDP per capita. For Poland, the 1939 ranking uses 1938 GDP per capita with 1939 population because full-year 1939 output is not meaningful after invasion and partition.

Historical data conflict areas: PPP benchmarks differ across Maddison versions; 1990 and 2011 PPP approaches imply different historical levels; national boundaries changed; colonial territories are difficult to map onto modern countries; official unemployment rates were not comparable; authoritarian and planned systems used non-market prices; and wartime controls distorted both prices and quantities.

Important data that simply does not exist in a globally comparable 1939 form: world GDP exact row verified here, global unemployment, global CPI, global current account, global R&D spending, global corporate revenue ranking, global stock-market index set, private household debt, real estate prices by major city, and consistent fiscal deficit/debt ratios for all top 20 economies.

Weakest evidence in this document: lower-end top-20 boundary rankings, country-level annual growth outside the United States, China and Poland GDP proxies, financial-market returns outside the United States, and sectoral employment shares outside countries with strong statistical offices.
