# MACRO/MICRO GLOBAL ECONOMIC ASSESSMENT - YEAR: 1959

### 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

1959 was the first full year of the restored postwar expansion after the 1957-1958 recession. The year was defined by a broad industrial rebound, renewed world trade growth, and a transition from emergency postwar exchange controls toward the more functional Bretton Woods system. The International Monetary Fund described industrial production in 1959 as roughly 10 percent above 1958, while the value of world trade rose about 6 percent. That rebound was not evenly shared: industrial economies, Japan, and parts of Western Europe accelerated, while many primary-producing and poorer countries gained less because commodity prices and foreign-exchange constraints remained difficult.

The monetary setting was unusually important. Western European currencies had achieved external convertibility at the end of 1958, so 1959 became the first year in which Bretton Woods operated in a more mature form. The fixed exchange-rate system, the dollar's official gold link, and greater current-account convertibility supported trade and capital flows. At the same time, the United States began to show the balance-of-payments pressures that would later undermine the system. IMF data show continuing transfers of gold and liquid dollar assets to non-U.S. holders, a clear early sign of the Triffin-dilemma problem that would become central in the 1960s.

A second defining feature was divergent growth models. Japan entered the high-growth phase with surging industrial output, exports, investment, and equity prices. The European Economic Community moved from treaty to operating customs-union process, with its first internal tariff reduction in January 1959. Spain adopted its Stabilization and Liberalization Plan, a decisive break from autarky. In Latin America, Brazil's developmentalism produced very rapid output growth but rising inflation and external financing stress, while Argentina's stabilization under Arturo Frondizi generated recessionary adjustment. In China, the Great Leap Forward moved from political campaign into economic disaster, with output estimates falling and famine conditions beginning.

The top 20 GDP ranking for 1959 is dominated by the United States, the Soviet Union, Germany, China, the United Kingdom, Japan, France, India, and Italy. The rankings below use Maddison Project Database 2023 GDP per capita and population, converted into total GDP in constant 2011 international dollars. They are not nominal 1959 U.S. dollars. Soviet bloc and other non-market estimates are especially uncertain, and Germany is reported by MPD as a current-border aggregate rather than as separate West Germany and East Germany. Those caveats matter because 1959 was a fixed-rate world in which market prices, PPP comparisons, and planned-economy output accounting could tell different stories.

### 2. KEY THEMES OF 1959

**1. Recovery from the 1957-1958 recession.** The most visible global theme was the cyclical rebound in industrial output and trade. The United States recovered despite a prolonged steel strike, Western Europe expanded through construction, consumption, exports, and inventory restocking, and Japan rebounded sharply from its 1958 pause. Historically, 1959 marks the return to the long postwar expansion rather than a new depression cycle. The forward linkage is clear: the 1960s opened with higher industrial capacity, stronger trade networks, and rising pressure on monetary authorities to prevent overheating.

**2. Full convertibility and the mature Bretton Woods phase.** External convertibility of major Western European currencies, achieved at the end of 1958, made 1959 a turning point. It reduced the importance of the European Payments Union style of clearing and made dollar-centered settlements more effective. The significance was not only financial convenience: it made trade liberalization, private capital flows, and multinational corporate expansion easier. The forward linkage was double-edged. Convertibility strengthened the postwar boom, but it also exposed the United States to gold and dollar-liability pressures that would intensify through the 1960s.

**3. Regional integration becomes operational in Europe.** The EEC had entered into force in 1958, but 1959 saw the first scheduled internal tariff reductions and the early test of the customs-union mechanism. The six member economies, especially West Germany, France, Italy, the Netherlands, and Belgium, began to benefit from clearer expectations of a larger market. The United Kingdom remained outside the EEC and explored alternative free-trade arrangements, foreshadowing the split between the Common Market and EFTA. The forward linkage was the 1960s European growth miracle and the gradual reshaping of trade patterns around the EEC core.

**4. Japan's high-growth model becomes unmistakable.** Japan's economy expanded rapidly through investment, technology adoption, export competitiveness, household income gains, and industrial upgrading. Industrial production and the Nikkei rose strongly. Heavy industry, machinery, automobiles, electronics, and chemicals became increasingly central. The forward linkage was Japan's 1960 Income Doubling Plan and the sustained high-growth era that would change global manufacturing competition.

**5. Planned economies show both scale and fragility.** The Soviet Union remained the world's second-largest economy in the MPD estimate, but its published output claims are difficult to compare with market economies. China was large because of its population, but the Great Leap Forward badly distorted production, incentives, and food distribution. Eastern European economies such as Poland and Czechoslovakia remained industrially significant but operated under planning systems that suppressed price signals and consumer choice. The forward linkage was the growing gap between quantitative production targets and living-standard performance.

**6. Developmentalism collides with stabilization.** Brazil, India, Mexico, Indonesia, and Spain illustrate different versions of development under foreign-exchange scarcity. Brazil emphasized infrastructure, industry, and the new capital Brasília but faced inflation and balance-of-payments stress. India pursued heavy industry under the Second Five Year Plan, constrained by imports and foreign exchange. Spain chose a stabilization and liberalization break in July 1959. Indonesia moved toward Guided Economy and currency devaluation. The forward linkage was the 1960s debate between import substitution, planning, liberalization, and external financing.

**7. Commodity producers recover unevenly.** Oil remained cheap by later standards, gold stayed fixed at $35 per ounce, and many agricultural and mineral exporters still faced weak terms of trade relative to industrial countries. The recovery in import demand helped raw-material producers, but the biggest gains accrued to industrial exporters. This uneven recovery mattered for Latin America, South Asia, the Middle East, and parts of Africa. It helped set up later debates over commodity stabilization, development finance, and producer coordination.

### 3. MACROECONOMIC OVERVIEW

**3A. Global Aggregates:**  
A reliable single nominal world GDP figure for 1959 is not available in the sources used here. The country ranking in Section 4 uses MPD 2023 constant 2011 international-dollar GDP, calculated from GDP per capita and population. This is suitable for long-run comparative ranking but not the same as a contemporaneous national-accounts nominal-dollar aggregate. IMF evidence indicates that global industrial production was about 10 percent higher than in 1958, and the value of world trade increased about 6 percent. The volume of world trade was roughly 8 percent above 1958 and around 6 percent above the 1957 level.

Reserve dynamics centered on the dollar. Under Bretton Woods, the dollar was the main reserve currency and was convertible into gold for official foreign holders at $35 per troy ounce. The system had newly strengthened because European external convertibility had been restored. Yet the U.S. balance-of-payments position deteriorated: the IMF reported a deficit on U.S. goods and services including military expenditure in 1959 and transfers of gold and liquid assets to non-U.S. holders. This was not yet a crisis, but with hindsight it was an early warning.

Key commodity prices were stable or weak relative to the later 1970s. Annual crude oil prices were around $3 per barrel in U.S. nominal terms in some historical crude-oil tables, while other long-run series using different crude benchmarks put 1959 nearer $2.08 per barrel. Gold's official monetary price was $35 per ounce. Copper, wheat, and agricultural staples did not have a single globally comparable price series in the sources used; where local or exchange-specific figures exist, they are not directly global. Commodity demand improved with industrial recovery, but the gains were uneven.

International capital flowed mainly from the United States and Western Europe toward reconstruction, development, and corporate expansion, but 1959 also saw rising private capital inflows into the United States and important short-term capital movements linked to interest-rate differentials. Canada is a good example: its current-account deficit was partly financed by foreign capital inflows, with short-term capital responding to the Canada-U.S. interest-rate spread.

**3B. Monetary & Financial Conditions:**  
The United States tightened monetary conditions during 1959. Federal Reserve discount rates were raised, reaching 4 percent at all Reserve Banks by late 1959, and U.S. Treasury bill rates rose sharply, with the 3-month bill rate moving from about 2.8 percent in December 1958 to about 4.6 percent twelve months later. Long-term U.S. government yields moved above 4 percent by mid-year, a high level by early postwar standards. The policy motive was to restrain inflationary pressure and credit demand during recovery.

The United Kingdom's Bank Rate was 4 percent through 1959. Western European monetary conditions varied. France was emerging from its 1958 devaluation and stabilization, West Germany remained strong, and the Netherlands and Italy benefited from trade and industrial expansion. Japan's exact 1959 policy-rate series was not available in the sources used here, but the macro stance supported high investment and industrial growth within a fixed exchange-rate regime at ¥360 per U.S. dollar.

Exchange rates were governed by Bretton Woods parities and capital controls. The main fixed parities included sterling at $2.80 per pound, the yen at ¥360 per dollar, the Deutsche mark near DM 4.20 per dollar, the Italian lira at about Lit 625 per dollar, the Dutch guilder near ƒ3.80 per dollar, and the Indian rupee near Rs 4.76 per dollar. Canada was a major exception because the Canadian dollar floated from 1950 to 1962. Non-market-economy official rates, including the Soviet ruble and Eastern European currencies, were not meaningful market exchange rates.

Equity markets were generally strong. The U.S. S&P 500 rose about 8.5 percent on price return, while the Dow Jones Industrial Average rose about 16.4 percent. Japan's Nikkei 225 rose about 31.3 percent. The IMF noted that U.S. stock price gains stalled in the second half as higher bond yields made equities less attractive. Bond markets were under pressure in countries where credit tightened.

**3C. Trade & Geopolitics:**  
Trade liberalization and Cold War geopolitics coexisted. The EEC's first internal tariff cut in January 1959 was a structural milestone for Western Europe. The United Kingdom remained outside and pursued alternative arrangements, feeding the future creation of EFTA. The United States faced increasing import competition from Western Europe and Japan, especially in manufactured goods. The Soviet bloc traded through planned channels and bilateral arrangements, limiting comparability with market trade data.

Cold War competition shaped investment, aid, and defense-industrial demand. The Soviet Union and United States competed in technology, space, heavy industry, and military production. The 1959 Cuban Revolution changed the political economy of the Caribbean and U.S.-Latin American relations, although Cuba was not among the top 20 economies. Decolonization continued to alter trade and capital relationships, especially for Britain, France, the Netherlands, and primary-producing economies.

**3D. Commodities & Energy:**  
Oil was abundant and cheap. OPEC did not yet exist; it would be founded in 1960. Major oil companies and producing states were operating under a pre-OPEC structure in which posted prices and concessions dominated. Iranian petroleum output rose in 1959, but lower prices limited foreign-exchange gains. Energy demand rose with industrial recovery, especially in the United States, Western Europe, and Japan. Coal remained important in Europe, but oil's share of energy consumption kept rising.

Metals demand improved with industrial production, but the United States experienced steel and copper strikes that affected output and imports. Gold mining remained important in South Africa and Canada, but the monetary gold price constrained sector economics. Agricultural conditions varied by region. India experienced rising wholesale prices, partly linked to food and raw-material supply conditions. China's grain crisis became catastrophic under the Great Leap Forward. In primary-producing countries, commodity-price weakness limited the benefits from the industrial-country rebound.

**3E. Labor & Demographics:**  
Labor markets tightened in recovering industrial economies but definitions are not comparable across countries. Manufacturing employment often rose less than output because productivity improved. The IMF emphasized that productivity gains in industrial countries allowed production to expand more than employment in some cases. Baby-boom cohorts were still young, supporting household formation, housing demand, school construction, and future labor-force growth. Urbanization continued in Japan, Latin America, India, China, and Indonesia.

Migration had economic significance. Southern European labor mobility and internal rural-to-urban migration helped industrial growth. Spain's stabilization would later accelerate migration to cities and abroad. In China, commune organization and procurement policy disrupted rural livelihoods. In Australia, Canada, and the United States, immigration and domestic household formation supported construction, consumer durables, and infrastructure demand.

The United States was the anchor economy of 1959. Recovery from the 1958 recession was strong, but the expansion generated credit demand and concern over inflationary pressure. Monetary policy became more restrictive, and Treasury bill yields rose sharply. The steel strike reduced output in the second half of the year and distorted trade, increasing demand for imported industrial materials and manufactured goods.

Externally, the United States was no longer simply the unquestioned surplus supplier of the postwar system. The IMF reported a deterioration in the goods and services balance and continued transfers of gold and liquid dollar assets to foreign holders. At the time this was manageable; in hindsight it was an early stage of the dollar overhang problem.

Micro/sectoral highlights: autos, steel, housing, consumer appliances, aerospace, electronics, oil, and chemicals remained central. Corporate America was large and internationally active, but 1959 also exposed vulnerability to labor disruptions in steel and copper. Household consumption benefited from rising incomes and suburbanization, while higher interest rates began to weigh on financial conditions. Banking was sound but under tighter reserve pressure.

The West German economy was in the Wirtschaftswunder phase, with high productivity, export strength, disciplined money, and strong industrial relations. China's 1959 economy was shaped by the Great Leap Forward. The campaign attempted rapid industrialization and agricultural collectivization through communes, mobilized labor, and politically driven output targets. Historical research identifies 1959 as the beginning of the Great Famine period. MPD shows contraction, but official statistics and reconstructed output estimates are deeply uncertain.

At the time, the full human and economic scale of the disaster was not visible to outside observers. With hindsight, 1959 was a catastrophic policy failure: distorted procurement, inflated production reports, labor diversion, and coercive collectivization damaged agriculture and food distribution.

Micro/sectoral highlights: agriculture was the critical failing sector. Backyard steel and politically mobilized industrial projects consumed labor and materials but often produced unusable output. Consumer welfare fell sharply in rural areas. Banking, corporate governance, and real estate were state-directed and not comparable with market economies.

### 6. FINANCIAL MARKETS

**6A. Equity markets:**  
The United States had a positive but maturing equity rally. The S&P 500 price return was about 8.5 percent, while the Dow Jones Industrial Average rose about 16.4 percent. The IMF observed that U.S. stock prices stopped rising in the second half of 1959 as higher bond yields made equities less attractive.

Japan was much stronger: the Nikkei 225 rose about 31.3 percent in 1959, reflecting the high-growth industrial outlook, export strength, and domestic investment boom. UK equity data for the FT 30 and continental European index-level annual returns were not available in the sources used. Qualitatively, BIS evidence indicates that London share prices rose steeply in 1959 before some profit-taking in early 1960. For many exchanges, modern index history is fragmented or not comparable.

**6B. Fixed income:**  
U.S. fixed income sold off during the recovery. The 3-month Treasury bill rate rose from about 2.8 percent in December 1958 to about 4.6 percent in December 1959, and medium-term government yields rose from about 3.7 percent to about 5.1 percent. Canadian Treasury bill yields exceeded 6 percent in late summer. French government yields also edged upward in late 1959 and early 1960. Comparable 10-year yields for all top-20 economies were not available.

**6C. Currencies:**  
Bretton Woods fixed parities dominated. The dollar was linked to gold at $35 per ounce for official holders. Sterling remained fixed at $2.80, the yen at ¥360, and major European currencies operated around par values. Canada floated. Spain devalued and stabilized at 60 pesetas per dollar under the 1959 plan. Indonesia used devaluation and note-value reduction amid multiple-rate complexity. Soviet bloc and Chinese exchange rates were official accounting prices, not market rates.

**6D. Alternative assets:**  
Gold was a monetary asset, not a freely floating investment asset in the modern sense. The official price was $35 per ounce. Real estate data by major city are not available in the sources used, but housing demand was strong in the United States, Western Europe, Australia, and Japan. Commodity assets were generally not in a 1970s-style boom. Oil remained cheap, and OPEC had not yet formed.

### 7. RISKS, CRISES & BLACK SWANS

The most severe crisis of 1959 was China, where the Great Leap Forward turned into the Great Famine period. At the time, the magnitude was hidden by censorship, distorted reporting, and limited external access. With hindsight, it was the largest economic and humanitarian disaster linked to policy in the year.

A second risk was the U.S. balance-of-payments problem. It was not yet a market panic, but the outflow of gold and liquid dollar assets was a systemic vulnerability. The very success of convertibility made the dollar-gold constraint more important.

A third risk was stabilization backlash. Argentina and Spain show how currency and fiscal stabilization could produce recession, social stress, and political conflict. Brazil shows the opposite danger: pushing development too hard without macro stabilization risked inflation and external financing dependence.

A fourth risk was commodity dependence. Primary producers did not gain as much from the 1959 industrial rebound as industrial exporters. This widened the perceived gap between rich and poor countries and encouraged later calls for development finance and commodity arrangements.

A fifth risk was labor disruption. The U.S. steel strike showed that major unionized sectors could affect national output, imports, and prices. Similar tensions existed in other industrial economies, even where they did not become systemic crises in 1959.

### 8. YEAR-OVER-YEAR DELTA (1959 vs. 1958)

1958 was a recession or slowdown year for much of the industrial world. 1959 was a recovery year. The shift was visible in industrial production, trade, interest rates, and equity markets. Output recovery was strongest in Japan, the United States, Italy, Germany, Brazil, Australia, and the Netherlands. Argentina and Spain were exceptions because stabilization compressed demand, while China contracted under the Great Leap Forward.

The monetary regime changed more structurally than the annual output numbers suggest. At the end of 1958, European external convertibility and the end of the European Payments Union regime moved the world closer to a functioning dollar-centered Bretton Woods system. In 1959, countries had to manage trade and capital flows under a more open payments environment.

Trade integration accelerated. The EEC's first tariff cuts made European integration operational, not merely treaty-based. The United Kingdom's outsider position became more consequential. Japan's export momentum strengthened. U.S. imports of industrial materials and manufactures rose, partly due to domestic strikes and partly due to foreign competitiveness.

Policy risks shifted. In 1958, the main risk was recession and weak demand. In 1959, the risks became overheating, credit tightness, balance-of-payments pressure, and stabilization politics. For developing and planned economies, the central issues were food supply, foreign exchange, state capacity, and investment efficiency.

### 9. DATA CONFIDENCE & GAPS

Highest-confidence data in this document: MPD population, GDP per capita, and derived GDP rankings for market economies; IMF descriptions of world production, trade, and selected country macro conditions; Federal Reserve discount-rate and U.S. rate information; Bank of England Bank Rate history; RBA 1960 annual-report discussion; and major fixed exchange-rate parities under Bretton Woods.

Medium-confidence data: derived GDP estimates for India, Brazil, Mexico, Argentina, Spain, Indonesia, and other developing countries. National accounts existed but were less complete than in later decades. Inflation, unemployment, fiscal balances, and current-account ratios are often missing, inconsistently defined, or not available as 1959 percentage-of-GDP figures.

Lowest-confidence data: Soviet Union, China, Poland, and Czechoslovakia GDP and growth. Planned-economy output accounting, administered prices, secrecy, and later revisions mean error bands may be large. The Former USSR aggregate and Germany aggregate also require special caution. Russia is not separately ranked because it overlaps with the USSR aggregate. Germany is an MPD current-border aggregate, not a clean 1959 political state.

Important gaps: a reliable nominal world GDP for 1959; comparable unemployment rates across all top 20 economies; standardized government debt/GDP; budget balance/GDP; current-account balance/GDP; corporate revenue and market-cap rankings; complete global equity-index returns; comparable real estate price series; and unified global commodity price series for copper, wheat, and other staples.
