# MACRO/MICRO GLOBAL ECONOMIC ASSESSMENT - YEAR: 1958

## Source and data note

This document treats 1958 as a historical GDP year, not as a projection backward from modern rankings. GDP rankings are reconstructed from the Maddison Project Database 2023 and cross-checked conceptually against Penn World Table and Our World in Data historical GDP concepts. GDP values in the country table are rounded ranges in constant 2011 international dollars, a PPP-style unit. They are not nominal current-dollar GDP. Exact official current-dollar GDP is not consistently available for 1958, especially for the USSR, China, Indonesia, and other non-market or partly administered economies. Where the evidence is weak, this document says so. For the Soviet bloc and China, GDP values should be treated as rough approximations, with error plausibly in the plus or minus 20 to 30 percent range.

### 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

1958 was a hinge year between postwar reconstruction and the high-growth 1960s. The world economy began the year under the shadow of the 1957 to 1958 U.S. recession, falling industrial production, and weaker raw-material prices. By mid-year, U.S. output had turned upward, monetary policy in several large economies had shifted from restraint toward ease, and confidence returned to equity markets. The year was not one of spectacular global expansion, but it marked a transition from recession anxiety to renewed industrial growth.

The most important institutional change was the practical deepening of Bretton Woods. At the end of 1958, major Western European countries restored external current-account convertibility, ending much of the postwar dollar shortage regime. The European Economic Community also came into force on January 1, 1958. These two developments did not instantly create a modern global capital market, but they widened trade and payments channels and set up the 1960s expansion in European trade integration.

The distributional story was uneven. Industrial economies recovered or continued rebuilding, while many primary-producing countries were hurt by the decline in commodity prices that had begun after the mid-1950s boom. Exporters of minerals and agricultural products faced weaker terms of trade, tighter foreign-exchange positions, and slower public investment. This divergence between manufacturing centers and commodity exporters was one of the defining economic tensions of the year.

Two non-market economy events were historically large but statistically difficult: the USSR continued heavy-industrial and military-linked growth under central planning, and China launched the Great Leap Forward. China reported strong industrial mobilization in 1958, but the quality of output, agricultural disruption, and later famine make conventional GDP interpretation especially hazardous. The economic signal was not sustainable productivity growth; it was coercive mobilization and misallocation that would become catastrophic in subsequent years.

At the micro level, 1958 was a year of mechanisms that later became macro forces. The jet age entered commercial service, the integrated circuit was demonstrated, Sony adopted its global corporate name, Toyota began U.S. exports, and NASA was created. These events did not dominate 1958 GDP, but they foreshadowed future productivity, trade, consumer electronics, aerospace, and transport changes.

### 2. KEY THEMES OF 1958

**1. Recession to recovery in the U.S. and its global spillovers.** The U.S. recession that began in August 1957 ended in April 1958. Because the United States was the largest economy and a central source of import demand, its downturn affected industrial production, trade expectations, and commodity prices worldwide. The Federal Reserve responded with discount-rate cuts, reserve requirement reductions, and open-market purchases before later moving away from emergency ease as recovery strengthened. Forward linkage: the rebound helped power the 1959 expansion, but it also reinforced the Bretton Woods tension between U.S. domestic policy and international gold-dollar confidence.

**2. Bretton Woods becomes operational in a fuller sense.** The restoration of current-account convertibility by major Western European economies in late 1958 was a major break from the postwar world of exchange controls and bilateral payments restrictions. The dollar remained anchored to gold at $35 per ounce, while other currencies operated through fixed parities and official intervention. Forward linkage: convertibility increased trade and payments flexibility, but it also exposed the system to the dollar-liquidity and gold-convertibility pressures that would grow in the 1960s.

**3. European integration moves from treaty to institution.** The Treaty of Rome entered into force on January 1, 1958, creating the EEC among France, West Germany, Italy, Belgium, the Netherlands, and Luxembourg. The EEC did not immediately eliminate barriers, but it gave a legal and institutional path toward a customs union and common market. Forward linkage: this became a central driver of Western European trade deepening, scale economies, and intra-European industrial competition.

**4. Commodity exporters remain under pressure.** The IMF described continuing price declines for many primary products from 1956 into 1958. Countries dependent on commodity exports faced weaker export earnings and harder balance-of-payments choices. The impact was strongest in Latin America, parts of Africa, Southeast Asia, and raw-material exporters outside the industrial core. Forward linkage: weaker terms of trade increased reliance on import controls, development loans, and state-led industrialization.

**5. Central planning at scale, with divergent outcomes.** The USSR remained a large heavy-industrial economy, but its GDP and productivity statistics are not directly comparable with market economies. China launched the Great Leap Forward, emphasizing communes, steel targets, and mass mobilization. At the time, some output claims looked impressive; in hindsight, the campaign produced severe misallocation and agricultural disruption. Forward linkage: China would suffer severe economic and humanitarian consequences from 1959 to 1961.

**6. Technology foundations move from laboratory to economy.** Jack Kilby's integrated circuit demonstration, NASA's creation, commercial jet service, and Japanese consumer electronics branding were not yet macro-dominant. They did, however, mark the early formation of semiconductor, aerospace, jet transport, and global electronics sectors. Forward linkage: the 1960s and 1970s would convert these into productivity, military, consumer, and trade transformations.

### 3. MACROECONOMIC OVERVIEW

#### 3A. Global Aggregates

**World GDP and growth.** Data not available as a single official 1958 world GDP figure from the consulted sources. Maddison/PWT-style PPP series imply a world economy in the broad range of 10 to 12 trillion constant 2011 international dollars. Growth was uneven: the U.S. contracted in real terms for part of the year, Western Europe slowed but generally avoided a deep synchronized recession, Japan remained in high-growth transition, and many commodity exporters weakened.

**Global trade.** World export value had exceeded $100 billion in 1957, then fell in the first half of 1958 before recovering in the second half. The second-half volume recovery was strong enough that world export volume reached a new high in that part of the year, but the annual trade story was still marked by weak commodity prices and a mid-year industrial slowdown.

**Reserve currency dynamics.** The U.S. dollar was the anchor currency under Bretton Woods, convertible into gold for official holders at $35 per ounce. European external convertibility in December 1958 reduced the postwar dollar-gap regime and increased the practical role of dollar-based payments. U.S. gold holdings still represented a very large share of world monetary gold, but U.S. balance-of-payments deficits and gold outflows were becoming a structural concern.

**Inflation trends.** Inflation pressure eased in many industrial countries after the 1956 to 1957 phase of restraint. The United States moved from inflation concern to recession response. France remained a special case, with inflation and currency stress leading to stabilization measures under the new Fifth Republic. In developing countries, inflation data are often not comparable or not available.

**Key commodities.** Gold remained officially fixed at $35 per ounce, though London market tensions briefly lifted the price above the official level. U.S. crude oil first-purchase prices remained around $2 per barrel. U.S. copper producer prices were weak by late-1950s standards, with the annual average near 26 cents per pound in available U.S. data. Agricultural staple prices varied by crop and region; globally comparable annual data are not available in the consulted sources. The most reliable general statement is that primary-product prices were under downward pressure.

**Capital flows.** Capital tended to flow from the United States toward Europe through private investment, reconstruction-linked finance, and official channels. European reserve gains were significant in 1958 to 1959. Developing-country capital access remained restricted and often depended on official lending, export earnings, and import controls rather than open capital markets.

#### 3B. Monetary & Financial Conditions

The monetary setting shifted from anti-inflation restraint to recession response. In the United States, the Federal Reserve lowered discount rates, reduced reserve requirements, and supplied reserves through open-market operations. As recovery developed, the downward movement in U.S. rates reversed later in 1958. In West Germany, the central bank eased from earlier restrictive settings, with the discount rate moving from around 4.5 percent early in the year to 4.0 percent from mid-year. The United Kingdom reduced Bank Rate from the very tight 1957 level as the external position improved. Japan remained under a managed credit regime, with policy influenced by balance-of-payments discipline and industrial finance needs.

The exchange-rate context was fixed-parity Bretton Woods. The pound sterling parity was $2.80; the Japanese yen parity was ¥360 per dollar; the Italian lira was around 625 per dollar; and many countries maintained pegs or multiple-rate systems. France changed its exchange-rate and stabilization regime at year-end. Capital controls remained common even after current-account convertibility expanded.

Equity markets rebounded sharply after the U.S. recession trough. The S&P 500 and Dow Jones Industrial Average produced large positive annual returns in 1958 after negative returns in 1957. Bond markets moved in two phases: yields declined during recession and monetary ease, then rose as recovery expectations strengthened. Comparable data for non-U.S. bond markets and many stock exchanges are limited.

#### 3C. Trade & Geopolitics

The most important trade development was European current-account convertibility and the EEC's launch. These changes supported trade liberalization, but quantitative restrictions, tariffs, and capital controls remained significant. GATT discussions focused on expanding trade and addressing the imbalance between industrial import demand and the export earnings of primary-producing countries.

Cold War geopolitics affected economic flows in several ways. The Soviet bloc traded largely within administered arrangements and Council for Mutual Economic Assistance channels. Western export controls limited strategic trade with communist countries. U.S. and Soviet space competition increased public R&D and aerospace spending. The geopolitical economy of 1958 was not free trade globalization; it was a partially liberalizing Western system alongside separated socialist trading systems and restricted developing-country finance.

#### 3D. Commodities & Energy

Oil was still a low-price, high-growth energy source. OPEC did not yet exist, and pricing power rested largely with major international oil companies and producing-country concessions. The Middle East, Venezuela, and the United States were central to supply. The year had no 1970s-style oil shock; energy was a growth enabler, not a macro crisis.

Metals were affected by industrial-cycle weakness. Copper prices softened, and many raw-material exporters faced terms-of-trade pressure. Steel demand was hit by the U.S. recession, but Japan, West Germany, Italy, the USSR, and China all pushed industrial output in different institutional forms.

Agriculture was regionally uneven. India was building its Second Five Year Plan around industrialization but still had a vast agricultural employment base. China reorganized agriculture through communes, a decision that would become disastrous. Latin American countries used import substitution and public investment to reduce vulnerability to imported manufactured goods, but food and rural productivity constraints persisted.

#### 3E. Labor & Demographics

Labor-market data are not internationally comparable. The U.S. recession raised unemployment; many Western European countries still had relatively tight labor markets and continued rural-to-urban movement. Japan's labor force shifted from agriculture toward manufacturing and urban services. In China, the Great Leap Forward moved labor into communes, public works, and small-scale steel production, distorting normal productivity measurement.

Demographically, the postwar baby boom was still affecting schools, housing, and future labor supply in North America, Western Europe, Australia, and parts of the Soviet bloc. Urbanization remained a structural trend across Japan, Latin America, the Soviet Union, and parts of Asia. Migration was economically significant in Western Europe, where labor shortages encouraged internal and cross-border movement, though detailed annual data for 1958 are incomplete.

#### 5B. Corporate landscape

The most transparent corporate revenue data are U.S.-based Fortune-style rankings. For 1958, General Motors was the largest U.S. corporation by revenue at about $11.0 billion, followed by Jersey Standard, Ford, U.S. Steel, and General Electric. These figures do not include Soviet, Chinese, or other state enterprises on a comparable corporate basis, so a true global top 20 by revenue is not available.

Major corporate themes included the dominance of autos, oil, steel, and electrical equipment. U.S. corporations had the clearest scale advantage. European firms were rebuilding and integrating into wider markets. Japanese companies were smaller but strategically important: Sony's 1958 name change marked the move toward a global electronics brand, and Toyota's first U.S. Crown exports marked the beginning of a long learning process in international auto competition.

Major M&A, IPOs, bankruptcies, and nationalizations: Data not available as a verified global list for 1958. Important nationalizations and state-enterprise expansions were more relevant in planned and developing economies than Western-style M&A.

#### 5C. Innovation & technology

The integrated circuit was demonstrated in 1958 at Texas Instruments, creating the foundation for modern semiconductors. NASA was created by law in July and opened in October, consolidating U.S. civilian space activity after Sputnik. Commercial jet travel crossed an important threshold with transatlantic jet services in 1958. These were not yet mass-market technologies, but they had large future productivity and industrial-organization effects.

R&D spending was strongly shaped by the Cold War, aerospace, electronics, and public procurement. Patent activity data are not available in a globally comparable annual table for this document. Infrastructure megaprojects included Brazil's Brasilia program, European road and industrial reconstruction, and state-led industrial projects in India and China.

#### 5D. Labor markets & human capital

Sectoral employment continued to move from agriculture into manufacturing and services in Japan, Western Europe, Latin America, and the Soviet bloc. In the U.S., the cyclical unemployment shock was more visible because labor-market statistics were better. In developing economies, underemployment mattered more than open unemployment.

Education and workforce upgrading were central but uneven. Japan and Western Europe benefited from rising technical skill and mass education. The USSR emphasized engineering and scientific training. India expanded technical and higher education within a planned development framework. China mobilized labor at scale but often away from productive agriculture and into low-quality industrial campaigns.

Union and labor movements remained important in Europe, the United States, Latin America, and Australia. Wage data are not available in comparable form, but the broad pattern was rising real wages in successful industrial economies and pressure on real incomes where inflation, shortages, or commodity weakness dominated.

#### 6B. Fixed income

U.S. 10-year Treasury yields fell into the spring recession phase and rose later in 1958 as recovery developed. Available series show a move from about 3.09 percent in January to about 2.88 percent in April, then up to about 3.86 percent in December. Comparable 10-year yield data for all top-20 economies are not available in the consulted sources. West German and UK short rates were eased during the year.

Credit spreads: Data not available. Sovereign debt events: no major top-20 sovereign default comparable to later emerging-market crises was identified in 1958, though many developing countries faced external financing constraints.

#### 6C. Currencies

The Bretton Woods fixed-rate system dominated. The dollar was pegged to gold, most major currencies maintained official parities, and capital controls remained common. The major 1958 currency event was Western European current-account convertibility at year-end. France's year-end stabilization and parity adjustment were also important.

Multiple exchange-rate systems were common in Latin America and parts of Asia. Therefore exchange-rate tables for Brazil, Argentina, Indonesia, and other controlled regimes are not comparable to simple market quotations.

#### 6D. Alternative assets

Gold was officially fixed at $35 per ounce, while the London market briefly traded above official parity. Real estate data for major cities are not available in a comparable annual form. Property markets in North America, Australia, Western Europe, and Japan were supported by urbanization and household formation, but annual price indices are incomplete. Art, collectibles, and private assets: Data not available.

### 7. RISKS, CRISES & BLACK SWANS

**Active crisis: U.S. recession.** The U.S. recession was the most important cyclical risk. It ended in April 1958, but early-year industrial weakness affected global sentiment, trade, and commodity prices.

**Active risk: commodity-price deterioration.** Primary producers faced lower prices and weaker export earnings. This was not a single dramatic crash, but it created balance-of-payments pressure and constrained development budgets.

**Active risk: French stabilization and currency pressure.** France faced inflation, fiscal and external pressure, and political transition. The year-end stabilization helped restore credibility, but the risk was real during the year.

**Active risk: China Great Leap Forward.** At the time, outsiders had limited reliable information. Hindsight shows that the campaign created severe economic distortions and food-system vulnerability. This is a hindsight-labeled risk because the full scale of the disaster was not openly knowable in 1958.

**Systemic vulnerability: Bretton Woods dollar-gold tension.** Convertibility deepened the system, but it also made the U.S. balance-of-payments position more important. The U.S. could supply world liquidity by running deficits, but persistent deficits created gold-convertibility doubts. This contradiction would become clearer in the 1960s.

**Near-miss: synchronized industrial slump.** A deeper U.S. recession could have pulled Western Europe and Japan into a much more severe downturn. Policy easing, U.S. recovery, and European reserve strength prevented that outcome.

### 8. YEAR-OVER-YEAR DELTA (1958 vs. 1957)

The shift from 1957 to 1958 was from inflation restraint and expansion to recession and recovery. In 1957, policymakers worried about overheating and external strain. In early 1958, recession and falling industrial output dominated. By late 1958, recovery and convertibility had become the larger story.

The international payments regime changed materially. In 1957, Europe was still closer to the postwar controlled-payments system. By the end of 1958, major European currencies had restored external convertibility for current transactions, making Bretton Woods more operational.

Europe also moved institutionally. The Treaty of Rome was signed in 1957 but entered into force in 1958. The EEC's practical start turned a treaty commitment into an economic framework.

China changed dramatically from the first Five Year Plan approach to the Great Leap Forward. This was a structural break in policy, not merely an acceleration. It shifted resources into mass mobilization and created the conditions for later collapse.

In financial markets, 1957 had been a negative year for U.S. equities. 1958 became a recovery-rally year. Bond yields moved from recession lows toward recovery expectations by year-end.

In technology and corporate history, 1958 had more durable inflection points than its macro data alone suggest: integrated circuits, NASA, commercial jet services, Sony's global name, and Toyota's first U.S. export push.

### 9. DATA CONFIDENCE & GAPS

**High-confidence claims.** The U.S. recession dates, the existence and timing of Federal Reserve easing actions, the EEC's entry into force, Western European current-account convertibility at the end of 1958, the Bretton Woods gold-dollar framework, NASA's creation, and the integrated-circuit demonstration are well supported.

**Medium-confidence claims.** GDP rankings among the top 10 are broadly stable across historical PPP approaches, but exact order among Japan, India, and France can vary by measure. Western European country narratives are reasonably reliable, though annual 1958 values for inflation, unemployment, current accounts, and fiscal balances are not always extracted here.

**Low-confidence claims.** Soviet, Chinese, Polish, Indonesian, and some Latin American GDP and sector metrics are estimates with large uncertainty. Planned-economy prices, administered exchange rates, rationing, and non-market allocation make direct comparison difficult. For China in 1958, official output claims are especially unreliable because Great Leap incentives encouraged misreporting.

**Metrics with major gaps.** Comparable unemployment rates, government debt/GDP, current-account balances as percent of GDP, budget balances, policy rates, 10-year bond yields outside the United States, stock index returns outside the United States, real estate prices, household debt, corporate revenue outside the United States, and sectoral employment shares are incomplete or not available in consistent annual form.

**Weakest evidence in this document.** The lower end of the top-20 ranking has the weakest evidence. Spain, Argentina, Mexico, Poland, Netherlands, Belgium, Sweden, Indonesia, Czechoslovakia, Turkey, South Africa, and Yugoslavia can shift depending on PPP benchmark, territorial definition, and data source. This document reports a defensible working ranking, not an exact official list.

**Important data that simply does not exist or is not comparable.** A single official 1958 world GDP, a global corporate revenue ranking including state enterprises, a uniform global unemployment table, and market exchange rates for nonconvertible or multiple-rate economies are not available. For these cases, the correct treatment is explicit uncertainty rather than false precision.
