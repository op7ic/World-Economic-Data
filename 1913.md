# MACRO/MICRO GLOBAL ECONOMIC ASSESSMENT - YEAR: 1913

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

1913 was the final full year of the pre-First World War world economy. It sat at the peak of the classical gold-standard and first-globalization order: large cross-border capital flows, high migration, fast transoceanic shipping, dense railway networks, and a trade share of world output near its prewar high. In Maddison-style historical purchasing-power estimates, the world economy was still dominated by a small group of industrial powers, but the largest population economies, China, the Russian Empire, and British India, remained among the biggest by total GDP even though their per capita income was far lower than that of the United States, Britain, Germany, France, Canada, Argentina, or Australia.

The dominant macroeconomic regime was not Bretton Woods, managed floating, or modern inflation targeting. It was the classical gold standard, with sterling and London at the center of international finance and with gold parities anchoring major currencies. This created confidence for cross-border trade and lending but also transmitted monetary tightening and balance-of-payments pressure across countries. There was no IMF, World Bank, OPEC, BIS, EEC, ASEAN, or modern central bank policy framework. Central banks and quasi-central banks defended convertibility, managed discount rates, and protected gold reserves. The United States did not yet have an operating central bank; the Federal Reserve Act was signed only on December 23, 1913, and the Federal Reserve Banks opened in 1914.

The year was economically mixed rather than uniformly buoyant. The world was highly integrated, but parts of the North Atlantic economy were already slowing. Canada’s wheat boom weakened as international wheat prices fell. The United States experienced a cyclical downturn and financial-market unease, even as it made a major institutional turn through the Federal Reserve Act and the Underwood-Simmons tariff. Germany remained the most dynamic European industrial challenger, Britain retained its financial and maritime primacy, France remained a major capital exporter, and Japan continued to consolidate Meiji-era industrialization. Russia, China, India, Indonesia, Brazil, and Mexico were large economies in total output because of population and land, but their statistical systems and national income measurement are much less reliable than those of Western Europe and North America.

Microeconomically, 1913 was a year of mechanism, not only a year of macro aggregates. Mass production reached a milestone when Ford introduced the moving assembly line at Highland Park. Railway and steamship networks structured global grain, meat, coal, textile, and metal markets. Telephones, telegraphs, electric power, chemicals, steel, and internal-combustion transport were changing firm scale and labor organization. Agriculture was still the largest employer in much of the world, while heavy industry, finance, and urban consumer goods were the leading engines of structural change in the richest economies.

The most important hindsight point is stark: many strengths of 1913 were also vulnerabilities. Gold convertibility, high capital mobility, food import dependence, imperial trade routes, and dense financial connections raised efficiency in peace but made the global economy fragile when geopolitical risk became real. The war of 1914 did not simply interrupt a self-contained national economy; it broke an integrated international system.

## 2. KEY THEMES OF 1913

### Theme 1: Peak first globalization before systemic rupture

The central theme of 1913 was the maturity of the first modern globalization. International trade relative to world output was near its prewar high. Transport-cost declines, steam shipping, railways, telegraphy, and gold-standard currency stability supported an integrated market for grain, meat, coal, metals, textiles, and capital. The major beneficiaries were Britain, Germany, France, the United States, Canada, Argentina, Australia, Japan, and the colonial port economies of Asia.

The significance is that 1913 became a benchmark year in economic history. Many later studies compare 1929, 1950, 1973, or 2000 with 1913 to measure how far globalization collapsed and later recovered. In 1913, the global trade system still appeared resilient, but that was partly because geopolitical risk was underpriced. The forward link is direct: 1914-1918 destroyed gold convertibility in practice, redirected shipping, subordinated markets to war finance, and reduced the automaticity of the prewar international order.

### Theme 2: Classical gold standard at its high point

The monetary regime of 1913 was anchored by gold, not by discretionary central bank inflation targets. Britain, Germany, France, the United States, Japan, Canada, Australia, and several Latin American economies operated in or near gold-standard arrangements. Sterling remained the most important international financial currency because London was the leading money market, insurance center, shipping finance center, and international bill market.

The economic significance is two-sided. Gold convertibility reduced exchange-rate uncertainty among participating countries and supported long-distance contracting. It also created a deflationary and contractionary bias when gold reserves, external deficits, or banking stress forced central banks to tighten. Countries most affected included Britain, France, Germany, the United States, Japan, Argentina, and Canada. The forward link is that wartime suspension and interwar restoration attempts became major sources of economic instability.

### Theme 3: Institutional reform in the United States

The United States entered 1913 as the world’s largest economy by Maddison total-GDP estimates, but it had no operating central bank. Its monetary system relied on national banks, seasonal currency mechanisms, New York money-market liquidity, and emergency private coordination. The Federal Reserve Act, signed in December, was a structural break, even though the Fed was not yet operational during most of the year. The Underwood-Simmons tariff also shifted the fiscal base by lowering tariff rates and introducing a federal income tax after the Sixteenth Amendment.

This theme mattered because the United States combined industrial scale with financial under-institutionalization. Its large domestic market, railways, steel, oil, machinery, and mass manufacturing were world-leading, but periodic money-market stress remained a weakness. The forward link is that the United States became more central to global finance after 1914, especially as European capital markets were strained by war.

### Theme 4: Industrial rivalry and uneven modernization

Germany, the United States, Britain, France, and Japan were the key industrial powers, but their sectoral profiles differed. The United States led in scale manufacturing, railways, oil, steel, machinery, and mass-market consumer durables. Germany was strong in chemicals, electrical equipment, steel, machine tools, and scientific industry. Britain led in finance, shipping, coal, textiles, and imperial trade networks, while France combined agriculture, luxury goods, metallurgy, and capital exports. Japan was industrializing quickly, with textiles, shipbuilding, railways, and state-guided industrial policy.

The significance is that 1913 was not a simple story of one hegemon. Britain still dominated finance and shipping, Germany was the key European industrial challenger, and the United States was the largest economy but not yet the undisputed organizer of the world monetary system. The forward link is that war mobilization and postwar debt flows changed relative power more quickly than peacetime trend growth would have done.

### Theme 5: Population size versus productivity

China, British India, and the Russian Empire ranked very high by total GDP, but their GDP per capita was far below that of Western Europe, North America, Argentina, and Australia. This gap reflected differences in industrialization, urbanization, literacy, infrastructure, state capacity, health, technology adoption, and agricultural productivity. These economies were not economically small, but they were less monetized, less industrialized, and statistically harder to measure.

The theme is significant because total GDP and GDP per capita told different stories. China and India mattered enormously as production and consumption systems, but their global role was constrained by low per capita output, colonial or semi-colonial constraints, and weak fiscal capacity. The forward link is that the twentieth century became partly a story of whether large population economies could close productivity gaps.

### Theme 6: Agriculture, migration, and frontier capitalism

Agricultural exports from Canada, Argentina, Australia, Russia, the United States, India, and parts of Southeast Asia formed a large share of world trade. Migration and land settlement shaped labor supply and capital demand in the Americas and Oceania. Canada’s prairie wheat boom weakened in 1913 as wheat prices fell, while Argentina remained a major exporter of beef, wheat, maize, and wool.

The significance is that global agriculture was not a backward residue; it was a central part of international capitalism. Railways, refrigerated shipping, grain elevators, port finance, futures markets, and land credit were the microeconomic infrastructure of the food system. The forward link is that war disrupted trade routes and changed demand for grain, meat, and raw materials.

### Theme 7: Corporate scale, technology, and labor organization

Ford’s moving assembly line in 1913 symbolized a shift toward standardized, high-throughput mass production. In steel, chemicals, electrical machinery, oil, shipping, finance, and railways, large firms and cartels were already central. Labor organization, unionization, and wage bargaining were uneven but increasingly important in industrial economies.

This theme matters because productivity growth came through firm-level organization as much as through national policy. The countries most affected were the United States, Germany, Britain, France, Japan, Canada, and Belgium. The forward link is that mass production, scientific management, and organized labor became defining features of twentieth-century industrial capitalism.

## 3. MACROECONOMIC OVERVIEW

### 3A. Global Aggregates

**World GDP:** The Maddison 2007 historical table gives world GDP in 1913 as approximately 2,733,365 million 1990 international dollars. The Maddison Project Database 2023 and Our World in Data provide more recent Maddison-style data in 2011 international dollars, adjusted for inflation and living-cost differences, but the extracted ranking table used here relies primarily on the older 1990 international-dollar series because it provides directly comparable historical 1913 country and region totals. Exact world real GDP growth for 1913 versus 1912 was not extracted from the consulted source set. **World GDP growth rate: Data not available.**

**Global trade volume:** NBER work on world trade estimates the ratio of world trade to output at roughly 21 percent in 1913, with a related table reporting trade near 39,762 million current 1913 US dollars and world GDP near 178,280 million current 1913 US dollars. The exact value differs by definition, especially whether trade is measured as exports alone or exports plus imports, but the direction is clear: 1913 was one of the high points of prewar trade globalization.

**Reserve currency dynamics:** Sterling and the London money market were central to world finance. Gold convertibility and gold points created relatively stable exchange rates among gold-standard countries. The US dollar was important because the United States was the largest economy, but the US did not yet have an operating central bank and New York had not displaced London as the leading international money market.

**Global inflation trends:** A single global CPI series for 1913 was not found in the prioritized sources. Under the classical gold standard, long-run price-level expectations were constrained by gold convertibility, but national price levels still moved with harvests, commodity prices, credit cycles, and gold flows. US CPI data begin in official BLS back-estimates for 1913, but comparable CPI data for many countries are absent or inconsistent. **Global inflation: Data not available as a single comparable figure.**

**Key commodity prices:** Gold was fixed at the US official gold price of $20.67 per troy ounce under the pre-1933 US gold standard, with sterling gold parity implying a stable dollar-sterling relationship. Comparable 1913 global oil, copper, wheat, and other commodity price series exist in historical datasets but were not consistently extracted from the prioritized sources used in this file. **Oil price: Data not available from the prioritized extracted source set. Copper price: Data not available. Global wheat price: Data not available**, though Canada-specific sources indicate wheat-price weakness in 1913.

**International capital flows:** Capital generally flowed from mature European saving centers, especially Britain and France, and also Germany and the Netherlands, toward railways, utilities, mines, government debt, ports, and land development in the Americas, Russia, colonies, and semi-peripheral economies. Argentina, Canada, Russia, and parts of Asia were important recipients. The flow direction is well established, but exact 1913 country-by-country net flows are not available in the extracted source set.

### 3B. Monetary & Financial Conditions

**United States:** No operating central bank existed during almost all of 1913. Monetary conditions depended on national banks, seasonal currency demand, gold flows, private clearinghouse arrangements, and New York money-market liquidity. The Federal Reserve Act was signed on December 23, 1913, but the Reserve Banks opened in 1914, so there was no Fed policy rate in 1913. **Policy rate: Data not available because no operating central bank policy rate existed.**

**United Kingdom:** The Bank of England Bank Rate series shows a relatively tight environment: 5.00 percent in 1913 Q1, 4.50 percent in Q2 and Q3, and 5.00 percent in Q4. This is consistent with a gold-standard system in which the Bank Rate was used to defend reserves and influence short-term capital flows. London remained the center of global discounting, trade finance, and sterling bills.

**Germany:** Germany remained on the gold standard and the Reichsbank managed discount policy. NBER and FRED historical datasets contain official German discount-rate series, but exact monthly or annual values for 1913 were not extracted for this file. **Germany policy/key interest rate: Data not available in the extracted source set.**

**France:** The Banque de France operated under the gold standard, with a reputation for comparatively stable discount policy and large gold reserves. NBER and FRED historical datasets contain Bank of France discount-rate series, but exact 1913 values were not extracted for this file. **France policy/key interest rate: Data not available in the extracted source set.**

**Japan:** The Bank of Japan historical statistics provide official discount-rate series covering 1882-1941, and Japan had been on gold since the late 1890s. The exact 1913 value was not extracted here. **Japan policy/key interest rate: Data not available in the extracted source set.**

**Exchange-rate regime context:** Bretton Woods did not exist. The IMF did not exist. Floating exchange-rate markets in the modern sense were not the dominant system. Major participating currencies were tied to gold, while some economies operated silver, paper, managed, or gold-exchange arrangements. Exchange controls were not a normal peacetime feature for the major gold-standard powers, but colonies and dependent territories often had administrative currency arrangements.

**Stock and bond markets:** London, New York, Paris, Berlin, Amsterdam, Brussels, Vienna, Tokyo, and other exchanges were active. Exact annual index returns for all major exchanges were not found in the prioritized extracted source set. **Major stock-index levels and returns: Data not available.** Bond markets were deep in Britain, France, Germany, the United States, and the Netherlands, but 1913 comparable 10-year yields for all major economies were not extracted.

### 3C. Trade & Geopolitics

1913 was a high-trade year but not a free-trade utopia. Tariffs remained significant in the United States, Germany, parts of Latin America, and other economies. The United States enacted the Underwood-Simmons tariff, lowering average tariff rates and introducing a federal income tax. This represented a domestic fiscal and trade-policy shift in the world’s largest economy.

There was no Cold War, no EEC, no ASEAN, and no GATT/WTO system. Trade rules were embedded in bilateral treaties, imperial preferences, tariffs, gold-standard convertibility, shipping networks, colonial administration, and informal commercial conventions. Geopolitically, the Balkan Wars and rising great-power rivalry increased background risk, but the full economic implications were not yet priced into global markets in a modern risk-management sense.

Trade tensions existed around tariffs, migration restrictions, imperial preferences, colonial markets, and access to raw materials. The most important bilateral economic networks were not necessarily the same as later twentieth-century alliances. Britain’s empire and sterling zone, French overseas lending, German industrial trade, US domestic scale and tariff policy, Russian grain exports, Indian and Chinese commodity flows, and Japanese East Asian trade all mattered.

### 3D. Commodities & Energy

Coal was the dominant industrial energy source. Britain, Germany, the United States, France, Belgium, Russia, and Japan were major users, and coal output underpinned steel, railways, shipping, electricity, and heavy industry. Oil was growing, especially in the United States, Russia, the Dutch East Indies, and parts of the Middle East, but oil had not yet become the central global energy commodity it became later in the twentieth century. OPEC did not exist.

Gold was both a commodity and a monetary anchor. Its monetary role was more important than day-to-day commodity speculation in shaping global macro conditions. The fixed official US gold price and gold convertibility in major economies stabilized nominal exchange rates among participating currencies but constrained domestic monetary policy.

Agricultural commodities were central. Grain, meat, wool, cotton, sugar, tea, rubber, coffee, and jute linked farms, plantations, colonial economies, steamships, railways, warehouses, and metropolitan consumers. Canada’s wheat-price weakness in 1913 shows how frontier economies were exposed to international commodity cycles. Rubber from Southeast Asia, coffee from Brazil, beef and grain from Argentina, cotton from the United States and India, and rice from Asia were important trade channels. Comparable global commodity-price tables for 1913 were not fully extracted, so price figures beyond gold are marked as data gaps.

### 3E. Labor & Demographics

Global labor markets in 1913 were shaped by agriculture, migration, industrialization, and empire. Agriculture still absorbed most employment in China, India, Russia, Indonesia, Mexico, Brazil, and much of Southern and Eastern Europe. Industrial labor was concentrated in the United States, Britain, Germany, France, Belgium, northern Italy, Japan, and selected ports and mining zones.

Migration was economically significant. Europeans migrated to the United States, Canada, Argentina, Brazil, Australia, and other settler economies. Asian migration was more constrained by law and empire but remained important in Southeast Asia and parts of the Pacific. Remittances, labor supply, land settlement, wage competition, and urban growth all depended on migration.

Unemployment data are not comparable across countries. Many countries had no modern labor-force surveys, and unemployment in agricultural economies often appeared as underemployment, seasonal labor slack, or distress migration rather than a measured rate. Wage growth versus productivity cannot be stated as a consistent global figure. **Global unemployment rate: Data not available. Global wage growth: Data not available.**

The United States was the largest economy in 1913 and the leading example of scale-driven industrial capitalism. Railways, steel, oil, machinery, farm equipment, meatpacking, electricity, and urban consumer goods formed the productive base. The country combined huge natural resources, a large internal market, high immigration, and rapid technological change. Yet the financial system still had structural weaknesses: no central bank, seasonal currency stress, and reliance on private coordination in crises.

The two major policy events were the Federal Reserve Act and the Underwood-Simmons tariff. The Federal Reserve Act was not a current-year monetary-policy lever because the system did not open until 1914, but it was a decisive institutional response to repeated banking panics. The tariff reform lowered average tariff rates and introduced a federal income tax, shifting the revenue base away from tariffs.

**Micro/sectoral highlights:** Ford introduced the moving assembly line at Highland Park in 1913, a milestone in mass production. US Steel, Standard Oil successor companies, railroads, meatpackers, farm-machinery firms, and electrical manufacturers were central corporate actors. Consumer credit was still early by later standards. Real estate and construction were locally important, but a comparable national real-estate indicator is not available. Banking remained fragmented and vulnerable to liquidity pressure.

China was the second-largest economy by total output in the Maddison ranking because of its population, not because of high per capita income. The Qing dynasty had fallen in 1911-1912, and the early Republic faced fiscal weakness, regional power fragmentation, foreign concessions, and limited central administrative reach. Agriculture, handicrafts, local markets, and treaty-port commerce dominated the economy.

Industrial and modern-sector activity was concentrated in treaty ports, rail corridors, mines, textiles, and merchant finance. Shanghai and other ports linked China to global silver, cotton, tea, silk, and manufactured-goods markets. The central economic problem was not absence of commerce, but low state capacity, low industrial productivity, and severe monetary fragmentation.

**Micro/sectoral highlights:** Agriculture, handicrafts, silk, tea, cotton textiles, and treaty-port shipping were key. Consumer markets were large but low-income and regionally fragmented.

### 5B. Corporate landscape

A fully comparable global top-10 or top-20 list by revenue or market capitalization for 1913 was not found in the prioritized sources. **Largest companies globally by revenue/market capitalization: Data not available.** However, the leading corporate and quasi-corporate systems are clear by sector and geography.

In the United States, railroads were among the largest enterprises, while US Steel, Standard Oil successor companies, General Electric, Westinghouse, International Harvester, meatpackers, and Ford represented large-scale industry. In Germany, BASF, Bayer, Hoechst, Siemens, AEG, Krupp, and the large universal banks were central. In Britain, shipping lines, railways, coal companies, banks, insurers, and merchant houses remained powerful. In France, large banks, railways, metallurgy, luxury goods, and colonial firms mattered. In Japan, Mitsui, Mitsubishi, Sumitomo, Yasuda, textile firms, and state-linked industrial enterprises were central.

Major M&A, IPOs, bankruptcies, and nationalizations for 1913 were not consistently extracted from the source set. **Global M&A table: Data not available. IPO table: Data not available. Bankruptcies table: Data not available.** The clearest firm-level event with global economic significance was Ford’s moving assembly line, which changed productivity, labor organization, and the economics of the automobile.

### 5C. Innovation & technology

The most economically important technologies were not isolated inventions but systems: mass production, electrification, chemical industry, internal combustion, rail logistics, steam shipping, telegraphy, telephony, and refrigeration. Ford’s moving assembly line was a microeconomic milestone because it reduced unit labor time and helped turn the automobile from a luxury into a mass-market good over the next decade.

Germany’s chemical and electrical industries were world-leading, supported by technical education and research links. The United States excelled in scale production, standardization, machinery, oil, electrical equipment, and consumer durables. Britain remained important in shipping, finance, coal, and engineering. Japan was transferring and adapting industrial technologies through state policy, zaibatsu investment, and export competition.

Global R&D spending and patent activity by country for 1913 were not extracted in a comparable form. **Global R&D spending: Data not available. Patent activity by country: Data not available.** Infrastructure megaprojects included railways, ports, canals, urban transit, electrification networks, and harbor facilities, but a comprehensive 1913 global project list was not found.

### 5D. Labor markets & human capital

Labor markets were segmented by skill, gender, migration status, race, colony, and region. Industrial economies had growing factory workforces, skilled trades, unions, and urban wage labor. Agrarian economies had seasonal underemployment, tenancy, household labor, and informal credit obligations that do not fit modern unemployment measures.

Education and human capital varied sharply. Germany, Britain, the United States, France, Sweden, Belgium, and Japan had strong or improving primary education and technical systems. China, India, Indonesia, Brazil, Mexico, and Russia had lower literacy and more uneven access, although urban and elite education systems were expanding.

Labor movements were visible in Britain, the United States, Germany, France, Italy, Belgium, Canada, and Australia. Unions mattered most in mining, railways, docks, textiles, and heavy industry. Ford’s high turnover before the 1914 Five Dollar Day shows that even advanced firms faced labor-discipline and retention problems. Comparable wage growth by country and sector for 1913 was not extracted. **Global wage growth: Data not available.**

## 6. FINANCIAL MARKETS

### 6A. Equity markets

Major stock exchanges existed in London, New York, Paris, Berlin, Amsterdam, Brussels, Vienna, Milan, Tokyo, and other centers. However, modern broad-market annual index data are not consistently available for all exchanges in 1913. **Index level and annual return for each major exchange: Data not available.**

Qualitatively, London remained the leading international securities market, especially for sovereign, railway, colonial, mining, and infrastructure issues. Paris was a major market for foreign bonds, particularly Russian securities. Berlin financed German industrial expansion and securities. New York was large but still less central to international finance than London. Tokyo’s exchange reflected Japan’s industrial and imperial expansion but was not yet a global capital-market center.

Sector rotation in modern terms cannot be reconstructed from the extracted data. Shipping, railways, mines, banks, government bonds, utilities, and heavy industry were central securities categories. Market-moving events included gold flows, Balkan geopolitical risk, commodity prices, US banking reform, tariff reform, and credit tightening.

### 6B. Fixed income

Government bond markets were crucial in 1913. British consols, French rentes, German government securities, US Treasury debt, Russian bonds, and colonial or railway bonds structured global portfolios. Britain and France were especially important as capital exporters, while Russia, Argentina, Canada, and many colonial or frontier economies were capital importers.

Comparable 10-year government bond yields for all major economies were not extracted from the prioritized sources. NBER and FRED historical datasets contain bond-yield and discount-rate series for several countries, but a complete 1913 table was not assembled. **10-year government bond yields: Data not available. Credit spreads: Data not available.**

Sovereign debt events in 1913 were not found as major global defaults comparable to later crises. However, external borrowers were vulnerable to sudden stops if London, Paris, or Berlin money markets tightened. This vulnerability became important in 1914 when war interrupted normal capital-market operations.

### 6C. Currencies

The key currency fact was gold-standard stability among major participating currencies. The dollar, pound, mark, franc, yen, and several other currencies were tied to gold, directly or through gold-exchange arrangements. The pound sterling was the principal international commercial and financial currency.

Major pair movements were limited among gold-standard currencies because gold points and convertibility constrained exchange-rate fluctuations. Countries outside the core gold system, or with silver, paper, colonial, or managed arrangements, had less straightforward exchange rates. **Full 1913 currency-pair table: Data not available.**

Capital controls were not the defining feature of the core prewar system. The system relied more on convertibility, discount-rate policy, gold flows, and market discipline. Colonial monetary arrangements were administratively controlled in ways that are not comparable to modern capital controls.

### 6D. Alternative assets

Gold was the central alternative asset and monetary anchor. The official US price was $20.67 per troy ounce. Gold was not merely a portfolio hedge; it was the standard of settlement and the reserve asset behind monetary systems.

Real estate was important in London, New York, Paris, Berlin, Vienna, Buenos Aires, Toronto, Montreal, Sydney, Melbourne, Shanghai, Bombay, Calcutta, and other cities, but comparable city-level price indices for 1913 were not extracted. **Major-city real estate prices: Data not available.** Land values in frontier economies such as Canada, Argentina, Australia, and the US West were closely tied to railways, crop prices, migration, and credit.

Other asset classes included railway securities, mining shares, shipping securities, colonial bonds, and commodity-linked assets. A modern global alternative-asset performance table is not available.

## 7. RISKS, CRISES & BLACK SWANS

The most important active risk in 1913 was geopolitical, but its full economic impact was not yet visible to contemporaries. The Balkan Wars had shown instability in southeastern Europe, while naval competition, alliance systems, and great-power rivalry increased systemic risk. Hindsight makes the danger clear, but markets and policymakers did not fully price the possibility of a prolonged world war.

Financial vulnerabilities were also present. The gold standard created credibility, but it required adjustment through gold flows, interest rates, credit conditions, wages, and prices. Countries with external deficits or reliance on short-term capital were vulnerable to sudden tightening. The United States still had no operating central bank, although reform had been legislated. Canada and Argentina were exposed to commodity prices and foreign capital. Russia depended partly on foreign finance and grain exports. Colonial economies depended on metropolitan trade routes and finance.

A major policy mistake visible in hindsight was overconfidence in the automatic stability of the gold-standard world. The system worked best under peace, credible convertibility, and functioning international markets. It was not designed for mass mobilization, capital-market closures, shipping disruption, and fiscal expansion on the scale that arrived in 1914.

Tail risks that did not fully materialize in 1913 included a global banking panic, a collapse of gold convertibility, or a synchronized sovereign-debt crisis. Tail risks that materialized soon after included war, suspension of normal financial markets, shipping disruption, inflationary war finance, and the breakdown of the first-globalization trade order.

## 8. YEAR-OVER-YEAR DELTA (1913 vs. 1912)

The major structural change from 1912 to 1913 was US institutional reform. The Federal Reserve Act created the legal basis for a central banking system, though not yet an operating one. The Underwood-Simmons tariff lowered tariff rates and introduced a federal income tax. These two reforms changed the architecture of US finance and fiscal policy more than they changed immediate 1913 output.

Global trade remained near its prewar high, but the environment became more fragile. Geopolitical risk increased after the Balkan Wars, and great-power tensions were more visible. The gold standard still functioned, but the conditions for its breakdown were building outside the purely economic domain.

Credit conditions in parts of the North Atlantic economy were tighter than the easy expansion phases of the previous decade. Britain’s Bank Rate was 5.00 percent at the start and end of 1913. Canada’s wheat boom weakened as international wheat prices fell, shifting a previously expansionary frontier economy toward recessionary pressure. The US economy also experienced cyclical weakness.

Industrial trends accelerated in microeconomic form. Ford’s moving assembly line was not a macro event by itself, but it marked a turning point in production organization. Germany’s chemical and electrical industries, Japan’s textile and heavy-industrial expansion, and US mass-production systems showed that productivity growth was increasingly tied to firm organization, technical knowledge, and scale.

Shifts in economic power were incremental, not sudden. The United States was already the largest total-GDP economy. Germany remained the leading European industrial challenger. Britain remained the financial center. China and India remained enormous by population but low-income. The dramatic reordering would come after 1914, not during 1913 itself.

## 9. DATA CONFIDENCE & GAPS

The highest-confidence countries are the United States, United Kingdom, Germany, France, Italy, Belgium, Netherlands, Sweden, and to a somewhat lesser extent Japan, Canada, Australia, and Argentina. These economies had better statistical systems, more developed historical national-account reconstructions, and more consistent monetary and financial records.

Medium-confidence estimates include China, British India, Mexico, Spain, and Dutch East Indies/Indonesia. These economies have historical estimates, but national accounts are reconstructed from partial evidence, and boundaries, colonial administration, silver or mixed monetary systems, and informal production complicate measurement.

The Russian Empire/former-USSR estimate is medium-low confidence. Maddison-style estimates exist, but boundaries, data quality, agricultural statistics, services, and later political discontinuities create major uncertainty. For Russia and other pre-1990 non-market or later Soviet-bloc territories, a rough uncertainty band of plus or minus 20-30 percent is appropriate.

Austria-Hungary is medium-low confidence because the imperial economy does not map cleanly onto later Maddison country units. The estimate used here is a specialist imperial historical estimate rather than a simple modern-country total. Brazil is low-medium confidence because the top-20 placement uses a derived estimate rather than a directly extracted Maddison country total in the source set.

The most important data gaps are exact 1913 year-on-year real GDP growth, CPI inflation, unemployment, debt/GDP, budget balance/GDP, current account/GDP, exchange-rate annual averages, stock-index returns, 10-year bond yields, corporate revenue rankings, market capitalization rankings, M&A, IPOs, bankruptcy tables, global wage growth, global R&D spending, and comparable real-estate prices. These data either did not exist in modern form, were not collected consistently, or were not extracted from the prioritized sources.

The GDP rankings are strongest at the top and weakest around ranks 11-20. Boundary choices can change the list. If the Ottoman Empire were estimated as a single imperial economy using a reliable total, it could plausibly enter the lower top 20, but a reliable 1913 total was not found in the prioritized extracted sources. Switzerland, separate Austria, South Africa, Korea, Cuba, and other economies may be close to the lower ranks depending on definitions. Therefore, the bottom of the table should be treated as an informed Maddison-based reconstruction, not a precise official ranking.

A final limitation is unit consistency. Maddison 2007 tables use 1990 international dollars, while Maddison Project Database 2023 and Our World in Data often present 2011 international dollars. This document uses 1990 international dollars for the top-20 table because the historical country and region values were available in that unit. Figures in 2011 international dollars are not directly interchangeable with 1990 international-dollar values.
