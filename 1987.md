# Macro/Micro Global Economic Assessment: 1987

### 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

1987 was an expansion year with unusually visible financial fragility. World GDP was about US$17.47 trillion current US$ World Bank/FRED. IMF reported continued output and trade growth: industrial-country real output rose more than 3 percent, world trade volume rose about 5.5 percent, and inflation in advanced economies stayed low versus the 1970s and early 1980s.

The main surprise was the October stock-market crash. On Black Monday, 19 October 1987, the Dow Jones Industrial Average fell 22.6 percent in one day, and the shock moved through global markets. It did not cause an immediate global recession or banking collapse, but it changed views of market plumbing, portfolio insurance, program trading, and central-bank liquidity support.

Exchange-rate management was the other core force. After the post-Plaza dollar depreciation, the February 1987 Louvre Accord attempted to stabilize major currencies. The United States still had large fiscal and trade imbalances; Japan and Germany remained surplus economies; markets were sensitive to policy coordination.

Growth was uneven. Korea, China, Spain, Turkey, and parts of the advanced economies grew strongly. Latin America remained constrained by debt and inflation. The Soviet bloc showed weak growth and rising reform pressure. Oil recovered from 1986 lows but stayed far below early-1980s levels, helping importers and straining oil-dependent fiscal systems.

### 2. KEY THEMES OF 1987

**1. Exchange-rate coordination moved from depreciation to stabilization.** The Louvre Accord followed the Plaza period and showed that policymakers feared excessive dollar weakness. The United States, Japan, Germany, France, the United Kingdom, and Canada were most affected. The forward linkage was Japan's domestic easing and the late-1980s asset boom.

**2. Black Monday exposed modern market-structure risk.** The crash was not just a fall in prices; it revealed how futures, portfolio insurance, trading rules, and settlement liquidity could transmit stress. Fed liquidity helped contain the shock. Later circuit breakers trace to this episode.

**3. Disinflation held, but asset inflation became harder to see.** Advanced-economy goods-price inflation was subdued, helped by policy credibility and low oil. This encouraged confidence, but in Japan especially it hid the growth of financial and property excess.

**4. East Asia gained industrial weight.** Japan remained a giant, Korea grew extremely fast, China continued reform-era expansion, and Asian production networks deepened. Trade frictions, especially in semiconductors, showed that export-led growth was politically contested.

**5. Socialist and state-led systems faced reform pressure.** China deepened market-oriented reforms; the USSR tried enterprise autonomy; Poland contracted. With hindsight, these were signs of institutional stress, though systemic collapse was not a settled 1987 expectation.

**6. Debt overhang split developing economies.** The IMF reported an improved aggregate developing-country current account, but growth slowed. Brazil, Mexico, and Argentina remained constrained by external debt, inflation, and limited access to voluntary bank lending.

**7. Regional integration became a firm-level planning issue.** The Single European Act entered into force in July 1987, and Canada and the United States agreed to CUSFTA in October. Firms began planning around larger integrated markets before all legal effects were fully implemented.

#### 3B. Monetary & Financial Conditions

The U.S. Federal Reserve raised the discount rate to 6.0 percent on 4 September 1987, then supplied liquidity after the October crash. Long-term U.S. interest rates had risen before the crash. Japan's discount rate was cut to 2.5 percent in February, and retrospective BOJ/BIS work treats 1987 as the start of the bubble period. Germany remained central to anti-inflation credibility and exchange-rate coordination. U.K. and Canadian policy-rate tables are Data not available in the working file.

The world used managed floating, not Bretton Woods. The Louvre Accord attempted to limit further dollar depreciation. Credit was available to high-grade borrowers, speculative in Japan, and constrained for many indebted developing countries.

#### 3C. Trade & Geopolitics

Trade policy mixed liberalization with conflict. Uruguay Round talks continued after the 1986 launch, with agriculture and services important. U.S.-Japan semiconductor tensions showed that high-technology trade was strategic. European integration advanced through the Single European Act. Canada and the United States agreed to free trade in October 1987. Cold War blocs still shaped trade and technology flows.

#### 3D. Commodities & Energy

Energy partly recovered from the 1986 oil-price collapse. Oil importers such as Japan, Germany, France, India, and Korea benefited from lower costs. Oil exporters and oil-dependent fiscal systems, including Mexico and Indonesia, faced pressure. #### 3E. Labor & Demographics

Demographics explain much of the difference between Maddison real-output rankings and current-dollar rankings: China and India were much larger in real-output scale than in market-exchange-rate GDP. Labor statistics are not directly comparable across market economies, informal economies, and command economies. Employment continued shifting toward services in the United States and United Kingdom, toward export manufacturing in Korea and China, and away from low-productivity agriculture in parts of Asia.

### 7. RISKS, CRISES & BLACK SWANS

The realized black swan was Black Monday. It raised fears of a 1929-style collapse, but liquidity support and no banking panic limited immediate macro damage. The policy legacy was market-plumbing reform and clearer central-bank liquidity roles.

Other risks were possible exchange-rate coordination failure, developing-country debt stress, Japan's asset bubble, and partial reform failure in socialist economies. The Japan and Soviet-bloc risks are partly hindsight: visible, but their later scale was not fully knowable in 1987.

### 8. YEAR-OVER-YEAR DELTA (1987 vs. 1986)

Oil shifted from collapse to partial recovery. Exchange-rate policy shifted from forcing dollar depreciation to stabilizing the dollar. Financial psychology shifted from strong equity-market optimism through late summer to recognition of market-structure fragility after October. Regional integration became more concrete because the Single European Act entered into force and CUSFTA was agreed. Reform pressure became more visible in China, the USSR, and Poland. In the Maddison top 20 there were no entries or exits; China moved above Japan and the Former USSR in the real-output ranking.

### 9. DATA CONFIDENCE & GAPS

High-confidence data include WDI nominal GDP, FRED world GDP, IMF aggregate output and trade descriptions, Federal Reserve Black Monday documentation, and EIA oil-price data. Medium-confidence data include Maddison for market economies. Low-confidence data include the Former USSR and Poland, where non-market output estimates may carry uncertainty of roughly plus or minus 20 to 30 percent.

Weakest areas are country-level CPI, unemployment, debt/GDP, current accounts, budgets, exchange rates, city real estate, corporate rankings outside the United States, and sectoral employment. Many exist in scattered national or IMF/OECD/BIS sources, but were not verified into a harmonized 1987 file here. The report therefore uses Data not available rather than guessing.

Nominal and real-output rankings conflict because exchange rates, price levels, controls, and non-market valuation matter. China and India are much larger in Maddison real output than in WDI current-dollar GDP. Japan and West Germany are larger in WDI current-dollar rankings than in real-output comparisons. The Former USSR has no clean market-rate equivalent.
