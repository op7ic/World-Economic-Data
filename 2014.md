### 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

2014 was a year of uneven post-crisis expansion. The global economy grew at a modest pace, with the United States and United Kingdom accelerating, the euro area escaping recession but remaining weak, Japan losing momentum after its consumption-tax increase, and many emerging markets slowing. The year did not look like a global crisis in aggregate statistics, but the distribution of growth became more asymmetric. Stronger U.S. labor markets and consumption contrasted with weak investment in much of Europe, decelerating Chinese property and investment activity, and increasing stress in commodity-linked economies.

The central macroeconomic event was the oil-price collapse that began in mid-2014. Brent crude moved from triple-digit prices to below $60 per barrel by year-end. The shock redistributed income from oil exporters to importers, lowered headline inflation, and exposed balance-sheet vulnerabilities in Russia, parts of Latin America, and several commodity producers. At the same time, it helped households and firms in oil-importing economies, although the demand boost was smaller than many forecasts expected because debt overhang, weak wage growth, and uncertainty still constrained spending.

Monetary-policy divergence became the dominant financial-market theme. The Federal Reserve ended its asset-purchase program while keeping rates near zero. The ECB cut its deposit facility below zero and prepared the ground for larger asset purchases. The Bank of Japan expanded quantitative and qualitative easing in October. The Bank of England kept rates at 0.5 percent despite stronger growth. These differences pushed the U.S. dollar higher, weakened the euro and yen, lowered core government bond yields, and tightened financial conditions for countries dependent on dollar funding.

At the micro level, 2014 also marked a clear acceleration of platform economics and mobile commerce. Alibaba's New York IPO became the largest IPO at the time, Facebook bought WhatsApp, smartphone penetration reshaped advertising and retail, and cloud computing moved deeper into enterprise spending. Meanwhile, traditional resource sectors began a capital-expenditure retrenchment after years of high commodity prices. The year therefore sits between two regimes: the post-2008 recovery era and the later period of lower commodity prices, dollar strength, emerging-market stress, and digital-platform scale.

### 2. KEY THEMES OF 2014

**1. Uneven post-crisis recovery.** The U.S. economy gained traction through job creation, household consumption, and firmer private demand. The U.K. also grew quickly, though productivity and real wages were weak. The euro area grew slowly and remained vulnerable to low inflation. Japan's domestic demand was hit by the April consumption-tax increase. This mattered historically because it showed that advanced economies were no longer moving together after the global financial crisis. The divergence shaped policy expectations through 2015 and 2016.

**2. Monetary-policy divergence and dollar strength.** The Fed ended net asset purchases in October, while the ECB and BoJ eased. The significance was not only in policy rates, which stayed low, but in expected direction. Capital moved toward dollar assets, major exchange rates adjusted, and global borrowers with dollar liabilities faced a tougher environment. Countries most affected included Russia, Brazil, Turkey, Indonesia, and other economies with external financing needs.

**3. Oil and commodity regime shift.** The oil-price fall was the year's most visible relative-price shock. The immediate winners were oil importers such as India, China, Japan, Turkey, and much of Europe. The losers included Russia, Saudi Arabia, Canada, Brazil's oil-linked investment complex, and other exporters. Hindsight shows that the 2014 collapse was not a one-quarter anomaly. It began a multi-year adjustment in energy investment, fiscal positions, and exchange rates.

**4. Emerging-market deceleration.** China slowed from double-digit growth to the mid-7 percent range, Brazil stagnated, Russia moved toward recession, and several economies with current-account deficits faced currency pressure. India was a partial counterexample, with growth improving and inflation easing. The economic-history significance is that the 2000s commodity and China-driven emerging-market supercycle was visibly maturing.

**5. Low inflation and debt overhang.** Headline inflation declined in advanced economies as oil prices fell, but low core inflation and weak credit growth in Europe signaled deeper demand weakness. High public debt in Japan, Italy, Spain, France, the U.K., and the U.S. limited fiscal space. The forward linkage was clear: low inflation pushed the ECB toward quantitative easing in 2015 and kept the global bond-yield environment exceptionally low.

**6. Geopolitics entered macro pricing.** Russia's annexation of Crimea and the conflict in eastern Ukraine brought sanctions, countersanctions, capital outflows, and a ruble crisis. Argentina's debt litigation triggered a selective default. The Ebola outbreak damaged activity in parts of West Africa, though it did not become a global macro shock. These events showed that geopolitical risk could move exchange rates, capital flows, commodity prices, and trade patterns even without a global banking crisis.

**7. Platform and mobile scale.** Digital firms increasingly became macro-relevant. Alibaba's IPO, Facebook's WhatsApp acquisition, fast smartphone adoption, cloud infrastructure, and online advertising growth changed capital-market narratives and sectoral investment. The forward linkage was the rise of platform firms as some of the world's largest companies by market value in the late 2010s.

### 3. MACROECONOMIC OVERVIEW

#### 3A. Global Aggregates

World nominal GDP was about $80.1 trillion in current U.S. dollars in the World Bank/FRED series. IMF estimates put world output growth at about 3.4 percent, a modest pace by pre-2008 standards. Advanced economies grew faster than in 2013, led by the United States and United Kingdom, while emerging and developing economies slowed from their earlier post-crisis pace.

Global merchandise trade volume rose only about 2.5 percent, roughly in line with world output and well below the pre-crisis pattern in which trade usually grew faster than GDP. The dollar value of merchandise trade was nearly flat because commodity prices fell and the U.S. dollar strengthened. Global FDI inflows declined sharply, with UNCTAD estimating a 16 percent fall to roughly $1.2 trillion, although developing economies still attracted a large share of inflows.

Reserve-currency dynamics favored the U.S. dollar. The dollar remained the main reserve currency, the euro stayed second, and the renminbi was not yet in the IMF SDR basket. Currency movements were driven by expected policy divergence: the dollar appreciated, the euro weakened into late 2014, and the yen depreciated after the BoJ expanded QQE.

Global inflation moved lower. Oil and other commodities pushed headline inflation down, while slack and weak wage growth held down underlying inflation in many advanced economies. In emerging markets, inflation was more mixed: India improved, Brazil and Turkey stayed high, and Russia's inflation accelerated with the ruble decline and import restrictions.

Commodity prices were the largest global aggregate shock. Brent crude averaged below the 2013 level and fell steeply in the second half. Gold averaged around the mid-$1,200s per troy ounce, lower than in 2013. Copper and many industrial metals softened as Chinese investment demand cooled. Food prices were mixed, with generally adequate grain supply but country-specific weather and currency effects.

#### 3B. Monetary & Financial Conditions

The U.S. federal funds target stayed at 0 to 0.25 percent, but the Fed ended its asset-purchase program in October. The ECB cut the main refinancing rate to 0.05 percent and moved the deposit facility to negative territory, first -0.10 percent in June and then -0.20 percent in September. The Bank of Japan expanded its monetary-base target to about 80 trillion yen per year in October. The Bank of England held Bank Rate at 0.5 percent and kept asset purchases at GBP 375 billion.

Credit conditions were easy in core advanced economies but not uniformly easy elsewhere. U.S. corporate credit markets were active, equity markets rose, and mortgage conditions improved gradually. In the euro area, bank lending remained weak and fragmentation persisted between core and periphery. In emerging markets, dollar funding became more expensive as the dollar rose and investors anticipated Fed normalization.

Major equity markets were positive in the United States, Japan, Germany, India, and China, but weaker in the U.K. and some commodity-linked markets. Government bond yields fell sharply in the United States, Germany, Japan, and the United Kingdom as low inflation and central-bank accommodation outweighed stronger U.S. growth. Yield curves generally flattened.

#### 3C. Trade & Geopolitics

The WTO's Trade Facilitation Agreement moved toward implementation after the General Council adopted the protocol of amendment in late 2014. Major regional negotiations, including the Trans-Pacific Partnership and Transatlantic Trade and Investment Partnership, continued but were not completed. The European Union remained the most advanced regional integration bloc, while ASEAN economies prepared for the ASEAN Economic Community target in 2015.

Sanctions became a major economic factor after Russia's annexation of Crimea. The U.S. and EU imposed measures on individuals, firms, finance, energy, and defense-linked areas; Russia retaliated with food import bans. This affected trade, capital flows, inflation, and investment sentiment. Argentina's debt litigation and selective default also reminded investors that sovereign-debt legal architecture could produce market stress even without a conventional fiscal crisis.

#### 3D. Commodities & Energy

Oil supply grew faster than expected, especially because of U.S. shale output, while demand expectations weakened. OPEC chose not to cut production sufficiently to defend high prices at its November meeting, and market expectations shifted abruptly. The result was a historic second-half price decline. The immediate macro result was lower inflation, lower import bills for oil-importing economies, and weaker fiscal and external positions for exporters.

Metals softened as China's construction and heavy-industrial demand cooled. Iron ore and copper markets moved from shortage narratives toward oversupply concerns. Agriculture was less globally disruptive than energy, but lower fuel costs began to affect input costs and freight rates. Energy investment plans were revised late in the year, with the full effect visible only in 2015.

#### 3E. Labor & Demographics

Labor markets improved in the United States and United Kingdom, stayed tight in Japan, and remained distressed in parts of Europe. Spain's unemployment rate stayed above 20 percent despite recovery. Youth unemployment remained a global concern, with ILO estimates showing youth strongly overrepresented among the unemployed.

Demographics reinforced structural differences. Japan and parts of Europe faced aging and slow labor-force growth. China continued urbanization but was moving toward a slower working-age population profile. India and Indonesia had younger labor forces and stronger potential labor-supply growth, although skills, infrastructure, and formal-job creation remained constraints.

The United States was the clear advanced-economy outperformer. The Fed judged that labor-market progress was sufficient to end net asset purchases, but it kept rates near zero because inflation was below target and wage growth was still moderate.

Housing improved but remained below pre-crisis construction intensity.

At the micro level, e-commerce and mobile payments expanded rapidly. Alibaba's IPO symbolized the scale of Chinese consumer internet firms. Heavy industry, steel, coal, and parts of construction faced excess capacity. The policy challenge was to support growth without reigniting financial imbalances.

Japan's Abenomics program hit a difficult test. The April consumption-tax increase front-loaded spending and then produced a contraction in household demand. The weaker yen supported exporters and equity valuations but raised import costs. Large manufacturers benefited more than households. Aging demographics, low trend growth, and the debt burden remained structural constraints. Hindsight shows that 2014 exposed how hard it was to combine fiscal consolidation with reflation.

#### 5B. Corporate landscape

The revenue leaders of 2014 were dominated by retail, energy, autos, and state-linked Chinese enterprises. Fortune's Global 500 list ranked Walmart, Royal Dutch Shell, Sinopec, China National Petroleum, Exxon Mobil, BP, State Grid, Volkswagen, Toyota, and Glencore among the top revenue companies. The composition shows how large the energy complex remained even as oil prices began falling.

Technology and platform firms were the most important capital-market story. Alibaba's IPO raised about $25 billion after the greenshoe option and became the largest IPO at the time. Facebook agreed to acquire WhatsApp in a transaction initially valued at about $16 billion plus $3 billion in restricted stock units, showing how mobile messaging and network scale had become strategically central.

Major corporate themes included renewed global M&A, telecom and internet consolidation, and resource-sector portfolio adjustment. Fiat and Chrysler completed the creation of Fiat Chrysler Automobiles. Lenovo completed the Motorola Mobility acquisition from Google. Glencore's sale of the Las Bambas copper mine to a Chinese-led consortium illustrated both Chinese resource demand and commodity-sector balance-sheet management. Portugal's Banco Espirito Santo failure and rescue showed that euro-area banking risk had not fully disappeared.

#### 5C. Innovation & technology

The main economic innovations were less about single scientific breakthroughs and more about diffusion: mobile broadband, smartphones, app ecosystems, cloud infrastructure, and data-driven advertising. These technologies changed retail logistics, media revenues, payment systems, and the valuation of network businesses. They also created new competition questions around platform power and data.

Energy innovation was also macro-relevant. U.S. shale production techniques had already scaled enough to alter world oil supply. In electricity, solar and wind costs continued to fall, although deployment depended on national subsidy and grid rules. Advanced manufacturing, robotics, and industrial software became more important in Germany, Japan, Korea, and the United States.

Infrastructure megaprojects were concentrated in China, Gulf economies, and major urban corridors. The constraint was often not technology but finance, governance, land acquisition, and regulatory approvals.

#### 5D. Labor markets & human capital

Employment improved in the United States and United Kingdom, but wage growth lagged productivity expectations. Europe had a two-speed labor market: Germany was strong, Spain and Italy still faced very high unemployment, and France remained structurally weak. Japan had a tight labor market but weak real wage momentum because the tax hike lifted prices.

Human capital and skills were increasingly tied to digital adoption. High-skill workers in software, data, finance, engineering, and healthcare benefited, while lower-skill and routine manufacturing workers faced more competition. Migration mattered for the U.K., Germany, Gulf economies, Canada, Australia, and the United States. In emerging markets, the central challenge was moving workers from informal or low-productivity activities into formal, higher-productivity services and manufacturing.

#### 6B. Fixed income

Core sovereign yields fell despite U.S. growth. U.S. 10-year Treasury yields declined from roughly 3 percent at the start of the year to around 2.2 percent by year-end. German Bund yields fell toward 0.5 percent, and Japanese yields moved even lower. The flattening reflected low inflation, central-bank accommodation, and safe-asset demand. Credit spreads were generally contained until late-year energy stress widened high-yield energy spreads.

Sovereign debt stress was not a global systemic event, but Argentina's selective default and Russia's market stress mattered. Euro-area periphery yields continued to benefit from ECB expectations, even as growth remained weak.

#### 6C. Currencies

The U.S. dollar strengthened broadly. EUR/USD moved from around 1.38 in spring to near 1.21 by year-end. USD/JPY rose toward 120 after the BoJ's October easing. The ruble collapsed in late 2014 as oil prices fell and sanctions constrained financing. The Brazilian real, Turkish lira, Indonesian rupiah, and Mexican peso also faced pressure. Saudi Arabia maintained its dollar peg, while Switzerland maintained the euro floor through the end of 2014.

#### 6D. Alternative assets

Gold declined on average compared with 2013 and did not behave as a strong inflation hedge because inflation was falling. Real estate was highly local: London, Vancouver, Sydney, parts of China, and major U.S. cities were strong, while Spain and Italy were still repairing from property busts. Farmland and commodity-linked assets became less attractive as the commodity cycle turned.

### 7. RISKS, CRISES & BLACK SWANS

Active crises included the Russia-Ukraine sanctions shock, the ruble crisis, Argentina's selective default, euro-area deflation risk, and the Ebola outbreak's economic damage in affected West African economies. Japan's tax-hike recession was a policy shock rather than a surprise disaster, but its intensity raised doubts about the sequencing of fiscal consolidation and reflation.

Near-misses included the possibility that the oil-price collapse could trigger broader credit contagion through energy high-yield debt or oil-exporter banking systems. That did not become a global crisis in 2014, but energy credit stress widened in 2015 and 2016. Another vulnerability was China's property and local-government debt cycle, which did not collapse but required policy support.

Hindsight risks visible from 2014 include Brazil's move toward recession, Russia's 2015 contraction, the ECB's need for full quantitative easing, and the fragility of emerging markets with current-account deficits under dollar strength. A policy mistake visible in retrospect was underestimating how persistent low inflation could be in Europe and Japan.

### 8. YEAR-OVER-YEAR DELTA (2014 vs. 2013)

The biggest structural break from 2013 was the commodity cycle. In 2013, high oil prices still supported exporters and shale investment. In 2014, the second-half oil collapse changed fiscal, external, and investment assumptions. This was not only a price move; it altered the bargaining position of OPEC, shale producers, consumers, and central banks.

Monetary expectations also changed. In 2013, the taper tantrum centered on the risk of Fed withdrawal from extraordinary stimulus. In 2014, the Fed actually completed tapering, but global financial conditions stayed easy because the ECB and BoJ moved in the opposite direction. The result was not a simple global tightening, but policy divergence and dollar strength.

The advanced-economy hierarchy shifted. The United States and United Kingdom looked more cyclical-growth positive than the euro area and Japan. The euro area moved from crisis management toward deflation management. Japan moved from early Abenomics optimism toward concern over domestic-demand fragility.

Emerging-market power also shifted. China remained the most important growth engine, but its slowdown became more obvious. India improved and gained investor attention. Brazil and Russia deteriorated. Indonesia and Turkey remained large, dynamic economies but with external-financing vulnerabilities.

### 9. DATA CONFIDENCE & GAPS

Data confidence is highest for nominal GDP, population, inflation, and broad national-account aggregates for OECD countries and major emerging economies. World Bank WDI, IMF WEO, OECD, national statistical offices, and central-bank publications provide consistent high-level series for 2014. GDP rankings can still differ slightly across sources because of revisions, exchange-rate conversion, calendar-year definitions, and national-account rebasing.

Confidence is medium for government debt, fiscal balance, and current-account ratios across the top 20. IMF WEO and national sources are broadly comparable at the general-government level, but some countries report central-government measures more prominently. China local-government debt, Saudi fiscal balance conventions, and India's central versus general government measures require caution.

Confidence is lower for unemployment comparability. The United States, Canada, Australia, Japan, Korea, and European countries have relatively standardized labor-force surveys. China uses registered urban unemployment for many historical comparisons, which understates broader labor-market stress. India has no single 2014 unemployment measure that is directly comparable with OECD labor-force surveys. Saudi Arabia's total unemployment and national unemployment describe different labor-market realities.

The weakest evidence in this document concerns some micro-sectoral and corporate interpretations where data are firm-specific, lagged, or qualitative. Sector performance is a synthesis of national accounts, market data, company reports, and policy sources. The document avoids fabricated figures by using "about" for rounded values and "Data not available" where a comparable number is not available in the compiled source base.

Maddison Project estimates are not required for this 2014 assessment because modern official national accounts exist for the top 20 economies. They would be relevant for pre-1990 or non-market historical comparisons, but not for this nominal GDP ranking.
