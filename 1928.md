# MACRO/MICRO GLOBAL ECONOMIC ASSESSMENT - YEAR: 1928


### 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

1928 was the last full year of apparent global prosperity before the crisis sequence that began in 1929. It was not a uniformly booming year, but it was a year in which the late-1920s international system still looked workable: the gold-exchange standard had been restored across most major economies, global trade was active, US equity prices surged, France completed legal stabilization of the franc, Germany still benefited from Dawes-era capital inflows, and Japan was preparing for a return to gold. The surface picture was orderly. The underlying structure was fragile.

The defining macroeconomic force was the interaction of gold-standard discipline, creditor-country monetary choices, and increasingly speculative US financial markets. The United States remained the dominant economy and financial center, and the Federal Reserve tightened in 1928, lifting the New York Reserve Bank discount rate from 3.50 percent early in the year to 5.00 percent by late summer. France, after stabilizing the franc at a low gold value, attracted gold and became a major gold accumulator. These forces did not yet produce a global collapse in 1928, but they shifted liquidity away from several debtor and trade-dependent economies.

The year also contained major structural transitions. The Soviet Union began the First Five-Year Plan on October 1, 1928, marking the decisive move away from the New Economic Policy toward coercive central planning and forced extraction from agriculture. China entered the Nanjing decade after nominal Nationalist consolidation, still on a silver monetary base rather than gold. Japan recovered from the 1927 banking crisis but remained financially fragile. Germany’s recovery remained dependent on short-term foreign capital. Britain’s older export industries continued to suffer under a strong sterling parity.

At the microeconomic level, 1928 was a year of mass-production capitalism, commodity-export dependence, and sectoral divergence. Automobiles, radios, electrical equipment, chemicals, ports, banking, and construction drove modern growth, while agriculture and older heavy industries were more vulnerable. Corporate giants such as General Motors, Ford, AT&T, Standard Oil successors, Royal Dutch Shell, IG Farben, Siemens, Mitsubishi, Mitsui, and major banks were central mechanisms through which macro trends became investment, employment, credit, and household behavior.

Data confidence is uneven. The United States, United Kingdom, France, Germany, Canada, Australia, and several Western European countries have relatively better monetary, trade, and financial documentation. China, India, Indonesia, Brazil, Mexico, the Former USSR, and several colonial or semi-colonial economies have useful historical reconstructions but far weaker contemporaneous statistical coverage. ### 2. KEY THEMES OF 1928

**1. The restored gold-exchange standard looked stable but was becoming deflationary.** By late 1928, most major economies had returned to some version of gold or gold-exchange discipline. France legally stabilized the franc in June 1928 at a gold content far below the prewar parity, which helped French competitiveness and attracted gold. The United States was already a major gold-standard center, and Britain remained on gold at prewar sterling parity. The significance is that the system appeared to anchor confidence while also transmitting tight money internationally. Countries most affected included the United States, France, Britain, Germany, Belgium, the Netherlands, Canada, and Australia. The forward linkage is direct: the gold standard became a major propagation mechanism of the Great Depression.

**2. US financial-market exuberance and monetary tightening converged.** US equity prices rose sharply, with the Dow Jones Industrial Average posting an annual gain near 49 percent in widely used historical series. At the same time, Federal Reserve policy tightened against speculation, and the New York Fed discount rate reached 5.00 percent by mid-1928. The historical significance is not that 1928 already contained a crash, but that it showed the contradiction between asset-price inflation, gold-standard reserve constraints, and limited central-bank tools. The countries most affected by the forward linkage were the United States, Germany, Canada, and Latin American borrowers dependent on New York credit.

**3. Germany’s recovery was strong but externally fragile.** Germany’s Maddison-based real GDP grew about 4.4 percent in 1928. Heavy industry, chemicals, machinery, and construction were active, and the Dawes Plan continued to frame reparations and foreign lending. The key vulnerability was maturity and funding risk: German prosperity depended on external borrowing, much of it short-term and dollar-linked. The forward linkage was the sudden stop after 1929 and the banking crisis of 1931.

**4. The Soviet economic regime changed category.** The First Five-Year Plan began in October 1928. The grain procurement crisis pushed the state toward coercive procurement and away from the mixed incentives of the New Economic Policy. This is one of the most important economic regime shifts of the twentieth century. It affected the Former USSR directly and changed global comparisons by creating a major non-market industrialization model. The forward linkage was forced collectivization, rapid heavy-industrial growth, and severe social and agricultural disruption.

**5. Commodity exporters were prosperous but exposed.** Canada, Argentina, Australia, Brazil, Indonesia, India, and parts of Latin America and colonial Asia depended heavily on export commodities: wheat, wool, beef, coffee, rubber, sugar, oil, tin, tea, jute, and cotton. In 1928 world trade and finance still supported this model. The significance is that commodity-export economies often looked healthy just before the price declines and trade contraction of the early 1930s. Brazil’s coffee economy and Argentina’s grain-meat model are key examples.

**6. Industrial modernity was sector-specific, not universal.** Automobiles, electricity, radio, chemicals, aviation, telephones, ports, and mechanized manufacturing represented modern growth. At the same time, coal mining, older textiles, small agriculture, and informal labor markets lagged or contracted in many places. This sectoral unevenness mattered inside countries: Britain’s London-centered services differed from coal districts; Japan’s zaibatsu and urban factories differed from silk villages; India’s mills differed from rural agriculture.

**7. Political economy mattered more than simple market indicators.** Fascist Italy pursued currency prestige and corporatist control. China’s Nationalist government sought fiscal and tariff consolidation under incomplete political unity. Mexico was still navigating post-revolutionary political risk. Spain used public works and protection under Primo de Rivera. These political-economic settings shaped firm decisions, labor markets, and external confidence.

#### 3B. Monetary & Financial Conditions

United States: The Federal Reserve tightened. The New York Fed discount rate was 3.50 percent in January, rose through the spring and summer, and stood at 5.00 percent from August through December in the FRED/NBER Macrohistory series. The intent was partly to restrain speculative credit, but the same tightening affected international borrowers and gold flows.

United Kingdom: Bank Rate was 4.50 percent throughout 1928 in the quarterly FRED series. Britain’s policy was constrained by the 1925 return to gold at prewar parity. Sterling credibility required external discipline even though domestic old-industry unemployment remained high.

#### 3C. Trade & Geopolitics

Trade policy in 1928 was still less restrictive than in the early 1930s, but the liberal pre-1914 world had not been fully restored. Tariffs, imperial preferences, colonial trade structures, reparations, and exchange controls in some countries shaped trade flows. The 1928 League of Nations statistical convention was part of a broader effort to standardize economic data, including trade statistics.

Major geopolitical-economic issues included German reparations under the Dawes framework, Soviet industrialization and grain procurement, China’s Nationalist consolidation, colonial commodity systems in India and Indonesia, and the role of sterling and dollar finance in global capital movements. The Cold War did not exist. ASEAN, the EEC, and Bretton Woods institutions did not exist.

Key bilateral tensions were economic rather than purely military: German reparations and Allied debts; British concerns over sterling competitiveness; US concern over speculative credit; Japanese policy debate over gold; and Chinese efforts to renegotiate fiscal and tariff authority after nominal unification.

#### 3D. Commodities & Energy

Oil was abundant and cheap by later standards. The US first-purchase crude price of $1.17 per barrel in 1928 reflects a market far removed from the OPEC era, which would not begin until 1960. Major producers included the United States, Mexico, Venezuela, the Soviet Union, the Dutch East Indies, and Persia. Oil mattered for autos, shipping, mechanization, and electricity, but coal remained central to many industrial economies.

Copper demand was linked to electrification, telephones, construction, industrial machinery, and power networks. The USGS price of 14.81 cents per pound suggests active but not crisis-level conditions. Steel, coal, tin, rubber, sugar, cotton, wheat, coffee, wool, and beef formed the commodity base of many top-20 economies.

Agricultural conditions were regionally specific. US farm income was still less dynamic than urban industry. Canada and Australia depended heavily on wheat and wool. Argentina depended on grain and meat. Brazil depended on coffee. India and Indonesia depended on colonial export commodities as well as subsistence production.

#### 3E. Labor & Demographics

Global employment statistics are Data not available in standardized form. The most important labor-market fact is non-comparability: unemployment in Britain’s insured trades, US historical estimates, colonial underemployment, Soviet plan allocation, and agrarian household labor are not measuring the same thing.

Demographically, high-population low-income economies such as China and India ranked near the top by aggregate GDP but low by GDP per capita. Urbanization was rising in the United States, Japan, Germany, Argentina, Brazil, and parts of Europe, but agriculture still employed very large shares of the workforce in India, China, Indonesia, Brazil, Mexico, the Former USSR, and Spain.

Migration still mattered economically, but the open migration regime of the pre-1914 world had narrowed. US immigration restrictions limited inflows. Settler economies such as Canada, Argentina, and Australia still reflected earlier migration and capital inflow patterns. Internal migration from countryside to city was increasingly important in Japan, the United States, Brazil, and Soviet industrialization.

**Economic narrative:**

The United States remained the largest economy in the Maddison-based ranking by a very large margin. Real output growth in the PPP reconstruction was modest because 1928 came late in a long expansion, but industrial production, consumer durables, electric utilities, radio, road transport, and securities markets still carried the public image of prosperity. Federal Reserve reporting for 1928 described a recovery from late-1927 weakness and a return of industrial activity to high levels, while agriculture remained much less buoyant than urban industry.

The year was defined by monetary tightening against speculative credit. The New York Reserve Bank discount rate moved from 3.50 percent early in the year to 5.00 percent after July. This was not a recessionary stance in immediate 1928 output data, but it signaled that the central bank was worried about securities credit, call loans, and bank balance sheets. With hindsight, this policy mix mattered because the United States was both the largest economy and a central gold-standard creditor.

Microeconomically, the consumer durables boom was visible in automobiles, radios, household electrification, advertising, branded consumer goods, and installment credit. Ford was shifting from the Model T to the Model A, while General Motors benefited from a more segmented model strategy. Banking consolidation, chain banking, and bank mergers also drew Federal Reserve attention. The weakest sector was still agriculture, where debt burdens and commodity-price volatility limited household purchasing power in farm regions.

**Micro/sectoral highlights:** Autos, electrical equipment, oil, steel, radio, advertising, retailing, and securities brokerage were the leading sectors. Farm regions were more fragile. Property and equity wealth gains supported consumption, but rising call-money and broker-loan exposure increased financial-system vulnerability.

In 1928, that vulnerability was not yet a crisis, but it was already a structural risk.

Industrial recovery was broad in chemicals, machinery, electrical equipment, metalworking, transport equipment, and urban construction. #### 5C. Innovation & technology

Technological change in 1928 was concentrated in electrification, radio, telephones, aviation, chemicals, automobiles, road infrastructure, agricultural mechanization, and industrial process engineering. This was a high-impact technology environment even though it predated computers, digital networks, container shipping, jet aviation, and antibiotics at modern scale.

Electrification increased demand for copper, machinery, appliances, and utility finance. Radio changed advertising, entertainment, and consumer culture. Automobiles changed oil demand, road spending, steel, rubber, glass, insurance, and consumer credit. Chemical firms such as IG Farben and DuPont linked research laboratories to industrial scale. Aviation was still small economically but strategically important.

#### 5D. Labor markets & human capital

Labor markets were segmented by sector, gender, skill, and political regime. Industrial workers in US auto plants, German chemical firms, Japanese textile mills, British coal mines, Indian jute mills, Soviet state industry, and Argentine ports faced fundamentally different wage-setting systems. A single global unemployment figure would be misleading; Data not available is the honest standardized answer.

Sectoral shifts were clear. High-income economies were moving labor toward manufacturing, construction, distribution, transport, and services. Lower-income economies still had large agrarian labor forces. The Soviet Union began a state-directed shift from agriculture to heavy industry. Japan continued rural-to-urban movement tied to textiles, heavy industry, and services. Brazil and Argentina had urbanizing labor markets tied to export wealth and domestic industry.

Union and labor-movement dynamics varied sharply. Britain had high unemployment and post-1926 labor tensions. Italy suppressed independent labor under fascist corporatism. The Soviet Union subordinated labor allocation to plan priorities. The United States had productivity gains but uneven union strength. Colonial economies combined formal industrial labor with very large informal and rural labor pools.

#### 6B. Fixed income

#### 6C. Currencies

The dominant currency regime was the interwar gold-exchange standard. The US dollar was fixed to gold at $20.67 per fine troy ounce. Sterling was on gold at about $4.8665 per pound. The French franc returned legally to gold in June 1928 with a dollar parity of about 3.9179 cents per franc, roughly 25.5 francs per dollar. The Dutch guilder and several other currencies operated within gold-standard contexts.

China was on silver, which made it an important exception. Japan had not yet restored gold convertibility and would do so only in January 1930. Spain was outside the full restored gold bloc. The Soviet Union’s currency was not convertible in the sense required for normal market comparison. Capital controls varied by country and are not standardized here.

#### 6D. Alternative assets

Gold was not an alternative floating asset in the modern sense inside gold-standard countries; it was the monetary anchor. The official US price was fixed at $20.67 per fine troy ounce. Gold flows, not market gold-price changes, were the key variable.

### 7. RISKS, CRISES & BLACK SWANS

Active risks in 1928 included US stock-market speculation, gold-standard liquidity strain, German dependence on foreign capital, Britain’s overvalued sterling and old-industry unemployment, Japan’s unresolved banking fragility, Soviet grain procurement conflict, China’s incomplete fiscal and monetary consolidation, and commodity-export concentration in Canada, Argentina, Australia, Brazil, Indonesia, and India.

Active crises included the Soviet grain procurement crisis and the policy break into the First Five-Year Plan. Japan was not in a 1928 crisis on the scale of 1927 or 1930-1931, but it was still living with the consequences of the 1927 banking crisis. Germany was not yet in collapse, but it was exposed to a sudden stop. Britain’s old industrial unemployment was chronic rather than a one-year shock.

Hindsight-labeled vulnerabilities: The biggest visible-in-retrospect mistake was treating the restored gold-exchange standard as a source of stability while ignoring how gold hoarding, reserve losses, and central-bank tightening could transmit deflation. The second was underestimating speculative leverage and securities-credit risk in the United States. The third was underestimating how quickly global capital flows could reverse, especially for Germany and commodity exporters.

Tail risks that did not yet materialize in 1928 included a US stock-market crash, German banking collapse, sovereign defaults, a collapse in world trade, mass unemployment on early-1930s scale, and Japan’s deflation after gold resumption. The fact that these did not materialize inside 1928 is precisely why the year is historically important: the system looked resilient until it did not.

### 8. YEAR-OVER-YEAR DELTA (1928 vs. 1927)

The operational Maddison-based available-country world estimate rose by about 3.2 percent from 1927 to 1928, using the same China interpolation method. This suggests continued late-cycle global expansion rather than stagnation. The top-20 composition did not change under this method.

The largest structural change was the Soviet break from NEP toward central planning. This was not a normal year-over-year macro fluctuation; it was a regime change. The First Five-Year Plan began in October 1928, and coercive grain procurement changed the relationship between agriculture, state finance, and industry.

France moved from de facto stabilization to legal gold-standard stabilization of the franc in June 1928. That strengthened confidence in France but also contributed to international gold-flow asymmetries. Compared with 1927, France became a more important monetary force.

The United States shifted from easier late-1927 conditions toward clear 1928 tightening. The New York Fed discount rate moved from 3.50 percent at the start of 1928 to 5.00 percent by late summer. This was a policy pivot with global implications because New York credit mattered to Germany and other borrowers.

Japan moved closer to gold resumption, though the actual return came in 1930. Compared with 1927, the immediate banking panic had passed, but the banking system and policy debate remained fragile.

Germany’s recovery continued, but the forward risk increased as US money tightened. Britain’s structural problems remained unresolved. Commodity exporters still looked healthy, but concentration risk grew because their prosperity depended on trade and credit conditions that would deteriorate after 1929.
