### 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The defining economic fact of 2024 was that the world economy avoided the recession many forecasters had feared after the 2022 to 2023 inflation shock. Global growth settled near 3 percent, slower than the pre-pandemic norm but more resilient than expected. The United States expanded faster than most other advanced economies, China hit its official growth target while fighting deflationary pressure and a property-sector correction, India remained the fastest-growing large economy, and the euro area was held back by weak manufacturing, high energy costs, and fiscal constraint.

The macro regime shifted from synchronized tightening to cautious easing. Inflation fell in most large economies, but services inflation and wage growth remained sticky enough to delay or slow rate cuts. The Federal Reserve cut late in the year, the European Central Bank began easing in June, the Bank of England cut in August and November, while the Bank of Japan moved in the opposite direction by ending negative-rate policy and raising rates. This made 2024 a transition year rather than a clean return to pre-2020 monetary conditions.

The microeconomic story was dominated by artificial intelligence investment, energy transition supply chains, resilient services consumption, and continuing stress in interest-rate-sensitive sectors such as commercial real estate and property development. Large technology firms captured an exceptional share of equity-market returns, while banks generally stabilized after the 2023 regional-bank shock but continued to face unrealized bond-loss and commercial-property exposures. Manufacturing was uneven: strong in semiconductors, defense-related production, and some emerging-market supply chains, weak in Germany and parts of Europe, and mixed in China because export strength coexisted with domestic demand weakness.

Geopolitics was unusually visible in economic data. Red Sea shipping disruption, Russia-Ukraine war effects, Middle East conflict risk, sanctions, export controls, and tariffs on Chinese electric vehicles, batteries, solar products, and semiconductors changed trade flows and corporate planning. The year also marked an acceleration in industrial policy: the United States, European Union, China, India, Japan, and South Korea all used subsidies, tariffs, procurement, and strategic investment to shape technology, energy, and defense supply chains.

### 2. KEY THEMES OF 2024

**1. Disinflation without a global recession.** The historical significance of 2024 is that large central banks brought inflation down from 2022 peaks without a synchronized collapse in employment. The United States combined real growth near 3 percent with lower inflation, while Europe achieved weaker growth and disinflation. Emerging markets were mixed: Brazil and Mexico still faced restrictive policy, India saw moderate inflation with strong growth, and Türkiye remained an outlier with very high inflation. The forward linkage was the 2025 debate over whether easing could continue without reigniting inflation.

**2. Divergent monetary cycles.** Most advanced-economy central banks moved toward easier policy, but Japan tightened after decades of near-zero rates. The Federal Reserve ended 2024 with the federal funds target range at 4.25 to 4.50 percent, the ECB deposit rate at 3.00 percent, the Bank of England rate at 4.75 percent, and the Bank of Japan short-term policy rate near 0.25 percent. This divergence mattered for exchange rates, bank funding, bond yields, and international capital flows. It also signaled the end of the globally synchronized emergency policy cycle that began in 2020.

**3. AI, semiconductors, and intangible capital as macro forces.** The AI investment cycle moved from a financial-market theme to a real-economy driver. Data centers increased electricity demand, semiconductor firms and cloud platforms drove capital expenditure, and AI-related equity concentration became a macro-financial risk. The United States, Taiwan, South Korea, Japan, the Netherlands, and China were most affected through chips, equipment, cloud infrastructure, and export controls. The forward linkage is the possibility that AI boosts productivity, or alternatively that it becomes a capital-spending bubble with uneven diffusion.

**4. China property weakness and global industrial overcapacity.** China reached around 5 percent growth, but private demand, housing, and local-government finance remained under pressure. At the same time, Chinese manufacturing capacity in electric vehicles, batteries, solar panels, and some industrial goods intensified trade tensions. The United States raised Section 301 tariffs on several China-linked strategic sectors, and the European Union imposed countervailing duties on battery electric vehicles from China. The forward linkage was a more fragmented trade system and a sharper distinction between consumer-price disinflation and geopolitical supply-chain risk.

**5. Fiscal space became a central constraint.** High public debt and higher real interest costs put pressure on both advanced and emerging economies. The United States ran a large fiscal deficit despite solid growth; France breached deficit expectations; Italy and Spain continued consolidation under European fiscal scrutiny; Brazil and Mexico faced fiscal credibility questions; and Russia sustained war-related spending despite sanctions. Debt service costs constrained policy more than in the 2010s and made bond markets more sensitive to fiscal news.

**6. Energy transition and energy security advanced together.** World energy demand rose in 2024, with electricity demand growing especially quickly. Renewables continued expanding, but oil, gas, and coal also remained important. Brent oil averaged near 80 dollars per barrel, gold averaged a record-high annual price, and power-system investment became a strategic priority. The countries most affected included China, the United States, India, Saudi Arabia, Russia, Germany, Indonesia, and Australia. The forward linkage was a more capital-intensive energy system shaped by climate policy, data centers, grid bottlenecks, and geopolitics.

**7. Trade recovered, but openness weakened.** Global goods and services trade volume recovered from weak 2023 conditions, but growth stayed below the high-globalization era. Trade became more regionalized and policy-conditioned. Shipping disruptions, sanctions, export controls, tariffs, and industrial policy mattered more than pure cost minimization. The most affected economies were China, the United States, Germany, Mexico, Vietnam outside the top 20, India, and the European Union. The forward linkage is a world where trade still grows, but less predictably and with more political conditionality.

### 3. MACROECONOMIC OVERVIEW

**3A. Global Aggregates:** World output grew at roughly 3 percent in 2024, with the World Bank estimating global growth near 2.7 percent and the IMF placing global growth slightly above 3 percent depending on data vintage and aggregation method. World GDP in current dollars was a little above 110 trillion dollars. Trade volume rebounded after the weak 2023 goods cycle; World Bank and WTO estimates put trade growth in the high 2 percent range. Global inflation continued to fall from 2022 to 2023 highs but remained above pre-pandemic norms, especially in services. The dollar remained the dominant reserve currency, with the euro second and the yen, pound, renminbi, Canadian dollar, Australian dollar, and Swiss franc far smaller shares. International capital flows favored dollar assets, AI-linked equities, high-grade debt, and selected emerging markets, while global foreign direct investment remained subdued after excluding conduit flows.

Key commodity prices reflected a world that was neither in recession nor in a new commodity supercycle. Brent crude averaged around 80 dollars per barrel, lower than 2023 but still elevated by pre-2020 standards. Gold averaged roughly 2,386 dollars per troy ounce and repeatedly reached record highs. Copper rose during parts of the year on energy-transition and China-stimulus expectations but remained sensitive to manufacturing demand. Agricultural staples were mixed: grain prices generally eased from war-shock extremes, while weather and regional supply conditions kept food inflation important in several economies.

**3B. Monetary & Financial Conditions:** The United States stayed restrictive for most of the year and cut only after clearer disinflation. The euro area cut earlier as growth weakened. The United Kingdom eased cautiously after inflation fell from very high levels. Japan ended negative-rate policy, making it the clearest major exception to global easing. Bond markets were volatile because investors had to price both rate cuts and persistent fiscal deficits. Yield curves remained unusually shaped in the United States for much of the year, with short rates high and long rates sensitive to inflation and Treasury supply.

Equity markets were strong but concentrated. United States large-cap technology and AI-related firms drove global indices. The S&P 500 rose about 23 percent on a price basis, the Nasdaq Composite rose about 29 percent, and global equities gained strongly but less evenly. Europe was mixed, with Germany outperforming France despite Germany's weak real economy. Japan's equity market reached historically significant levels as corporate-governance reforms, yen weakness, and foreign buying supported valuations. Emerging-market equity performance diverged sharply across India, China, Brazil, Mexico, Türkiye, and Saudi Arabia.

**3C. Trade & Geopolitics:** 2024 was marked by trade recovery and trade fragmentation at the same time. The United States increased tariffs on Chinese electric vehicles, batteries, solar cells, semiconductors, and selected medical and industrial goods. The European Union imposed definitive countervailing duties on China-made battery electric vehicles. China challenged the EU action at the WTO and continued to use industrial policy to support strategic sectors. Sanctions on Russia continued to reshape energy, shipping, and finance. Red Sea shipping disruptions increased freight uncertainty and lengthened some routes. Regional integration and nearshoring supported Mexico, India, Southeast Asia outside the top 20, and parts of Eastern Europe, but the gains were not uniform.

**3D. Commodities & Energy:** Oil markets were shaped by OPEC+ supply management, resilient consumption, Middle East risk, and non-OPEC supply growth, especially from the Americas. Saudi Arabia's fiscal and growth outlook remained tied to oil production limits and Vision 2030 investment. Russia redirected more trade under sanctions and sold energy through altered channels. Natural gas markets were calmer than in 2022, but European industry still faced higher energy costs than United States competitors. Electricity demand rose quickly because of cooling demand, electrification, data centers, industrial activity, and emerging-market growth. Renewables supplied a large share of incremental electricity, but fossil fuels still carried much of the system.

**3E. Labor & Demographics:** Global unemployment stayed around 5 percent, but labor-market conditions were not uniform. The United States had low unemployment but a cooler labor market than in 2023. Europe kept employment relatively resilient despite weak output. China faced youth employment concerns and underemployment pressure. India benefited from demographic scale but still faced job-quality and female-labor-force-participation challenges. Japan and Germany remained constrained by aging populations and skill shortages. Migration continued to matter economically: it supported labor supply in North America and parts of Europe, but it also became politically contested.

**5B. Their sector mix shows the 2024 economy's dual structure: consumer distribution, energy, state-linked utilities, technology, healthcare, and financial conglomerates. By market capitalization, United States technology firms dominated global equity value, especially companies tied to AI infrastructure and platforms.

M&A activity recovered selectively. ExxonMobil completed its acquisition of Pioneer Natural Resources, reinforcing the importance of scale in United States shale. IPO markets reopened but remained selective; India was notably active, and Reddit, Puig, Amer Sports, Astera Labs, and Kioxia were among the more visible global offerings. **5C. Innovation & technology:** AI was the year's most important innovation theme. The economic impact was visible in semiconductor demand, data-center construction, cloud spending, power demand, and equity-market concentration. The broader productivity effect remained uncertain in 2024 because adoption outside technology, finance, and professional services was still early.

Other economically important technologies included obesity and metabolic drugs in healthcare, battery manufacturing and recycling, grid-scale storage, green hydrogen pilots, electric vehicles, digital public infrastructure, payments, and defense-related manufacturing systems. Patent and R&D intensity remained concentrated in the United States, China, Japan, South Korea, Germany, and selected European economies.

**5D. Labor markets & human capital:** Labor markets cooled but did not collapse. The key sectoral shift was from pandemic-era goods and logistics extremes back toward services, healthcare, government, education, and technology infrastructure. Skill premiums remained high for AI, data engineering, cybersecurity, semiconductors, healthcare, and advanced manufacturing. Union activity remained economically visible in the United States and Europe, especially in autos, logistics, education, and public services. Migration supported labor supply in North America, the United Kingdom, Australia, Spain, and the Gulf, but it also intensified political debate and housing pressure.

**6B. Fixed income:** Government bond markets were volatile. United States 10-year Treasury yields ended near the mid-4 percent range after moving with inflation data, Fed expectations, and fiscal supply. Emerging-market local-currency bonds diverged: Brazil and Türkiye faced inflation and fiscal concerns, while some Asian markets benefited from lower inflation and currency management.

**6C. Currencies:** The dollar remained strong because United States growth and yields outperformed. The yen was weak and required official attention. Crypto assets rose strongly late in 2024, but this report treats them as high-volatility financial assets rather than macro anchors.

### 7. RISKS, CRISES & BLACK SWANS

Active crises included the Russia-Ukraine war, Middle East conflict risk, Red Sea shipping disruption, China's property adjustment, high public debt, and lingering commercial-real-estate stress. These were not isolated events; each affected trade routes, energy prices, inflation expectations, fiscal policy, and corporate investment plans. A key 2024 near-miss was that high interest rates did not produce a broad banking crisis, even though vulnerabilities remained.

Systemic vulnerabilities building in 2024 included high sovereign debt in the United States and parts of Europe, very high private and public leverage in China-linked property channels, AI equity concentration, data-center power bottlenecks, and fiscal dependence on commodity revenues in hydrocarbon exporters. With hindsight, the year showed that the post-pandemic economy was more resilient than expected, but also more dependent on fiscal deficits, market concentration, and policy-managed supply chains than a standard soft-landing story implied.

Policy mistakes visible in retrospect are debated. Some economists argue that central banks waited too long to ease, risking unnecessary output weakness. Others argue that premature easing would have risked reaccelerating inflation. On fiscal policy, the consensus concern was clearer: many countries used too much deficit space during a period when unemployment was low and borrowing costs were high.

### 8. YEAR-OVER-YEAR DELTA (2024 vs. 2023)

The biggest structural break from 2023 to 2024 was the shift from inflation shock management to disinflation and selective easing. In 2023 the dominant question was whether high rates would break the economy. In 2024 the question became how quickly central banks could normalize without reviving inflation. The United States proved more resilient than expected, while Germany's weakness became more structural and China's property weakness more persistent.

Trade changed from post-pandemic normalization to explicit strategic fragmentation. Compared with 2023, 2024 had stronger trade-volume growth but more tariffs, export controls, sanctions, and shipping disruptions. Industrial policy moved from legislation and announcements toward implementation in chips, energy, EVs, batteries, and defense supply chains.

Financial markets also changed. The 2023 regional-bank shock faded, but equity-market concentration increased. The AI theme moved from narrative to capital expenditure, earnings, and power demand. Bond markets remained sensitive to fiscal deficits, showing that the low-rate 2010s regime had not returned.

The balance of economic influence shifted further toward the United States and India among large economies, while China remained enormous but more constrained by domestic imbalance and external pushback. Spain outperformed within Europe; Germany underperformed. Oil exporters had to balance production management with diversification investment. Emerging-market credibility increasingly depended on inflation control and fiscal anchors.

### 9. DATA CONFIDENCE & GAPS

Data confidence is highest for the United States, euro-area members, the United Kingdom, Canada, Japan, Australia, Switzerland, South Korea, and the Netherlands, where statistical agencies publish frequent, detailed, internationally comparable data. Even there, revisions can alter quarterly growth, fiscal balances, and current-account figures. GDP rankings in current dollars are especially sensitive to exchange rates and later national-account revisions.

Confidence is medium for India, Brazil, Mexico, Indonesia, Saudi Arabia, and Türkiye. The broad GDP and inflation numbers are usable, but labor-market definitions, informality, fiscal perimeter, and off-budget obligations reduce comparability. India uses fiscal-year data for many headline releases, which requires caution when mapping to calendar-year GDP. Türkiye's inflation environment makes nominal comparisons difficult.

Confidence is lower for Russia's sectoral, fiscal, and external data because sanctions, wartime spending, capital controls, and trade rerouting reduce transparency. China's headline GDP data are official and detailed, but the property sector, local-government financing vehicles, youth employment, and private-sector confidence require caution. Saudi fiscal and project-level data can be incomplete where public investment funds and state-linked entities carry out quasi-fiscal activity.

The weakest evidence in this document concerns exact global sector-level performance outside publicly traded firms, standardized annual returns for every major exchange, and globally comparable unemployment across countries with high informality. Important missing data include consistent household balance-sheet stress metrics across emerging economies, real-time local-government debt in China, comprehensive war-economy fiscal detail for Russia, and comparable commercial-real-estate exposure across banking systems.
