# MACRO/MICRO GLOBAL ECONOMIC ASSESSMENT - YEAR: 2025

### 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The world economy in 2025 was defined by resilience under pressure. Global output continued to expand even after several years of inflation, high interest rates, geopolitical fragmentation and tariff uncertainty. The IMF's April 2026 reference forecast placed 2025 world output growth at 3.4 percent, while the OECD's late-2025 assessment put global growth at 3.2 percent. The difference is not a contradiction so much as a reminder that 2025 data were still being revised in early 2026. The broad conclusion is consistent across sources: the global economy avoided recession, but growth was uneven and increasingly dependent on a few engines.

The most important engines were the United States, India, parts of emerging Asia, services, and the AI-linked investment cycle. The global semiconductor industry rebounded sharply, AI-related venture investment surged, and data-center construction became a macroeconomic issue because it affected power demand, capital expenditure, equity valuations and industrial policy. China met its growth target, but its growth mix depended heavily on exports, manufacturing policy and stimulus while private domestic demand and property remained weak. Europe lagged, with Spain outperforming while Germany's old export-manufacturing model looked strained.

Inflation continued to fall from the extreme levels of 2021 to 2023, but it did not disappear as a policy problem. The IMF put world consumer-price inflation at 4.1 percent for 2025, with advanced economies near 2.5 percent and emerging and developing economies above 5 percent. Central banks therefore eased only carefully. The Federal Reserve, Bank of England and European Central Bank ended the year less tight than at the peak of the cycle, but policy remained restrictive compared with the 2010s. Japan was the exception in regime terms: it continued its move away from ultra-low-rate policy.

Trade produced one of the year's surprises. Early in 2025, tariff uncertainty and trade-policy conflict made a trade slowdown plausible. Instead, world goods and services trade expanded strongly in value terms, with WTO and UNCTAD data pointing to gains near 7 percent, helped by front-loading before tariff changes, services expansion and AI-related goods. The same pattern also created fragility: trade growth was not proof that globalization had returned to the pre-2016 model. It was partly an inventory and timing effect, and partly a new technology-cycle effect.

The year also exposed a public-finance and market-structure problem. Long-term bond yields stayed high or rose in many countries even as short-term policy rates stabilized or fell. Public debt, defense spending, energy-security investment, aging populations and infrastructure needs competed for fiscal space. Equity markets, especially in the United States and Asia, rewarded AI-linked firms, while gold reached a record annual average. In hindsight, 2025 was not a crisis year; it was a balance-sheet and allocation year in which vulnerabilities accumulated beneath apparently solid headline growth.

### 2. KEY THEMES OF 2025

**1. Resilient growth with weaker safety margins.** The significance of 2025 in economic history is that the global economy absorbed high rates, tariff uncertainty and geopolitical stress without a synchronized recession. IMF and OECD assessments agree on continued global expansion. Yet resilience was not evenly distributed. The United States, India and emerging Asia carried more of the growth load, while Germany, Italy, Mexico and parts of Europe were weak. The forward linkage is that 2026 began with less room for policy mistakes: public debt was high, private credit had grown, and long-term yields were less forgiving.

**2. AI moved from a technology story to a macro driver.** In earlier years, AI was mostly discussed as a productivity possibility. In 2025, it became measurable in semiconductor sales, venture capital, data centers, electricity demand, equity-market concentration and capital expenditure. Semiconductor sales rose by more than 25 percent in industry data, and OECD research reported that AI firms accounted for more than half of global venture-capital investment. The United States, South Korea, Taiwan, the Netherlands, Japan and China were most affected. The forward linkage is double-edged: faster productivity is possible, but disappointment could destabilize investment plans and equity valuations.

**3. Trade fragmentation coexisted with trade growth.** The year showed that higher trade barriers do not automatically mean a collapse in trade volumes. Firms front-loaded shipments, rerouted supply chains and invested in alternative suppliers. WTO and UNCTAD data show strong 2025 trade values, but the causes were partly defensive. China, Mexico, the United States, Germany, South Korea, Japan and Southeast Asia were most exposed. The forward linkage is that inventory cycles and tariff timing can flatter trade data temporarily while reducing efficiency over time.

**4. Disinflation did not restore the 2010s policy regime.** Inflation fell, but the interest-rate environment did not return to near zero. The Federal Reserve cut to a 3.50 to 3.75 percent target range by year end, the Bank of England ended at 3.75 percent, and the ECB held rates in December after earlier easing. Japan's move away from ultra-easy policy was historically significant. The countries most affected were those with high household debt, high public debt or interest-sensitive housing markets, including Canada, Australia, the United Kingdom and parts of Europe.

**5. China became a global demand and supply imbalance story.** China reached roughly 5 percent real growth, but the IMF flagged weak private demand, muted inflation and export reliance. This mattered globally because China's industrial scale affects prices, margins and investment in autos, batteries, solar, steel, shipbuilding and electronics. The countries most affected included Germany, South Korea, Japan, the United States, India, Mexico and emerging manufacturing economies. The forward linkage is rising trade conflict if domestic demand does not absorb more Chinese output.

**6. Energy shifted from shortage fears to oversupply in oil, while electrification demand rose.** Oil prices fell in 2025 because supply growth outpaced demand, with IEA and EIA data describing large inventory builds and an average Brent price well below the 2022-2024 energy-shock period. At the same time, electricity demand from AI data centers and electrification became more important. Oil exporters such as Saudi Arabia and Russia faced a different macro constraint than in the price-spike years. The forward link is that energy security, grid investment and commodity volatility will remain central to inflation and fiscal outcomes.

**7. Fiscal space became the binding constraint.** Public debt and rising sovereign borrowing needs were central to the OECD and BIS assessments. Germany's 2025 fiscal-framework reform was one of the most important policy shifts in Europe because it opened room for infrastructure and defense spending. In many countries, the problem was not only the debt stock, but the cost and term structure of financing. The forward linkage is a steeper trade-off among defense, welfare, climate investment, aging and tax capacity.

For real activity, the IMF April 2026 reference forecast gives 2025 world output growth of 3.4 percent; the OECD late-2025 report gives 3.2 percent. Both show steady expansion rather than recession.

World trade volume grew faster than output. The IMF estimate for world trade volume in goods and services was 5.1 percent in 2025. WTO and UNCTAD trade-value releases describe a stronger dollar-value expansion, near 7 percent for goods and services, with AI-related goods, services trade and front-loaded imports playing important roles. This means trade data for 2025 should not be read as a simple return to frictionless globalization.

Reserve-currency dynamics changed slowly. The IMF COFER note for the second quarter of 2025 reported that the US dollar's reserve share was little changed in exchange-rate-adjusted terms. The dollar remained the core reserve and funding currency, but diversification into gold and selected non-dollar assets continued. Gold's 2025 performance is consistent with reserve diversification, geopolitical hedging and investor demand.

Global inflation cooled but remained above the pre-pandemic norm. IMF staff estimated world consumer-price inflation at 4.1 percent in 2025, advanced-economy inflation at 2.5 percent and emerging-market and developing-economy inflation at 5.2 percent. The key global commodity price movements were oil down 14.4 percent in the IMF commodity-price table, nonfuel commodities up 9.6 percent, and gold at a record annual average according to World Gold Council data. International capital flows favored the United States and AI-linked equity markets, while high yields kept pressure on weaker sovereign and corporate borrowers.

#### 3B. Monetary & Financial Conditions

The 2025 interest-rate environment was one of cautious easing, not a full return to easy money. The Federal Reserve ended the year at a 3.50 to 3.75 percent target range. The Bank of England cut Bank Rate to 3.75 percent in December by a narrow vote. The ECB kept rates unchanged in December after easing earlier in the cycle. The Bank of Japan remained the main regime-change story, gradually moving away from the ultra-low-rate framework that had defined Japanese policy for years.

Exchange rates remained important because nominal GDP rankings are expressed in current US dollars. The 2025 IMF ranking put the United Kingdom above India in nominal current-dollar GDP, while India remained far larger on a PPP basis and much faster-growing in real terms. This is a reminder that nominal rankings reflect exchange rates, inflation, market prices and timing, not only real output.

Credit conditions were mixed. Large firms, especially in technology and AI infrastructure, had strong access to capital. Smaller firms, commercial real estate borrowers and lower-rated issuers faced tighter conditions. OECD debt work and BIS analysis both emphasized that long-term yields and sovereign issuance needs were becoming more important than short-term policy rates alone.

Equity markets were strong, led by AI and semiconductor exposure. US indices posted double-digit returns, with the S&P 500 price return around 16 percent and the Nasdaq above 20 percent in market data. Hong Kong, Japan, Germany and parts of China also performed strongly. Bond markets were more uncomfortable: long-term yields stayed elevated as investors priced fiscal supply, inflation risk and less central-bank buying.

#### 3C. Trade & Geopolitics

Trade policy uncertainty was one of the defining features of 2025. Tariff changes and threats affected shipment timing, inventory management and investment decisions. WTO releases initially warned about trade downside risk, but later data showed stronger-than-expected goods trade because firms front-loaded shipments and AI-related products grew quickly. The United States, China, Mexico, Germany, Japan, South Korea and Southeast Asian exporters were most exposed.

Geopolitics shaped energy, defense spending, sanctions and supply chains. Russia remained under extensive sanctions, with its economy shaped by war-related state spending and import restrictions. China's export model generated greater pushback from trading partners. Europe increased attention to defense capacity and energy security. ASEAN and India benefited from supply-chain diversification, but not all nearshoring expectations turned into immediate investment.

Regional integration was mixed. The European Union remained a deep market, but its internal growth divergence widened. ASEAN benefited from manufacturing and trade rerouting. North America remained highly integrated, yet US-Mexico-Canada trade was more politically uncertain than in earlier years. The Middle East's economic story centered on oil, sovereign investment and diversification, with Saudi Arabia still highly influential.

#### 3D. Commodities & Energy

Oil was the clearest commodity reversal. EIA data describe crude oil prices falling in 2025 as oversupply developed, with Brent moving from around $79 per barrel early in the year to the low $60s by December and averaging about $69 per barrel. IEA data describe supply growth near 3 million barrels per day versus demand growth below 1 million barrels per day, producing inventory builds. The IMF's average oil-price measure was $67.74 per barrel for 2025.

Gold was the opposite story. The World Gold Council reported a record annual average gold price of about $3,431 per ounce, 53 new all-time highs during the year and total demand including OTC above 5,000 tonnes. Gold's strength reflected central-bank demand, investment demand, geopolitical hedging and concern about fiscal and currency regimes.

Metals and agricultural commodities were mixed. Copper and critical minerals benefited from electrification, grid investment and AI-infrastructure narratives, but demand was sensitive to China construction and global manufacturing. Agricultural staples were affected by weather, fertilizer costs, trade restrictions and currency movements. #### 3E. Labor & Demographics

Global labor markets were stable but less tight than in 2022-2023. ILO and OECD material pointed to unemployment rates near historically low levels in many advanced economies, but with signs of weakening and persistent youth, gender and informality challenges. OECD unemployment was around 4.9 percent in mid-2025, but country definitions are not directly comparable.

Demographics were a major hidden driver. India and Indonesia benefited from young populations and labor-force expansion. Japan, Germany, South Korea, Italy and China faced aging and labor scarcity. Migration mattered for Canada, Australia, Spain, the United Kingdom and the United States because it affected labor supply, housing demand and public-service capacity.

Wages and productivity did not move uniformly. Japan's wage gains were historically important because they supported the shift away from deflation psychology. In the United States, real-income gains were uneven and asset-price gains favored wealthier households. In Europe, wage recovery supported consumption but also kept services inflation sticky. The structural labor-market theme was clear: firms responded to labor scarcity with automation, AI, outsourcing, migration and higher wages, but productivity gains were still uncertain.

Sectoral employment shifted toward services, technology infrastructure, health care, logistics and construction linked to public investment or data centers. Manufacturing employment was more complex: high-value chip and equipment jobs expanded, while traditional export manufacturing faced automation and China-driven competition. Aging economies used immigration, automation and higher wages to offset labor scarcity.

Union and wage dynamics mattered most in Japan, Europe and parts of North America. Japan's wage gains supported a historic monetary-policy transition. In Europe, wage recovery after the inflation shock supported consumption but complicated services disinflation. In the United States, labor-market cooling reduced wage pressure without producing a broad recession.

### 7. RISKS, CRISES & BLACK SWANS

The most important active risks in 2025 were trade fragmentation, public debt, China's property and demand imbalance, oil-market volatility, AI-valuation concentration, commercial real estate, and geopolitical conflict affecting sanctions, energy and defense spending. None produced a synchronized global recession in 2025, but several increased fragility.

Trade-policy risk materialized through front-loading and rerouting rather than immediate collapse. This means the data can be misleading: strong 2025 trade growth may partly represent shipments pulled forward from future periods. The risk that did not fully materialize in 2025 was a broad trade-volume contraction. The risk that did materialize was reduced predictability for investment and supply chains.

China's property-sector adjustment remained a slow-moving crisis. It did not create a sudden global financial crash, but it weakened domestic demand, local-government finance and household confidence. The global spillover was excess manufacturing supply and greater dependence on exports. This became a source of trade conflict.

Public debt and long-term yields were a systemic vulnerability. The problem was not a single sovereign default among the top economies. The problem was that many governments needed to finance defense, aging, infrastructure, green investment and interest costs at the same time. Hindsight label: 2025 looks like a year in which bond investors began enforcing a stricter fiscal constraint even as central banks cut short-term rates.

AI was both upside and black-swan risk. The upside was faster productivity and new investment. The downside was an investment bubble, power bottlenecks, equity concentration or a sudden repricing if revenue failed to match infrastructure spending. In 2025, optimism dominated. The risk was building rather than resolved.

Oil was a surprise in the opposite direction from 2022. Instead of an oil-price spike, 2025 delivered lower prices because supply growth exceeded demand growth. This helped inflation and oil-importing consumers, but pressured exporters and contributed to OPEC+ tensions. The unresolved risk was that geopolitical disruption could reverse the oversupply story quickly.

### 8. YEAR-OVER-YEAR DELTA (2025 vs. 2024)

The biggest 2025 versus 2024 change was the shift from inflation shock management to post-shock allocation. In 2024, many economies were still focused on whether disinflation could proceed without recession. In 2025, that question was largely answered in the affirmative, but new questions appeared: who pays for higher debt, who benefits from AI, and who absorbs China's manufacturing surplus?

Nominal GDP rankings shifted but did not transform. The top-20 membership was unchanged in the IMF/Worldometer extraction. The United Kingdom moved above India in nominal current-dollar terms, even though India remained much faster-growing and much larger on a PPP basis. Russia moved up in nominal ranking versus 2024, partly reflecting exchange-rate, inflation and wartime-economy measurement effects. Poland was close to the cutoff but remained rank 21.

Trade accelerated versus the pessimistic early-2025 forecasts. Goods and services trade grew strongly by value, and the IMF estimate for trade volume exceeded world output growth. The delta is important: 2025 trade strength was not simply a healthy globalization signal. It partly reflected tariff front-loading and AI-related hardware demand.

Commodity conditions changed materially. Oil fell from earlier elevated levels, easing inflation pressure for importers and limiting fiscal upside for exporters. Gold surged to records, signaling demand for hedges against geopolitical and monetary uncertainty. This combination, weak oil and strong gold, captures the unusual mix of lower near-term energy inflation and higher structural uncertainty.

Policy regimes changed at the margin. The Fed, ECB and BoE moved further into cautious easing, while Japan moved further out of ultra-easy policy. Germany's fiscal-framework reform was a structural break with implications beyond 2025. AI investment also changed the industrial-policy map: power grids, chips, data centers and advanced manufacturing became central macro issues.
