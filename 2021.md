# MACRO/MICRO GLOBAL ECONOMIC ASSESSMENT - YEAR: 2021

### 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

2021 was the year of rapid but uneven reopening after the 2020 pandemic shock. Global output rebounded strongly, merchandise trade volumes surged above pre-pandemic levels, and many household-facing service sectors reopened in phases. The rebound was not a simple return to the 2019 economy. It was driven by extraordinary fiscal transfers, ultra-loose monetary policy, accumulated household savings in advanced economies, rapid goods demand, and still-disrupted global production networks. The result was a high-growth year that also planted the inflation, debt, and policy-normalization problems that dominated 2022.

The biggest macro surprise was the speed with which inflation became broad enough to challenge the assumption that price pressures were only temporary. Energy prices, shipping costs, semiconductor shortages, labor-market mismatches, rent and housing pressures, and demand rotation from services to goods all contributed. Central banks in the United States, euro area, and Japan still kept policy rates near zero or negative, while a number of emerging-market central banks began hiking aggressively. By late 2021, financial markets were pricing a transition away from emergency settings, even though the largest advanced-economy central banks had not yet completed that pivot.

At the micro level, 2021 was defined by scarcity in some sectors and abundance in others. Technology, cloud computing, semiconductors, logistics, green-energy hardware, pharmaceuticals, and private capital markets expanded. Travel, tourism, small retailers, urban services, and parts of hospitality remained constrained by public-health restrictions and consumer caution. Automobiles experienced one of the most visible supply shocks, with strong demand but reduced production because chips and other inputs were scarce. Housing markets in many advanced economies accelerated on low mortgage rates, tight supply, and pandemic-related space demand.

The top 20 economies were broadly the same as in 2020, but their relative momentum diverged. The United States and China remained dominant in nominal GDP, India rebounded sharply from its 2020 contraction, commodity exporters such as Canada, Russia, Brazil, Australia, and Saudi Arabia benefited from higher prices, and Europe recovered with help from policy support but remained exposed to energy and industrial bottlenecks. China’s property-sector stress, symbolized by Evergrande, marked a structural inflection. With hindsight, 2021 was not just a recovery year. It was the transition from pandemic crisis management into an inflationary, supply-constrained, geopolitically fragile regime.

### 2. KEY THEMES OF 2021

**1. Reopening with uneven recovery.** The year’s central economic theme was reopening, but not synchronized reopening. Advanced economies with faster vaccination, larger fiscal space, and stronger household balance sheets recovered more quickly. Emerging economies had more uneven public-health capacity, less fiscal room, and larger labor-market scarring. Services recovered later than goods. Tourism-dependent economies lagged while goods exporters, technology suppliers, and commodity producers benefited.

**2. Inflation returned as a macro constraint.** Inflation was initially described by many policymakers as transitory because it was tied to reopening frictions and base effects. During the year, price pressures broadened through energy, used cars, shipping, food, housing, and wages in selected sectors. The historical significance is that 2021 ended the post-2008 assumption that advanced economies were structurally unable to generate sustained inflation. The policy linkage to 2022 was direct: rapid monetary tightening began after the groundwork was laid in 2021.

**3. Supply chains became a macroeconomic variable.** Before the pandemic, logistics were usually treated as a micro or trade-cost issue. In 2021, port congestion, container shortages, semiconductor bottlenecks, and fragile just-in-time networks affected inflation, output, corporate margins, and trade policy. Automobiles, electronics, machinery, and retail inventories were most affected. This shifted the policy debate toward resilience, inventories, local capacity, and industrial policy.

**4. Fiscal and monetary policy reached peak emergency support, then began to diverge.** The United States, Europe, Japan, and many other economies continued large fiscal and central-bank support, but the direction changed during the year. The Federal Reserve began tapering asset purchases, the Bank of England raised Bank Rate in December, and emerging-market central banks in Brazil, Russia, Mexico, and Türkiye faced inflation or currency pressure. The IMF’s historic SDR allocation also reinforced international liquidity for vulnerable economies.

**5. Commodity and energy markets moved from pandemic slump to scarcity risk.** Oil, natural gas, coal, copper, iron ore, and agricultural prices rose as demand recovered while supply was slow to respond. Energy-intensive European industries felt pressure from gas prices. Commodity exporters gained external revenue and fiscal relief. Importers faced worsening terms of trade and food-price pressure. These dynamics intensified in 2022 after Russia’s invasion of Ukraine, but the vulnerability was visible in 2021.

**6. China shifted from growth-first property leverage to risk containment.** China’s 2021 growth was strong, but the second half showed slowing activity, electricity shortages, regulatory pressure on platform firms, and property-sector stress. The Evergrande crisis did not become a global financial crisis in 2021, but it revealed the cost of an investment-heavy, debt-financed growth model. The forward linkage was weaker Chinese property demand, lower confidence, and global spillovers through commodities and construction-linked industries.

**7. Capital markets boomed under abundant liquidity.** Equity markets, IPOs, private equity, venture capital, SPACs, and M&A activity surged. Financing was cheap, investor risk appetite was high, and technology valuations expanded. This was historically important because it showed how pandemic-era liquidity supported both productive innovation and speculative excess. The reversal in 2022 exposed the fragility of business models reliant on cheap capital.

### 3. MACROECONOMIC OVERVIEW

**3A. Global Aggregates:** World nominal GDP was roughly in the high USD 90 trillion range in current dollars, depending on source and later national-account revisions. Real global GDP growth is reported around 5.5 percent by the World Bank for 2021 and around 6.1 percent by the IMF’s April 2022 estimate. Merchandise trade volume rose about 9.7 percent according to WTO data, after contracting in 2020. This made trade a faster-growing part of the rebound than GDP, although the rebound was concentrated in goods while travel and tourism services remained below pre-pandemic norms.

Global inflation rose sharply. Advanced-economy inflation was still lower than emerging-market inflation on average, but the change in direction was more important than the level: energy, food, rents, durable goods, and transport costs all moved upward. Capital flows were supported by low global rates and strong risk appetite. UNCTAD reported global FDI flows of about USD 1.58 trillion, up 64 percent from 2020, with a large boost from M&A, retained earnings, and project finance. Reserve currency composition changed only gradually. The US dollar remained dominant, with IMF COFER data putting its allocated-reserve share at roughly 59 percent in 2021 Q4.

Commodity prices rose broadly. Brent crude averaged about USD 71 per barrel, copper averaged above USD 9,000 per metric ton, and gold remained elevated near USD 1,800 per troy ounce. Food prices increased across grains and oils. Oil demand recovered but was still affected by travel restrictions; natural gas and coal markets tightened as electricity demand rebounded and renewable output was uneven in some regions.

**3B. Monetary & Financial Conditions:** Monetary conditions were highly accommodative in the largest advanced economies. The Federal Reserve kept the federal funds target range at 0.00 to 0.25 percent through year-end and began tapering asset purchases late in the year. The ECB kept the main refinancing rate at 0.00 percent and the deposit rate at -0.50 percent while preparing to end net PEPP purchases in March 2022. The Bank of Japan maintained its -0.1 percent short-term policy rate and yield-curve-control framework. The Bank of England became the first major advanced-economy central bank to raise rates in the cycle, lifting Bank Rate to 0.25 percent in December.

Emerging-market monetary policy diverged. Brazil, Russia, Mexico, and several Central and Eastern European economies raised rates in response to inflation and currency pressure. Türkiye cut rates despite high inflation, contributing to a lira crisis. Yield curves in the United States and several other advanced economies steepened early in the year, then flattened as markets priced future tightening. Equity markets were strong in most advanced economies: the S&P 500 rose about 27 percent, the CAC 40 about 29 percent, the DAX about 16 percent, and the FTSE 100 about 14 percent. Chinese and Hong Kong equities were weaker because of regulatory tightening and property-sector stress.

**3C. Trade & Geopolitics:** The multilateral trading system remained strained but did not collapse. US-China tariffs from the previous administration largely remained in place. The US-EU Boeing-Airbus dispute moved toward suspension of punitive tariffs, while the US and EU also began arrangements on steel and aluminum trade. Brexit frictions affected UK-EU trade, especially smaller exporters. The Regional Comprehensive Economic Partnership was signed in 2020 and was moving toward implementation in Asia. Sanctions remained important for Iran, Russia, Venezuela, and selected entities in China. At the end of 2021, Russia’s military buildup near Ukraine was a geopolitical risk, but the full economic shock of the invasion was still a 2022 event.

**3D. Commodities & Energy:** OPEC+ managed gradual supply increases after the 2020 oil-price collapse. Oil-price recovery helped fiscal positions in Saudi Arabia, Russia, Canada, and other producers. Natural gas prices rose sharply in Europe and parts of Asia. Coal use rebounded, especially in electricity generation, and the IEA later reported record-high energy-related CO2 emissions in 2021. Copper and other industrial metals benefited from manufacturing recovery and green-transition demand. Agricultural markets faced higher fertilizer, energy, and transport costs, making food affordability a growing issue in lower-income economies.

**3E. Labor & Demographics:** Labor-market recovery was uneven. In the United States, job vacancies surged and the phrase “Great Resignation” entered common use, while labor-force participation remained below pre-pandemic levels. Europe relied more heavily on job-retention schemes and saw lower measured unemployment volatility. Emerging markets had larger informal-sector damage and weaker social protection. Migration flows were disrupted by border rules, labor shortages appeared in agriculture, logistics, healthcare, and hospitality, and demographic aging continued to weigh on Japan, Europe, China, and South Korea.

**5B. Corporate landscape:** The largest revenue companies remained concentrated in retail, energy, automobiles, technology, and healthcare. Walmart, State Grid, Amazon, China National Petroleum, Sinopec, Apple, CVS Health, UnitedHealth, Toyota, and Volkswagen were among the largest by revenue in the 2021 Fortune Global 500 cycle. By market capitalization, technology and platform firms dominated: Apple, Microsoft, Alphabet, Amazon, Meta, Tesla, Nvidia, TSMC, and Tencent were central to investor narratives.

M&A and IPO markets were exceptionally active. EY reported 2,388 global IPOs raising about USD 453 billion in 2021. Major deals announced in 2021 included Microsoft-Nuance, AT&T’s WarnerMedia transaction with Discovery, Rogers-Shaw, Amazon-MGM, and large infrastructure, telecom, and healthcare transactions. SPAC activity remained high early in the year but weakened as regulatory scrutiny and market fatigue increased.

**5C. Innovation & technology:** The most economically important innovations were not single inventions but deployment curves: mRNA vaccines at scale, cloud infrastructure, AI-enabled enterprise software, electric vehicles, batteries, and semiconductor manufacturing investment. The chip shortage pushed governments toward industrial policy, including new semiconductor incentives in the United States, Europe, Japan, South Korea, and China. Renewable energy investment continued, but fossil-fuel demand also rebounded sharply.

**5D. Labor markets & human capital:** Sectoral employment shifted toward logistics, healthcare, technology, and remote-enabled services. Hospitality and travel had difficulty rehiring. Wage pressure was strongest in lower-wage service sectors in the United States and in specialized skilled occupations globally. Education disruption created longer-run human-capital risk, especially where school closures were lengthy. Migration restrictions reduced labor supply in agriculture, care work, construction, and universities.

### 6. FINANCIAL MARKETS

**6A. Equity markets:** Major equity markets mostly rose. Approximate 2021 price returns were: S&P 500 +27 percent, Nasdaq Composite +21 percent, Dow Jones Industrial Average +19 percent, FTSE 100 +14 percent, DAX +16 percent, CAC 40 +29 percent, Nikkei 225 +5 percent, S&P/TSX +22 percent, ASX 200 +13 percent, and India’s Sensex about +20 percent. China-linked markets were weaker: the Hang Seng fell sharply and Chinese internet stocks were hit by regulatory action. Sector rotation favored energy, financials, materials, and cyclicals in many markets, while high-growth technology remained strong until late-year rate expectations began to bite.

**6B. Fixed income:** Advanced-economy sovereign yields rose from 2020 lows but remained historically low. The US 10-year Treasury yield ended near 1.5 percent. German Bund yields stayed negative. Japanese government bond yields remained around zero under BOJ yield-curve control. Credit spreads were generally contained because liquidity was abundant and default expectations improved. Emerging-market local bonds were pressured where inflation and currency risks rose.

**6C. Currencies:** The US dollar strengthened in real effective terms during 2021 as markets anticipated Fed tightening. The euro weakened late in the year. The yen also weakened, reflecting Japan’s low-rate stance. Commodity currencies such as the Canadian dollar were supported by energy prices. The Turkish lira was the most prominent major-economy currency crisis, weakening sharply after rate cuts. The Saudi riyal remained pegged to the dollar.

**6D. Alternative assets:** Gold stayed high but did not surge despite inflation because real-rate and dollar expectations changed. Housing appreciated strongly in many advanced economies. Cryptoassets had a boom-bust-boom pattern during the year, with higher institutional attention but large volatility. Commercial real estate was split: logistics and industrial space strengthened, offices remained uncertain, and retail property was uneven.

### 7. RISKS, CRISES & BLACK SWANS

Active 2021 crises included COVID waves, Delta and Omicron, supply-chain congestion, semiconductor scarcity, energy-price spikes, China property stress, and Türkiye’s currency crisis. These were visible in real time. A major near-miss was the possibility that Evergrande could cause a disorderly financial crisis; policymakers contained the immediate spillover, but the property downturn continued.

Systemic vulnerabilities building with hindsight included overstretched asset valuations, inflation persistence, high public debt, underpriced geopolitical risk in Europe, and excessive reliance on global supply chains with little redundancy. The Russia-Ukraine war was not a 2021 economic event, but late-2021 military mobilization made geopolitical risk knowable. The scale of the 2022 shock is hindsight.

Policy mistakes are debated. One view is that advanced-economy fiscal and monetary policy stayed too loose for too long, allowing inflation to broaden. Another view is that policymakers faced genuine uncertainty and prioritized employment recovery after an unprecedented shock. The stronger consensus is that supply constraints were underestimated and that communication around “transitory” inflation became too rigid.

### 8. YEAR-OVER-YEAR DELTA (2021 vs. 2020)

The biggest shift from 2020 to 2021 was from collapse to rebound. Global GDP moved from contraction to strong growth, world trade from decline to rapid expansion, and oil from crisis pricing to near-normal levels. Corporate profits recovered, IPO activity surged, and capital markets were more willing to finance risk.

The second shift was from disinflation fear to inflation risk. In 2020, the dominant concern was deflationary demand collapse. In 2021, the concern became supply-constrained demand, energy scarcity, and wage-price dynamics in selected sectors. This changed the future path of central-bank policy.

The third shift was from public-health restriction as the main economic constraint to supply-chain and labor-market friction as the main constraint. Lockdowns still mattered, but shortages of chips, containers, workers, energy, and housing became increasingly central.

The fourth shift was geopolitical and structural. China’s policy stance turned more restrictive toward property and platforms, while the US, Europe, Japan, and Korea moved toward industrial policy for semiconductors and strategic supply chains. The world economy in 2021 was more state-directed than the pre-pandemic economy.

### 9. DATA CONFIDENCE & GAPS

Data confidence is highest for nominal GDP, population, CPI, and broad fiscal variables in advanced economies because national statistical offices, the IMF, OECD, and World Bank provide consistent series. Confidence is medium for emerging markets where revisions, informality, exchange-rate swings, and fiscal definitions create larger uncertainty. China’s GDP and unemployment data are official, but labor-market indicators are less comparable to OECD ILO-style measures. India’s unemployment figures vary substantially across official surveys, CMIE-style private surveys, and ILO modeled estimates, so this report avoids a single definitive number where comparability is weak.

Government debt and budget balances are especially sensitive to definitions: central government, general government, public sector, and augmented balances can differ materially. This matters for China, India, Mexico, Brazil, and Saudi Arabia. Exchange-rate conversions also affect rankings. Current-dollar GDP rankings are not the same as PPP rankings, and 2021 nominal rankings reflect currency moves as well as real output.

The weakest evidence in this document concerns cross-country unemployment comparability, some policy-rate comparisons where central-bank operating frameworks differ, and sector-level global performance where comprehensive annual data are not uniformly available. Corporate rankings depend on whether the metric is revenue, market capitalization, profit, or assets. For private firms and state-owned enterprises, transparency varies.

Important data gaps include timely global small-business failures, informal employment damage, learning loss translated into future productivity, distribution of excess savings by income class outside advanced economies, and consistent cross-country commercial real-estate stress. Some 2021 outcomes were revised materially after the year closed, so this document uses rounded values and flags approximations rather than implying false precision.
