# MACRO/MICRO GLOBAL ECONOMIC ASSESSMENT - YEAR: 1926

### 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

1926 was a late-interwar expansion year, but not a simple boom year. The world economy was recovering from the First World War, postwar inflation, debt overhangs, and trade disruption, while also rebuilding an international monetary order around the gold-exchange standard. The United States was far ahead as the largest economy; China and India had huge aggregate scale but much lower GDP per capita; Germany, the United Kingdom, the Former USSR, France, Japan, and Italy formed the next industrial tier. The year also exposed the tension between monetary stabilization and real-sector adjustment.

The dominant macroeconomic force was the return to currency discipline. Britain had returned to gold in 1925 at the prewar parity, a decision that weighed heavily on coal, textiles, shipbuilding, and export regions. France entered a dramatic stabilization under Poincare in 1926, while Italy announced the lira revaluation policy known as Quota 90. Germany continued operating under the Dawes Plan stabilization framework. These choices reduced currency chaos but often shifted adjustment costs onto wages, employment, exports, and debtor balance sheets.

Microeconomically, 1926 was defined by divergent sectoral experiences. The United States saw consumer durables, automobiles, electric utilities, roads, radios, and credit-linked retailing expand. Britain suffered coal conflict and a general strike. Japan looked stable in GDP terms but carried banking fragility into the 1927 Showa financial crisis. The Soviet Union was still in NEP recovery, with state industry and market agriculture coexisting. China was fragmented by the Warlord Era and the Northern Expedition, making any aggregate GDP estimate unusually uncertain.

The year therefore sits at an inflection point. It was close enough to 1929 that historians can see vulnerabilities building: commodity dependence, gold-standard rigidity, external-debt dependence, bank balance-sheet weakness, and rising equity-market speculation. But it was also early enough that many contemporaries saw stabilization as success. This document treats hindsight carefully: risks that later mattered are labeled as such, rather than presented as obvious to policymakers in 1926.

### 2. KEY THEMES OF 1926

**1. Gold-exchange-standard reconstruction and its costs.** The interwar monetary order was moving from postwar floating and inflation toward renewed gold links. The economic significance was high: stabilization lowered currency risk and helped revive international lending, but it also made domestic adjustment harder for countries that stabilized at overvalued exchange rates. Britain was the clearest case in 1926, while France and Italy moved through stabilization in different directions. The forward link is direct: gold-standard rigidity later transmitted and amplified the Great Depression.

**2. The United States as the global demand and credit anchor.** The U.S. economy was much larger than any rival and combined mass production with deep financial markets. Automobiles, construction, utilities, and consumer credit made the American expansion more microeconomically modern than most of Europe. The forward link is also important: U.S. credit conditions and securities-market psychology later mattered for global capital flows and the 1929 shock.

**3. Postwar European stabilization remained uneven.** Germany, France, Belgium, Italy, Poland, and Czechoslovakia were all still dealing with the consequences of war finance, border changes, reparations, inflation, and debt. Growth could be positive while balance sheets remained fragile. The countries most affected were Germany through foreign-credit dependence, France through public-debt stabilization, Italy through exchange-rate revaluation, and Poland through state-building and currency stabilization.

**4. Labor conflict as macro adjustment.** The British General Strike and coal lockout showed how exchange-rate policy, global coal competition, productivity problems, and wage bargaining could combine into a national economic crisis. The theme is not just a labor event; it is a mechanism by which deflationary policy was transmitted to households and regions. The forward link was the 1927 Trade Disputes and Trade Unions Act and the continuing decline of older British staple industries.

**5. Commodity-export economies looked healthy but concentrated.** Argentina, Canada, Australia, Brazil, Indonesia, Mexico, and parts of India depended heavily on agricultural or extractive exports. In 1926, many benefited from trade normalization and capital flows. With hindsight, their vulnerability was concentration: a downturn in commodity prices or external credit would quickly affect fiscal revenue, exchange rates, and employment.

**6. Non-market and politically fragmented economies are difficult to measure.** The Former USSR, China, and many colonial economies are not comparable to market economies with modern national accounts. MPD estimates are valuable, but China requires interpolation for 1926 in the source worksheet used here, and Former USSR output is a former-country estimate for a non-market economy. The forward link is methodological: these data should be used for scale and direction, not precision.

### 3. MACROECONOMIC OVERVIEW

**3A. Global Aggregates:**

World GDP for 1926 in a fully comparable modern national-account sense is not directly available from official contemporary sources. MPD/OWID long-run series provide the best available framework, but the country-level screen in this file is built from GDP per capita times population in 2011 international dollars and includes interpolation where MPD has benchmark rather than annual GDP per capita. World growth in 1926 is therefore best described qualitatively as continued postwar recovery with large country variation, rather than as a single high-confidence statistic.

Global trade continued to normalize after the disruptions of war and the early-1920s inflation-stabilization cycle. League of Nations and later UN historical trade compilations are the main contemporary-style sources, but coverage and valuation differ by country. Reserve-currency dynamics were dominated by sterling and the U.S. dollar, with the dollar gaining importance as New York money markets deepened and the United States held large gold reserves. Inflation trends varied: some countries were stabilizing from postwar inflation, while others were under deflationary pressure from gold-standard restoration.

Commodity markets were mixed. Gold's monetary price was fixed in gold-standard currencies such as the U.S. dollar. Oil production expanded in the United States, Mexico, the Soviet sphere, and colonial fields, while petroleum's long-run importance rose as transport and industry shifted gradually from coal. Copper, wheat, coffee, rubber, sugar, wool, and other primary commodities mattered heavily to exporters, but exact globally harmonized 1926 price series are not used here. International capital flowed mainly from the United States and other financial centers toward Europe, Latin America, and settler economies, although country access varied with credibility and politics.

**3B. Monetary & Financial Conditions:**

The central financial fact of 1926 was the gold-exchange-standard environment. The United States operated as a gold-standard reserve center. Britain had restored sterling's prewar gold parity in 1925, putting pressure on export industries. France stabilized after the Poincare crisis, and Italy moved toward Quota 90. Germany's Reichsmark stabilization and Dawes Plan framework supported a return of foreign lending. Japan had not yet resolved its banking weaknesses, which would erupt in 1927.

Interest-rate comparisons are difficult because central-bank operating frameworks differed and policy rates were often discount rates, not modern overnight targets. Comprehensive harmonized 1926 policy-rate and yield-curve tables are data not available in this document. Stock-market data are also uneven: the Dow Jones Industrial Average ended 1926 almost flat in price terms after a strong 1925, while broader U.S. equity datasets generally start in 1926 or later. London and other exchanges existed and were important, but comparable annual index levels are not used here unless specifically reconstructed by later scholarship.

**3C. Trade & Geopolitics:**

Trade was shaped by the postwar treaty system, reparations, tariffs, colonial preferences, and the restoration of monetary stability. Germany's admission to the League of Nations in 1926 improved diplomatic normalization, while the Dawes Plan continued to structure reparations and international lending. China entered the Northern Expedition, which affected fiscal control, railways, military finance, and treaty-port commerce. Colonial economies such as India, Indonesia, and parts of Africa were deeply integrated into imperial trade networks, but without sovereign macro policy.

Regional integration in the modern post-1945 sense did not yet exist. There was no EEC, Bretton Woods system, IMF, World Bank, ASEAN, or WTO. The relevant institutions were the League of Nations, central banks, imperial administrations, reparations agencies, and private financial houses. Bilateral tensions were often about exchange rates, debts, reparations, tariffs, and colonial trade rather than modern trade agreements.

**3D. Commodities & Energy:**

Coal remained the central industrial fuel, but oil's strategic and commercial importance was rising. The British coal crisis was both a sectoral and macroeconomic event. Oil production and refining supported the growth of automobiles, shipping, and military logistics, but OPEC did not exist. Metals such as copper, iron ore, steel inputs, and tin mattered for construction, electrification, and manufacturing. Agricultural staples shaped welfare in low-income economies and export earnings in countries such as Canada, Argentina, Australia, Brazil, India, and Indonesia.

**3E. Labor & Demographics:**

Global employment statistics for 1926 are not comparable. Most countries lacked modern labor-force surveys, and unemployment definitions differed sharply. The most visible labor event was Britain's General Strike and coal lockout. Demographically, high-population, low-income economies such as China and India remained enormous in aggregate GDP because of population scale, while settler economies such as the United States, Canada, Australia, and Argentina had much higher GDP per person. Urbanization, migration, and mechanization were slowly reshaping labor demand, especially in North America, Western Europe, and Japan.

### 6. FINANCIAL MARKETS

**6A. Equity markets:**

The New York Stock Exchange was the most important equity market for the emerging late-1920s boom. The Dow Jones Industrial Average ended 1926 at about 157.20, almost unchanged from 1925 in price terms. This makes 1926 a transition year: 1925 had been strong, 1926 was flatter, and the famous acceleration came later, especially 1927-1929. CRSP-style broad U.S. equity return datasets begin around this period, which is useful for modern finance but does not solve global comparability.

London remained a major securities market, especially for domestic, imperial, and foreign securities, but broad annual index levels are reconstructed by later scholarship rather than supplied by a single modern official series. Paris, Berlin, Amsterdam, Brussels, Milan, Tokyo, Toronto, Buenos Aires, and other exchanges existed, but comparable annual return data are data not available in this document. Sector rotation was strongest toward growth narratives: utilities, autos, radio, chemicals, and financials in advanced markets, while commodity shares depended on export prices.

**6B. Fixed income:**

Government bond yield curves in the modern comparable sense are data not available. The main fixed-income story was stabilization and credibility. France's debt and bill market was central to the Poincare stabilization. Germany's access to foreign lending remained tied to the Dawes Plan. Britain defended gold parity through monetary orthodoxy. U.S. capital markets supplied a growing share of international credit. Sovereign debt risk was not absent; it was embedded in war debts, reparations, stabilization loans, and fiscal credibility.

**6C. Currencies:**

The currency system was the gold-exchange standard, not Bretton Woods and not a modern managed-float system. Sterling and the dollar were central reserve currencies, with the dollar gaining importance. France stabilized the franc in 1926 after severe pressure. Italy announced the Quota 90 lira policy. Germany's Reichsmark stabilization held. Countries outside the core often managed pegs, exchange controls, or colonial currency arrangements. Exact annual average exchange rates for all top-20 countries are data not available in this document.

**6D. Alternative assets:**

Gold was a monetary anchor rather than a freely floating alternative asset in the modern sense. U.S. statutory gold parity was fixed at $20.67 per fine ounce. Real estate markets varied: U.S. construction was active, while many European markets were still affected by postwar finance and rent controls. Commodity-linked assets mattered greatly in Argentina, Canada, Australia, Brazil, Indonesia, Mexico, and colonial economies. Comparable global real-estate price series are data not available.

### 7. RISKS, CRISES & BLACK SWANS

The active crisis of 1926 was the British coal and labor crisis. It was not a financial panic, but it was systemic for the United Kingdom because it combined export competitiveness, gold parity, wage bargaining, coal productivity, transport, and public order. The General Strike ended quickly, but the miners' dispute continued and left lasting effects on labor law and coal communities.

France faced a currency and debt-confidence crisis that stabilized after Poincare's return. Italy chose a politically powerful but economically restrictive lira policy. Japan carried unresolved bank and corporate balance-sheet problems into 1927. Germany's recovery depended heavily on foreign capital. The Former USSR's NEP recovery masked political conflict over grain procurement and industrialization. China faced military and fiscal fragmentation. Commodity exporters depended on global demand and credit.

Hindsight-only vulnerabilities were also accumulating. The U.S. was not yet in the final speculative surge, but consumer credit, securities-market enthusiasm, and sectoral imbalances were already visible. Gold-standard restoration looked like stabilization in 1926, but with hindsight it reduced flexibility in the face of later shocks. Commodity-export concentration looked manageable in a growing world economy, but became dangerous when prices and credit fell after 1929.

### 8. YEAR-OVER-YEAR DELTA (1926 vs. 1925)

The largest structural change from 1925 to 1926 was the shift from postwar monetary debate toward active stabilization. Britain had already returned to gold in 1925 and experienced the real-sector consequences in 1926. France moved from currency crisis to stabilization. Italy announced Quota 90. Germany continued under Dawes Plan stabilization. This made 1926 a year of restored financial order but also of deflationary and distributional pressure.

The top-20 ranking changed internally but not in membership. The United Kingdom fell from third to fifth in the MPD/interpolation screen because output contracted. India and Germany moved ahead of it. The Former USSR grew rapidly from a lower postwar recovery base. The United States widened its lead over every other economy. Commodity exporters such as Canada, Argentina, Mexico, Indonesia, and the Netherlands showed strong or solid growth.

New risks emerged in 1926. Britain's labor conflict showed how gold parity could turn sectoral weakness into national crisis. Japan's unresolved banking weaknesses were close to eruption. France's stabilization created a strong franc regime that later affected gold flows and international adjustment. U.S. financial markets remained orderly, but the ingredients of late-1920s speculative finance became more visible.

### 9. DATA CONFIDENCE & GAPS

Data confidence is highest for the GDPpc and population values directly observed in MPD 2023 and cross-described by OWID. It is lower for countries where MPD has benchmark rather than annual GDP per capita data. In the top 20, China and Poland use log-linear interpolation for GDP per capita; China is especially uncertain because political fragmentation and weak national statistics make a precise 1926 GDP concept impossible. Former USSR is a former-country and non-market estimate; uncertainty should be treated as at least plus or minus 20-30 percent for broad comparisons.

Unemployment, CPI inflation, policy rates, government debt/GDP, current account balances, and budget balances are not presented as comparable numerical series because definitions, coverage, and accounting conventions varied widely. Some national or central-bank figures exist for specific countries, but harmonizing them across the top 20 would require a separate statistical appendix. Where no harmonized figure was used, the tables state Data not available.

Historical GDP rankings are also sensitive to territorial definitions. MPD country entities are not the same as empires, colonies, customs unions, or modern sovereign states. British India, China, the Dutch East Indies, the Former USSR, and former-country series such as Czechoslovakia and Yugoslavia require special caution. The ranking should be read as a real-PPP country/entity scale estimate, not as a nominal-dollar list and not as a list of fully sovereign modern states.

The weakest claims in this document are those involving global commodity price levels, cross-country financial-market returns outside the United States and United Kingdom, and precise macro indicators for colonial or non-market economies. The strongest claims are the qualitative identification of monetary stabilization, the British coal/general strike crisis, the Poincare stabilization, the Quota 90 announcement, the Soviet NEP recovery context, and the broad GDP scale ranking derived from MPD/OWID methodology.
