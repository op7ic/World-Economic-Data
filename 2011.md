### 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

2011 was the year when the post-2008 recovery lost momentum and became visibly uneven. Global activity was still expanding, but the recovery was no longer synchronized. The United States grew slowly while unemployment remained high, Japan was hit by the Great East Japan Earthquake and Fukushima nuclear accident, and the euro area moved from a sovereign-debt problem on the periphery to a systemic bank-sovereign confidence crisis. Emerging economies, especially China, India, Indonesia, Turkey, Brazil, Russia, and Saudi Arabia, continued to carry a large share of world growth, but many of them faced inflation, credit tightening, current account pressure, or commodity volatility.

The defining macroeconomic tension was between weak demand in advanced economies and overheating or commodity-driven inflation in several emerging markets. The Federal Reserve and Bank of Japan stayed at the effective lower bound, the Bank of England expanded asset purchases, and the ECB first raised rates in April and July, then reversed course in November and December as financial stress rose. Fiscal policy shifted from crisis stimulus toward consolidation in much of Europe and in several advanced economies, even though labor markets had not fully repaired.

Commodity markets were central. Brent crude averaged above $100 per barrel for the first time in a full year, supported by emerging-market demand and supply disruption from Libya. Food and metals prices were high early in the year, intensifying inflation and social stress in import-dependent economies. Gold reached record territory as investors sought protection against financial instability, currency concerns, and negative real yields. These price movements transferred income toward oil exporters such as Saudi Arabia and Russia while squeezing consumers and trade balances in energy importers such as India and Turkey.

At the micro level, 2011 exposed the fragility of global production networks. Japan's earthquake and Thailand's floods disrupted autos, electronics, and hard-disk-drive supply chains. At the same time, the corporate landscape tilted further toward energy, commodities, Chinese state-owned enterprises, and digital platforms. Walmart remained the largest company by revenue, but oil majors and Chinese energy and grid firms dominated the top global revenue list. Glencore's large IPO, LinkedIn's public listing, Groupon and Zynga IPOs, Microsoft's Skype acquisition, and Google's announced Motorola Mobility purchase showed how capital markets were still funding platform and technology stories even as broad equity markets were under stress.

### 2. KEY THEMES OF 2011

**1. The euro-area sovereign-bank crisis became systemic.** The crisis that began with Greece in 2010 broadened in 2011 to Italy, Spain, banks with sovereign exposure, and the institutional design of the monetary union. Rising sovereign spreads, bank funding pressure, ratings downgrades, and emergency summits made the euro area the main global tail risk. Germany, France, Italy, Spain, and the Netherlands were directly affected, while U.S., U.K., Swiss, and Asian markets reacted through funding, exports, and safe-haven flows. The forward linkage was clear: the December 2011 ECB long-term refinancing operation was a bridge to 2012's more decisive policy response, including the later "whatever it takes" commitment.

**2. Advanced-economy monetary policy diverged sharply.** The Federal Reserve held the federal funds target at 0 to 0.25 percent and launched a $400 billion maturity extension program. The Bank of England kept Bank Rate at 0.5 percent and expanded asset purchases to GBP 275 billion. The Bank of Japan maintained a virtually zero overnight call rate and asset purchases after the earthquake. In contrast, the ECB tightened twice before reversing course late in the year. This showed the difficulty of calibrating policy when headline inflation was energy-driven but demand was weak.

**3. Emerging-market growth stayed strong but became less comfortable.** China grew near 10 percent but tightened credit to fight inflation and property risk. India had high inflation, a widening current account deficit, and aggressive rate increases. Brazil slowed sharply after 2010's surge, Russia benefited from oil, Indonesia remained resilient, and Turkey posted extremely rapid growth with a very large current account deficit. The theme matters because 2011 marked a transition from the simple "emerging-market decoupling" story to a more nuanced view of credit cycles, external balances, and policy constraints.

**4. Commodity and food prices linked geopolitics to inflation and distribution.** Oil, food, and metals were not just market variables. They affected household purchasing power, fiscal subsidies, external balances, and political stability. The Arab Spring and Libya's civil conflict reduced oil supply and changed risk premia. Energy exporters received windfalls, while energy importers faced inflation and weaker balances. The later years showed how commodity cycles could amplify both fiscal positions and political pressure.

**5. Global supply chains proved efficient but fragile.** The Japanese earthquake temporarily reduced output in autos, electronics, power equipment, and components. Thailand's floods later disrupted hard-disk-drive and electronics production. These shocks were not merely local disasters; they changed inventories, production scheduling, and corporate risk management across North America, Europe, and Asia. With hindsight, 2011 was an early warning about supply-chain concentration that became much more visible in the 2020s.

**6. Fiscal consolidation collided with labor-market scars.** Advanced economies entered 2011 with high public debt, high unemployment, and weak private demand. Europe moved toward austerity under market pressure, while the U.S. debt-ceiling crisis and sovereign-rating downgrade highlighted political limits to fiscal management. Labor-market recovery lagged output recovery, especially in the United States, Spain, Italy, and the United Kingdom. This shaped the subsequent decade's debate over secular stagnation, hysteresis, and fiscal multipliers.

**7. Reserve-currency and safe-haven dynamics intensified.** The U.S. dollar remained the dominant reserve currency, but safe-haven flows also strengthened the yen and Swiss franc. The Swiss National Bank responded to extreme appreciation pressure by setting a minimum EUR/CHF exchange rate of 1.20 in September. The renminbi continued gradual managed appreciation but was still far from full reserve-currency status. The pattern showed that crisis-era capital flows could overwhelm small open economies and complicate monetary autonomy.

### 3. MACROECONOMIC OVERVIEW

#### 3A. Global Aggregates

World GDP measured at market exchange rates was about USD 70 trillion in 2011, while the World Bank's 2011 International Comparison Program put PPP-based world GDP at about USD 90.6 trillion. IMF-style PPP-weighted real world output growth was about 3.9 percent, slower than 2010's rebound. WTO calculations using market-exchange-rate output showed global output growth of about 2.4 percent, paired with a sharp deceleration in merchandise trade volume growth to 5.0 percent from 13.8 percent in 2010. The difference between PPP-weighted and market-exchange-rate measures is important: emerging economies carried more weight in PPP measures and therefore made global growth look stronger than dollar-converted advanced-economy measures.

Global trade still expanded, but the pattern shifted from rebound to slowdown. Merchandise export values rose strongly because commodity prices were high, but volumes decelerated. Europe was the weakest major demand center, while Asia remained the largest source of production momentum. International capital flows were split between yield-seeking flows to emerging markets and risk-off flows to U.S. Treasuries, German Bunds, JGBs, the yen, and the Swiss franc. By late 2011, euro-area stress encouraged bank deleveraging and repatriation, pressuring cross-border credit.

Inflation rose in many countries because of energy and food. Advanced-economy inflation was often above central-bank targets, but policymakers increasingly treated it as a supply-price shock rather than a sign of excess domestic demand. Emerging-market inflation was more difficult because food and fuel had larger household-budget weights and because credit growth was stronger.

Key commodity prices: Brent crude averaged roughly USD 111 per barrel, WTI roughly USD 95 per barrel, and the World Bank's average crude oil index was around USD 104 per barrel. Gold averaged roughly USD 1,570 per troy ounce and reached record nominal highs during the year. Copper traded near record levels early in 2011 and averaged around USD 4 per pound before falling with global growth fears. Grain and food prices stayed elevated after the 2010-2011 food price surge, with country impacts depending on subsidies, exchange rates, harvests, and import dependence.

#### 3B. Monetary & Financial Conditions

The U.S. maintained a zero-rate stance. The Federal Reserve kept the federal funds target at 0 to 0.25 percent and announced the maturity extension program, buying longer-term Treasuries and selling or redeeming shorter maturities to put downward pressure on long yields without expanding the balance sheet by the same amount as QE2. The 10-year Treasury yield fell sharply during the year as growth concerns and safe-haven demand rose.

The euro area had the most consequential policy reversal. The ECB raised the main refinancing rate from 1.00 percent to 1.25 percent in April and to 1.50 percent in July. As the sovereign-bank crisis worsened, it cut to 1.25 percent in November and 1.00 percent in December. The late-2011 three-year LTRO addressed bank funding stress but did not immediately solve sovereign solvency concerns.

The Bank of England held Bank Rate at 0.5 percent and expanded asset purchases by GBP 75 billion to GBP 275 billion in October. The Bank of Japan kept the overnight call-rate target around 0 to 0.1 percent and used asset purchases and liquidity operations to stabilize markets after the earthquake. China, India, Brazil, Korea, and several other emerging economies tightened earlier in the year to contain inflation, though Brazil began easing before year-end as growth slowed.

FX markets reflected stress and policy intervention. The yen and Swiss franc strengthened on safe-haven flows; Japan intervened against yen appreciation, and Switzerland imposed the EUR/CHF floor. The euro was volatile, ending the year weaker as sovereign stress deepened. The renminbi appreciated gradually under management. Oil exporters generally benefited from higher terms of trade, while Turkey and India came under pressure from external deficits.

Equity markets were bifurcated. U.S. large-cap indices were roughly flat to modestly positive by year-end after a highly volatile summer. European and many emerging-market indices fell sharply, especially banks, peripheral Europe, commodity-sensitive names, and markets exposed to global risk aversion. Bond markets showed a similar split: safe sovereigns rallied, while Italian, Spanish, and other stressed sovereign yields rose.

#### 3C. Trade & Geopolitics

The WTO-centered trading system remained open, but trade momentum slowed. Regional agreements continued: the EU-South Korea Free Trade Agreement provisionally applied from July 2011, and the United States approved free trade agreements with Korea, Colombia, and Panama. Trade disputes continued around aircraft, autos, steel, solar panels, exchange-rate policy, and market access. China-U.S. economic tension centered on currency, trade balances, intellectual property, and industrial policy.

The Arab Spring changed risk perceptions across North Africa and the Middle East. Libya's conflict removed a large volume of light sweet crude from the market before production recovery later in the year. Egypt, Tunisia, Syria, and other countries saw tourism, investment, subsidy, and fiscal pressures. South Sudan became independent in July 2011, creating new oil-revenue and pipeline disputes with Sudan.

The euro crisis became the main geopolitical-economic risk for trade and finance. It reduced confidence, raised funding stress, and threatened the global banking network. Emerging Asia watched Europe as a key export market; commodity exporters watched Europe as a demand and financial risk channel.

#### 3D. Commodities & Energy

Oil was the central commodity story. Brent's first full-year average above USD 100 per barrel reflected demand from emerging economies, Libya's supply shock, low spare capacity concerns, and financial risk premia. The Brent-WTI spread widened because North American transport bottlenecks, especially around Cushing, weakened WTI relative to seaborne crude. Saudi Arabia and other producers increased output to stabilize markets, while the IEA made an emergency oil stock release to offset Libyan disruption.

Metals prices were strong early in the year, then weakened as China tightened credit and European stress hit industrial-demand expectations. Copper was a bellwether because it linked Chinese construction, power-grid investment, electronics, and global manufacturing. Gold rose as real rates stayed low and crisis hedging intensified.

Agricultural markets remained sensitive after the 2010-2011 food price spike. Weather, export restrictions, energy prices, and low stocks affected wheat, maize, sugar, cotton, and other staples. Food-importing countries with large subsidy systems faced fiscal pressure. Poorer households were hit hardest because food occupied a high share of consumption.

#### 3E. Labor & Demographics

Labor-market recovery lagged output recovery in advanced economies. U.S. unemployment declined but remained near 9 percent, with long-term unemployment unusually high. Spain's unemployment exceeded 20 percent, youth unemployment was severe, and Italy also faced labor-market weakness. Germany was the main advanced-economy exception, benefiting from prior labor reforms, export strength, and short-time work legacies.

Emerging economies absorbed labor through construction, manufacturing, services, and informal sectors, but inflation cut real-income gains for households. China continued massive urbanization and manufacturing upgrading. India had a large youth cohort but faced persistent underemployment and inflation. Gulf economies combined hydrocarbon revenue with high expatriate labor dependence and limited private employment opportunities for nationals.

The United States expanded, but 2011 felt like a weak recovery rather than normalization. Household deleveraging, depressed housing construction, state and local fiscal cuts, and high long-term unemployment weighed on demand. Monetary policy was highly accommodative. The Federal Reserve shifted toward forward guidance and maturity extension after QE2 ended. Corporate profits were strong, large firms held cash, and technology and energy sectors were resilient, but small-business credit and housing remained constrained.

**Micro/sectoral highlights:** Autos recovered from the crisis period but supply chains were hit by Japan-related component shortages. Shale oil and gas investment expanded. Technology platforms, smartphones, cloud services, and social media attracted capital. Housing remained the weakest major sector, while household debt service improved gradually.

#### 5B. Corporate landscape

The largest companies by revenue were dominated by retail and energy. Walmart ranked first, followed by Shell, Exxon Mobil, BP, Sinopec, CNPC, State Grid, Toyota, Japan Post, and Chevron. This composition shows the 2011 economy's dependence on oil, logistics, mass retail, and state-linked Chinese infrastructure and energy groups.

Major corporate events reflected both crisis and transformation. Glencore completed a roughly USD 10 billion dual listing in London and Hong Kong, revealing the scale of commodity trading and mining finance. LinkedIn became the first prominent U.S. social-networking company to go public, while Groupon and Zynga also tested investor demand for internet platform models. Microsoft's acquisition of Skype and Google's announced acquisition of Motorola Mobility showed that software, mobile, patents, and communications platforms were converging.

Financial stress created failures and rescues. MF Global collapsed after European sovereign-debt exposure and liquidity problems. Dexia required rescue and restructuring. Several European banks raised capital, sold assets, or reduced cross-border balance sheets. In autos, Saab filed for bankruptcy, while large global carmakers dealt with Japan supply disruptions and demand shifts.

#### 5C. Innovation & technology

The smartphone and tablet ecosystem moved from novelty to central growth engine. Apple's iPhone and iPad, Android devices, mobile app stores, and cloud services reshaped consumer electronics, advertising, telecom data demand, and semiconductor supply chains. Social media and professional networks attracted public-market capital even amid broader risk aversion.

Industrial innovation was also important. Electric vehicles and batteries gained attention, but commercialization was uneven. Solar power costs fell, yet some firms failed under competitive pressure, including high-profile U.S. clean-tech failures. In energy, shale oil and gas drilling continued to change North American supply expectations. In logistics, the Japan and Thailand shocks encouraged firms to rethink lean inventories and single-source supplier risk.

#### 5D. Labor markets & human capital

Advanced economies had a large employment scar from the 2008-2009 crisis. Long-term unemployment rose in the United States and parts of Europe, while Spain had mass unemployment. Germany's labor market outperformed due to export strength, prior reforms, and firm-level employment practices.

Emerging economies saw more job creation, but often in informal or low-productivity services. China continued moving workers from rural to urban employment, though wages rose in coastal manufacturing. India faced a mismatch between high-potential demographics and infrastructure, education, and formal-job constraints. Gulf economies continued to rely on expatriate labor while trying to raise national employment.

#### 6B. Fixed income

Safe sovereign yields fell. The U.S. 10-year Treasury yield ended near 1.9 percent, far below early-year levels. German Bund yields also fell as investors sought euro-area safety. Japanese government bond yields remained near 1 percent. In contrast, Italian and Spanish yields rose sharply, with Italy briefly trading around or above levels considered dangerous for debt sustainability. Credit spreads widened in European financials and high-yield markets during the summer and autumn.

Sovereign-debt events were central: Greece's restructuring negotiations, Portugal's EU-IMF assistance program, Italian and Spanish spread widening, and euro-area rescue-fund debates. The United States experienced a political default scare during the debt-ceiling episode, but market pricing treated Treasuries as safe assets.

#### 6C. Currencies

The dollar remained the dominant reserve currency, but 2011 was not a simple dollar-strength story. The yen and Swiss franc strengthened because of safe-haven flows. The Swiss franc's strength became so disruptive that the SNB set a minimum exchange rate against the euro. The euro weakened late in the year as sovereign stress rose. Commodity currencies such as the Australian and Canadian dollars were strong for much of the year but volatile. The Turkish lira and Indian rupee weakened under external deficits and risk aversion. The renminbi appreciated gradually under management.

Capital controls and macroprudential tools were part of the policy toolkit in several emerging markets, especially where inflows, inflation, and currency strength complicated monetary policy. Brazil used measures to manage inflows and credit; China relied on reserve requirements, administrative credit guidance, and managed exchange-rate policy.

#### 6D. Alternative assets

Gold was the standout alternative asset, rising to record nominal levels during the crisis. Its drivers included negative real interest rates, sovereign-risk concerns, currency uncertainty, and investor demand for crisis hedges. Real estate was split: U.S. housing remained depressed, Spain and parts of Europe were weak, China showed signs of policy-induced cooling, Canada and Australia were watched for valuation risk, and prime London and Swiss property benefited from safe-haven and global capital flows.

Commodity-linked assets were volatile. Oil stayed high, copper weakened after early strength, and agricultural commodities remained sensitive to weather and policy. The year showed how alternative assets could behave both as inflation hedges and as risk assets depending on the shock.

### 7. RISKS, CRISES & BLACK SWANS

The euro-area crisis was the main active systemic risk. The immediate danger was a self-reinforcing loop: sovereign bond losses weakened banks; weak banks increased potential fiscal costs; rising fiscal costs weakened sovereigns. Italy's market size made the risk global. The ECB's late-year liquidity operations reduced acute bank funding stress but did not fully remove solvency concerns.

Japan's earthquake, tsunami, and Fukushima accident were major real-economy shocks. The disaster damaged local capital stock, disrupted power supply, and transmitted through global supply chains. The shift away from nuclear generation increased fossil-fuel imports and changed Japan's energy balance.

The Arab Spring and Libya's oil shock were both geopolitical and economic. Oil prices, tourism revenue, subsidies, fiscal balances, and investor confidence changed across the region. The social background included food-price pressure, unemployment, governance grievances, and distributional stress.

The U.S. debt-ceiling crisis was a near-miss. It did not create a Treasury funding crisis, but it damaged confidence and highlighted the role of political risk in advanced-economy sovereigns. MF Global's failure showed how sovereign-risk trades and weak liquidity management could become a firm-level collapse.

Hindsight risks visible in 2011 included China's local-government debt and property cycle, Turkey's external-financing vulnerability, the persistence of advanced-economy labor-market scarring, and the long-term fragility of just-in-time supply chains. These were not all fully priced at the time.

### 8. YEAR-OVER-YEAR DELTA (2011 vs. 2010)

The world moved from rebound to slowdown. Merchandise trade volume growth fell from 13.8 percent in 2010 to 5.0 percent in 2011. Global output growth slowed, especially in advanced economies. The 2010 narrative of synchronized recovery gave way to a 2011 narrative of uneven growth and financial fragility.

The euro crisis changed in kind, not just degree. In 2010, markets focused on Greece, Ireland, and Portugal. In 2011, Italy and Spain came under pressure, and bank-sovereign feedback became the central concern. The issue moved from fiscal rescue programs to the institutional survival of the monetary union.

Policy regimes shifted. The Fed moved from QE2 to maturity extension and stronger forward guidance. The Bank of England returned to larger asset purchases. The ECB's 2011 tightening was reversed within the same year. Emerging markets shifted from tightening against inflation to worrying about slowdown and capital-flow reversals.

Commodity dynamics changed household and fiscal conditions. Oil and food prices were higher than in 2010, supporting exporters but pressuring importers. Saudi Arabia and Russia improved fiscal and external positions; India and Turkey faced larger external stress. Brazil's 2010 boom slowed as policy tightening and global risk weighed on activity.

The balance of economic power continued shifting toward emerging markets, but with more visible vulnerabilities. China, India, Brazil, Indonesia, Turkey, and Russia were large enough to shape global demand and commodity prices, yet 2011 showed that inflation, credit, external balances, and governance constraints mattered as much as headline growth.

### 9. DATA CONFIDENCE & GAPS

High-confidence statistical systems include the United States, Canada, Japan, Australia, South Korea, Switzerland, the United Kingdom, and most euro-area members. National accounts, CPI, unemployment, fiscal, and external-balance data are generally robust, though revisions still occur.

Medium-confidence areas include China, India, Brazil, Russia, Indonesia, Turkey, Mexico, and Saudi Arabia. These economies have official data systems, but comparability problems are larger. China's official government debt understates broad public-sector and local-financing risks. India's unemployment and informal-sector data are not directly comparable with OECD labor-market data. Saudi labor-market statistics differ sharply depending on whether expatriate workers, total labor force, or nationals are considered.

GDP ranking confidence is high for broad order but not for false precision. Current-dollar GDP rankings depend on exchange rates and later data revisions. WDI-derived sources used for this document show slight discrepancies for the United Kingdom and South Korea, so the figures are rounded and the conflict is flagged. PPP rankings would be materially different and would move China and India higher.

Weakest evidence in this document: exact year-over-year changes for fiscal, debt, and current-account indicators in some countries; comprehensive stock-exchange annual returns for every exchange existing in 2011; real-estate price conditions outside major economies; and private or state-owned corporate financial details in countries where disclosure was limited. For those items, the document uses cautious estimates or states "Data not available".

Important data that do not exist in a clean comparable form for 2011 include globally standardized unemployment for all top-20 countries, comparable household debt across all countries, comprehensive local-government contingent liabilities in China, comparable fiscal subsidy burdens across oil-importing emerging markets, and complete corporate-sector balance-sheet data for private firms and state-owned enterprises.
