# MACRO/MICRO GLOBAL ECONOMIC ASSESSMENT - YEAR: 1921

### 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

1921 was defined by the global aftershock of World War I: a violent transition from wartime inflation and shortages to peacetime deflation, inventory liquidation, falling commodity prices, and labor-market stress. The downturn was most visible in the United States, the United Kingdom, Canada, and other commodity-linked economies, but the adjustment was global in scope. The U.S. recession reached its trough in July 1921 under the NBER chronology, while Britain faced a severe unemployment crisis and continental Europe struggled with reparations, reconstruction, currencies, and fiscal exhaustion.

The year was not only a recession year. It was also a policy-regime transition. Central banks that had tightened after the wartime inflation began to ease as prices and credit demand fell. The United States moved from a 7 percent New York Fed discount rate toward lower rates. The Bank of England reduced Bank Rate through the year, even as policymakers still aimed to restore sterling credibility. In Russia and the territories later grouped as the USSR, the New Economic Policy began a partial return to market exchange after War Communism, one of the largest ideological and institutional economic pivots of the early twentieth century.

The central data problem for 1921 is that modern national accounts did not exist for many economies. This assessment uses Maddison Project Database 2023 as the main GDP ranking source and cross-checks against Maddison Database 2010. The GDP figures below are PPP estimates in 2011 international dollars, not nominal U.S. dollars. For China, MPD 2023 provides population but no 1921 GDP per capita in the Full data sheet, so aggregate GDP is Data not available. That is a major gap and means the top-20 ranking should be read as the top 20 among economies with annual 1921 MPD GDP estimates, with China explicitly unranked rather than guessed.

Economically, the most important surprise was the speed and severity of nominal deflation. Agricultural prices, wholesale prices, and some asset prices fell sharply, transferring stress to farmers, exporters, and debtors. Yet the year also set up recovery: lower inventories, easier money, and restored private incentives in some regions created the base for the later 1920s expansion. With hindsight, 1921 also marked the start of several vulnerabilities that would matter later: German reparations and currency collapse, protectionist pressure, the fragility of restored gold-standard thinking, and dependence on external commodity cycles.

### 2. KEY THEMES OF 1921

**1. Postwar deflation and recession.** The most visible economic theme was the collapse of wartime inflation. The United States, Britain, Canada, and many commodity exporters moved through falling prices, inventory liquidation, and weaker employment. In the United States, annual CPI inflation was negative and wholesale prices fell sharply. The forward linkage was the 1922-1923 recovery in several economies, but also political pressure from farmers and workers who bore the adjustment costs.

**2. Hard-money restoration versus economic pain.** Policymakers wanted to restore credibility after wartime finance. In Britain this meant an eventual return to gold at the old parity; in the United States it meant maintaining the dollar's gold parity while letting prices fall. In practice, interest rates were cut during 1921 as the slump deepened. The longer-term consequence was the restored gold-standard system of the mid-1920s, which later proved brittle.

**3. Reparations, war debts, and unstable European currencies.** Germany's reparations burden became more concrete after the London Ultimatum. France and Belgium wanted reparations to support reconstruction, while Germany faced fiscal and external-transfer pressures. These tensions linked diplomacy to exchange rates and inflation expectations. The forward linkage is direct: 1921 helped set the stage for the 1922-1923 German hyperinflation and later stabilization.

**4. NEP as a state-versus-market turning point.** Soviet Russia's New Economic Policy began in 1921. It replaced grain requisitioning with a tax in kind and allowed limited private trade and small-scale enterprise. The shift mattered because it showed that ideological command policies could be reversed under production stress. It also created a mixed economy that lasted until the late 1920s.

**5. Commodity exporters under pressure.** Canada, Argentina, Australia, Brazil, Mexico, Indonesia, and other export-linked economies were highly exposed to falling prices for agricultural goods, raw materials, and energy. The shock hit rural incomes, fiscal revenue, railway traffic, and bank credit. The forward linkage was growing pressure for tariffs, marketing boards, debt relief, and state support.

**6. Protectionism and migration restriction.** The United States enacted the Emergency Tariff Act and the Emergency Quota Law in 1921. These policies reflected the domestic politics of recession: protecting farmers and limiting labor-market competition were presented as emergency responses. The forward linkage was the higher-protection environment of the 1920s.

**7. Early consumer and technology transformations.** 1921 was not a high-technology boom year by modern standards, but it mattered for future productivity and consumer markets. Insulin was discovered in 1921, radio commercialization accelerated, chain-store and standardized-service models spread, and mass production in automobiles and consumer goods continued to scale.

#### 3B. Monetary & Financial Conditions

United States: The New York Fed maintained a 7 percent commercial-paper discount rate until May 5, 1921, then reduced rates as the slump deepened. Credit conditions moved from tight to easier, but the transition was painful because debt burdens rose in real terms during deflation.

United Kingdom: Bank Rate began the year around 7 percent and fell through 1921, reaching about 5.5 percent in the third quarter. The exchange-rate context was shaped by the desire to restore sterling credibility, even though a full return to gold came later.

Germany and continental Europe: Monetary conditions were fragmented and heavily affected by reparations, fiscal weakness, and currency instability. Germany's mark depreciation became more consequential after the London Ultimatum. Comparable policy-rate and yield-curve data are Data not available.

#### 3C. Trade & Geopolitics

The main trade-policy shift was the U.S. Emergency Tariff Act of May 27, 1921, which imposed temporary duties on selected agricultural products. The law reflected farmer distress and falling commodity prices. The Emergency Quota Law of 1921 placed numerical limits on U.S. immigration and had labor-market and demographic consequences.

The most important geopolitical-economic issue was the postwar settlement. Germany's reparations, Allied war debts, French and Belgian reconstruction claims, and British fiscal restraint all influenced capital flows and exchange-rate expectations. Regional integration institutions such as the EEC and ASEAN did not exist. Trade was still organized through empires, bilateral relations, shipping networks, and national tariff systems.

#### 3D. Commodities & Energy

Oil prices weakened sharply in 1921 after the postwar boom. U.S. crude production remained important and oil demand was increasingly linked to automobiles, trucks, and mechanized industry. The price decline stressed producers but supported later energy consumption.

Agricultural commodities were central to the recession. Wheat, cotton, wool, meat, coffee, and sugar markets were affected by the fall in wartime demand, restored production, and tighter credit. Canada, Argentina, Australia, Brazil, India, and the Dutch East Indies were directly exposed.

Metals and minerals also weakened as construction and industrial demand slowed. Coal remained the dominant industrial fuel in Europe and Japan, while oil was becoming more important in transport and industry.

#### 3E. Labor & Demographics

Labor markets were disrupted by demobilization, wage cuts, industrial disputes, and weak export demand. Britain experienced a major unemployment crisis in 1921, with coal and heavy industry especially affected. U.S. unemployment estimates differ by method, but all show a sharp deterioration from 1920.

Migration policy changed materially. The U.S. Emergency Quota Law reduced immigration inflows and marked a shift toward numerical restriction. Urbanization continued in many economies, but 1921 was not a single demographic turning point globally. Wage growth versus productivity is Data not available in a comparable global series.

**Economic narrative:**
The United States was still the largest economy by a wide margin, but 1921 was the trough year of the sharp postwar recession. The NBER chronology places the U.S. contraction from January 1920 to July 1921. The MPD 2023 GDP estimate shows a real GDP decline of about 2.9 percent from 1920 to 1921, while consumer prices fell sharply. This was a hard nominal adjustment rather than a modern demand-management cycle: prices, wages, inventories, farm incomes, and credit conditions all reset after the wartime boom.

Policy moved from restraint to easing during the year. The New York Federal Reserve Bank kept a 7 percent commercial-paper discount rate until May 5, 1921, then reduced rates as market rates and commodity prices fell. Fiscal policy also moved toward peacetime retrenchment. The year is remembered by some economists as a rapid liquidation and recovery episode, but that interpretation is contested because the social costs for farmers, workers, and indebted households were substantial.

**Micro/sectoral highlights:**
Farm prices collapsed, inventories were liquidated, and many rural banks and borrowers were under stress. The Emergency Tariff Act of May 27, 1921 protected selected agricultural products. Manufacturing moved from wartime and postwar overstocking toward lower prices and renewed output. Consumer business models were changing at the margin: White Castle was founded in 1921, radio commercialization accelerated after the 1920 broadcast breakthrough, and the automobile industry continued to scale through Ford, General Motors, and related suppliers.

### 7. RISKS, CRISES & BLACK SWANS

The active crisis of 1921 was the postwar deflationary recession. It combined falling prices, tighter real debt burdens, weak employment, and inventory liquidation. In the United States the downturn officially ended in July, but farm distress and debt problems persisted. In Britain, unemployment and coal-sector conflict were severe.

Europe's systemic risk was the unresolved war-finance problem. Germany owed reparations; France and Belgium expected reparations to support reconstruction; Britain and France had war debts; the United States was the strongest creditor. This was not only a diplomatic issue. It affected exchange rates, budgets, central-bank policy, and private expectations.

The Former USSR faced a political-economic survival crisis after civil war, famine conditions, and production collapse. The New Economic Policy was a response to systemic failure in provisioning and incentives.

Hindsight risk: The 1921 reparations and currency situation in Germany was an early warning for the hyperinflation of 1922-1923. That full outcome was not mechanically inevitable in 1921, but the vulnerabilities were visible.

Hindsight risk: The attempt to restore prewar gold-standard norms helped create a fragile interwar financial architecture. That fragility would matter in the late 1920s and early 1930s, but in 1921 many policymakers still saw gold restoration as credibility, not as a future constraint.

### 8. YEAR-OVER-YEAR DELTA (1921 vs. 1920)

The biggest change from 1920 to 1921 was the transition from inflationary postwar boom conditions to deflationary recession. In the United States, CPI inflation moved from strongly positive in 1920 to sharply negative in 1921. Wholesale and commodity prices dropped. Credit conditions began tight and shifted toward easing as the downturn deepened.

GDP rankings changed in ways that reflect both real shocks and data gaps. The United States remained number one. India moved above the United Kingdom in MPD 2023 as Britain contracted. Germany moved above Britain in MPD 2023, although Maddison 2010 reverses that ordering. Former USSR fell from seventh to eighth among available estimates. Canada fell from twelfth to fourteenth because of a severe contraction. Austria and Sweden entered the available-estimate top 20, while Switzerland fell to twenty-first and Poland became unrankable for 1921 because GDP per capita is missing.

Policy changed from restraint toward easing. The New York Fed and Bank of England both reduced rates during the year. In trade and migration, the United States moved toward protection and restriction through the Emergency Tariff Act and Emergency Quota Law. In Russia, the NEP began a structural pivot away from War Communism. In Germany, the London Ultimatum made reparations more concrete and intensified the link between fiscal capacity and currency expectations.

The new risks that emerged were currency instability, political radicalization in some European economies, agricultural debt distress, and the political appeal of protectionism. The risks that partly resolved were the immediate wartime shortages and the peak of the inflationary surge. The world economy did not return to the prewar order; it entered an unstable interwar transition.

### 9. DATA CONFIDENCE & GAPS

Highest-confidence data in this document: the existence and date range of the U.S. business-cycle contraction; U.S. CPI and wholesale-price deflation; Federal Reserve and Bank of England rate changes; the U.S. Emergency Tariff Act and Emergency Quota Law; the basic timing of the London Ultimatum; and the timing and institutional content of the New Economic Policy.

Medium-confidence data: MPD 2023 GDP rankings and growth rates for countries with complete annual GDP per capita and population entries. These are reconstructed PPP estimates, not contemporary national accounts. They are useful for relative scale, but they should not be treated like modern national accounts.

Low-confidence or high-uncertainty data: Former USSR GDP, Czechoslovakia and Austria after postwar boundary changes, colonial economies such as India and the Dutch East Indies, and countries where population and income series are reconstructed from sparse historical data. For Soviet territories and other disrupted economies, uncertainty can easily be very large.

Source conflicts: MPD 2023 and Maddison 2010 differ in the 1921 Germany versus United Kingdom ranking. MPD 2023 ranks Germany slightly above the United Kingdom, while Maddison 2010 places the United Kingdom slightly above Germany. This document uses MPD 2023 because it is the newer Maddison Project release, while explicitly flagging the conflict.

Major missing data: China lacks a 1921 GDP per capita entry in MPD 2023 Full data, so its aggregate GDP is Data not available here despite its large population and historical importance. Poland has a 1920 GDP per capita entry but not a 1921 entry. Turkey, Egypt, South Africa, and several other important economies also lack complete annual 1921 data in the source set. Global GDP, global trade volume, capital flows, current-account balances, budget balances, debt-to-GDP ratios, unemployment rates, and corporate revenue rankings are not available in a consistent global 1921 framework.

Unemployment comparability: Unemployment definitions differed widely, and many countries had no modern labor-force survey. Where estimates are used for the United States and United Kingdom, they should be read as historical estimates, not directly comparable modern rates.

Corporate and microeconomic data: Modern-style global corporate rankings, IPO lists, M&A databases, bank balance-sheet comparables, and real-estate indices are Data not available. The corporate section therefore identifies important firms and business models qualitatively rather than fabricating rankings.
