# MACRO/MICRO GLOBAL ECONOMIC ASSESSMENT - YEAR: 1945

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

1945 was the hinge year between the wartime command economy and the postwar international economic order. The defining facts were the end of World War II, the collapse or occupation of the German and Japanese economies, the start of demobilization in the United States and Commonwealth economies, and the formal coming into force of the Bretton Woods institutions. The year was not simply a recession or recovery year. It was a regime transition in production, money, trade, finance, labor allocation, and state capacity.

The United States was the dominant economic power. In the Maddison 2010 historical GDP table, US GDP in 1945 was far larger than any other listed economy, even after a 4.0 percent fall from 1944 as war contracts began to unwind. By contrast, Germany, Japan, and Italy recorded severe or very severe declines, while France, Belgium, the Netherlands, and other liberated economies show rebound patterns from very low wartime conditions. The USSR remained one of the world's largest economies, but its GDP estimates are especially uncertain because Soviet national accounts did not map cleanly onto market-economy GDP.

The microeconomic mechanisms behind the macro shift were physical and institutional. The world economy did not face a normal business-cycle problem. It faced damaged ports and railways, rationing systems, displaced labor, demobilized soldiers, state-controlled prices, contract cancellations, military plants that had to be converted to civilian use, and huge pent-up household demand. In the United States, Canada, Australia, Sweden, and Switzerland, capital stocks were largely intact. In Germany, Japan, Poland, China, and parts of the Soviet Union, the binding constraint was reconstruction of basic capacity.

The forward-looking significance of 1945 lies in the creation of the postwar framework: IMF and World Bank foundations, dollar-gold anchoring, low-rate public debt management, the beginning of European and Japanese occupation economies, and early decolonization pressures. The Marshall Plan, GATT, West German currency reform, Japanese stabilization, and postwar growth miracles were still ahead. But the institutional and balance-sheet conditions that made those events possible were set in 1945.

## 2. KEY THEMES OF 1945

### Theme 1: The shift from total war to reconversion

**Significance in economic history:** 1945 was the largest modern transition from command-style war production to civilian production. The transition changed measured GDP, employment, capital allocation, public budgets, and household demand all at once.

**Specific data/events in 1945:** Maddison data show large declines in the United States, United Kingdom, Canada, Australia, Germany, Italy, Japan, Spain, and Turkey, while some liberated economies rebounded from disrupted 1944 output. In the United States, the issue was not lack of productive capacity but the cancellation of procurement and retooling of factories.

**Countries most affected:** United States, United Kingdom, Canada, Australia, Germany, Japan, Italy, USSR, France.

**Forward linkage:** The reconversion process led into the 1946-48 reopening of consumer markets, inflation pressures where controls were lifted, and the durable-goods and housing booms of the late 1940s.

### Theme 2: Bretton Woods and the dollar-centered reserve order

**Significance in economic history:** The IMF Articles of Agreement entered into force in December 1945, and the dollar-gold framework became the institutional basis for postwar monetary cooperation. The system was designed around fixed but adjustable exchange rates and a dollar convertible into gold at the official parity.

**Specific data/events in 1945:** The IMF and IBRD moved from wartime agreement to institutional reality. The gold anchor remained $35 per troy ounce for official purposes. The system was not fully operational in day-to-day current-account convertibility, but the architecture was set.

**Countries most affected:** United States, United Kingdom, Western Europe, Canada, Latin American members, and later Japan and Germany through reconstruction.

**Forward linkage:** Dollar shortage, sterling convertibility problems, IMF quotas, and the World Bank's reconstruction role shaped the later 1940s.

### Theme 3: Reconstruction and the economics of destroyed capital

**Significance in economic history:** 1945 showed that GDP estimates alone cannot capture destroyed infrastructure, housing loss, transport disruption, and institutional collapse. Reconstruction economics became a practical policy field.

**Specific data/events in 1945:** Germany and Japan suffered sharp output collapses. France, Belgium, and the Netherlands moved from occupation into recovery. The USSR faced reconstruction of western regions while retaining a command allocation system.

**Countries most affected:** Germany, Japan, USSR, France, Netherlands, Belgium, Italy, China, Poland, Korea.

**Forward linkage:** Reconstruction needs led to occupation policy, nationalizations, planning, external aid, and later the Marshall Plan.

### Theme 4: State intervention remained the default policy regime

**Significance in economic history:** Price controls, rationing, capital controls, credit direction, nationalization, and state procurement did not end with the war. They became the starting point for postwar mixed economies.

**Specific data/events in 1945:** France nationalized key assets and moved toward planning. Britain elected a Labour government committed to welfare-state and nationalization measures. The United States maintained Treasury yield support. Germany and Japan were placed under occupation administration.

**Countries most affected:** United Kingdom, France, United States, Germany, Japan, USSR, India, Italy.

**Forward linkage:** The late 1940s combined welfare expansion, state industry, development planning, and controlled finance before later liberalization waves.

### Theme 5: Food, labor, and migration were macroeconomic variables

**Significance in economic history:** Millions of demobilized soldiers, displaced persons, refugees, and workers moved or had to be reallocated. Food supply was not merely a humanitarian issue. It set real wages, labor productivity, inflation pressure, and political stability.

**Specific data/events in 1945:** Europe faced displaced-persons logistics. India, Japan, the Netherlands, Germany, and parts of the USSR faced serious food supply constraints. The United States and Canada had abundant agricultural and food-export capacity but also needed to manage demobilization.

**Countries most affected:** Germany, USSR, Poland, Netherlands, Japan, India, United States, Canada, Australia.

**Forward linkage:** These pressures shaped UNRRA relief, migration policy, housing programs, and agricultural trade.

### Theme 6: Technology moved from military laboratories to civilian platforms

**Significance in economic history:** Wartime R&D created technologies with postwar productivity effects: radar, aviation, electronics, operations research, penicillin production, nuclear science, early computing, and rocketry.

**Specific data/events in 1945:** Many breakthroughs were still military or government-controlled, but the industrial base and human capital were already in place. ENIAC was completed in 1945 and publicly announced in 1946. Commercial aviation, chemicals, pharmaceuticals, and electronics were positioned for postwar growth.

**Countries most affected:** United States, United Kingdom, Germany, Japan, USSR, Canada.

**Forward linkage:** The postwar military-industrial research system, commercial aviation, computing, and nuclear energy policies developed from this wartime base.

## 3. MACROECONOMIC OVERVIEW

### 3A. Global Aggregates

**World GDP and growth rate:** Official globally standardized GDP did not exist for 1945. Maddison-style historical GDP provides reconstructed constant-price PPP estimates, but the world aggregate and many country entries are estimates rather than official statistics. **Global trade volume:** **Data not available** as a globally comparable 1945 volume index in the source set. Qualitatively, trade was still shaped by wartime shipping controls, convoy losses, military logistics, exchange controls, and reconstruction import needs. The dollar shortage and shortage of shipping made trade capacity a binding constraint.

**Reserve currency dynamics:** The US dollar became the center of the planned postwar monetary system. Bretton Woods created a fixed-but-adjustable exchange-rate regime anchored on the dollar and gold. Sterling remained important through the sterling area, but Britain's wartime debt and export weakness reduced its relative position.

**Global inflation trends:** **Data not available** as a comparable global CPI figure. Price controls, rationing, black markets, and occupation currencies make cross-country CPI comparisons unusually weak. Inflation risk was highest where war finance had left large monetary overhangs and output capacity was damaged.

**Key commodity prices:** Gold's official monetary parity was $35 per troy ounce. Oil-price data for 1945 are not consistently available in the source set, and modern WTI series commonly begin in 1946. Copper, wheat, and other staple-price figures for a single global 1945 price are **Data not available** here. The economic reality was physical allocation rather than free global spot pricing.

**International capital flow direction:** Private capital markets were limited by controls. The main flows were public and quasi-public: Lend-Lease settlement, relief, military occupation finance, sterling-area balances, and emerging Bretton Woods institutional capital.

### 3B. Monetary & Financial Conditions

The United States maintained a wartime yield-curve policy. Treasury bills were pegged around 3/8 percent and long-term Treasury bonds were held near a 2.5 percent ceiling. This was not modern inflation-targeting monetary policy. It was fiscal-financial repression designed to keep the cost of war debt low and stabilize the government securities market.

In the United Kingdom, Bank Rate remained at 2 percent. Monetary conditions were dominated by exchange controls, public borrowing, rationing, and external financing needs. Germany and Japan did not have normal monetary-policy regimes after defeat; occupation policy, destroyed fiscal credibility, and shortages dominated.

Equity markets were functioning mainly in intact economies. The Dow Jones Industrial Average rose strongly in 1945, with reported annual performance of 26.65 percent and an end-year close of 192.91 in common historical series. In Germany and Japan, equity markets were not meaningful macro indicators because of defeat and occupation. Bond markets were dominated by government debt and administrative controls.

### 3C. Trade & Geopolitics

No GATT system existed yet in 1945. The year was a bridge between wartime trade controls and later multilateral liberalization. Major trade relationships were shaped by the end of Lend-Lease, sterling balances, dollar shortage, relief shipments, occupation needs, and the reopening of European ports. The United Nations was founded in 1945, while IMF and IBRD implementation began.

Geopolitically, 1945 began the economic division that would become the Cold War. Soviet control in Eastern Europe, Allied occupation zones in Germany and Austria, the division of Korea, civil conflict in China, and Japanese occupation all shaped trade and investment flows. Decolonization pressure increased, especially in India and Southeast Asia.

### 3D. Commodities & Energy

Energy supply was still an allocation problem. Coal was critical for European recovery, rail transport, steel, and household heating. Oil was strategically important, especially for the United States, Venezuela, the Middle East, and Allied logistics. Global oil institutions like OPEC did not exist; OPEC was created only in 1960.

Metals and minerals were influenced by war inventories and reconstruction demand. Steel, aluminum, copper, and coal capacity mattered more than spot prices because many markets were controlled. Agricultural commodities were central to welfare. Wheat, rice, meat, and edible oils shaped relief needs and wage politics.

### 3E. Labor & Demographics

The labor shock of 1945 was global. Demobilization released soldiers into civilian labor markets. Women who had entered war industries faced changing employment opportunities. Displaced persons in Europe, forced labor return, refugees, and colonial troop demobilization reshaped labor supply.

Demographically, the year came immediately before the postwar baby boom in the United States and several other countries. Urban housing shortages were severe in Europe, Japan, North America, and Australia. Wage and productivity comparisons are **Data not available** in globally comparable form because rationing, nonmarket allocation, and military employment distort conventional labor measures.

**Economic narrative:**

The United States ended 1945 as the central production, financial, and reserve-power economy of the postwar system. Real war output had begun to crest as contracts were cancelled after victory in Europe and Japan, and the Maddison total GDP series shows a 4.0 percent decline from 1944. That decline does not mean a civilian depression in the usual sense. It reflects the beginning of demobilization from an extreme war-production economy in which military procurement had displaced much private consumption and investment. The Federal Reserve maintained the wartime Treasury yield pattern, with short bills near 3/8 percent and the long-bond ceiling near 2.5 percent, making fiscal finance the dominant monetary priority.

Microeconomically, the central story was reconversion. Aircraft, shipbuilding, vehicles, munitions, machine tools, chemicals, and steel were still huge, but the private sector was already retooling toward automobiles, appliances, housing, and consumer durables. Household balance sheets held high forced savings because rationing and shortages had limited consumption. Equity investors looked through the end of procurement and bid up industrial shares, with the Dow Jones Industrial Average posting a strong annual gain. The risk was not a shortage of demand, but a disorderly shift from public war demand to private consumer demand under price controls and tight material allocation.

**Micro/sectoral highlights:**

- War-contract cancellation and civilian reconversion dominated manufacturing.
- Household savings, ration books, and pent-up demand shaped consumer markets.
- Commercial banking remained liquid and heavily invested in government securities.
- Housing and autos were capacity-constrained rather than demand-constrained.

### 5D. Labor markets & human capital

### 6B. Fixed income

US government bond markets were managed under wartime yield-curve control. The front end was anchored near 3/8 percent and the long bond near 2.5 percent. This created a stable government-finance environment but limited independent monetary policy. UK rates were also low and controlled, with Bank Rate at 2 percent. Comparable 10-year yields for Germany, Japan, France, Italy, and the USSR are **Data not available** in this source set or not meaningful because of occupation, controls, or nonmarket finance.

### 6C. Currencies

The international exchange-rate regime was in transition. Bretton Woods set the design, but full convertibility was not yet restored. Many currencies operated under capital controls, rationing, clearing arrangements, and official parities. The dollar became the central reserve anchor. Sterling remained systemically important but financially weakened. German and Japanese currency data for 1945 are not comparable because defeat and occupation undermined normal monetary institutions.

### 6D. Alternative assets

Gold's official monetary price was fixed at $35 per troy ounce under the dollar-gold framework. Private gold markets and capital controls varied by country, so a single global market interpretation is not appropriate. Real estate data for major cities are **Data not available** in a comparable 1945 global format, but housing shortages were severe in Europe, Japan, North America, and Australia. Other alternative assets, including art, farmland, and private business claims, lack reliable global data for 1945.

## 7. RISKS, CRISES & BLACK SWANS

The active crises of 1945 were not hidden tail risks. They were visible: defeated economies, destroyed transport, food shortages, displaced populations, fiscal overhangs, demobilization, and inflationary pressure under price controls. Germany and Japan were systemic economic collapses. The USSR faced reconstruction of a vast damaged territory. India and other colonial economies faced political transition and food-market fragility.

Near-misses and systemic vulnerabilities included the possibility of a US postwar unemployment shock, a sterling and dollar shortage crisis, a collapse of European food distribution, uncontrolled inflation after removing price controls, and political instability in occupied or liberated economies. Hindsight shows that the United States avoided a deep postwar depression, but that was not guaranteed to contemporaries.

Policy mistakes visible in retrospect include underestimating the difficulty of immediate convertibility, the burden of European dollar shortage, and the depth of occupation-economy disruption in Germany and Japan. Hindsight also shows that wartime R&D and public procurement created long-lived productivity platforms, although contemporaries often viewed them mainly as demobilization problems.

## 8. YEAR-OVER-YEAR DELTA (1945 vs. 1944)

The central structural break from 1944 to 1945 was the end of total war. In 1944, Allied production was still climbing or operating at full wartime intensity. In 1945, military victory caused procurement cancellation, demobilization, occupation administration, and reconstruction planning. In the Maddison data, the United States fell 4.0 percent, Britain 4.4 percent, Canada 3.1 percent, Australia 5.0 percent, Germany 28.8 percent, Italy 21.7 percent, and Japan 50.0 percent. These changes are not comparable with ordinary peacetime recessions.

Risks shifted. In 1944, the primary economic risk was war production and military logistics. In 1945, it was the peace transition: inflation, unemployment, trade financing, relief, reconstruction, and currency stabilization. Economic power shifted even more decisively toward the United States. Western Europe remained economically important but financially dependent. The USSR emerged militarily and geopolitically stronger, while Germany and Japan lost economic sovereignty under occupation.

Policy also pivoted. The Bretton Woods institutions moved from agreement to legal existence. National planning and public ownership gained influence in Britain and France. Occupation policy became the key macro regime in Germany and Japan. Developing and colonial economies faced the beginning of a new era in which independence, state-led industrialization, and import substitution would become central.

## 9. DATA CONFIDENCE & GAPS

**High confidence:** The broad qualitative chronology of 1945, the existence and design of Bretton Woods, the US Federal Reserve's wartime yield policy, the Bank of England's low Bank Rate environment, and the US equity-market performance are well supported. Maddison-style GDP estimates are widely used for long-run comparative economic history, but they remain reconstructed estimates.

**Medium confidence:** GDP levels and growth rates for the United States, United Kingdom, Canada, Australia, Sweden, Belgium, Switzerland, and several Western European or Latin American economies are more usable than those for war-damaged or nonmarket economies. Even here, price controls and wartime production make GDP less comparable with peacetime welfare.

**Low confidence:** Soviet GDP, German GDP, Japanese GDP, and other war-collapse values should be treated cautiously. Soviet bloc and nonmarket estimates can easily have large uncertainty. Germany and Japan in 1945 are estimates of disrupted economies rather than normal market output. Unemployment, inflation, budget, current-account, and exchange-rate indicators are often **Data not available** or non-comparable.

**Missing-country problem:** China, Indonesia, Poland, South Africa, Iran, Egypt, Thailand, and several other countries have no 1945 GDP entries in the downloaded Maddison 2010 country GDP table. Some of these likely would be in, or close to, a complete top 20. The document therefore does not fabricate 1945 GDP figures for them. It flags the top-20 table as a ranking among economies with reported Maddison 1945 GDP entries.

**Weakest claims:** Corporate rankings, global commodity prices, global trade volume, and country-by-country macro tables outside GDP, population, and GDP per capita are the weakest areas. Where no verifiable figure was found, the document uses **Data not available**.
