Current-dollar World Bank-derived values are included as a cross-check where available, but they are **not** the ranking basis.

Treat non-market economy GDP as approximate, with possible error of roughly plus or minus 20 to 30 percent. ### 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

1968 was an expansion year with a monetary system under visible strain. Maddison-based world real GDP was about **12.417 trillion 1990 international dollars**, up about **5.5 percent** from 1967. The United States remained far larger than any other economy, Japan was in the fastest phase of its postwar growth surge, Western Europe recovered from the 1966 to 1967 slowdown, and several middle-income economies, notably Brazil, Mexico, Spain, Indonesia, Venezuela, and Turkey, grew quickly. The year was not a global recession story. It was a story of rapid growth, inflationary pressure, and weakening confidence in the gold-dollar link.

The defining macro event was the March 1968 gold crisis. The London Gold Pool was suspended, the London gold market was temporarily closed, and a two-tier gold market emerged: official transactions among monetary authorities remained at $35 per fine ounce, while private-market gold traded separately. This did not end Bretton Woods in 1968, but it showed that the system had moved from stable convertibility to managed defense of a strained reserve-currency regime.

Policy tightened in the United States and the United Kingdom while Japan began to ease from earlier restraint. The U.S. Federal Reserve raised its discount rate in March and April, the U.S. Congress enacted a tax surcharge and expenditure restraint at mid-year, and U.S. banks operated in a high-rate environment with Regulation Q constraints. The United Kingdom was still adjusting to the November 1967 sterling devaluation, with high Bank Rate and balance-of-payments discipline. Japan, by contrast, cut its official discount rate in August 1968 and later removed strict window-guidance lending limits.

On the micro side, 1968 was a year of industrial consolidation, technology formation, and mass-consumption deepening. Intel was founded in July, British Leyland was created in January, Boeing rolled out the 747 in September, and the global corporate hierarchy was still dominated by automobiles, oil, steel, chemicals, utilities, and mass consumer durables. The industrial structure of 1968 looked mature in autos and oil, but seed technologies in semiconductors, computing, jet transport, and telecommunications were beginning to reshape future productivity and market structure.

### 2. KEY THEMES OF 1968

**Theme 1: Bretton Woods strain became visible but not yet terminal.** The March 1968 gold crisis was historically significant because it separated official monetary gold from private gold trading. The dollar still anchored the system, and currencies still operated under fixed or managed parities, but the confidence mechanism had changed. The countries most affected were the United States, United Kingdom, France, Germany, Japan, and other reserve-holding economies. Forward linkage: the two-tier market foreshadowed the 1971 suspension of dollar-gold convertibility and the 1973 move to generalized floating among major currencies.

**Theme 2: Expansion returned after the 1966 to 1967 industrial-country slowdown.** Maddison data show strong 1968 real growth in the United States, Japan, Germany, Italy, the Netherlands, Canada, Spain, and many developing economies. The IMF described renewed industrial-country activity after the 1966 to 1967 slowdown. Forward linkage: the same expansion, especially in the United States, contributed to inflation, external imbalance, and future policy tightening.

**Theme 3: U.S. fiscal and monetary restraint arrived late.** The United States entered 1968 with very strong private demand and war-related federal spending. The Federal Reserve raised the discount rate, and the Revenue and Expenditure Control Act imposed an income-tax surcharge and spending restraint. Significance: the policy mix showed the difficulty of controlling demand when fiscal tightening lags monetary pressure. Forward linkage: inflation did not disappear, interest rates rose again by year-end, and the late-1960s overheating fed the 1970s inflation problem.

**Theme 4: Japan remained the standout high-growth industrial economy.** Japan's Maddison real GDP growth was about **12.9 percent** in 1968, far above the other top economies. Manufacturing, exports, consumer durables, steel, machinery, electronics, and autos were central. The Bank of Japan reduced the official discount rate in August and moved away from quantitative lending restraints in October. Forward linkage: Japan entered the 1970s as the strongest challenger to U.S. and European industrial incumbents in autos, electronics, machine tools, and shipbuilding.

**Theme 5: European integration reached a concrete trade-policy milestone.** On 1 July 1968, the European Economic Community completed its customs union among the original six members, removing internal customs duties and replacing national external tariffs with a common external tariff. This directly affected France, Germany, Italy, the Netherlands, Belgium, and Luxembourg, and indirectly affected EFTA, the United Kingdom, and global exporters. Forward linkage: the customs union became a foundation for later single-market integration.

**Theme 6: Political shocks translated into economic shocks in France and Eastern Europe.** France's May to June strikes disrupted production, generated large wage concessions, intensified inflation pressure, and contributed to capital flight and later currency pressure. In the Soviet bloc, the Prague Spring and its suppression created political risk inside a planned-economy system, although comparable macro data are limited. Forward linkage: France devalued the franc in 1969, and Eastern European reform debates narrowed after 1968.

**Theme 7: Producer-country oil politics strengthened before the 1970s oil shock.** OPEC adopted a declaratory petroleum policy in June 1968 calling for greater member-state participation in concessions, conservation rules, and government influence over posted or tax-reference prices. Oil prices were still low and stable compared with the 1970s, but the institutional shift mattered. Forward linkage: the policy trajectory pointed toward stronger resource nationalism and the bargaining changes that became decisive in 1973 to 1974.

**3B. Monetary & Financial Conditions:**

The United States ran a restrictive monetary stance. The Federal Reserve raised the discount rate from 4.5 percent to 5 percent in March and to 5.5 percent in mid-April. Market rates eased after tax legislation prospects improved, then rose again later in the year, with the discount rate again at 5.5 percent in December and prime rates rising into early 1969. The U.S. banking system faced disintermediation pressure from market rates above deposit-rate ceilings.

The United Kingdom remained in a post-devaluation defense posture. Sterling had been devalued in November 1967, and 1968 policy focused on restoring external confidence. Bank Rate was high, with contemporary sources indicating 8 percent early in 1968 and a later reduction to 7.5 percent after fiscal measures. Credit restraint and import compression were central to policy.

Germany and other continental European surplus economies faced the opposite side of adjustment: easy monetary conditions were encouraged to support global adjustment while avoiding excessive reserve accumulation. Japan eased from earlier tight money, lowering the official discount rate to 5.84 percent on 7 August 1968 and discontinuing strict window-guidance quantitative loan restrictions from October.

Bretton Woods remained formally intact. Exchange rates were fixed or tightly managed, capital controls were common, and many developing or non-market economies used administrative exchange systems. The gold market, however, no longer operated as a single unified official-private market.

**3C. Trade & Geopolitics:**

The most important trade-policy milestone was the EEC customs union, completed on 1 July 1968. It removed customs duties and restrictions between the six EEC members and established a common external tariff. This mattered for manufacturing scale, intra-European supply chains, and tariff planning.

The Kennedy Round tariff cuts remained relevant. UK parliamentary material from March 1968 discussed accelerated implementation of remaining tariff reductions, conditional on other major economies and on the United States avoiding restrictive measures such as import surcharges. In geopolitical terms, the Cold War shaped trade, technology, capital flows, and defense procurement. Trade between OECD economies and centrally planned economies remained small relative to intra-OECD trade, although it was expanding in selected commodities and machinery.

**3D. Commodities & Energy:**

Oil remained cheap and stable compared with the post-1973 world, but the institutional balance between international oil companies and producer states was shifting. OPEC's June 1968 petroleum policy statement emphasized member-state exploration, participation in existing concessions, conservation rules, and government determination of posted or tax-reference prices. Venezuela, Iran, Indonesia, and other oil producers were direct beneficiaries of oil rent, although price levels were not yet the central inflation shock they later became.

Gold was the major commodity-financial asset of the year. The official $35 price survived for central-bank transactions, but the private gold market was allowed to float separately after the London market reopened. **3E. Labor & Demographics:**

Demographically, the late baby-boom cohort and rapid urbanization supported labor-force expansion in North America, Western Europe, Latin America, Turkey, India, China, and Indonesia. Migration remained economically important: Turkish and Southern European workers moved toward Western Europe, Commonwealth migration mattered for the UK and Australia, and rural-to-urban migration shaped Mexico, Brazil, Spain, India, and Indonesia.

Labor markets were not comparable across countries. U.S. unemployment was low by late-1960s standards, while many European countries still had institutionally different unemployment definitions. Planned economies reported labor statistics that are not comparable with market-economy unemployment. Wage pressure was important in France after the Grenelle wage settlement and in the United States where tight demand raised cost pressure.

### 7. RISKS, CRISES & BLACK SWANS

The most important active crisis was the gold crisis. It was systemic because it directly tested the reserve-currency architecture. The London market closure and two-tier gold regime reduced immediate pressure but did not solve the underlying U.S. external imbalance and gold-convertibility problem.

France's May to June labor and political crisis was a domestic macro shock with European monetary implications. The immediate results were production disruption, higher wages, pressure on margins, capital flight, and later currency vulnerability. The French devaluation did not occur until 1969, so the 1968 assessment must distinguish immediate pressure from later outcome.

The United States faced overheating risk. With hindsight, the 1968 policy mix was too slow to prevent the inflation dynamics that later worsened. This is hindsight: contemporaries recognized inflation and external imbalance, but the full 1970s inflation regime was not yet known.

Oil was not yet a price shock, but the institutional risk was building. OPEC's 1968 policy statement showed that producer states wanted more control over concession terms and prices. With hindsight, this was part of the road to the 1973 to 1974 oil shock, but the sharp price break had not yet materialized.

Political risk in the Soviet bloc increased after the Prague Spring crisis. Economic reform space narrowed, and planned economies remained subject to political discipline rather than market correction. Data risk is also a black-swan-style problem for historians: some non-market GDP and unemployment series are not directly comparable with market-economy statistics.

### 8. YEAR-OVER-YEAR DELTA (1968 vs. 1967)

The world economy accelerated from the 1966 to 1967 slowdown into a strong 1968 expansion. Maddison world real GDP growth was about 5.5 percent. Japan's growth remained extraordinary, Italy and the Netherlands accelerated, Brazil moved ahead of Canada in the Maddison ranking, and France moved ahead of China because China's estimated output contracted while France still grew despite strikes.

The monetary regime changed more than the real-economy ranking. In 1967, sterling devaluation was the main currency shock. In 1968, the gold crisis showed that the dollar itself was under pressure. The two-tier gold market was a structural break in the operation of Bretton Woods even though formal parities survived.

Policy also pivoted. The U.S. moved from delayed fiscal restraint to enacted tax surcharge and expenditure control. The Fed remained restrictive. The UK stayed in external-adjustment mode after devaluation. Japan shifted from credit squeeze toward easing. Continental Europe had to balance domestic expansion with external adjustment.

European integration moved from treaty aspiration to tariff fact through the EEC customs union. Oil politics moved from price stability to stronger producer-state claims. Corporate technology moved from mainframe and industrial electronics toward the semiconductor startup ecosystem, with Intel's founding as the clearest marker.

### 9. DATA CONFIDENCE & GAPS

**High-confidence data:** Maddison GDP, population, GDP per capita, and computed real GDP growth are internally consistent within the Maddison Historical Statistics workbook. The modern Maddison Project Database and Our World in Data pages support the long-run PPP-style methodology, although the exact 1968 table here is computed from the accessible 2010 horizontal workbook. World Bank-derived current-dollar GDP values for market economies are useful cross-checks where available.

**Medium-confidence data:** General macro narratives for the United States, United Kingdom, Japan, EEC customs union, gold crisis, and OPEC policy are well supported by primary or institutional sources. Some exact market returns, commodity prices, and country-specific annual macro indicators are less consistently available.

**Low-confidence or estimate-heavy data:** Soviet Union, China, and Poland GDP are reconstructed and not market-price national accounts in the normal sense. Unemployment across planned economies, developing economies, and informal labor markets is not comparable. Exchange rates in countries with capital controls and multiple or administered rates are not comparable with market exchange rates.

**Major data gaps:** Harmonized 1968 CPI, unemployment, government debt/GDP, current account/GDP, fiscal balance/GDP, policy-rate series, and 10-year bond yields for all top-20 economies are Data not available in the accessible source set. A global 1968 trade-volume figure, copper price, agricultural staple price table, global R&D spending, patent counts, and globally comparable company revenue or market-cap rankings are Data not available.

**Weakest claims:** Sectoral and corporate narratives outside the United States, Western Europe, Japan, and selected official institutional events are less precisely quantified.
