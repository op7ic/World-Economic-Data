# MACRO/MICRO GLOBAL ECONOMIC ASSESSMENT - YEAR: 2006

### 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

2006 was the last full year of the mid-2000s global boom before the U.S. subprime shock became a visible systemic crisis. World output expanded rapidly, with the IMF projecting global real growth at about 5.1 percent for the year, while World Bank WDI/WITS put world GDP at roughly USD 51.86 trillion in current dollars. Merchandise trade growth was exceptionally strong: the WTO estimated real merchandise exports rose about 8 percent, while the IMF's later statistical tables put world trade in goods and services volume growth at about 9.2 percent. The basic story was synchronized expansion, high commodity prices, abundant global liquidity, and rising financial leverage.

The center of gravity continued shifting toward emerging Asia. China ranked fourth by current-dollar GDP in the World Bank series, overtaking several advanced European economies in nominal scale and recording double-digit real growth. India remained smaller in current-dollar terms but grew rapidly and deepened its services, telecom, finance, and infrastructure expansion. Resource exporters such as Russia, Brazil, Canada, and Australia benefited from high oil, metals, and agricultural prices, while current account surpluses in China, Germany, Japan, Russia, the Netherlands, Sweden, Switzerland, and oil exporters fed the global savings imbalance.

At the same time, the vulnerabilities that later defined 2007 and 2008 were already visible. The Federal Reserve raised the federal funds target to 5.25 percent by June, the ECB raised its main refinancing rate to 3.50 percent by December, the Bank of Japan ended zero interest rates in July, and other central banks tightened. U.S. housing activity weakened sharply, and early stress in subprime mortgages was noted in IMF analysis. Credit spreads remained compressed and equity markets rose in many countries, showing how financial markets still treated the downturn as contained.

Microeconomically, 2006 was a year of corporate scale, energy profits, bank listings, and cross-border consolidation. ICBC completed a record global IPO, Arcelor and Mittal created a steel giant, AT&T acquired BellSouth, and NYSE and Euronext agreed to merge. Technology, telecommunications, finance, energy, autos, steel, mining, and construction were key transmission channels. Consumers in the U.S., U.K., Spain, and Australia relied heavily on housing wealth and credit; consumers in China, India, Russia, Brazil, and Turkey were pulled into widening domestic markets.

### 2. KEY THEMES OF 2006

**1. The global expansion reached late-cycle speed.** The economic significance of 2006 is that growth was still broad-based, but it was no longer early-cycle recovery. The United States grew moderately while housing slowed; the euro area accelerated after years of weak domestic demand; Japan continued its post-deflation recovery; China, India, Russia, Brazil, and Turkey expanded quickly. Forward linkage: the strong 2006 data encouraged confidence, but the same late-cycle conditions made the 2007 credit shock more dangerous.

**2. The world economy became more commodity constrained.** Oil averaged about USD 64.29 per barrel on the World Bank crude oil average series, up from about USD 53.39 in 2005. The IMF reported oil prices up about 20.5 percent in U.S. dollar terms and nonfuel commodities up about 28.4 percent, with metals up about 56.5 percent. World Bank commodity notes reported record or multi-year highs in copper, zinc, crude oil, gold, sugar, rubber, and other commodities at different points in the year. Forward linkage: these prices drove capital spending in mining and energy and strengthened resource exporters, but also pressured inflation and household purchasing power.

**3. Monetary normalization spread across advanced economies.** The Fed, ECB, Bank of England, Bank of Canada, RBA, Riksbank, and other central banks either tightened or held policy rates at restrictive or less accommodative levels. The Bank of Japan's July rate hike from zero to 0.25 percent was historically important because it marked the exit from the zero-interest-rate policy after years of deflation pressure. Forward linkage: tighter funding conditions tested leveraged borrowers, especially U.S. housing and structured credit.

**4. Global imbalances became central rather than peripheral.** The United States ran a very large current account deficit, while China, Germany, Japan, Russia, oil exporters, and several northern European economies ran surpluses. China also operated a managed exchange rate, allowing gradual renminbi appreciation while reserve accumulation continued. Forward linkage: these imbalances shaped exchange-rate politics, reserve management, and the flow of savings into dollar credit markets before the crisis.

**5. Trade globalization was strong, but multilateral governance stalled.** The WTO estimated real merchandise exports grew about 8 percent in 2006, but the Doha Development Agenda negotiations were suspended in July after gaps among negotiators remained too wide. Forward linkage: trade kept growing, but global trade policy shifted further toward bilateral, regional, and sectoral bargaining.

**6. Finance and corporate control were globalizing rapidly.** Banks, exchanges, and industrial companies used cheap financing and high valuations to consolidate. The ICBC IPO signaled China's arrival in global capital markets; the NYSE-Euronext agreement signaled exchange consolidation; Arcelor-Mittal signaled global steel consolidation. Forward linkage: corporate globalization continued, but the leverage and risk transfer mechanisms used in 2006 became stress points in 2007-2008.

**7. Housing cycles diverged, but the U.S. cycle turned.** U.S. residential investment weakened and subprime mortgage stress began to receive explicit attention. Spain, the U.K., Canada, Australia, the Netherlands, and some emerging markets still had strong property or construction cycles. Forward linkage: the U.S. housing downturn became the first major channel through which financial fragility emerged.

#### 3B. Monetary & Financial Conditions

The United States tightened first and hardest among the largest economies. The FOMC raised the federal funds target to 5.00 percent in May and 5.25 percent in June, then paused as housing cooled. The yield curve flattened and inverted at points, showing markets expected slower growth or future easing. U.S. equities recovered from a spring correction and the S&P 500 price index gained about 13.6 percent for the year, while total return was about 15.8 percent.

The euro area moved from accommodative toward neutral. The ECB lifted the main refinancing rate to 3.50 percent in December after multiple increases during the year. Germany's recovery was especially strong, and euro-area credit growth remained robust. The Bank of England lifted Bank Rate to 5.00 percent by November and maintained it in December. The Bank of Japan ended zero rates in July, raising the overnight call rate target to 0.25 percent, but Japanese policy remained highly accommodative by global standards.

Financial markets remained confident. BIS analysis for the period emphasizes strong profits, buybacks, mergers and acquisitions, low credit spreads, and strong risk appetite. There was a May-June correction in risky assets, particularly emerging-market equities and commodities, but it was short-lived. This was a classic late-cycle market pattern: investors recognized inflation and credit risks but continued to price them as manageable.

#### 3C. Trade & Geopolitics

Trade volumes were one of the brightest indicators of 2006. Asia led export volume growth, with China continuing to gain manufacturing share. The United States, Europe, Japan, and emerging markets all participated in the upswing. The stalling of the WTO Doha Round was the year's most important trade-policy failure. It did not stop trade growth in 2006, but it showed that global liberalization through a single multilateral package had become politically harder.

Geopolitical shocks had direct economic channels. The Russia-Ukraine gas dispute in January briefly affected European gas supply and showed the strategic risk embedded in pipeline energy dependence. The Middle East remained central to oil risk premia. Iran-related concerns and other supply risks supported oil and gold. OPEC responded to falling oil prices late in the year with production cuts, including a 1.2 million barrel per day cut taking effect in November.

#### 3D. Commodities & Energy

Energy was central. Crude oil prices reached new highs in mid-2006, then fell in the autumn as demand concerns increased and inventories improved. OPEC's late-year cuts aimed to stabilize the market. Natural gas politics were visible in Europe through the Russia-Ukraine dispute. Coal prices were relatively stable compared with oil and metals, but energy-intensive industries faced elevated input costs.

Metals were the clearest signal of capacity pressure. The World Bank reported copper, nickel, and zinc at all-time highs in May due to low stocks, capacity constraints, and supply disruptions. Mining investment, exploration, and resource nationalism all became more important. Agriculture was mixed: many markets were well supplied, but sugar and rubber saw sharp price moves linked to energy prices and biofuel demand.

#### 3E. Labor & Demographics

Labor markets improved in most advanced economies, but definitions remain non-comparable. The U.S. unemployment rate averaged about 4.6 percent. Japan's rate was around 4.1 percent. Germany still had high unemployment but was improving as export strength and labor-market reforms began to show results. Spain and Italy had lower unemployment than in earlier years but still had structural joblessness. China and India continued absorbing labor into manufacturing, construction, services, and urban employment, though official unemployment measures do not capture underemployment well.

Demographically, advanced economies faced aging, while emerging Asia still had rising working-age populations or large labor reservoirs. Migration mattered economically: eastern European labor mobility into the U.K., Ireland, and other EU economies after the 2004 enlargement continued to shape wages and services, while remittances and emigration were significant for Mexico, India, China, Turkey, Russia's neighbors, and parts of eastern Europe.

#### 5B. Corporate landscape

The corporate landscape was dominated by oil, retail, autos, finance, and heavy industry. The Fortune Global 500 2007 ranking, based on 2006 revenues, placed Wal-Mart first, Exxon Mobil second, Royal Dutch Shell third, BP fourth, Toyota fifth, Chevron sixth, ING seventh, Total eighth, General Motors ninth, and ConocoPhillips tenth. The mix tells the story of 2006: high oil prices, global retail scale, autos under pressure, and finance expanding rapidly.

Major corporate events included AT&T's acquisition of BellSouth, a transaction valued at USD 67 billion at announcement by the U.S. Department of Justice and widely reported at USD 86 billion at closing depending on valuation basis; Mittal's acquisition of Arcelor, creating the largest steelmaker; ICBC's IPO of about USD 21.9 billion, then the largest global IPO; and the NYSE-Euronext merger agreement. Other important transactions included Freeport-McMoRan's announced purchase of Phelps Dodge, Boston Scientific's acquisition of Guidant, and Tata Steel's pursuit of Corus, announced in 2006 and completed in 2007.

Bankruptcies and failures were not yet the defining story, but early stress existed in U.S. mortgage finance. The bigger story was balance-sheet expansion, structured finance, private-equity leverage, and international listings. With hindsight, 2006 was the year when risk was being transferred and hidden rather than eliminated.

#### 5C. Innovation & technology

The economic technology story was not one single breakthrough but diffusion. Mobile telephony expanded rapidly in China, India, Brazil, Turkey, and Russia. Broadband penetration rose in advanced economies. Semiconductors, consumer electronics, LCD panels, telecom equipment, and software services drove global supply chains. India strengthened its IT and outsourcing role; China moved from low-end assembly toward more complex electronics and capital goods.

Digital platforms were still early relative to the 2010s. Google, Apple, Microsoft, Yahoo, Amazon, eBay, Samsung, Nokia, Ericsson, Sony, and telecom carriers were major actors. The iPhone had not yet launched, so the smartphone platform shift was still just ahead. R&D spending was concentrated in the U.S., Japan, Germany, Korea, Switzerland, Sweden, and large multinationals.

Infrastructure megaprojects mattered in China, India, the Gulf outside this top-20 list, and parts of Europe. Ports, airports, highways, power networks, and urban real estate absorbed large capital flows.

#### 5D. Labor markets & human capital

The global labor market was split. Advanced economies saw improving employment but wage restraint in many sectors. Germany's wage moderation improved competitiveness. The U.S. labor market was tight by unemployment measures, but manufacturing employment remained pressured. Japan's labor market improved, yet wages stayed weak.

Emerging economies absorbed workers into cities, construction, manufacturing, services, and retail. China and India had enormous labor reallocation from agriculture to urban sectors, but official unemployment data understate underemployment. Migration shaped labor supply in the U.K., Spain, Canada, the U.S., Russia, and Gulf economies outside this top-20 ranking. Union power was generally weaker than in earlier decades, though France, Italy, Korea, and parts of Latin America maintained visible labor mobilization.

The most important equity theme was not just performance but risk appetite. The May-June correction showed vulnerability to inflation, rate, and liquidity fears, but the rebound showed investors still believed in the global expansion.

#### 6B. Fixed income

The U.S. Treasury curve flattened and inverted during parts of 2006 as the Fed reached 5.25 percent and markets priced slower growth. German Bund yields rose with ECB tightening. Japanese government bond yields rose from very low levels but remained low globally. Credit spreads were tight, especially in investment grade, high yield, leveraged loans, and structured credit. This was one of the key warning signs with hindsight: the market was being paid little for taking increasing leverage and maturity risk.

Sovereign debt stress was limited among the top 20 in 2006. Emerging-market spreads generally compressed after recovering from the mid-year sell-off. Brazil, Russia, Turkey, and Mexico all benefited from better macro frameworks than in the 1990s, but Turkey remained sensitive to global risk appetite.

#### 6C. Currencies

The dollar weakened against the euro during the year after earlier strength. The yen remained relatively weak and was used in carry trades. Sterling was firm, reflecting U.K. rates and financial inflows. Commodity currencies such as the Canadian dollar and Australian dollar were supported by resource prices. China's renminbi appreciated gradually under the post-2005 managed-float framework, but not enough to end trade tensions.

Emerging-market currencies experienced a sharp but temporary stress episode in May-June. Turkey was one of the most affected among the top 20, while Brazil and Russia were cushioned by commodities and reserve accumulation.

#### 6D. Alternative assets

Gold averaged about USD 604 per troy ounce and reached about USD 725, reflecting oil prices, inflation concerns, geopolitical risk, and dollar weakness. Real estate was the most important alternative asset class for households. U.S. housing turned down; Spain, the U.K., Australia, Canada, and parts of emerging Asia were still strong. Commercial real estate benefited from easy credit and private-equity flows. Commodities became a larger institutional asset class through index products and commodity-linked strategies.

### 7. RISKS, CRISES & BLACK SWANS

Active risks in 2006 included U.S. housing deterioration, subprime mortgage credit quality, high oil prices, geopolitical energy disruptions, inflation pressure, the Doha Round suspension, emerging-market capital-flow volatility, and compressed credit spreads. The Russia-Ukraine gas dispute was a live energy-security shock. The May-June sell-off in equities, commodities, and emerging markets was a near-miss that showed risk appetite could reverse quickly.

With hindsight, the most important vulnerability was the U.S. mortgage and structured-credit chain. At the time, many observers saw subprime as a contained segment. Later evidence showed that mortgage securitization, wholesale funding, bank and broker-dealer leverage, rating-agency assumptions, and global demand for AAA structured products created systemic channels.

Another hindsight risk was European bank exposure to global credit. Banks in Germany, Switzerland, the U.K., Belgium, the Netherlands, and France looked profitable in 2006, but several had large structured-credit and wholesale-funding exposures. Spain's risks were more domestic: real estate, cajas, construction employment, and external deficits.

Risks that did not materialize in 2006 included a hard landing in China, a generalized oil-supply crisis, a collapse in the dollar, a major sovereign debt crisis among the top 20, or a global recession. Instead, the year ended with markets optimistic and most macro indicators strong.

### 8. YEAR-OVER-YEAR DELTA (2006 vs. 2005)

Compared with 2005, 2006 was faster globally and more late-cycle financially. World GDP growth remained above trend, and merchandise trade growth accelerated. Commodity prices moved higher, especially oil and metals, although oil fell from its mid-year peak by autumn. China became more visibly central to world demand, not only as an exporter but as a commodity and capital-market force.

The main policy shift was monetary normalization. The Fed moved from 4.25 percent at end-2005 to 5.25 percent by mid-2006. The ECB moved from 2.25 percent at end-2005 to 3.50 percent by end-2006. The Bank of Japan left zero rates. The Bank of England, RBA, Riksbank, and many emerging-market central banks tightened or remained restrictive. Liquidity was still ample, but the cost of funds rose.

The main structural break was in U.S. housing. In 2005 the housing boom still looked like a driver of household wealth and construction employment. In 2006 it became a drag on GDP and the first clear source of credit deterioration. This did not yet dominate global markets, but it changed the risk map.

In trade policy, 2006 was worse than 2005 because the Doha Round suspension signaled an institutional failure. In corporate finance, 2006 was more aggressive than 2005: larger IPOs, larger M&A, more exchange consolidation, stronger private equity, and broader leveraged finance.

Economic power continued shifting. The United States remained dominant, but China moved rapidly up the nominal GDP ranking and became more central in trade, commodities, and capital markets. India gained visibility as a services and domestic-demand growth story. Russia and Brazil benefited from the resource cycle. Germany regained strength within Europe.

### 9. DATA CONFIDENCE & GAPS

Highest-confidence data in this assessment are current-dollar GDP ranking values for countries where World Bank WITS summary pages returned explicit 2006 GDP values, global GDP from World Bank WITS, global growth and trade from IMF and WTO, major central-bank policy rates from official central-bank releases, and commodity annual averages from World Bank Pink Sheet data.

Medium-confidence data include country-level inflation, unemployment, current-account balances, debt, and fiscal balances. These are usually available from IMF WEO, OECD, Eurostat, World Bank, and national statistical agencies, but vintages and definitions differ. Unemployment is especially non-comparable: registered urban unemployment in China, household-survey unemployment in advanced economies, and informality-heavy labor markets in India, Mexico, Brazil, and Turkey do not measure the same economic condition.

Lower-confidence or explicitly incomplete items include exact 2006 reserve-currency shares, some country budget-balance measures, some government debt measures for emerging economies, exact annual average copper and agricultural staple prices in the retrieved primary source set, and full comparable sectoral employment by country. These are marked as Data not available where the retrieved source set did not provide a defensible number.

The top-20 GDP ranking is nominal, current-dollar, market-exchange-rate based. It is not PPP GDP and not real GDP. Exchange-rate movements can change nominal rankings even when real output rankings do not change. For non-market economies this caveat would be larger, but the 2006 top 20 does not include the Soviet Union or pre-transition centrally planned economies. China's GDP figures are official national-account data in WDI/IMF, but analysts still debate local-government reporting incentives and later revisions.

The weakest claims are those involving corporate market capitalization, exact sectoral global performance, and some financial-market returns for non-U.S. indices where exchange, total-return, and price-index series differ. In those cases the document uses rounded or qualitative language and flags approximate values.
